# TRUSTPILOT: NAVIGATING THE INTEGRITY–MONETISATION PARADOX ON A TWO-SIDED PLATFORM

## A Strategic Management Case Study

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Trustpilot occupies a structurally privileged but strategically precarious position in the online review economy. Founded in 2007, the platform has grown to host over 300 million reviews, achieved statutory profitability in 2024, and is now capitalising on a new wave of AI-driven search visibility. Yet its core business model harbours a deep, unresolved tension: Trustpilot sells subscription services to the very businesses whose reputations its consumer-facing platform adjudicates. This case study terms this the **Integrity–Monetisation Paradox** and analyses it using three interlocking frameworks — Two-Sided Platform / Network Effects Theory, Porter's Five Forces, and SWOT analysis, supported by a Value Chain lens — before deriving a coherent five-point action plan. The reasoning chain is explicit throughout: each analytical finding feeds the next, and each recommendation is traceable to a specific analytical conclusion.

## SECTION 1: BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### 1.1 Company Overview

Trustpilot operates primarily on a subscription-based revenue model, catering to the diverse needs of businesses ranging from small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) to large corporations. It operates an open, two-sided review platform where consumers submit reviews of recent experiences and businesses claim profiles, invite reviews, and showcase ratings to boost conversion and trust. Its platform links verified feedback with business discovery and performance marketing.

### 1.2 Financial Trajectory: From Growth-at-all-Costs to Profitable Scale

Trustpilot's financial arc over 2023–2025 is the backdrop against which the strategic situation must be read.

Revenue grew 18% in constant currency to $210.7 million in FY2024 (FY2023: $176.4 million), and the company delivered a record adjusted EBITDA of $24.1 million (FY2023: $15.5 million). Profit before tax was $5.2 million (FY2023: $1.9 million loss). Trustpilot saw a 21% increase in bookings and record net dollar retention. The company reached $250 million in annual recurring revenue.

By FY2025, the trajectory had accelerated further. Full-year revenues for 2025 were £261.1 million, up 24%, while adjusted EBITDA was £40.7m, a rise of 69%, and profit before tax was £14.1m, up 172%.

### 1.3 The Strategic Situation: The Integrity–Monetisation Paradox

Despite this financial momentum, a defining structural tension runs through the business. Trustpilot always faced a fundamental conflict in its business model as a third-party review site that generates its revenues from selling subscriptions to companies. Trustpilot must balance the integrity of reviews with the commercial imperative to serve paying subscribers. The clearest symptom of this tension is the scale of fake review activity on the platform.

Trustpilot removed 4.5 million fake reviews — amounting to 7.4% of the total number of reviews submitted in 2024. In 2023, it was 6.1% — demonstrating how its technology is scaling and improving as the number of reviews on the platform increases.

Simultaneously, a new strategic dimension has emerged. A strategic focus within the business drove a dramatic rise in Large Language Models (LLM) exposure, with click-throughs from AI search up 1,490%. The site ranked as the fifth most cited domain globally on ChatGPT in January 2026. Authentic, high-volume review data has become structural infrastructure for AI systems — but only if integrity is maintained. This is the central strategic tension this case study analyses.

## SECTION 2: ANALYTICAL FRAMEWORKS

### 2.1 Two-Sided Platform Analysis and Network Effects

**Framework Rationale:** Trustpilot is, at its core, a two-sided platform. Every strategic choice — pricing, openness, enforcement — flows from platform dynamics, so this is the foundational framework.

#### The Two Sides and Indirect Network Effects

Trustpilot operates an open, two-sided review platform where consumers submit reviews of recent experiences and businesses claim profiles, invite reviews, and showcase ratings to boost conversion and trust. This creates classic **indirect (cross-side) network effects**: more consumer reviews make the platform more useful to businesses, incentivising subscription spend; more businesses actively managing their profiles increases the platform's SEO authority, attracting more consumers. Trustpilot aggregates verified reviews, boosts visibility in search, and sells subscription tools that help companies capture, analyse, and act on customer sentiment; this flywheel underpins its monetisation and growth.

#### The Open-Platform Decision and Its Consequences

Trustpilot resolved the classic two-sided platform bootstrapping problem by making the platform open: anyone can leave a review at any time, without a verified purchase. Anyone with a genuine experience can leave a review at any time. This maximised review volume — 61 million reviews were posted in 2024, a 15% year-on-year increase — and accelerated network effects. However, it created the integrity vulnerability that defines the strategic situation: an open platform is inherently more susceptible to manipulation than a closed, verified-purchase model.

#### Monetisation Asymmetry and the Root of the Paradox

Consistent with two-sided platform theory, Trustpilot subsidises the consumer side (free access) and charges the business side. SaaS subscription tiers (Standard, Growth, Scale) form the backbone of the Trustpilot business model. Usage-based fees for review invitations raise Average Revenue Per Tenant as volume grows. This is rational — businesses have a higher willingness to pay for reputational and conversion benefits. However, it creates a structural conflict of interest: the paying side has a financial incentive to manipulate the very content that the non-paying side relies upon for impartial information.

Companies that can afford to pay for premium services may exploit the platform to create a misleadingly positive image, thereby jeopardising the integrity of the review process.

#### Scale as a Compounding Integrity Advantage

A critical feature of Trustpilot's platform is that scale itself improves integrity — a virtuous cycle that smaller competitors cannot replicate. Crucially, 90% of those 4.5 million fake reviews were removed automatically by technology that uses machine learning, neural networks, and generative AI to identify patterns linked to fabricated content. Implementation of this technology, used in conjunction with Trustpilot's specialist teams, led to 53% more reviews being removed automatically than in 2023. This means the data moat deepens with scale: more reviews → better AI training data → better fraud detection → higher platform integrity → more consumer trust → more reviews. It is a structural advantage that late entrants cannot easily replicate.

### 2.2 Porter's Five Forces Analysis

**Framework Rationale:** Having established the platform's structural mechanics, Porter's Five Forces situates Trustpilot within its competitive industry context, identifying where its position is strong and where it is exposed.

#### Force 1: Competitive Rivalry — HIGH

Trustpilot is an open review platform that comes with a number of features to help you collect reviews from your customers, and a brand that sort of helps prospective customers believe those reviews are legitimate. However, there is zero discoverability potential. This structural limitation — Trustpilot's traffic is almost exclusively brand-search traffic — exposes it to intense rivalry from platforms with broader utility.

The competitive landscape of Trustpilot now combines regulatory resilience, AI capability, and social integration as differentiators versus peers like Google Reviews, Yelp, and emerging niche sites. In the B2B software review niche, rivalry has intensified through consolidation: on January 29, 2026, G2 acquired several of its competitors: Capterra, Software Advice, and GetApp.

#### Force 2: Threat of New Entrants — MODERATE

The review platform industry has significant network-effect-based barriers to entry: Trustpilot's corpus of over 300 million reviews represents a data moat that is difficult to replicate. Trustpilot's competitive edge derives from an open, high-authority platform with a continuously refreshed review corpus, strong integrity systems that detect and remove fraudulent reviews, and network effects that increase search ranking and merchant retention. However, AI-native review aggregators and first-party review tools represent credible new-entrant threats. The threat is moderate rather than high because of the scale advantages incumbents possess.

#### Force 3: Bargaining Power of Buyers (Business Subscribers) — MODERATE-HIGH

Paid plans start at $299/month for businesses, and many alternatives exist. Google reviews are critical to being found on Google Maps. They are also made visible in the knowledge panel when googling a brand. Multi-homing (businesses collecting reviews on multiple platforms simultaneously) is common, limiting switching costs. The countervailing factor is Trustpilot's Google partnership: Trustpilot offers a feature called Google Store Ratings, which allows reviews collected by Trustpilot to be displayed in Google Ads. These reviews appear under your search ads and in Google Shopping, providing additional visibility and credibility. This integration creates meaningful switching costs for e-commerce subscribers.

#### Force 4: Bargaining Power of Suppliers — LOW

Trustpilot's primary "suppliers" are consumers who write reviews voluntarily and for free. Since consumer participation is free and Trustpilot has no contractual obligation to individual reviewers, supplier power is structurally low. The platform's open model means it does not depend on any single source of content.

#### Force 5: Threat of Substitutes — HIGH

This is the most significant force threatening Trustpilot's long-term position. The number one reason people look at reviews is usually to be found more on platforms like Google Maps, Yelp, TripAdvisor, Amazon, etc. Google Reviews functions as a near-perfect substitute for most consumer use cases and is free for businesses. By self-preferencing its own offerings, Google increases its share of users and advertising revenue at the expense of the consumer experience and local search rivals. This structural advantage — Google Reviews embedded within the world's dominant search engine — is the most acute substitution threat Trustpilot faces.

AI-generated summaries add a second substitution dimension: if AI systems present review syntheses directly to consumers, the need to visit Trustpilot.com may diminish. Paradoxically, however, Trustpilot's review corpus is a primary data source for those same AI systems.

**Porter's Summary:** The online review platform industry is structurally challenging — high rivalry, high substitution threat, and moderate buyer power. Trustpilot's survival and growth depend on building competitive advantages that transcend commodity review hosting. The framework points toward two strategic imperatives: (1) deepen the data and integrity moat to make the platform irreplaceable for AI systems; and (2) increase switching costs for business subscribers through deeper integrations and analytics. Both imperatives require resolving, not merely managing, the Integrity–Monetisation Paradox.

## SECTION 4: RECOMMENDATIONS AND ACTION PLAN

The five recommendations below address the identified challenges in a coherent, sequenced manner. They are ordered by urgency and interdependence: Recommendation 1 creates the governance foundation on which all others rest.

### Recommendation 1: Structurally Separate Integrity Enforcement from Commercial Operations

**The Problem (traced to analysis):** The Integrity–Monetisation Paradox (Two-Sided Platform analysis) is not merely a perception problem — it is a structural governance problem. As long as enforcement teams report to the same organisational hierarchy as revenue teams, there is an inherent conflict of interest that will periodically manifest in enforcement decisions and will always be available as a reputational attack vector. Trustpilot always faced a fundamental conflict in its business model as a third-party review site that generates its revenues from selling subscriptions to companies.

**The Recommendation:** Establish an independent **Trust & Integrity Board** — analogous to an audit committee — with external members including consumer advocates, academics, and former regulators, who have oversight of content moderation policies and enforcement decisions. The Chief Trust Officer should report directly to this Board, not to the CEO. Publish quarterly enforcement statistics disaggregated by subscriber tier to demonstrate that paid status does not influence enforcement outcomes.

**Strategic Logic:** This converts a structural vulnerability into a competitive differentiator: Trustpilot becomes the only major review platform with independently audited enforcement. This is particularly important as AI systems begin to assess the trustworthiness of their data sources — a platform with audited integrity will command premium data value. Trustpilot was cited 27 times in the FTC's final ruling — it should leverage that credibility by leading on governance, not merely on enforcement technology.

---

### Recommendation 2: Introduce Verified-Purchase Integration as a Premium Tier Feature

**The Problem (traced to analysis):** The open platform model (W2 in SWOT) means reviews cannot be verified as genuine purchase experiences. Closed, verified-purchase competitors (Feefo, Reviews.io) offer higher credibility to businesses in certain verticals. Trustpilot's open nature means anyone can leave a review without an invitation, which has led to persistent concerns about fake reviews.

**The Recommendation:** Develop a **Verified Purchase Badge** programme, integrated with major e-commerce platforms (Shopify, Salesforce Commerce Cloud, WooCommerce), allowing businesses to tag reviews as verified purchases. This should be offered as a premium feature within the Scale subscription tier. Consumer-facing profiles should clearly distinguish verified-purchase reviews from open reviews, enabling consumers to filter by verification status.

**Strategic Logic:** This recommendation addresses the open-model weakness without abandoning it. It creates a tiered credibility architecture: open reviews provide volume and network effects; verified reviews provide premium credibility for high-stakes decisions. Critically, it also increases switching costs for Scale-tier subscribers — directly addressing the moderate-high buyer power identified in Porter's Force 3. Enterprise NLP and sentiment tools increase willingness to pay and lock in higher-tier clients — verified purchase integration extends this logic to the review collection layer itself.

---

### Recommendation 3: Launch a Dedicated "AI Trust Signals" Product Suite

**The Problem (traced to analysis):** The AI-era opportunity (O1 and O2 in SWOT) is currently monetised only indirectly. Businesses do not yet have tools to understand, optimise, or measure their Trustpilot visibility in AI search — despite this being the most rapidly growing source of consumer discovery.

**The Recommendation:** Launch a dedicated **AI Visibility Intelligence** product suite built on the "3Rs" framework (Recency, Relevance, Ranking). Trustpilot introduced new tools built on the "3Rs" framework — recency, relevance and ranking — enabling businesses to strengthen trust signals and improve visibility across AI-driven discovery. This suite should include:
- An **AI Search Dashboard** showing businesses how often they are cited by ChatGPT, Perplexity, Claude, and Google AI Overviews.
- **Review Recency Optimisation** tools, since user reviews affect AI citations through review volume and recency, which indicate that a brand is actively operating and generating real customer experiences.
- **Sentiment Theme Analysis** showing which review themes are most frequently cited by AI systems.
- **Competitor Benchmarking** in AI search visibility.

**Strategic Logic:** This converts the AI-era opportunity into a concrete, monetisable product. It creates a new premium tier above Scale, targeting enterprise clients. It also deepens the data moat: businesses using the AI Visibility Intelligence suite become more deeply integrated with Trustpilot and face higher switching costs — directly addressing the moderate-high buyer power (Porter's Force 3) and the substitution threat (Force 5) by making Trustpilot irreplaceable for AI-era marketing.

### Recommendation 4: Develop a Proactive DSA Compliance Framework to Protect European Review Integrity

**The Problem (traced to analysis):** German businesses systematically exploit Digital Services Act notification mechanisms to remove critical reviews, while platforms like Google, TripAdvisor, and Trustpilot err on caution to avoid legal liability. This threatens the integrity of Trustpilot's European corpus — its strongest market — and represents the most acute external threat to platform integrity (T2 in SWOT, W4 in SWOT).

**The Recommendation:** Develop a **DSA Integrity Shield** programme:
- Establish a dedicated legal team specialising in DSA Article 16 challenges, with the mandate to contest frivolous removal requests rather than defaulting to removal.
- Create a **Trusted Reviewer** verification system for consumers who have a track record of genuine, non-retracted reviews, giving their reviews additional procedural protection against business challenges.
- Publish a DSA Enforcement Transparency Report showing the proportion of Article 16 challenges contested and upheld, disaggregated by country.
- Engage proactively with the European Commission and national Digital Services Coordinators to advocate for amendments to Article 16 that require businesses to provide stronger evidence before reviews are removed.

**Strategic Logic:** Systematic abuse of EU Digital Services Act Article 16 reporting mechanisms creates artificial rating inflation across review platforms. If left unaddressed, this will systematically erode consumer trust in Trustpilot's European ratings — the foundation of the platform's value proposition in its core market. This recommendation also reinforces Recommendation 1 by demonstrating that Trustpilot will defend consumer interests even under regulatory pressure, converting compliance into a competitive differentiator.

### Recommendation 5: Accelerate North American Penetration Through Vertical-Specific Partnerships

**The Problem (traced to analysis):** Trustpilot's brand recognition and market share in North America significantly lags Europe. Trustpilot's ability to drive sustained margin improvements and solidify its position in North America suggests that its upward trajectory has significant runway — but the US market is dominated by Google Reviews and Yelp, making broad consumer brand-awareness campaigns costly and slow.

**The Recommendation:** Pursue a **vertical-specific partnership strategy** in North America, targeting sectors where Trustpilot has demonstrated European strength and where Google Reviews and Yelp are weakest — specifically fintech, e-commerce, B2B services, and healthcare/telemedicine. In 2024, Deloitte announced a strategic partnership with Trustpilot to integrate review analytics into its broader customer experience management (CEM) solutions. This partnership should be the template: embed Trustpilot data into enterprise workflows rather than competing head-on for consumer mindshare.

Pair this with a **consumer awareness campaign** positioning Trustpilot as the "verified, independent" alternative to Google Reviews — leveraging the FTC's 2024 rule to highlight integrity advantages.

**Strategic Logic:** Vertical-specific partnerships solve the chicken-and-egg problem (Two-Sided Platform analysis) in targeted US segments without requiring broad consumer brand awareness. The company achieved a record net dollar retention rate and a 21% increase in bookings, particularly in the UK, US, Germany, and Italy — confirming that US market momentum is building and that targeted investment is warranted. Geographic expansion, particularly in the US, accelerates ARR and diversifies revenue concentration. With $68.9 million in cash at year-end 2024 and $43 million returned to shareholders via buybacks, Trustpilot has the financial firepower to fund this investment without compromising its balance sheet.

The Integrity–Monetisation Paradox — the structural conflict between adjudicating business reputations and selling services to those businesses — is not a problem to be eliminated but a tension to be governed. The platform's value is generated precisely because it sits at the intersection of consumer trust and business utility.

The analytical frameworks applied in this case study converge on a clear conclusion: Trustpilot's long-term competitive advantage lies not in the volume of reviews it hosts, but in the **trustworthiness of those reviews**. In the age of AI, trust is more important than ever. In the AI era, where Trustpilot's data is increasingly consumed by large language models, integrity is not just an ethical obligation — it is a strategic imperative.
