# Manchester United: From Brand to Burden — A Strategic Case Study

## The Strategic Situation

Manchester United Football Club (MUFC) presents one of the most instructive paradoxes in contemporary sports management: a club whose commercial assets remain world-class, yet whose on-field and financial performance has entered a prolonged crisis. This case study identifies the core strategic situation as a **misalignment between the club's brand-derived resource base and its governance and operational architecture**, which has systematically destroyed value over more than a decade. The analysis proceeds through five frameworks — the Resource-Based View (RBV), VRIO analysis, Porter's Five Forces, a Stakeholder Map, and a SWOT analysis — before developing a coherent action plan. Each inferential step is warranted by attributable evidence.

---

## 1. Background and Context

Manchester United's 141-year heritage has produced 66 trophies, including a record 20 English league titles, enabling the club to develop what it describes as one of the world's leading sports brands and a global community of 1.1 billion fans and followers. Forbes valued Manchester United at approximately $6.5 billion in 2024, reflecting unmatched recognition and sponsor appeal.

Yet this brand power has not translated into sustained sporting or financial health. In 2023–24, a campaign marred by injuries saw the club finish eighth in the Premier League — their worst position since 1989–90, with a negative goal difference. It was the club's second season under Erik ten Hag. The following season was worse: after joining in November 2024 from Sporting, Ruben Amorim oversaw United's lowest Premier League finish, ending the 2024–25 season in 15th place with just 42 points. United also lost the Europa League final to Tottenham Hotspur in Bilbao.

Financially, the club achieved record revenues for 2024–25 of £666.5m — yet still made an overall loss of £33m. The club's annual accounts showed they earned greater revenue than ever before despite not having Champions League football, finishing 15th in the Premier League — their lowest top-flight finish in 51 years.

The governance context is equally troubled. In February 2024, Sir Jim Ratcliffe completed a deal to purchase a 27.7% stake in the club. The Glazer family, which has owned Manchester United since 2005, retain a majority stake under the terms of the agreement. Ratcliffe and INEOS took responsibility for football operations.

## 2. Framework 1: Resource-Based View (RBV) and VRIO Analysis

The RBV, developed by Barney (1991), holds that sustained competitive advantage derives from resources that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Organised (VRIO). Applying this to Manchester United reveals a critical structural problem: the club's most powerful resources are brand-based, not football-operational — and the organisation has failed to exploit its brand resources through sporting performance.

### 2.1 Valuable and Rare Resources: The Brand

Manchester United's brand is genuinely rare and valuable. The club claims a global community of 1.1 billion fans and followers. This scale generates commercial revenues that few rivals can match: the shirt sponsorship agreement with Qualcomm, effective 2024–25, is reportedly worth $75.0m per year, versus the prior TeamViewer agreement that reportedly generated £47.0m per year. Manchester United and Adidas extended their kit deal until 2035 in what is the biggest kit deal in Premier League history; Adidas will pay United a total of £900 million over the new 10-year contract.

**Warrant:** These figures confirm that the brand resource is both valuable (it generates premium commercial income) and rare (no other Premier League club commands equivalent shirt or kit fees). This satisfies the first two VRIO criteria.

### 2.2 Inimitability: Partially Sustained

The brand's heritage — 20 league titles, two Champions League trophies, the mythology of Munich and the Busby Babes — is historically inimitable. However, inimitability is being eroded. Manchester United's commercial revenue remained the club's largest revenue segment, but the club was second among its domestic 'Big 6' peer group for 2022–23 and 2023–24, and is expected to be fourth for 2024–25 as Manchester City and Liverpool each exceed £700m. Rivals are closing the commercial gap precisely because on-field performance is no longer reinforcing brand equity.

**Warrant:** The erosion of commercial rank from second to fourth within two years demonstrates that brand inimitability is contingent on sporting credibility. When performance deteriorates, the brand's premium weakens, undermining the inimitability condition.

### 2.3 Organisation: The Critical Failure

This is where VRIO analysis exposes the core strategic problem. Organisation — the ability to exploit resources through systems, processes, and governance — is severely deficient. From David Moyes to Ruben Amorim, United have gone through 10 permanent and interim managers in just 13 years, a stark contrast to the consistency and dominance under Ferguson's remarkable 27-year reign. Since Ferguson's departure in 2013, the Red Devils have sunk around $99.9 million into compensation packages for six permanent coaches and their respective support staff.

**Warrant:** Managerial churn is a direct measure of organisational failure. Each sacking destroys accumulated tactical and cultural capital, resets squad development, and imposes direct financial costs — all of which prevent the brand resource from being exploited into sporting results. The VRIO framework makes explicit why United's brand has not produced competitive advantage: the 'O' condition is unmet.

## 3. Framework 2: Porter's Five Forces

Porter's (1980) Five Forces model analyses the competitive environment in which a firm operates. Applied to Manchester United's position within the Premier League ecosystem, it reveals that competitive intensity has structurally increased while United's relative position has weakened.

### 3.1 Rivalry Among Existing Competitors (HIGH)

The Premier League's competitive intensity has risen sharply. United have failed to win the Premier League since Ferguson's exit in 2013. Meanwhile, Manchester City (backed by Abu Dhabi sovereign wealth), Liverpool (owned by Fenway Sports Group with a sophisticated data-driven model), and Arsenal (with sustained investment under Arteta) have all developed structural advantages. Manchester City and Liverpool each exceeded £700m in revenue in 2024–25, giving them transfer war chests and wage capacities that United, burdened by debt, cannot match.

**Warrant:** Revenue superiority among rivals translates directly into player acquisition capacity. Because player quality is the primary input to on-field performance, United's relative financial weakness intensifies the competitive threat from rivals — a classic Five Forces dynamic where resource asymmetry compounds rivalry.

### 3.2 Threat of New Entrants (MODERATE but RISING)

Traditional barriers to entry in top-flight football (promotion systems, stadium assets, fan loyalty) are high. However, state-backed ownership models represent a structural new-entrant threat. Manchester City and Newcastle United (Saudi PIF-backed) have accessed capital that bypasses conventional financial constraints. UEFA is phasing in a new Squad Cost Control Ratio system that will eventually limit clubs to spending 70% of turnover on wages, transfers, and agent fees. While this regulation may constrain state-backed clubs, its effect on United depends on revenue recovery: United's revenue of approximately £648m would give them a limit of £453.6m under the new rules, with their wage bill of £331m leaving £122.6m to spend on amortisation.

**Warrant:** The SCR rules simultaneously constrain United's rivals and United itself. Given United's current revenue trajectory (falling relative to peers), the rules may prove more restrictive for United than for City or Liverpool — making the new-entrant threat from state-backed clubs a durable structural risk.

### 3.3 Bargaining Power of Suppliers (HIGH)

The primary 'suppliers' to a football club are elite players and their agents. Top-tier player wages have escalated significantly across European football. United's wage bill stood at £331m, representing a substantial proportion of revenue. The club's weak negotiating position — demonstrated by its inability to attract elite free agents during periods of Europa League football — reflects high supplier power.

### 3.4 Bargaining Power of Buyers (LOW but SHIFTING)

Fans exhibit low price sensitivity and high switching costs — the Premier League analysis literature confirms that "the Premier League acts like a monopoly, with low consumer elasticity due to high switching costs for fans." However, fan anger continues to be focused on the Glazers, who refuse to sell their controlling stake, with hostile chants from supporters in recent weeks. Declining attendee satisfaction and growing supporter activism represent a slow but material risk to matchday and commercial revenue if the brand is further damaged.

### 3.5 Threat of Substitutes (LOW)

Football faces limited direct substitution from other sports in its core markets. However, the proliferation of streaming entertainment competes for attention, particularly among younger demographics. The club's expanding social media presence — with approximately 65.2 million monthly global searches — is a critical tool for retaining younger fans, but this asset is also eroding under prolonged poor performance.

## 4. Framework 3: SWOT Analysis

The SWOT analysis synthesises the internal and external analyses, linking them to the strategic situation.

### Strengths

**S1 – Global Brand and Commercial Scale:** Forbes valued Manchester United at approximately $6.5 billion in 2024. Commercial revenue of £333.3m in 2024–25 remains the club's largest revenue segment.

**S2 – Long-term Commercial Contracts:** The Adidas deal worth £900m over 10 years (to 2035) is the biggest kit deal in Premier League history, providing revenue certainty regardless of short-term performance.

**S3 – Stadium Capacity:** Old Trafford remains the largest club stadium in English football with a capacity of 74,197; in 2024–25, the club averaged 73,747 fans per game — effectively a sell-out every match. Matchday revenue is structurally robust.

**S4 – New Ownership Direction:** Sir Jim Ratcliffe acquired a 27.7% stake and took responsibility for football operations in February 2024, bringing professional management discipline.

### Weaknesses

**W1 – Structural Debt Burden:** By the end of 2024–25, total debt had risen to £637 million, partially offset by £86 million in cash reserves. Servicing this debt costs the club around £36 million annually in interest. Manchester United have paid a total of more than £1bn on interest fees generated from the debt piled onto the club by the Glazer family since the 2005 leveraged buyout.

**W2 – Managerial Instability:** United have gone through 10 permanent and interim managers in just 13 years. This has prevented any coherent playing identity from developing.

**W3 – Flawed Transfer Strategy:** In total, Manchester United spent £1.53 billion on transfers in the decade to 2024 and brought in £398.4 million in income, leaving a net spend of £1.13 billion — yet have not won the Premier League since 2013. The return on investment has been catastrophically poor.

**W4 – Performance-Contingent Revenue:** Broadcasting revenue for the 12 months ending June 30, 2025 decreased by £48.9m to £172.9m after the men's team played in the Europa League rather than the Champions League. Champions League absence directly costs approximately £50m per season in broadcast income alone.

### Opportunities

**O1 – New Stadium:** Manchester United's Chief Operating Officer Collette Roche confirmed the club will pay £2 billion to build a new 100,000-seat Old Trafford, with the UK government asked to support funding for local infrastructure and area regeneration. A new stadium would materially increase matchday revenue and modernise the fan experience.

**O2 – Champions League Return:** With Michael Carrick appointed as permanent manager on a two-year deal after guiding United to a third-place Premier League finish and Champions League qualification for 2025–26, the club has an immediate opportunity to restore broadcast revenue and attract elite players.

**O3 – Women's Football Growth:** The improved Adidas deal is largely attributable to the Manchester United women's team becoming mainstream since their introduction in 2018, indicating commercial upside from continued investment in the women's game.

**O4 – Regulatory Equalisation:** Incoming UEFA Squad Cost Control Ratio rules may constrain state-backed rivals, potentially narrowing the financial gap if United can grow revenue through Champions League participation.

### Threats

**T1 – State-Backed Competitor Spending:** Manchester City and Newcastle United's ownership structures give them structural financial advantages that conventional clubs cannot replicate.

**T2 – Performance-Dependent Sponsorship Penalties:** Manchester United will forfeit £10 million from the Adidas deal in seasons without European competition, creating a direct feedback loop between on-field failure and commercial income loss.

**T3 – Ownership Governance Conflict:** The Glazer family are highly uncomfortable diluting their own equity or taking on high-interest debt to fund stadium construction, meaning the £2bn new stadium plan faces internal governance risk.

**T4 – Talent Drain:** Prolonged underperformance risks deterring elite player recruitment and accelerating departures of key assets.

## 5. Framework 4: Stakeholder Analysis

Mendelow's (1991) Power/Interest Matrix maps stakeholders by their power to influence the club and their interest in its activities, enabling strategic prioritisation.

| Stakeholder | Power | Interest | Quadrant | Strategic Implication |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Glazer Family | High | Medium (financial returns) | **Manage Closely** | Majority shareholders retain strategic veto; must be aligned with investment plan |
| INEOS / Ratcliffe | High | High (sporting success) | **Manage Closely** | Football operations now under INEOS; key driver of reform |
| Fans / Supporters' Trusts | Medium | Very High | **Keep Informed** | Brand equity depends on fan loyalty; protest risk is real |
| Premier League / UEFA | High | Medium | **Manage Closely** | Regulatory compliance (PSR/SCR) is existential; non-compliance risks points deductions |
| Commercial Sponsors (Adidas, Qualcomm) | Medium | High | **Keep Satisfied** | Performance-linked clauses mean sponsor revenue is directly tied to results |
| Players / Agents | Medium | High | **Keep Satisfied** | Elite players will not join a club without Champions League football |
| Manager (Michael Carrick) | Medium | High | **Manage Closely** | Stability of this appointment is critical to the reform agenda |

**Key Insight:** The most dangerous stakeholder tension is between the Glazer family (who prioritise financial extraction) and fans/INEOS (who prioritise sporting investment). From 2016, the Glazers paid themselves annual dividends from the club at over £20 million every year from 2016 to 2020. In that same period, the club's debt repayments "all but ceased," while interest payments continued. The total dividend payments reached £155 million in seven years, a policy that angered supporters. This principal-agent conflict — where controlling shareholders extract value rather than reinvest it — is the root cause of the club's underinvestment in both infrastructure and playing talent.

**Warrant:** Agency theory (Jensen and Meckling, 1976) predicts that where ownership and management are separated, controlling shareholders with short time horizons will prioritise dividends over long-term investment. The Glazer dividend history is a textbook illustration of this dynamic, and it explains why the club's debt was not reduced during years of strong commercial revenue.

## 6. Coherent Diagnosis: The Strategic Problem

The five frameworks converge on a single, coherent diagnosis: **Manchester United has a world-class brand (RBV: valuable, rare, partially inimitable) that is being systematically under-exploited because the organisation fails the 'O' condition of VRIO, due to a governance structure that prioritises extraction over investment (Stakeholder/Agency analysis), in a competitive environment that has intensified significantly (Five Forces), creating a self-reinforcing cycle of underperformance → revenue loss → reduced transfer capacity → further underperformance (SWOT).**

The causal chain is as follows:

1. **Glazer leveraged buyout (2005)** loaded the club with debt, creating annual interest obligations exceeding £50m that diverted resources from investment. Manchester United have paid more than £1bn in interest since 2005.

2. **Dividend extraction (2016–2022)** compounded the underinvestment. Dividends exceeded £20m annually from 2016–2020 while debt repayments "all but ceased."

3. **Managerial instability** destroyed tactical coherence. Ten permanent and interim managers in 13 years prevented squad development and cost approximately $99.9m in compensation.

4. **Misallocated transfer spending** failed to build a competitive squad. A net spend of £1.13 billion over a decade produced no league title.

5. **Sporting decline** triggered revenue loss. Europa League participation rather than the Champions League cost £48.9m in broadcast revenue in 2024–25 alone.

6. **Revenue loss** constrains future investment, perpetuating the cycle.

## 7. Recommendations and Action Plan

The recommendations are structured around three strategic horizons: immediate (0–12 months), medium-term (1–3 years), and long-term (3–7 years). Each recommendation is warranted by the preceding analysis.

### 7.1 Immediate Priority: Stabilise and Consolidate Under Carrick (0–12 months)

**Recommendation 1: Protect managerial continuity.**
The single most destructive pattern identified by VRIO analysis is managerial churn. Michael Carrick has already guided United to a third-place finish and Champions League qualification, providing the first genuine platform for squad development since Ferguson. The board must commit publicly and contractually to a minimum three-year tenure, insulating Carrick from short-term results pressure. The warrant is direct: VRIO analysis shows that the 'O' condition can only be met through organisational stability, and stability requires managerial continuity.

**Recommendation 2: Enforce Champions League retention as the non-negotiable KPI.**
Each season outside the Champions League costs approximately £49m in broadcast revenue. This revenue loss is not merely a financial inconvenience — it restricts transfer capacity, weakens sponsor compliance, and signals brand decline. Champions League participation should be established as the minimum acceptable outcome, with squad investment decisions evaluated against this threshold.

### 7.2 Medium-Term Priority: Fix the Financial Architecture (1–3 years)

**Recommendation 3: Accelerate debt reduction.**
Total debt stands at £637 million, costing approximately £36 million annually in interest. This dead-weight cost must be reduced. The return to Champions League football generates the incremental broadcast revenue (~£50m+) needed to service accelerated repayment. The warrant from Five Forces analysis is that financial flexibility is a prerequisite for competing in the player market against state-backed rivals; every pound of interest saved is a pound available for squad investment.

**Recommendation 4: Reform the transfer model from volume to precision.**
A net transfer spend of £1.13 billion over a decade with no league title demonstrates that volume spending without a coherent recruitment philosophy is value-destructive. INEOS should implement a data-driven recruitment model — analogous to Liverpool's under Fenway Sports Group — with a defined playing style under Carrick, clear positional profiles, and a bias toward younger players with resale value. The RBV warrant is that player assets need to be both valuable (performance) and tradeable (financial flexibility), which requires systematic rather than reactive recruitment.

**Recommendation 5: Resolve the Glazer governance conflict.**
The stakeholder analysis identifies the Glazer family's financial extraction as the structural root of the club's problems. Total dividend payments of £155 million over seven years represent capital that could have funded squad or infrastructure investment. INEOS should use its growing stake — now at 28.9% — to negotiate a binding covenant preventing future dividend payments until net debt falls below £300m. The agency theory warrant is explicit: aligning shareholder incentives with long-term club value creation requires removing the mechanism by which short-term extraction is possible.

### 7.3 Long-Term Priority: Rebuild the Infrastructure Foundation (3–7 years)

**Recommendation 6: Deliver the new stadium on schedule.**
The proposed 100,000-seat New Trafford, costing £2 billion, with UK government support for surrounding infrastructure, is the single most important long-term value creation lever available to the club. A 100,000-capacity stadium would add approximately £30–40m in annual matchday revenue over the current figure (based on the incremental ~26,000 seats at average Premier League ticket prices), while the regeneration project creates broader community goodwill that strengthens the fan stakeholder relationship. The SWOT warrant is that matchday revenue is the only revenue stream fully under the club's control, independent of broadcast distributions or sponsor performance clauses.

**Recommendation 7: Invest in the women's team and academy pipeline.**
The improved Adidas deal is partly attributable to the women's team becoming mainstream since 2018, demonstrating that investment in the women's programme generates measurable commercial returns. Simultaneously, developing academy talent reduces transfer dependency: home-grown players carry no transfer fee amortisation, reducing wage-bill pressure under the SCR rules. UEFA's Squad Cost Control Ratio will eventually limit clubs to 70% of turnover on squad costs, making academy output a structural financial advantage.
