# Strategic Case Study: Merck & Co. (MSD) — Navigating the Keytruda Patent Cliff

## A Biopharma Strategic Analysis

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This case study examines the defining strategic challenge facing Merck & Co. (NYSE: MRK) — the imminent loss of exclusivity for Keytruda (pembrolizumab), the world's best-selling drug, whose core patents expire in 2028. The situation is not merely a product lifecycle event; it is a structural inflection point that threatens to destabilise the company's entire revenue architecture. Using Porter's Five Forces, PESTEL analysis, the VRIO framework, Ansoff's Growth Matrix, and strategic portfolio theory, this paper diagnoses the competitive pressures Merck faces and constructs a coherent, evidence-grounded action plan. The analysis proceeds in a chain: environmental forces establish why the cliff is uniquely dangerous in the current biopharma landscape; VRIO establishes what Merck actually owns that can be defended; Ansoff maps the growth directions available; and the recommendations synthesise these into a prioritised strategic programme.

## PART I: CASE BACKGROUND — THE SITUATION

### 1.1 Merck & Co. and the Keytruda Franchise

Merck & Co. is one of the world's largest biopharmaceutical companies, with revenues of approximately $63–64 billion in 2024. At the centre of its commercial success sits Keytruda (pembrolizumab), a PD-1 immune checkpoint inhibitor approved across dozens of oncology indications. At the centre of Merck's challenge is Keytruda — the world's top-selling drug in 2024 at $29.5 billion in sales — which loses U.S. market exclusivity in late 2028. In the first three quarters of 2024 alone, Keytruda generated $21.6 billion in sales — growing 22% year-on-year — confirming that the franchise is still accelerating even as its cliff approaches. According to industry analysis, Keytruda accounted for more than half of the company's pharmaceutical sales in 2025.

This concentration of revenue in a single product is the strategic problem. When a molecule of this scale approaches loss of exclusivity (LOE), the consequences are systemic, not merely financial.

### 1.2 The Patent Cliff: An Industry-Wide Threat Amplified for Merck

The patent cliff is not unique to Merck, but the company is among the most exposed. The US market alone is projected to lose more than $230 billion in revenue between 2025 and 2030. Among the most exposed are Merck and Pfizer, whose portfolios include several blockbuster small-molecule drugs reaching loss of exclusivity. The pharmaceutical industry is undergoing a major change as patent exclusivity expires for popular drugs. This shift will open up a $236 billion opportunity for generic and biosimilar manufacturers between 2025 and 2030.

For Merck specifically, the exposure is compounded by a regulatory dimension: Keytruda was selected for Medicare price negotiation in 2026 (new discounted prices active January 2028), compounding the post-LOE revenue hit. This unprecedented situation — a flagship oncology drug facing both biosimilar competition and government price-setting nearly simultaneously — means Merck must prepare for Keytruda U.S. sales to peak in 2027–28 and then fall sharply.

Historical analogues from oncology are sobering. Each major oncology drug that lost exclusivity saw rapid revenue declines — Avastin dropped over 40% post-biosimilar entry, Gleevec lost more than 50% of U.S. sales within a year of generic launch, and Revlimid faced a projected 50% erosion despite staggered generic entry. Extrapolating this pattern to Keytruda's $29.5 billion revenue base, Merck faces a potential revenue erosion of $12–15 billion annually within five years of LOE — a figure that exceeds the total annual revenues of most mid-sized biopharma companies.

### 1.3 The Broader R&D Productivity Crisis

The challenge is compounded by an industry-wide deterioration in R&D economics that limits Merck's ability to replace Keytruda revenues organically. The average cost for a Big Pharma to develop a drug in 2024 was $2.23 billion, up from $2.12 billion the year before. Deloitte attributed the rising R&D costs to five factors: increases in trial times, more intricate and complex research areas, macroeconomic factors, tech advancements and high attrition rates; the pharma cohort spent $7.7 billion on trials for candidates that were terminated in 2024.

Critically, the pipeline success rate is deteriorating at the earliest and most formative stage. Pipeline attrition rates continue to grow, with the success rate for Phase 1 drugs plummeting to just 6.7% in 2024, compared to 10% a decade ago. This means that for every 15 Phase 1 candidates Merck advances, only one is expected to reach the market — a structural inefficiency that makes organic pipeline replacement of a $29.5 billion franchise extremely difficult to achieve within the 2028 timeframe.

## PART II: ANALYTICAL FRAMEWORKS

### Framework 1: PESTEL Analysis — The Macro-Environment

A PESTEL analysis reveals that Merck's strategic challenge is not merely internal; it is shaped by converging macro forces that simultaneously compress revenue and elevate the cost and risk of renewal.

**Political / Legal:** The most consequential political force is the Inflation Reduction Act (IRA). The IRA undermines innovation in small-molecule drugs by subjecting them to price controls after 9 years, whereas large-molecule drugs (biologics) are allowed 13 years of market pricing. Since Keytruda is a biologic, it benefits from the 13-year window — but it was nonetheless selected for Medicare negotiation, meaning the IRA's pricing mechanism arrives ahead of LOE. Two manufacturers recently stated they cancelled further research of certain drugs due to disincentives built into the law and its implementation. This chilling effect on R&D investment is directly relevant to Merck's pipeline renewal ambitions: the IRA distorts capital allocation across the industry at precisely the moment Merck needs to invest most heavily in successor assets.

**Economic:** Deal-making, particularly in the form of mergers and acquisitions, has been subdued in recent years. The cumulative value of biopharma deals in 2024 is on track to hit its lowest level in over half a decade, exacerbated by high borrowing costs and historically high M&A premiums in the biopharma sector. This matters because Merck's renewal strategy depends partly on acquisitions: a high-interest-rate, high-premium environment increases the cost of inorganic pipeline replenishment.

**Social:** Ageing populations in high-income markets sustain oncology demand, providing a structural demand tailwind for Merck's core therapeutic area. IQVIA forecasts oncology drug spending to hit $440 billion by 2028, with the largest growth drivers being checkpoint inhibitors. This validates continued investment in the oncology space even as Keytruda itself approaches LOE.

**Technological:** Artificial intelligence is reshaping the economics of drug discovery. Insilico Medicine used GENTRL, a generative adversarial network approach, to complete an AI drug discovery challenge within 21 days. A growing number of AI-enabled drug pipelines are now advancing into clinical phases globally, signalling a shift from experimentation to scaled application. The growing impact of AI is underscored by the 2024 Nobel Prize in Chemistry, awarded to scientists behind AlphaFold for using neural network–based AI to predict complex protein structures and enable the design of entirely new proteins. For Merck, AI investment is not optional — it is a competitive necessity that can compress discovery timelines and improve Phase 1 attrition rates.

**Environmental:** Supply chain resilience is an increasingly material concern. Geopolitical tensions — including threatened US pharmaceutical tariffs — risk disrupting active pharmaceutical ingredient (API) supply chains, raising manufacturing costs at a time when Merck needs to protect margins.

**Legal / Regulatory:** In 2024, the FDA approved 38 new molecular entities for therapeutic use, a decline from 47 in the previous year. Clinical trials now demand greater complexity, as well as increased data and diversity requirements, resulting in extended timelines and increased costs. The regulatory bar is rising, which lengthens the time between pipeline investment and commercial return — compressing the window available for Merck to bring successor assets to market before 2028.

**PESTEL Inference:** The macro environment creates a "double squeeze": pricing pressure from the IRA reduces the revenue upside of new drugs, while rising R&D costs, regulatory complexity, and capital constraints increase the cost and risk of developing them. This makes Merck's strategic choices more consequential and less forgiving than at any prior patent cliff.

### Framework 2: Porter's Five Forces — Industry Structural Analysis

Porter's Five Forces reveals why the biopharma industry, despite high barriers to entry, is structurally hostile to incumbents facing LOE events.

**Threat of New Entrants (Low–Moderate):** The threat of new entrants is low. The most important hindrance is strict regulatory compliance; drugs have to go through phased trials and a cumbersome approval process. The initial capital investment and high R&D cost is another major hindrance. However, for Keytruda specifically, the relevant "new entrant" threat is biosimilar manufacturers — established pharmaceutical companies who face a lower bar because they are copying a proven molecule. Competitors including Celltrion, Samsung Bioepis, and Amgen have biosimilar candidates in late-stage development. These are not small start-ups; they are well-capitalised manufacturers with existing oncology commercial infrastructure.

**Bargaining Power of Suppliers (Moderate):** Merck's manufacturing of biologics requires specialised contract development and manufacturing organisations (CDMOs) and highly skilled scientific labour. As the industry scales up biologics production, CDMO capacity is constrained, giving suppliers moderate leverage. The IRA's incentive to shift toward biologics (with their longer pricing protection window) further tightens this constraint industry-wide.

**Bargaining Power of Buyers (High and Rising):** This is the most powerful force acting on Merck. Buyers — Medicare, private payers, pharmacy benefit managers (PBMs) — have acquired unprecedented leverage through the IRA's Medicare Drug Price Negotiation Programme. The IRA grants the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) the authority to set drug prices. These price controls reduce the incentives for biopharmaceutical innovation and thus reduce U.S. biopharmaceutical production. For Keytruda, this means that even before biosimilar entry, the government will have already compressed the drug's U.S. pricing. Buyer power is therefore not a future threat — it is an active, present constraint.

**Threat of Substitutes (High in Oncology):** The oncology immunotherapy space is characterised by intense substitution risk. Combination therapies, next-generation checkpoint inhibitors, bispecific antibodies, and antibody-drug conjugates (ADCs) all compete for the same patient populations. Rare diseases, accounting for 45% of clinical trials starting in 2024, are gaining prominence due to faster development timelines, strong pricing power, and high success rates, especially when overlapping with rare oncology. This signals a structural shift in R&D investment toward indications where substitution risk is lower — a strategic direction Merck should pursue.

**Competitive Rivalry (Very High):** Oncology makes up nearly half of all R&D activity among the 20 largest biopharma companies, with the top five therapeutic areas accounting for 83% of R&D programmes. Hypercompetition in therapeutic areas is contributing to the rising costs of clinical trials. Merck competes in a space where Bristol Myers Squibb (Opdivo), Roche, AstraZeneca, and Johnson & Johnson are all investing heavily in next-generation oncology assets. The competitive intensity of this field means that even successful pipeline assets face pricing pressure and market share erosion from rivals.

**Five Forces Inference:** The combined force analysis reveals that Merck's competitive position — dominant today — will be structurally weakened from 2028 onward by the simultaneous activation of buyer power (IRA pricing), new competitive entry (biosimilars), and substitution (next-generation oncology modalities). The industry structure does not become more favourable over time; it becomes less so. This makes early strategic action imperative.

### Framework 3: VRIO Analysis — Internal Competitive Resources

| ADC pipeline (Daiichi Sankyo partnership) | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ | ⚠️ | **Potential sustained advantage** |
| AI/data capabilities | ✅ | ⚠️ (spreading) | ⚠️ | ⚠️ | Temporary — needs investment to sustain |
| Financial scale ($63B+ revenue base) | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ | **Sustained advantage** |
| Regulatory relationships & approval track record | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ | **Sustained advantage** |
| Subcutaneous Keytruda formulation | ✅ | ✅ | ⚠️ | ✅ | Temporary lifecycle extension |

**VRIO Inference — Key Finding:** Merck's most durable competitive advantages are not product-specific but *capability-specific*: its oncology clinical development expertise, its financial scale, and its regulatory relationships. These are the resources that are genuinely hard to imitate and that should be the foundation of any renewal strategy. Keytruda itself, while currently the most valuable resource, is not inimitable in the long run — its patent protection is a legal artefact, not a capability. This distinction is critical: strategy must be built on capabilities, not on the assumption that the current product franchise will persist.

The ADC partnership with Daiichi Sankyo is particularly significant. New positive data on investigational candidates from Merck's pipeline were presented, including for patritumab deruxtecan (HER3-DXd), an antibody-drug conjugate being developed in collaboration with Daiichi Sankyo, and for sacituzumab tirumotecan (sac-TMT), an anti-TROP2 ADC being developed in collaboration with Kelun-Biotech. ADCs represent a genuinely differentiated modality — targeted delivery of cytotoxic payloads directly to tumour cells — that is difficult to replicate without the specific biological and chemical engineering expertise embedded in these partnerships.

Merck has also taken steps to defend Keytruda's commercial position through lifecycle management. Merck has developed the subcutaneous Keytruda and won FDA approval for KEYTRUDA QLEX™ across most solid tumour indications in September 2025. This will help defend market share given the coming LOE for Keytruda. The subcutaneous formulation is a classic lifecycle management tactic — it creates a differentiated product that biosimilar manufacturers cannot immediately replicate, because biosimilar approval pathways require demonstrating equivalence to the reference product's specific formulation.

### Framework 4: Ansoff's Growth Matrix — Strategic Direction Options

Ansoff's matrix maps four strategic growth directions against the axes of existing/new products and existing/new markets. Applied to Merck's post-Keytruda challenge, it generates four strategic options of varying risk and feasibility.

**Quadrant 1 — Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets):** Merck can deepen Keytruda's market penetration by expanding into earlier-stage oncology settings (adjuvant and neoadjuvant indications) and broadening its combination therapy approvals. This is already underway — the KEYNOTE-522 trial in early-stage triple-negative breast cancer and KEYNOTE-A18 in cervical cancer represent exactly this logic. However, this strategy has a ceiling: as Keytruda approaches LOE, incremental label expansions generate diminishing returns because biosimilar manufacturers will inherit the full approved indication set.

**Quadrant 2 — Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets):** Geographic expansion — particularly into emerging markets where Keytruda penetration remains low — offers near-term revenue growth. However, pricing constraints in these markets limit the revenue impact, and the IRA's domestic pricing pressure cannot be offset by emerging market volumes alone.

**Quadrant 3 — Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets):** This is the highest-priority strategic direction and the most natural extension of Merck's oncology capabilities. Merck's most durable long-term defence against the Keytruda patent cliff lies in developing next-generation immunotherapy assets that can succeed pembrolizumab in clinical practice before biosimilar substitution erodes the brand. The successor pipeline encompasses three broad categories: enhanced PD-1/PD-L1 molecules with improved pharmacokinetics or reduced immunogenicity, bispecific antibodies pairing PD-1 blockade with a second immune checkpoint target, and novel subcutaneous formulations that provide patient convenience advantages over biosimilar IV pembrolizumab. This is the Ansoff quadrant where Merck's VRIO-identified capabilities (oncology expertise, clinical trial networks, regulatory relationships) are most directly applicable.

**Quadrant 4 — Diversification (New Products, New Markets):** Merck's acquisition of EyeBio (ophthalmology) and expansion into pulmonary arterial hypertension (WINREVAIR) represent diversification moves. Rare diseases, accounting for 45% of clinical trials starting in 2024, are gaining prominence due to faster development timelines, strong pricing power, and high success rates. The average time to peak share for non-oncology rare therapies is just four years, per 2024 ZS analysis — compared with 10 years for non-rare ones. Diversification into rare diseases carries the highest risk (Merck lacks established commercial infrastructure outside oncology) but offers the highest pricing power and lowest competitive intensity — a meaningful hedge against oncology hypercompetition.

**Ansoff Inference:** Merck should pursue a *portfolio of strategies across all four quadrants*, with priority weighting toward Product Development (next-generation oncology assets, ADCs) and selective Diversification (rare disease, cardiovascular). Market Penetration and Market Development are necessary but insufficient to replace Keytruda's revenue base alone.

### Framework 5: Strategic Portfolio Analysis — The Pipeline as a Portfolio

Strategic portfolio theory (analogous to BCG Matrix logic) frames Merck's pipeline not as a collection of individual bets but as a portfolio requiring balance between near-term cash generation and long-term growth investment.

Applying this logic to Merck's current position:

- **"Cash Cows":** Keytruda (declining trajectory from 2028), GARDASIL (vaccines), WINREVAIR (early commercial stage). These generate the cash that funds pipeline investment.
- **"Stars":** ADC pipeline (patritumab deruxtecan, sac-TMT), subcutaneous Keytruda formulation, MK-0616 (oral PCSK9 inhibitor for cardiovascular). High growth potential, high investment requirement.
- **"Question Marks":** Early-stage oncology pipeline, rare disease assets (EyeBio). Uncertain commercial outcomes; require selective investment based on probability-adjusted returns.
- **"Dogs":** Legacy products approaching generic competition; should be managed for cash extraction rather than incremental investment.

Pharmaceutical companies are grappling with a pipeline crisis as patent cliffs threaten revenues. In 2024, deals like Pfizer-Seagen and Novartis-Chinook showcased the urgency to acquire late-stage assets ready for commercialisation. This validates the portfolio logic: Merck cannot rely solely on organic pipeline development; it must supplement internal R&D with targeted acquisitions of late-stage assets that can contribute revenues within the critical 2026–2030 window.

The industry trend supports this approach. Pharmaceutical companies have moved away from simple transactional licensing to more integrated partnership models in 2024. Data from PharmaIntel shows that 63% of new deals now include shared development responsibilities, compared to just 38% in 2021. This shift reflects a strategic response to rising R&D costs and the complexity of modern drug development. Merck's Daiichi Sankyo ADC partnership exemplifies this model — it is a co-development alliance that shares both cost and scientific risk, rather than a simple licensing transaction.

## PART III: SYNTHESIS — THE COHERENT STRATEGIC LOGIC

The five frameworks, taken together, generate a coherent diagnostic chain:

1. **PESTEL** establishes that the macro environment is structurally hostile: pricing power is being legislated away (IRA), regulatory costs are rising, and the capital environment constrains acquisitions.

2. **Porter's Five Forces** confirms that industry structure amplifies this hostility: buyer power (payers/government) is high and rising; biosimilar entry is imminent and credible; competitive rivalry in oncology is intense.

3. **VRIO** identifies that Merck's durable advantages are capability-based (clinical expertise, financial scale, regulatory relationships) rather than product-based (Keytruda's patent). This means the strategy must leverage capabilities, not defend a declining asset.

4. **Ansoff** maps the growth directions that Merck's capabilities can sustain: Product Development (next-generation oncology, ADCs) is the highest-priority direction; Diversification into rare disease is a necessary hedge.

5. **Portfolio Analysis** reveals that Merck must balance near-term cash generation (Keytruda lifecycle management, GARDASIL) with long-term growth investment (ADCs, rare disease) — and that the investment must be funded and deployed *now*, before the cliff arrives.

The chain of reasoning is as follows: **because** the macro environment is compressing future drug revenues (IRA, rising R&D costs) and **because** industry structure will intensify competitive pressure from 2028 (biosimilars, buyer power), and **because** Merck's sustainable competitive advantage lies in its capabilities rather than its current product, **therefore** the correct strategic response is to redeploy those capabilities into next-generation assets — through a combination of internal R&D acceleration (ADCs, bispecifics), targeted acquisitions (late-stage rare disease and oncology assets), and AI-enabled productivity improvement — before the Keytruda revenue base begins its structural decline.

## PART IV: RECOMMENDATIONS AND ACTION PLAN

### Recommendation 1: Accelerate and Protect the ADC Pipeline — Immediate Priority

**What:** Merck should treat its ADC pipeline — particularly the Daiichi Sankyo and Kelun-Biotech partnerships — as the single highest-priority near-term investment. ADCs represent the most credible near-term successor to Keytruda in oncology: they target specific tumour antigens, carry novel mechanisms of action, and are significantly harder to biosimilar than a monoclonal antibody.

**Why the evidence warrants this:** Pairing pembrolizumab with Merck's own agents (belzutifan HIF-2α, ADCs) could yield new combination approvals patent-protected beyond Keytruda's LOE, creating protected niches. This is not merely a pipeline diversification move — it is a strategy to extend Keytruda's commercial relevance into combination regimens that are protected by new intellectual property, even as the core molecule faces biosimilar entry. The VRIO analysis supports this: the ADC partnerships embed scientific capabilities (antibody engineering, linker chemistry, payload biology) that are genuinely inimitable in the short term.

**How:** Merck should increase milestone payments and co-development investment in the Daiichi Sankyo partnership to accelerate Phase 3 timelines for HER3-DXd and TROP2-ADC. It should also pursue regulatory Fast Track and Breakthrough Therapy designations wherever clinical data support them, to compress the timeline to approval.

### Recommendation 2: Pursue Targeted Acquisitions of Late-Stage Rare Disease Assets — 12–24 Month Horizon

**What:** Merck should use its financial scale to acquire late-stage or commercial-stage rare disease assets, diversifying revenue away from oncology hypercompetition and into markets with stronger pricing power and lower competitive intensity.

**Why the evidence warrants this:** Rare disease therapies are consistently entering less saturated markets, with 38% of launches between 2022 and 2024 being first-in-disease, far more than in cancer, chronic or acute settings. The average time to peak share for non-oncology rare therapies is just four years — compared with 10 years for non-rare ones. With fewer direct competitors, companies can carve out a defensible foothold that deters new entrants, especially in markets too small to justify broad investment. This directly addresses the Five Forces finding that competitive rivalry is most intense in oncology: rare disease markets are structurally less competitive.

The acquisition rationale is further supported by market conditions. Two-thirds of public biotechs are under-capitalised with less than 12 months of cash runway. Biotech valuations remain suppressed, making public targets more affordable. This creates a buyer's market for Merck, whose strong balance sheet (funded by Keytruda's peak revenues in 2026–2027) provides acquisition capacity at a time when target valuations are relatively depressed.

**How:** Merck should establish a dedicated business development team focused on rare neurological, metabolic, and immunological disease assets at Phase 2 or later. Deal structures should follow the industry trend toward co-development agreements rather than outright acquisitions where scientific risk remains high, thereby sharing downside while retaining upside.

**What:** Merck should make a substantial, structured investment in AI-enabled drug discovery and clinical trial design to improve Phase 1 attrition rates and compress development timelines.

**Why the evidence warrants this:** The Phase 1 success rate of 6.7% in 2024 means that Merck is absorbing the cost of failed trials at a rate that is structurally unsustainable as Keytruda revenues decline. Companies using AI in drug discovery have reported reductions in development times by 30–50%, offering a potential competitive edge in accessing the market. If AI can improve Merck's Phase 1 success rate from 6.7% to even 10% — the rate of a decade ago — the compound effect on pipeline productivity over a 10-year horizon is transformative.

Companies that can balance innovation, regulatory compliance, and patient-centric strategies — while leveraging advanced analytics and AI — will likely be well positioned to navigate risks and seize new opportunities in the evolving biopharma landscape. Deloitte's analysis identifies this as a structural differentiator, not merely an operational improvement. The VRIO analysis reinforces this: AI capabilities are currently not rare (all large pharma companies are investing), but the *integration* of AI with Merck's specific oncology domain knowledge and clinical trial infrastructure would create a combination that is harder to replicate.

**How:** Merck should establish or expand partnerships with AI-native drug discovery platforms (analogous to Novo Nordisk's collaboration with Nvidia's BioNeMO framework), focusing specifically on oncology target identification, biomarker-driven patient stratification, and adaptive trial design. The goal is to reduce the $7.7 billion the industry collectively spent on terminated trial assets in 2024 — Merck's proportional share of which represents a significant drag on R&D ROI.

### Recommendation 4: Implement Keytruda Lifecycle Management as a Bridge Strategy — Ongoing

**What:** Merck should maximise the commercial life of Keytruda through formulation innovation, label expansion, and combination therapy approvals that extend exclusivity and differentiate from biosimilars.

**Why the evidence warrants this:** Historical biosimilar launches in oncology suggest that innovator brands can retain 40–60% market share in the first two years post-biosimilar entry — but that share erodes significantly over a five-year horizon. Lifecycle management tactics — particularly the subcutaneous formulation — can extend the period of market share retention, because biosimilar manufacturers must separately develop and gain approval for alternative formulations. This buys time for the ADC pipeline and acquired assets to generate revenue.

The subcutaneous KEYTRUDA QLEX approval in September 2025 is a concrete execution of this logic. The subcutaneous Keytruda win will help defend market share given the coming LOE for Keytruda. Merck should now prioritise physician and payer education to drive switching from IV to subcutaneous Keytruda, creating a new reference product that biosimilar manufacturers cannot immediately match.

**How:** Merck's commercial teams should develop a systematic switching programme targeting high-volume oncology centres. Simultaneously, the medical affairs function should pursue additional combination approvals (Keytruda + ADC regimens) that create new, IP-protected treatment protocols even as the core molecule becomes generically available.

### Recommendation 5: Restructure for Strategic Clarity — Organisational Enabler

**What:** Merck should complete and deepen the organisational restructuring into dedicated Oncology and Specialty/Infectious Diseases business units, with clear P&L accountability and investment mandates for each.

**Why the evidence warrants this:** "Patent cliffs were once managed primarily through pipeline replenishment. Today, mega-blockbusters drive deeper transformation, organisational redesign, governance restructuring, leadership recalibration, and diversification strategies. The winners will not be those who merely predict cliffs, but those who re-architect early enough." The strategic logic here is not merely administrative: a dedicated Oncology Business Unit with its own P&L will make more focused, accountable investment decisions in the ADC pipeline than a monolithic pharmaceutical division. Similarly, a Specialty unit with its own mandate will pursue rare disease acquisitions with greater urgency than a division whose identity and culture are built around oncology.

**How:** Merck should ensure that each business unit has a dedicated business development function empowered to execute acquisitions and partnerships within defined therapeutic areas, with investment mandates calibrated to the 2028 revenue replacement target.

## CONCLUSION

The Keytruda patent cliff is the most consequential strategic event in Merck's recent history — not because it is unprecedented in the industry, but because the scale of the exposed revenue ($29.5 billion) coincides with a macro environment that is uniquely hostile: pricing legislation (IRA) compresses future revenue upside, rising R&D costs and attrition rates make organic pipeline replacement harder, and biosimilar manufacturers are better resourced and more experienced than at any prior LOE event.

The analytical chain constructed in this case study demonstrates that the correct strategic response is neither defensive (merely managing Keytruda's decline) nor speculative (betting on a single pipeline asset). It is a *portfolio-based, capability-led* renewal strategy that:

1. Defends Keytruda's near-term revenue through lifecycle management (subcutaneous formulation, combination approvals);
2. Accelerates the ADC pipeline as the highest-probability near-term successor, leveraging Merck's inimitable oncology clinical capabilities;
3. Acquires rare disease assets to diversify into structurally less competitive markets;
4. Invests in AI-enabled R&D productivity to improve the economics of the entire pipeline; and
5. Restructures organisationally to ensure that each strategic direction has focused leadership and investment accountability.

The companies that successfully navigate patent cliffs are not those that predict them most accurately — they are those that re-architect their capabilities and portfolios early enough to ensure that the successor revenue engine is already generating momentum before the cliff arrives. For Merck, with Keytruda's LOE in 2028, that window is now.
