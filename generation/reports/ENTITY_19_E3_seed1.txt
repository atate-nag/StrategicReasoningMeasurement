# Strategic Case Study: Zoom Video Communications — From Pandemic Darling to Platform Contender

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Zoom Video Communications entered the COVID-19 pandemic as a profitable niche player and exited it as one of the most recognised software brands in the world — and one of its most strategically exposed. This case study examines the strategic situation Zoom faced between 2022 and 2024: a growth plateau following hypergrowth, intensifying competition from Microsoft Teams and Google Meet, a deteriorating Online (consumer/SMB) segment, and the imperative to reposition from a single-product video-conferencing tool into a multi-product enterprise platform. Using Porter's Five Forces, the Resource-Based View (RBV) / Dynamic Capabilities framework, Porter's Generic Strategies, the Ansoff Matrix, and a PESTEL scan, this analysis identifies the root causes of Zoom's strategic vulnerability and produces a coherent, evidence-based action plan.

---

## PART 1 — CASE BACKGROUND AND SITUATION OVERVIEW

### 1.1 The Rise

After the 2022 high, Zoom's revenue declined to $4.7 billion in 2023, signalling a post-pandemic normalisation in usage and enterprise spending. Revenue remained stable at approximately $4.7 billion in 2024, indicating a plateau phase following earlier hypergrowth. To understand why this plateau is strategically dangerous, one must recall how dramatically Zoom's fortunes shifted. The company went public in April 2019 at $36 per share — a rarity among tech unicorns because it was already profitable. Less than a year later, the COVID-19 pandemic turned Zoom into a global necessity. At its peak in October 2020, the stock soared to $588, giving the company a valuation exceeding $160 billion.

### 1.2 The Strategic Problem

The pandemic inflated Zoom's growth rates beyond what its underlying competitive position could sustain. The stock has lost more than 85% of its value since peaking in October 2020. Operationally, the crisis was compounded by over-hiring. In January 2023, Zoom Video Communications eliminated 15% of its workers — approximately 1,300 jobs — as the service adapted to slower growth. CEO Eric Yuan acknowledged that the company "overhired during the pandemic to meet surging demand." In addition to the layoffs, Zoom reduced Yuan's salary by 98% in the coming fiscal year and the wages of the executive team by 20%, and both Yuan and the executives forfeited their corporate bonuses. These actions signal not merely a cost problem but a structural strategic challenge: a product that became commoditised, a customer base that was eroding, and a competitive landscape that had fundamentally changed.

The core strategic tension is this: **Zoom's core product — video meetings — has become a commodity feature bundled inside rival ecosystems, while Zoom's route to survival requires it to build a sticky, multi-product enterprise platform before its customer base migrates to those ecosystems.**

## PART 2 — EXTERNAL ENVIRONMENT ANALYSIS

### 2.1 PESTEL Analysis

**Political/Regulatory:** Data privacy regulation (GDPR in Europe, CCPA in California) imposes compliance costs and restricts how Zoom can use customer data to train AI models. In August 2023, Zoom issued clarifications to its terms and conditions after a public relations and customer trust crisis, ultimately stating that it does not use customer audio, video, chat, or screen sharing to train Zoom's or third-party artificial intelligence. This episode illustrates how regulatory and reputational pressure constrains Zoom's AI data strategy — a key competitive input — warranting careful governance design.

**Economic:** The post-pandemic macro environment suppressed enterprise IT spending. Zoom experienced headwinds in terms of currency impact, Online contraction, and a difficult macro environment in both the U.S. and international markets in FY2023. Rising interest rates ended the "grow first and ask questions later" era, increasing pressure on Zoom to demonstrate profitability rather than pure top-line growth.

**Social:** Hybrid working has become the structural norm. The global enterprise collaboration market was valued at USD 54.67 billion in 2024 and is expected to reach USD 107.03 billion by 2030, growing at a compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of 12.1% from 2025 to 2030, driven by the adoption of remote and hybrid work models, increasing demand for real-time communication, and the shift to cloud-based collaboration. This is a *structural opportunity*: hybrid work is not receding, meaning the total addressable market for collaboration platforms remains large and growing. This warrants Zoom investing in platform depth rather than retreating.

**Technological:** Generative AI is rapidly becoming the primary battleground for differentiation in enterprise software. Zoom introduced AI Companion 2.0, which synthesises information from across Zoom Workplace — including Meetings, Team Chat, Docs, Mail, and connected third-party tools — and can identify and complete next steps and action items. This matters strategically because AI-driven productivity features create new switching costs and justify premium pricing, addressing two of Zoom's core vulnerabilities simultaneously.

**Environmental:** Data centre energy consumption is under increasing scrutiny. Cloud providers face pressure to report and reduce Scope 2 emissions, which may raise infrastructure costs but also presents a reputational opportunity for firms that lead on sustainability.

**Legal:** Zoom has faced security-related scrutiny. There have been concerns about the level of encryption used by Zoom, and the rise of fake Zoom apps distributed by cybercriminals in January 2023 illustrates that the platform is "low-hanging fruit" for bad actors. For enterprise buyers — particularly in regulated industries such as finance, healthcare, and government — security credibility is a purchase criterion, not merely a hygiene factor. Legal liability from data breaches can therefore translate directly into customer loss.

### 2.2 Porter's Five Forces Analysis

**Force 1 — Threat of New Entrants: LOW-TO-MODERATE**

Building a credible video-conferencing platform requires substantial R&D investment, global infrastructure, and brand trust. However, the underlying technology is not proprietary, and well-capitalised entrants (e.g., Meta with Workplace, Amazon with Chime) can enter. The more significant threat is *adjacent* entrants: existing enterprise software vendors adding video as a feature. This is precisely what Microsoft has done.

**Force 2 — Bargaining Power of Suppliers: LOW**

Zoom's primary suppliers are cloud infrastructure providers (AWS, Oracle). While these represent real cost lines, Zoom has multi-cloud architecture, limiting dependency on any single vendor. This force does not constitute an acute strategic threat.

**Force 3 — Bargaining Power of Buyers: HIGH**

Customers in the video conferencing market have significant bargaining power because Microsoft Teams, integrated with Office 365 subscriptions, provides a significant competitive advantage by bundling communication tools. This means Zoom must not only compete on per-user pricing but also on the overall feature set and value proposition. Enterprise procurement teams can credibly threaten to consolidate onto Microsoft 365, giving them leverage to demand price concessions or additional features from Zoom. This force is the most immediately dangerous to Zoom's revenue base and directly connects to the bundling competitive threat discussed in Section 3.

**Force 4 — Threat of Substitutes: HIGH**

The competitive landscape, including Microsoft Teams and Google Meet, significantly pressures Zoom's pricing strategies. In Q3 2024, Microsoft Teams revenue grew by 20%, underscoring the pressure on Zoom. Because Teams is bundled into Microsoft 365 at no marginal cost for existing subscribers, it functions as a zero-price substitute for Zoom's core product — a particularly severe form of substitute threat. Companies switch to Teams primarily for three reasons: cost savings through Microsoft 365 bundling, better integration with existing Microsoft tools, and the need for persistent collaboration features beyond just meetings. This substitution logic is not about Teams being superior; it is about Teams being *free enough* for many buyers — a structural price disadvantage Zoom cannot overcome by competing on meetings alone.

**Force 5 — Intensity of Competitive Rivalry: HIGH**

Microsoft Teams has approximately 420 million monthly active users (FY2025), Google Meet serves 6 million paying businesses, and Cisco Webex contributes collaboration revenue of $4.6 billion in FY2025. These rivals force Zoom into costly feature races, margin pressure, and wallet-share battles. Feature parity drives a "Red Queen's Race": when every player matches every feature, no one gains advantage — only cost increases.

**Five Forces Summary:** The combined picture is of a high-rivalry, high-substitution environment where buyers hold significant power. The single most important implication is that Zoom **cannot win by competing on its core product alone**. The five forces analysis therefore directly motivates the platform-expansion and differentiation strategy assessed in Part 4.

## PART 3 — INTERNAL CAPABILITY ANALYSIS

### 3.1 Resource-Based View (RBV) and the VRIN Framework

The RBV holds that sustainable competitive advantage derives from resources and capabilities that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN). Applying this framework to Zoom reveals a mixed picture.

| Resource / Capability | Valuable? | Rare? | Inimitable? | Non-substitutable? | Strategic Implication |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Brand recognition ("Zoom" as a verb) | ✓ | ✓ | Partially | Partially | Durable but not sufficient alone |
| Ease-of-use / UX quality | ✓ | Partially | No (Teams improving) | No | Eroding advantage |
| Global infrastructure / reliability | ✓ | No | No | No | Table stakes |
| Enterprise customer relationships | ✓ | Partially | Yes (relationship-specific) | No | Core asset to defend |
| AI Companion / proprietary AI stack | ✓ | Partially | Yes (if data moat built) | Partially | Emerging advantage |
| Developer / ISV ecosystem | ✓ | No | No | No | Needs investment |

The analysis shows that Zoom's most durable VRIN assets are its **brand** and its **enterprise customer relationships**, while its traditional technical differentiation (ease of use, video quality) is eroding. This is consistent with the five forces finding: Zoom's product is becoming commoditised. The RBV therefore directs strategic attention toward *deepening* enterprise relationships (expanding wallet share per customer) and *building new inimitable capabilities* through AI — the one domain where proprietary data and model investment can create barriers to imitation.

### 3.2 Dynamic Capabilities

Dynamic capabilities theory (Teece, Pisano, and Shuen) argues that in rapidly changing environments, competitive advantage comes not from static resources but from the capacity to **sense** new opportunities, **seize** them through investment, and **transform** the organisation accordingly. Zoom's post-2022 actions can be evaluated against this framework:

- **Sensing:** Zoom correctly identified the shift to hybrid work, the commoditisation of meetings, and the emergence of AI as a productivity layer. Zoom's strategic focus on AI integration and new product development has been a central theme, with the company introducing AI Companion 2.0 and Zoom Tasks, signalling its evolution into an AI-first workplace ecosystem.

- **Seizing:** Zoom moved to acquire Workvivo and expand Zoom Phone. Zoom's acquisition of Workvivo, an employee experience provider, is intended to extend the Zoom platform by offering new ways to keep workers informed, engaged, and connected wherever they are working. Zoom Phone reached approximately 7 million seats by FY2024, signalling traction in the $70–90 billion global UCaaS (Unified Communications as a Service) opportunity.

- **Transforming:** The 2023 restructuring — cutting 15% of the workforce — was a necessary but painful transformation of the cost base. However, transformation of *organisational identity* (from video-call company to enterprise platform) is incomplete, as the market perception analysis in Section 3.3 shows.

### 3.3 The "COVID Stock" Perception Problem

A critical intangible barrier is reputational. Some institutional investors still view Zoom as a "COVID stock," making it difficult for the share price to achieve a significant premium valuation. This matters strategically because a depressed stock price limits Zoom's acquisition currency and increases the cost of equity capital needed to fund platform investment. The dynamic capabilities framework implies that Zoom must not only transform its product but its *strategic narrative* — demonstrating to investors, enterprise buyers, and partners that it is a platform company, not a pandemic-era artefact.

## PART 4 — STRATEGIC OPTIONS ANALYSIS

### 4.1 Porter's Generic Strategies

Porter identifies three generic strategies: Cost Leadership, Differentiation, and Focus. For Zoom, the options map as follows:

**Cost Leadership is not viable.** For organisations already paying for Microsoft 365, Teams inclusion makes competitive video solutions hard to justify on price alone. Microsoft's bundling advantage dominates at the enterprise scale. Competing on price against a product that is effectively zero-cost to Microsoft 365 subscribers is structurally unwinnable. Zoom cannot be the low-cost provider.

**Broad Differentiation is the appropriate generic strategy.** Zoom must differentiate on the *breadth and integration* of its platform — delivering capabilities (AI-driven productivity, Contact Centre, employee experience, telephony) that Microsoft Teams does not match in quality, and doing so within a single, seamlessly integrated workspace. Zoom's diversified product suite — Zoom Phone, Zoom Rooms, Contact Centre, Zoom Customer Experience, Zoom Revenue Accelerator, Workvivo — and its enterprise focus aim to make it a comprehensive communication platform, increasing customer lifetime value. This is the correct generic positioning, but it must be executed with genuine capability depth, not superficial feature addition.

**Focus (niche) strategies** are available as fallback positions (e.g., healthcare, education, government verticals where security and compliance requirements are high), but pursuing focus alone would concede the broad enterprise market to Microsoft and Google — an unacceptable strategic retreat given Zoom's existing customer base.

### 4.2 Ansoff Matrix — Growth Vectors

The Ansoff Matrix maps four growth strategies against existing/new products and markets. Zoom's situation calls for a sequenced multi-vector approach:

**Quadrant 1 — Market Penetration (existing products, existing markets):** Zoom should deepen AI feature adoption among its 220,400 enterprise customers. By Q3 2024, Zoom reported that its AI Companion features were being used by over 50% of eligible customers, demonstrating strong adoption of AI-driven productivity tools. Because AI features are being offered within existing plans, the near-term task is converting AI adoption into *premium tier upgrades* (Custom AI Companion add-ons) — increasing average revenue per user (ARPU) without requiring new customer acquisition.

**Quadrant 2 — Market Development (existing products, new markets):** Zoom's geographic opportunity is significant. The shift to an 'AI-first' company is a massive opportunity. The company needs to focus on localising the partner-centric sales model to capture value in international markets. Expansion into Asia-Pacific and emerging markets — where Microsoft's bundling advantage is less entrenched — represents a lower-rivalry growth path.

**Quadrant 3 — Product Development (new products, existing markets):** This is Zoom's primary investment thesis. Zoom introduced 700+ features to its Contact Centre in one year to penetrate the approximately $20 billion contact centre market, with the goal of offering a unified experience for internal collaboration and external customer engagement, increasing wallet share per customer. Zoom Phone and Contact Centre represent the clearest execution of this quadrant: selling new products to customers who already trust the Zoom brand.

**Quadrant 4 — Diversification (new products, new markets):** The Workvivo acquisition is a cautious diversification into employee experience for distributed/frontline workforces — a segment not well-served by Microsoft Teams. Workvivo's capabilities address the needs of remote, hybrid, and distributed workforces, particularly frontline workers, who need ongoing connection and support. This is the highest-risk quadrant and should be managed carefully, with clear integration milestones.

**Ansoff Summary:** The sequencing logic is: **first** extract more revenue from existing customers via AI upsells (Quadrant 1); **simultaneously** expand geographically (Quadrant 2); **invest heavily** in product development for enterprise platform depth (Quadrant 3); and **manage** diversification acquisitions (Quadrant 4) with discipline. This sequence mirrors the Amazon growth pattern — using a strong core to fund adjacencies — and is grounded in Zoom's demonstrated ability to grow enterprise revenue faster than total revenue (Enterprise revenue for FY2024 was $2,619.3 million, up 8.7% year over year, while total revenue grew only 3.1%), confirming that the enterprise segment is Zoom's viable growth engine.

## PART 5 — SYNTHESIS: THE COHERENT STRATEGIC LOGIC

The five analytical lenses converge on a single coherent diagnosis:

1. **PESTEL** shows that hybrid work is a durable structural driver (the market is growing), but AI governance risk and competitive intensity are rising — meaning the window for platform repositioning is open but not unlimited.

2. **Five Forces** shows that Zoom's core product is commoditised by bundling substitutes (Teams), buyer power is high, and rivalry is intense — meaning competing on meetings alone is a losing strategy.

3. **RBV/Dynamic Capabilities** shows that Zoom's durable assets are its brand and enterprise relationships, and its emerging capability is AI — meaning the path to advantage runs through deepening enterprise relationships via AI-powered platform expansion, not through price competition.

4. **Porter's Generic Strategies** confirms that Differentiation (not Cost Leadership) is the only viable generic position, and that differentiation must be grounded in platform breadth and AI quality.

5. **Ansoff Matrix** operationalises the growth path: ARPU expansion in existing accounts first, then geographic expansion and product development, with disciplined diversification.

The chain of reasoning is: *because* video meetings are commoditised (Five Forces), Zoom *cannot* win on product parity (RBV), and *must* differentiate via platform breadth and AI (Generic Strategies), using its enterprise customer base as the foundation for expansion (Ansoff), while the growing hybrid-work market provides the structural tailwind (PESTEL). Each step in this chain is warranted by evidence, not assertion.

## PART 6 — RECOMMENDATIONS AND ACTION PLAN

### Recommendation 1: Accelerate AI Monetisation Through Tiered Upselling (0–12 Months)

**What:** Convert the 50%+ AI Companion adoption rate into paid Custom AI Companion and AI Expert Assist subscriptions by introducing a clearly structured premium AI tier.

**Why:** By Q3 2024, Zoom reported that its AI Companion features were being used by over 50% of eligible customers, but the base AI features are included in existing plans at no additional charge. This represents a monetisation gap: adoption is proven, but revenue conversion is incomplete. Premium AI tiers (Custom AI Companion, role-specific AI agents) create a new revenue stream from the existing base.

**How it connects:** The RBV analysis identified AI as Zoom's most promising emerging VRIN capability; the Ansoff Matrix identifies ARPU expansion in existing accounts (Quadrant 1) as the lowest-risk, fastest-return growth vector. This recommendation executes both simultaneously.

**Metric:** Increase AI-related ARR contribution from near-zero to 10% of total enterprise ARR within 12 months.

---

### Recommendation 2: Defend and Deepen Enterprise Relationships via Multi-Product Bundling (0–18 Months)

**What:** Proactively offer enterprise customers a "Zoom Workplace" bundle — Meetings + Phone + Contact Centre + Workvivo + AI Companion — at a discount to individual product pricing, with multi-year contract incentives.

**Why:** Zoom had approximately 220,400 Enterprise customers at the end of Q4 FY2024, with a trailing 12-month net dollar expansion rate of 101% — meaning existing enterprise customers are barely spending more year-on-year. A net dollar expansion rate of 101% is dangerously close to flat; best-in-class SaaS platforms target 120%+. Multi-product bundling is the proven mechanism to raise this rate. Zoom Phone, Contact Centre, and AI features provide a unified experience for internal collaboration and external customer engagement, increasing wallet share per customer.

**How it connects:** The Five Forces analysis established that buyer power is high because Teams provides a bundled alternative. Zoom's counter-strategy must be its own bundle — one that is *superior* in quality for communication-intensive use cases, even if Microsoft's is cheaper. Porter's Differentiation strategy requires that the bundle be meaningfully better, not merely equivalent.

**Metric:** Raise the enterprise net dollar expansion rate from 101% to 115%+ within 18 months.

### Recommendation 3: Accelerate Zoom Phone to 15 Million Seats (12–36 Months)

**What:** Invest in Zoom Phone channel partnerships, carrier integrations, and geographic rollouts to double the seat count.

**Why:** Zoom Phone has exceeded 10 million paid seats globally in Q3 of Fiscal Year 2026, making it one of the fastest-growing products in the company's history. Telephony is a high-retention, recurring-revenue product that creates deep operational integration within a customer's IT infrastructure — precisely the kind of switching cost that the RBV analysis identified as lacking in Zoom's core meetings product. Every Zoom Phone seat makes a customer *less* likely to migrate to Teams for meetings, because displacing Zoom would now require replacing the phone system too.

**How it connects:** The Ansoff Matrix Quadrant 3 (product development in existing markets) identifies Zoom Phone as the primary product-expansion lever. The RBV analysis identifies switching costs as a missing VRIN attribute; Zoom Phone directly addresses this gap by embedding Zoom into mission-critical telephony infrastructure.

**Metric:** 15 million Zoom Phone paid seats by end of FY2027.

**What:** Develop dedicated compliance and security certifications (FedRAMP High, HIPAA BAA, ISO 27001) and go-to-market teams for healthcare, government, and financial services verticals.

**Why:** Security and encryption concerns remain a persistent vulnerability for Zoom, particularly given the distribution of fake Zoom apps and ongoing questions about data privacy. However, this weakness can be converted into a strength: organisations in regulated industries have *higher* security requirements than Microsoft's standard Teams deployment can satisfy, and Zoom's focused investment in compliance certifications can create a defensible niche. Cisco Webex supports approximately 45,000 Fortune 500 and government customers through its emphasis on secure hybrid solutions — demonstrating that a security-focused positioning is commercially viable in enterprise software.

**How it connects:** Porter's Focus strategy is deployed here not as a retreat but as a *complement* to the broad differentiation strategy: Zoom pursues broad differentiation in general enterprise while also building focused positions in verticals where security requirements create natural barriers to Microsoft substitution. The PESTEL analysis flagged legal/security risk as a threat; this recommendation converts that threat into a source of competitive advantage.

**Metric:** Achieve FedRAMP High authorisation and sign 500 new government/healthcare enterprise contracts within 24 months.

## CONCLUSION

Zoom's strategic situation is not a story of irreversible decline but of a company at a critical inflection point. Its core product has been commoditised by bundling rivals; its Online segment is contracting; and its stock market perception lags its operational reality. However, its brand, its 220,400 enterprise customer relationships, its growing multi-product portfolio, and its early AI Companion traction constitute a genuine platform foundation. The five analytical frameworks applied here — PESTEL, Porter's Five Forces, RBV/Dynamic Capabilities, Porter's Generic Strategies, and the Ansoff Matrix — all converge on the same strategic logic: Zoom must pursue **broad differentiation through AI-powered platform depth**, using its existing enterprise relationships as the monetisation base, Zoom Phone as the switching-cost anchor, and vertical security focus as the competitive moat against Microsoft's bundling dominance. Executed in the sequence recommended above, this strategy gives Zoom a credible path from pandemic artefact to durable enterprise platform.
