# STRATEGIC CASE STUDY
## Vattenfall AB: Navigating the Dual Mandate — Commercial Viability and the Fossil-Freedom Mission in Sweden's Electrifying Economy

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Vattenfall AB, Sweden's wholly state-owned energy utility, occupies a unique and increasingly tense strategic position. As Sweden's electricity demand is projected to double by 2045, Vattenfall must simultaneously serve a commercial mandate (generating returns for its state owner), a public-policy mandate (accelerating the fossil-free energy transition), and an energy-security mandate (ensuring reliable supply). These three imperatives are not always compatible. The company's recent strategic choices — shelving an offshore wind project due to a 40% cost overrun, committing SEK 170 billion in investment over 2025–2029, pursuing small modular reactors (SMRs), and contemplating divestment of district heating — reveal a company at a genuine strategic crossroads. This case study analyses Vattenfall's situation using PESTEL, Porter's Five Forces, the Resource-Based View (RBV), and the Ansoff Matrix, before synthesising these into a coherent set of strategic recommendations grounded in the Dual Mandate tension.

## 1. BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### 1.1 Origins and Ownership

Vattenfall was established in 1909 as *Kungliga Vattenfallsstyrelsen* — a Swedish state agency created to harness the country's hydropower resources — and since its founding has operated as an instrument of the Swedish state: initially to promote industrialisation, later as an element of industrial policy. In 1992, Vattenfall was reformed as the joint-stock company Vattenfall AB; at the same time, the transmission grid (220 kV and 400 kV lines) was transferred to the newly formed state agency Svenska kraftnät, which also became responsible for the operation of the national power system.

Between 1990 and 2009, Vattenfall expanded considerably — acquiring stakes in HEW, the Polish heat company EW, Danish Elsam A/S, and Dutch Nuon — becoming the third-largest electricity producer in Germany. Following the expansion period, Vattenfall started to divest parts of its business in Denmark and Poland in a strategy to focus on three core markets: Sweden, the Netherlands, and Germany. This European expansion, however, drew sharp criticism: Vattenfall's CO2 emissions in Germany alone were bigger than those in the whole of Sweden — generating a profound tension between commercial growth and Sweden's environmental values.

A strategic reversal followed. Vattenfall signed an agreement to sell its lignite operations to Czech energy company EPH with its financial partner PPF Investments — a divestment described as "good strategically but also financially given current and expected market conditions." The sale meant more than 75% of Vattenfall's production would be climate neutral, compared to about 50% before. The exit came at a cost: Vattenfall took a US$3 billion hit to exit its German coal operations, reducing its CO2 footprint from over 80 million tonnes per year to less than 25 million tonnes.

Today, the Swedish state-owned power utility Vattenfall posted 2024 revenues of SEK 246 billion (€22 billion, -15% compared to 2023) and a profit of SEK 33 billion (€3 billion, x3.3), with power generation of approximately 100 TWh.

### 1.2 The Strategic Situation

The central strategic situation is this: **Sweden's electricity system faces a structural demand shock**. The Swedish government's 2024 Energy Bill targets annual electricity demand of at least 300 TWh by 2045 — more than double current consumption — with supply and demand needs to be clarified for each geographic zone every five years between 2030 and 2045. As industries switch from fossil fuels to electricity and transportation and heating become increasingly electrified, demand for electricity is growing rapidly.

Vattenfall, as the dominant state-owned utility, is expected to be the primary vehicle for meeting this demand — yet it faces significant strategic contradictions:

1. **The Offshore Wind Cost Crisis**: Although demand for fossil-free electricity is greater than ever, the market for offshore wind power is challenging. Higher inflation and capital costs are affecting the entire energy sector, but the geopolitical situation has made offshore wind and its supply chain particularly vulnerable — with overall cost increases up to 40%, leading to an impairment for wind power in Norfolk, UK, with a total impact on earnings of SEK 5.5 billion.

2. **The Nuclear Opportunity**: Vattenfall has narrowed its small modular reactor (SMR) supplier selection to two finalists — GE Vernova Hitachi Nuclear Energy's BWRX-300 and the Rolls-Royce SMR — for potential construction of new nuclear capacity adjacent to its Ringhals plant site on Sweden's Värö Peninsula, representing Sweden's first potential new nuclear build in more than 40 years.

3. **The Portfolio Rationalisation**: As part of its continuous portfolio evaluation, Vattenfall will assess future options for its district heating operations in the UK, Sweden, and the Netherlands, including potential divestment.

4. **The Investment Imperative**: Vattenfall is committed to enabling a fossil-free future and therefore plans to invest SEK 170 billion over the period 2025 to 2029, focusing on fossil-free electricity generation and electricity grid, with growth investments totalling SEK 92 billion, with the largest share — SEK 59 billion — planned for investment in new wind power.

## 2. ANALYTICAL FRAMEWORKS

### 2.1 PESTEL Analysis

#### Political
The Swedish government introduced an energy bill to parliament in March 2024 based on two central pillars: system planning and security of supply. A key objective is to ensure that Sweden can meet annual electricity demand of at least 300 TWh by 2045. The government has also reversed its nuclear phase-out policy: Swedish utility Vattenfall has applied for state financing to build new reactors at its Ringhals plant — the first company to do so under a scheme the government hopes will lead to a renaissance in nuclear power.

The government also controls Vattenfall directly, setting ownership directives and appointing the board — creating a principal–agent dynamic in which the government (principal) may impose non-commercial objectives on the firm (agent). Geopolitically, Sweden has demanded that Germany introduce price zones in its electricity market, arguing this would significantly reduce the impact of high German electricity prices on Sweden.

#### Economic
Electricity prices in the Nordics were on average 36% lower for the full year of 2024 compared to 2023, driven primarily by strong hydrological balance and increased renewable generation — compressing Vattenfall's revenues despite high output. Meanwhile, Swedish industrial electricity demand will increase by 88 TWh by 2035, corresponding to almost two-thirds of Sweden's current total electricity demand of 140 TWh. This creates a long-run demand pull that is strategically favourable for a major generator.

The short-run price environment is also volatile: the imbalance price on 7 April 2024 reached -1005.04 EUR/MWh for 15:00 to 16:00 in several bidding zones in the Nordics including SE1, SE2, SE3, SE4, illustrating the system-balancing challenge that variable renewables create.

#### Social
Quite a few politicians, environmentalists, and journalists hold the opinion that Vattenfall has transformed from a national jewel with a high level of social responsibility and low prices into a profit-driven company without regard to green criteria — the worst example being that Vattenfall's CO2 emissions in Germany alone were bigger than those in the whole of Sweden. There is now broad social support for the fossil-freedom mission, while rising electricity prices in southern Sweden create political pressure to ensure affordability.

#### Technological
Launched in 2016 as a partnership between steel producer SSAB, mining company LKAB, and Vattenfall, HYBRIT plans to replace coal with fossil-free hydrogen produced using renewable electricity. The project, co-financed by the Swedish Energy Agency, has been declared technically successful and ready for scale-up to support large-scale hydrogen users such as direct reduction (DRI) plants. HYBRIT has extended the pilot through 2026 to refine designs and prepare for future commercial-scale hydrogen storage. Critically, the transition to HYBRIT technology is expected to reduce Sweden's total carbon dioxide emissions by over 10%.

#### Environmental
Vattenfall's share of fossil-free electricity generation increased to 90% in 2024, with the Hollandse Kust Zuid and Vesterhav projects being large contributors. The production of fossil-free electricity amounted to 99.6 TWh. A directional decision has also been taken to extend the operating time of Forsmark and Ringhals reactors from 60 to 80 years.

#### Legal/Regulatory
Sweden's electricity market is divided into four price zones (SE1–SE4). A change as extensive as moving from four price areas to one would have a major impact on electricity prices in Sweden: prices would fall in the current SE3 and SE4 price areas, while SE1 and SE2 can expect price increases — a reform with profound implications for Vattenfall's revenue from its predominantly northern generation assets. Vattenfall also faces ongoing litigation complexity: it has been claiming almost €4.7 billion against the German government at ICSID for the 2011 nuclear phase-out decision.

### 2.2 Porter's Five Forces

#### Threat of New Entrants: **Moderate–High**
Vattenfall's competitors include Ørsted, Statkraft, Electricite de France, ENGIE, Endesa, RWE, Fortum, and E.ON. Capital requirements remain high in generation (a barrier), but falling technology costs and government auction mechanisms have reduced entry barriers in wind and solar. In nuclear and hydro, barriers remain very high — regulatory, technical, and financial — giving Vattenfall durable protection in its core generation assets.

#### Bargaining Power of Suppliers: **High (and rising)**
Vattenfall suspended development of its 1.4 GW Norfolk Boreas wind farm, citing inflation driving up costs by 40% within a year. Just one year earlier, Vattenfall had been awarded a record-low Contract-for-Difference for the wind farm at £37.35/MWh, which equated to £51.10/MWh in current money. This illustrates the structural pricing power of a concentrated offshore wind supply chain. Similarly, nuclear supply chains — for SMR components, fuel, and specialist engineering — are highly concentrated.

#### Bargaining Power of Buyers: **Moderate**
In wholesale electricity markets, Vattenfall is a price-taker. In retail markets, switching costs are low and competition is intense — Vattenfall entered the British retail energy market in June 2017 but announced in November 2019 that it was considering pulling out again, citing unfavourable market conditions including strong competition and government-imposed price controls. In B2B industrial segments, large customers like SSAB and LKAB have significant negotiating power but also create long-term partnership value through initiatives like HYBRIT.

#### Threat of Substitutes: **Moderate**
For district heating, substitutes include heat pumps and individual boilers. However, the electrification megatrend structurally reduces substitution threats for electricity — as transport and industry electrify, demand for electricity grows. Distributed generation (rooftop solar, battery storage) represents a disruptive substitute in some retail segments.

#### Competitive Rivalry: **High**
Vattenfall is one of the largest producers of electricity in Europe, operating in several countries and competing in a diverse and dynamic market. The competitive landscape is shaped by strategic investments in renewable energy sources, technological advancements, and the evolving needs of consumers and industry. Rivalry is intensifying as European utilities race to deploy renewable capacity and secure grid connections.

**Summary**: Vattenfall's most durable competitive position lies in its legacy hydro and nuclear assets (high barriers, captive resources). Its most exposed position is in offshore wind development, where supplier power is high and rivalry is intense.

| State ownership / sovereign balance sheet | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | Partial | Enables long-horizon investment; risk of political interference |

| Offshore wind development capability | ✓ | Partial | No | No | Valuable but not rare; exposed to cost inflation |
| Integrated utility model | ✓ | Partial | Partial | Partial | Provides hedging value; under strategic review |

Vattenfall's ambition is to be a world leader in the operation of both nuclear and hydro power with high safety and sustainability standards as well as cost-efficient fossil-free electricity production with high availability. This ambition is well-aligned with the VRIN analysis: hydro and nuclear are the assets where Vattenfall's inimitability is strongest.

Vattenfall generates electricity and heat from many types of energy sources, including hydro, wind, solar, biomass, waste, nuclear, and natural gas, and is one of the largest providers of fossil-free electricity in Europe. Hydro power provides large-scale, stable renewable electricity and helps balance intermittent generation.

**Key RBV insight**: Vattenfall's truly VRIN resources are its *physical, geography-locked assets* (hydro reservoirs, nuclear plant sites) and its *state-backed balance sheet*. Its offshore wind development capability — currently the centrepiece of its growth strategy — is not VRIN: it is contested by well-capitalised rivals and exposed to supply-chain volatility. This suggests the growth strategy should be rebalanced towards assets where the company has genuine inimitability.

### 2.4 Ansoff Matrix

The Ansoff Matrix (Ansoff, 1957) maps growth strategies against existing/new products and existing/new markets.

| | **Existing Markets** | **New Markets** |
|---|---|---|
| **Existing Products** | **Market Penetration**: Expanding hydro and nuclear output in Sweden; deepening retail electricity in Nordic markets | **Market Development**: Exporting HYBRIT technology; expanding offshore wind in new geographies |
| **New Products** | **Product Development**: SMRs at Ringhals; EV charging infrastructure; flexibility services | **Diversification**: Industrial decarbonisation-as-a-service; hydrogen economy |

Vattenfall's business model is centred around creating value for its stakeholders, contributing to the electricity generation and supply system with its portfolio of hydro and nuclear power and strong growth in large-scale renewables such as wind, as an integrated utility present throughout the full value chain.

The potential divestment of district heating, which also includes the electricity distribution business in the UK, is driven by the need to prioritise investments in the energy transition, particularly in fossil-free electricity generation, distribution, and storage solutions. Vattenfall's strategic focus on the energy transition is evident in its planned total investment of SEK 170 billion between 2025 and 2029.

**Ansoff risk assessment**: Vattenfall is simultaneously pursuing all four quadrants — an ambitious but potentially overextended strategy. The highest-risk quadrant — diversification — is where HYBRIT and hydrogen sit. These are strategically important for Sweden's industrial decarbonisation but require patience and tolerance for long payback periods. The lowest-risk quadrant — market penetration in existing fossil-free assets (hydro + nuclear extension) — is where Vattenfall's VRIN resources are concentrated and where returns are most predictable.

## 3. THE CORE STRATEGIC TENSION: THE DUAL MANDATE

The analytical frameworks converge on a single, overarching tension: **Vattenfall's Dual Mandate**.

As a state-owned enterprise, Vattenfall is asked to do two things simultaneously:
1. **Generate commercial returns** sufficient to justify continued state ownership and fund its investment programme.
2. **Advance Sweden's public policy objectives** — fossil-free energy transition, energy security, industrial decarbonisation — even where these are not commercially optimal.

This is a classic principal–agent problem. Since the start, Vattenfall has been a tool in the hands of its owner, the Swedish state: initially to promote the country's industrialisation, later as an element of industrial policy. The tension has been present throughout its history and has repeatedly produced strategic drift:

- The European expansion (1990–2009) was commercially motivated but produced the lignite contradiction.
- The lignite exit (2016) was policy-motivated but commercially costly (US$3 billion loss).
- The Norfolk Boreas pause (2023) was commercially rational but politically awkward for a company positioned as the engine of fossil freedom.
- The SMR investment (2025 onwards) is policy-critical but commercially uncertain, dependent on state financing.

Although big steps have been taken towards fossil freedom during the year, society is still heavily dependent on fossil fuels. It not only endangers our climate, but also our energy supply and our way of life. This statement from Vattenfall's own 2024 Annual Report captures the dual mandate precisely: the company is simultaneously a commercial actor and a vehicle for societal transformation.

The dual mandate is not simply a governance problem; it is the central strategic problem from which all other issues flow. A coherent strategy must resolve — or at least explicitly manage — this tension.

## 4. SYNTHESIS: THE ANALYTICAL CHAIN

The four frameworks connect into a coherent chain of reasoning:

**PESTEL establishes the macro-context**: a structurally growing electricity demand (political + economic forces), a technology landscape that is promising but supply-chain constrained (technological), and a regulatory environment that is broadly supportive but in flux (legal). These macro forces create a long-run opportunity for a large-scale, fossil-free generator — but they also create short-run volatility and policy uncertainty that make capital allocation difficult.

**Porter's Five Forces identifies where Vattenfall is strong and where it is exposed**: strong in legacy generation (high barriers, VRIN assets, limited rivalry), exposed in offshore wind development (high supplier power, intense rivalry, demonstrated cost overrun risk) and retail (low switching costs, competitive intensity). The Five Forces analysis therefore suggests a strategic retreat from exposed positions and a reinforcement of protected ones.

**The RBV confirms what Vattenfall should protect and build**: hydro and nuclear are the true sources of sustainable competitive advantage — geography-locked, inimitable, and not subject to the same supply-chain dynamics as offshore wind. Offshore wind development capability is contested and replicable; it should be pursued selectively, not as the primary growth engine. HYBRIT is a rare and potentially inimitable capability that deserves strategic elevation.

**The Ansoff Matrix reveals the risk profile of the current strategy**: Vattenfall is spread across all four quadrants simultaneously, creating capital allocation and management attention challenges. The highest-return, lowest-risk path is market penetration in existing fossil-free assets (hydro + nuclear extension), supplemented by selective product development (SMRs, flexibility services, HYBRIT scaling).

**The Dual Mandate tension explains why the strategy has been inconsistent**: without a clear resolution of the commercial vs. policy mandate, strategic decisions oscillate between commercial rationality (pausing Norfolk Boreas) and policy obligation (pursuing SMRs at uncertain returns). This produces strategic incoherence that undermines both commercial performance and policy effectiveness.

**The logical conclusion**: Vattenfall needs a strategy that *explicitly acknowledges* the dual mandate, *allocates different parts of the portfolio* to commercial vs. policy objectives, and *establishes clear governance mechanisms* to manage the trade-offs — rather than pretending the tension does not exist.

## 5. RECOMMENDATIONS AND ACTION PLAN

### Recommendation 1: Formalise the Dual Mandate Architecture
**What**: The Swedish government and Vattenfall's board should formally separate the company's portfolio into two strategic buckets:
- **Commercial Core** (hydro, operating nuclear, retail, distribution): managed for risk-adjusted return, with clear commercial KPIs.
- **Policy Frontier** (SMRs, HYBRIT/hydrogen, grid infrastructure): managed for strategic/societal return, with explicit state risk-sharing, separate financing structures, and longer payback horizons.

**Why**: The RBV and principal–agent analysis both point to the same problem — applying commercial return criteria to policy-frontier investments (like SMRs) leads to underinvestment; applying policy logic to commercial assets (like hydro) leads to suboptimal pricing and capital misallocation. The application submitted by Vattenfall's subsidiary Videberg Kraft AB for state financing — backed by several large Swedish industrial corporations through the Industrikraft i Sverige AB consortium — is already a step in this direction, and should be formalised into a permanent governance architecture.

**How**: Establish a formal ownership directive from the Swedish Ministry of Finance distinguishing between the two buckets, setting different KPIs for each, and committing state financing instruments (guarantees, concessional loans, contracts for difference) to the Policy Frontier bucket.

---

### Recommendation 2: Rebalance the Growth Strategy from Offshore Wind to Nuclear + Hydro
**What**: Reduce the share of the SEK 170 billion investment plan allocated to new offshore wind (currently SEK 59 billion / 35%) and reallocate towards: (a) extending and uprating existing hydro and nuclear assets, (b) accelerating the Ringhals SMR programme, and (c) grid flexibility and storage.

**Why**: Porter's Five Forces shows that offshore wind development is the segment where Vattenfall is most exposed. Vattenfall had suspended development of its 1.4 GW Norfolk Boreas wind farm at a cost of £415 million, after more than a decade of development — the project having been awarded a record-low Contract-for-Difference just one year earlier. This is not an isolated incident but a structural feature of offshore wind economics. The RBV confirms that hydro and nuclear are Vattenfall's VRIN assets. Vattenfall said the SMR project is necessary to meet rising electricity demand, bolster energy security, and advance the country's net-zero targets — with the Swedish utility's board formally deciding on 18 July 2025 to build the new project.

**How**: Apply a higher risk-adjusted hurdle rate to offshore wind projects that reflects demonstrated supply-chain inflation. Proceed only with projects where supply-chain costs are locked in via long-term contracts. Redirect freed capital to hydro uprating (pumped storage for system flexibility) and to accelerating the SMR permitting and engineering timeline.

---

### Recommendation 3: Accelerate and Scale the HYBRIT / Industrial Decarbonisation Platform
**What**: Elevate HYBRIT from a pilot/R&D project to a core strategic growth platform, developing Vattenfall as the primary provider of fossil-free electricity to Sweden's industrial decarbonisation.

**Why**: The HYBRIT project is the first in the world to show that the fossil-free value chain — from ore to steel — works on a semi-industrial scale, with research results spanning from 2018 to 2024, multiple patents secured, and the process now continuing in the next phase of implementation. This is the clearest example of Vattenfall's VRIN capabilities in action — a first-mover, patented, technically validated platform. In southern Sweden, the electricity deficit is expected to almost triple from 11 TWh in 2030 to 30 TWh in 2035, creating urgent demand for reliable, large-scale fossil-free supply that Vattenfall's hydro-nuclear baseload is uniquely positioned to provide.

**How**: Structure dedicated long-term PPAs (15–25 year terms) for industrial decarbonisation customers, backed by hydro and nuclear baseload. Create a dedicated "Industrial Decarbonisation" business unit within Vattenfall. Pursue international licensing of HYBRIT technology as a revenue stream.

---

### Recommendation 4: Complete the Portfolio Rationalisation — Divest Non-Core Assets
**What**: Proceed with the divestment of district heating operations in the UK and Netherlands, and use proceeds to fund the SMR programme and grid investment.

**Why**: The Ansoff analysis and RBV both indicate that district heating in the UK and Netherlands is not a core strategic asset. It does not leverage Vattenfall's VRIN resources, ties up capital in infrastructure with limited growth potential, and creates operational complexity. Vattenfall is a provider of district heating to the equivalent of approximately 550,000 households — a significant operational commitment that diverts management attention and capital from the energy transition priorities.

**How**: Proceed with the ownership assessment announced in 2025. Prioritise sale of UK and Netherlands operations (lowest strategic fit). Retain Swedish district heating selectively where integrated with fossil-free heat networks. Reinvest proceeds into the Ringhals SMR programme and grid flexibility assets.

---

### Recommendation 5: Establish a Proactive Regulatory and Market Design Engagement Strategy
**What**: Vattenfall should take a leading, proactive role in shaping Sweden's electricity market design — particularly on price zone reform, grid investment, and nuclear financing — rather than reacting to government decisions.

**Why**: The PESTEL analysis shows the regulatory environment is in flux. To keep pace with planned needs, Sweden needs to add at least ten new terawatt hours of electricity per year — a target that requires coordinated investment planning between Vattenfall, Svenska kraftnät, and the government. The government's 300 TWh planning target for electricity demand by 2045 is not yet underpinned by concrete planning for the sources of generation, locational needs, and grid infrastructure to support it. Without clarity on future energy infrastructure, companies face investment uncertainty. Vattenfall's unique position as a state-owned company gives it a legitimate and powerful voice in closing this planning gap.

**How**: Establish a dedicated Government Affairs and Market Design function at the group level. Publish Vattenfall's position on price zone reform with full economic modelling. Actively support Svenska kraftnät's "Fossil-free in Upper Norrland" grid investment programme as a complementary investment that increases the value of Vattenfall's northern generation assets.

## 6. CONCLUSION

Vattenfall's strategic situation is not primarily a question of which technologies to invest in — it is a question of how to govern a state-owned enterprise that is asked to be simultaneously a commercial utility and an instrument of national energy policy. The analytical chain from PESTEL through Porter's Five Forces, RBV, and Ansoff converges on a clear diagnosis: Vattenfall's most durable competitive advantages lie in its legacy hydro and nuclear assets, its state-backed balance sheet, and its first-mover position in industrial decarbonisation (HYBRIT). Its most exposed position is in offshore wind development, where it lacks genuine inimitability and faces severe supply-chain risk.

The recommended strategy is not a retreat but a *reorientation*: protect and extend the VRIN core (hydro + nuclear), formalise the dual mandate to resolve governance ambiguity, scale the HYBRIT platform as a strategic differentiator, divest non-core assets to concentrate capital, and engage proactively in regulatory design. This approach is coherent because it aligns Vattenfall's resource base with its strategic choices, resolves the principal–agent tension through explicit mandate architecture, and positions the company to meet Sweden's 300 TWh electricity demand target by 2045 — on terms that are both commercially sustainable and strategically distinctive.

The direction is clear; the pace is not. What this analysis adds is a framework for ensuring the pace is not merely reactive to market shocks, but driven by a coherent, source-grounded strategy.
