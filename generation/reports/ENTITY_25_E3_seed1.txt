# CASE STUDY: MTN GROUP — NAVIGATING THE PLATFORM PIVOT
### Strategic Analysis of a Pan-African Telecom at an Inflection Point

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

MTN Group, Africa's largest mobile network operator by subscribers, faces a defining strategic inflection point. Its legacy connectivity business is being eroded by macroeconomic shocks (notably naira devaluation), regulatory constraints, and intensifying competition from Airtel Africa, Orange, and Safaricom. Simultaneously, MTN's fintech platform (MoMo) and its digital infrastructure arm (Bayobab) are demonstrating accelerating growth and offer a credible path to a higher-margin, more resilient business model. The central strategic question is: **how should MTN allocate resources and sequence its pivot from a connectivity-led operator to a three-platform technology group — and what must it do to protect value during the transition?**

This case study applies five analytical frameworks — PESTEL, Porter's Five Forces, the Resource-Based View (RBV), the Ansoff Growth Matrix, and BCG Portfolio Analysis — to diagnose the strategic situation and generate a coherent action plan. Each claim is sourced, each claim is connected to the analysis, and each inferential step states why the evidence warrants the conclusion.

## 1. THE STRATEGIC SITUATION

### 1.1 Company Background

MTN's MoMo mobile money platform, serving 100 million users across 16 African nations, has emerged as a cornerstone of its digital strategy. MTN Group posted excellent operational and financial results for 2025, delivering significantly on its Ambition 2025 strategy and transitioning to Ambition 2030 priorities to capture value from the attractive structural growth opportunities brought about by accelerated data adoption and financial inclusion across Africa. As MTN looks beyond 2025, the company is streamlining its focus into three principal platforms: "Connectivity," "Fintech," and "Digital Infrastructure."

### 1.2 The Problem

MTN's financial performance in 2023–2024 exposed a critical vulnerability: over-concentration in markets subject to severe currency and regulatory risk. The company reported a loss before tax of R9 billion in the six-month period ended June 30, 2024, compared with a restated profit of R8.3 billion a year earlier. MTN Nigeria, once the MTN Group's top-performing market with a $5.03 ARPU in Q1 2023, has seen its user revenue fall with an ARPU of $2.09 by Q3 2024.

The paradox is striking: the volume of fintech transactions on MTN's networks rose by 15% to more than 20 billion valued at over US$320 billion, while the number of active Mobile Money (MoMo) users rose to 63 million. Yet these gains were masked in reported figures by currency translation losses: reported Group service revenue declined 15.4% to R177.8 billion, impacted by local currency weakness. The strategic challenge is therefore not merely operational but architectural: MTN must restructure its business model so that its growth engines are financially legible and protected from macro shocks, while its legacy connectivity base continues to fund the transition.

## 2. PESTEL ANALYSIS: THE MACRO ENVIRONMENT

### Political

African telecom markets are characterised by high political risk and unpredictable regulatory intervention. In January 2024, MTN's offices in Guinea-Conakry's Almamyah and Coleah were sealed by the local post and telecommunications regulator over a licensing payment issue. In February 2024, MTN concluded the sale of its Afghanistan business to Beirut-based M1 New Ventures for $35 million. In Nigeria, the regulator approved tariff adjustments of up to 50% for industry operators in January 2025, and MTN Nigeria began to phase in the pricing adjustments across its offerings from mid-February 2025. This approval followed years of regulatory resistance to tariff increases despite record inflation — a pattern that illustrates a structural political risk: regulators in many African markets are subject to populist pressure to keep tariffs low, creating a systematic wedge between operator costs (largely USD-denominated) and revenues (local currency).

### Economic

The economic environment is the most acute near-term threat. MTN Nigeria Communications Plc recorded a N400.4 billion loss after tax in the full year of 2024 despite a 35.9% growth in revenue to N3.3 trillion. MTN's financial performance continues to be impacted by record-high inflation and the naira's devaluation, exacerbating the businesses' operational expenses. The telco's net foreign exchange losses increased by 24.98% to N925.36 billion from N740.43 billion in 2023. This is not an isolated shock: Nigeria's telecom industry ARPU for major telcos like MTN and Airtel has plummeted by over 40% in the past year — MTN Nigeria's ARPU falling from $5.03 in Q1 2023 to $2.09 by Q3 2024, while Airtel Nigeria fell from a $3 ARPU to $1.60.

Yet the macro picture is not uniformly negative. By 2030, mobile revenues will reach $61 billion in sub-Saharan Africa. The mobile industry's $140 billion contribution to GDP in 2023 is projected to reach $170 billion by 2030 if key connectivity barriers are addressed. By 2030, 5G alone is expected to contribute $10 billion to the region's economy, accounting for 6% of the mobile sector's total economic impact. This long-run demand trajectory provides strategic justification for continued investment, even amid near-term turbulence.

### Social

Africa's financial inclusion gap represents both a social challenge and MTN's most important commercial opportunity. Financial inclusion in sub-Saharan Africa has grown steadily over the past decade, rising from 34% of adults owning a financial account in 2014 to 58% in 2024. Mobile money has served as a key driver of financial inclusion in the region, where 40% of adults had a mobile money account as of 2024, the highest level of all world regions. Yet the unbanked population still represents a $1.3 trillion addressable opportunity. Adults in rural areas are 25% less likely to use mobile internet than their urban counterparts — a gap that widens to 48% in sub-Saharan Africa. This means MTN's growth runway in both connectivity and fintech remains substantial, but requires active strategies to reduce device costs and improve affordability.

### Technological

The technological environment is simultaneously an opportunity and a competitive threat. The shift from voice to data is accelerating: MTN Ghana's data revenue grew by 53.8%, totalling GHS 9.0 billion ($581 million), now accounting for 50.2% of total service revenue. Data traffic grew 27% in 2025, with average monthly consumption per user reaching 12.5 GB, up from 10.8 GB the previous year. However, 4G and 5G deployment requires massive capital expenditure. The GSMA estimates that operators in the region will need to spend $62 billion on their networks over the same period (2023–2030). 4G is projected to overtake 3G by 2027, and 5G adoption in the region is expected to gain momentum in the second half of this decade, rising to 17% by 2030.

### Environmental

Network energy costs are a growing operational concern, particularly given Africa's unreliable power grids. Conditions in Sudan remained challenging amidst the ongoing conflict in the country, and MTN's operation continued to be impacted by power outages, fuel shortages and other network disruptions. Broader energy transition pressures add to the investment burden facing operators.

### Legal/Regulatory

Regulatory fragmentation across 16 markets creates compliance complexity and unpredictable cost. MTN's structural separation of fintech operations is partly a regulatory strategy. The structural separation of fintech operations from core GSM services, which saw overwhelming shareholder approval of 99.9% in MTN Uganda in July 2025, aims to foster greater scale and efficiency within the fintech segment. This creates standalone regulated entities that can engage directly with central banks and financial regulators on their own terms.

**PESTEL Warrant:** The PESTEL analysis reveals that MTN's core strategic challenge is structural rather than merely competitive: the business model of a USD-cost, local-currency-revenue telecom operator in politically volatile markets is inherently fragile. This warrants a strategic response that either (a) reduces USD cost exposure through asset ownership (IHS Towers acquisition), (b) generates USD or hard-currency-equivalent revenues (fintech cross-border remittances, enterprise services), or (c) builds regulatory buffers through structural separation and proactive stakeholder engagement. Without this structural response, no amount of operational efficiency will fully insulate MTN from the macro risks identified above.

## 3. PORTER'S FIVE FORCES: INDUSTRY STRUCTURE

### Threat of New Entrants — Moderate but Rising

Telecom infrastructure requires enormous capital investment, and spectrum licences are government-controlled, creating high barriers to entry for traditional MNOs. However, the threat of non-traditional entrants — technology platforms offering OTT messaging and financial services — is rising. Safaricom's entry into Ethiopia signalled the end of state-owned Ethio Telecom's decades-long monopoly. This demonstrates that even markets once considered impenetrable can be opened by well-capitalised new entrants. In fintech, startups with lower regulatory burdens can attack specific segments of the MoMo value chain.

### Bargaining Power of Suppliers — Moderate to High

MTN's key suppliers are network equipment vendors (priced in USD) and tower companies. Equipment priced in USD creates the currency mismatch at the heart of MTN's financial problems. MTN's proposed acquisition marks a strategic change for the company, which has historically relied on independent tower companies like IHS Towers to run its telecom infrastructure. By bringing these operations in-house, MTN is seeking greater control over network quality, costs and long-term expansion. The $2.2 billion IHS acquisition directly addresses supplier power in the tower segment.

### Bargaining Power of Buyers — Moderate

African mobile consumers are price-sensitive, and regulators act as proxies for consumer bargaining power — as demonstrated by Nigeria's years-long tariff freeze. However, MTN and Airtel ARPU jumped 32% after the January 2025 tariff hike. This recovery confirms that when all operators raise prices simultaneously, consumers have limited substitution options — reducing effective buyer power in practice, even if political bargaining power remains high.

### Threat of Substitutes — High

Substitution pressure is intense and multi-directional. In voice and messaging, OTT platforms (WhatsApp, Telegram) substitute directly for traditional revenue streams. In financial services, bank-led mobile apps and state-owned platforms (Ethiopia's Telebirr) compete with MoMo. MTN Group is discontinuing its messaging and super app, Ayoba, marking the end of a years-long attempt to build a homegrown African alternative to global platforms like WhatsApp. At its peak, the app recorded over 35 million monthly active users by April 2024, with ambitions to scale to 100 million users. Its failure against WhatsApp's dominance illustrates the power of globally scaled substitutes.

### Competitive Rivalry — High

Five telecom giants are battling it out in Africa's mobile internet market: Orange, MTN, Maroc Telecom, Vodafone and Airtel. In mobile money, Orange Money saw 20.4% revenue growth, and mobile money processed $1.68 trillion in 2024. In Ghana, a Telecel–AirtelTigo merger is proposed to challenge MTN's dominance in the telecom sector.

**Five Forces Warrant:** The industry structure analysis reveals that MTN's most sustainable competitive position lies not in voice connectivity — where substitution threats are high, buyer power is politically amplified, and rivalry is intense — but in fintech and digital infrastructure, where network effects, regulatory moats, and scale create more defensible positions. This conclusion directly motivates the Ambition 2030 three-platform strategy and provides the industry-level justification for the resource allocation choices that follow.

## 4. RESOURCE-BASED VIEW: SOURCES OF COMPETITIVE ADVANTAGE

The RBV framework (Barney, 1991) holds that sustainable competitive advantage derives from resources and capabilities that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-Substitutable (VRIN). Applying this to MTN:

### Subscriber Scale and Network Coverage

With approximately 230 million subscribers, MTN currently holds the title of Africa's largest mobile network operator — a figure that has since grown to over 300 million. This scale is valuable (large addressable base for cross-selling), rare (no competitor matches this pan-African footprint), and difficult to imitate (replicating 30 years of licence acquisition, network build-out, and brand development is prohibitively costly). This scale is the foundation on which all other competitive advantages are built.

### MoMo Fintech Platform

MoMo is MTN's most strategically valuable asset by the VRIN test. It is **valuable**: fintech transaction value surged 38.0% to US$342.3 billion, reflecting rapid adoption of digital financial services. It is **rare**: no competitor operates a mobile money platform at comparable scale across 16 African markets simultaneously. It is **inimitable**: MoMo's value derives from network effects (more merchants attract more users; more users attract more merchants) and agent/merchant networks built over years — a $200 million investment from Mastercard in 2024, which granted MTN a 7.17% stake in MoMo Payment Service Bank, has accelerated the platform's expansion into digital banking and cross-border remittances. This external validation by a global payments giant confirms the platform's inimitable value. It is **non-substitutable** in the near term: 40% of sub-Saharan African adults use mobile money as their primary financial account, and for this population MoMo is not easily replaced by bank apps requiring formal documentation and physical branches.

### Bayobab Digital Infrastructure

Bayobab claims 127,000 km of open-access fiber, a presence in 54 African countries, access to 24 subsea cables and 235 points of presence. This physical infrastructure is valuable (essential for data traffic growth), rare (no competitor has comparable pan-African fiber coverage), inimitable (the capital and time required to replicate 127,000 km of fiber is prohibitive), and increasingly non-substitutable as data demand grows. Bayobab's partnership with TMCEL in Mozambique, providing international voice, roaming and IPX services, demonstrates that this infrastructure can generate revenue from third-party operators — a new business model that transforms a cost centre into a profit centre.

### Brand

MTN's $2.7 billion brand value makes it Africa's most valuable telecom brand. Brand equity reduces customer acquisition costs, supports premium pricing, and facilitates regulatory relationships. However, the Ayoba failure demonstrates that brand alone cannot overcome the network effects of globally dominant platforms — an important limit on this resource's strategic reach.

**RBV Warrant:** The RBV analysis confirms that MTN's durable competitive advantages are concentrated in three assets: subscriber scale, the MoMo platform, and Bayobab's infrastructure. These are precisely the three platforms around which Ambition 2030 is organised. The strategic implication — which flows directly from VRIN analysis — is that resource allocation should concentrate on deepening these advantages rather than defending declining legacy revenues (voice, SMS) or pursuing platform strategies (super-apps) where MTN lacks sustainable differentiation. The Ayoba failure is the empirical proof of this warrant: without VRIN resources in super-app development, brand and subscriber base alone were insufficient to compete.

## 5. ANSOFF GROWTH MATRIX

### Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets)

The January 2025 tariff increase in Nigeria is a market penetration move: extracting more value from existing subscribers in an existing market. MTN and Airtel ARPU jumped 32% after the tariff hike. However, the structural limits of market penetration in voice are clear: MTN Nigeria remains the group's 12th-ranked market by ARPU at $2.09, well behind Ghana's $5.60, MTN's highest-earning market. Penetration strategy alone cannot restore pre-devaluation margins because the underlying currency mismatch persists.

### Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets)

MTN's geographic expansion has been its historical growth engine. MTN served 301.3 million customers at the end of September 2025, including 165.8 million internet users. However, MTN has simultaneously been exiting markets — Afghanistan, Yemen, Syria — rationalising its footprint to focus on core African markets. This selective market development — deepening presence in high-potential African markets while exiting non-core geographies — is strategically coherent given the currency and political risks identified in the PESTEL analysis.

### Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets)

MoMo's expansion into advanced financial services represents product development within MTN's existing subscriber base. MoMo currently facilitates US$100 million worth of loans per month. MTN Ghana's Mobile Money (MoMo) revenue rose by 54.4% to GHS 4.4 billion ($284 million), contributing 24.9% to total service revenue. Fintech revenue increased by almost 29% in constant-currency terms in 2024. This confirms that product development in fintech is MTN's highest-growth strategic quadrant.

### Diversification (New Products, New Markets)

MTN aims to evolve from a traditional telecom operator into a technology company. Bayobab's provision of infrastructure services to third-party African operators represents related diversification: a new product (wholesale infrastructure services) sold to new customers (rival operators). By bringing tower operations in-house, MTN is seeking greater control over network quality, costs and long-term expansion. Tower lease revenues from third parties can be structured in USD, directly addressing the currency mismatch.

**Ansoff Warrant:** The matrix analysis reveals that MTN is pursuing all four quadrants simultaneously, which risks spreading resources too thinly. The evidence — specifically, the 29% constant-currency fintech growth versus the structural limits on voice ARPU recovery — demonstrates that product development (fintech deepening) and related diversification (infrastructure monetisation) offer the highest risk-adjusted returns. The Ayoba shutdown is the empirical proof: product development without VRIN resources (identified in the RBV analysis) destroys value. The Ansoff and RBV frameworks thus reinforce each other: only product development and diversification initiatives anchored in VRIN assets should receive growth capital.

## 6. BCG PORTFOLIO ANALYSIS

Mapping MTN's business units onto the BCG growth-share matrix:

### Stars (High Growth, High Market Share)

**MoMo Fintech:** MTN's fintech ecosystem remains a critical growth driver, with total transaction volumes increasing 13.9% to 16.9 billion and value up 38.0% to US$342.3 billion. Mobile Money (MoMo) monthly active users grew by 5.3% to 64.3 million. MTN is the market leader in mobile money across its footprint. This is MTN's clearest Star — it requires continued investment to maintain leadership against Orange Money and M-Pesa, but generates strong and accelerating returns.

**Bayobab Digital Infrastructure:** 127,000 km of fiber, pan-African coverage, growing third-party revenue. MTN's fintech division processed more than 23 billion transactions in 2025, with total transaction value exceeding $500 billion — and the infrastructure enabling this scale is Bayobab's. As data demand grows and 5G deployment accelerates, Bayobab's infrastructure will become increasingly valuable.

### Cash Cows (Low Growth, High Market Share)

**South Africa Connectivity:** MTN South Africa sustained a resilient overall performance, with service revenue growth of 3.1% to R43.2 billion for the year. As a mature, high-share market, South Africa generates the cash flows that fund investment in growth markets. Treating this as a Cash Cow — harvesting cash while maintaining quality — is the appropriate strategic posture.

**West and Central Africa Voice/SMS:** Legacy voice revenues in established West African markets are declining in real terms but remain cash-generative. As part of its expense efficiency programme (EEP), MTN realised sustainable savings of R3.8 billion in 2024, targeting R7–8 billion between 2024 and 2026. These markets should be managed for efficiency rather than growth investment.

### Question Marks (High Growth, Low Market Share)

**Nigeria Connectivity Post-Tariff Recovery:** Nigeria was a strong contributor to the Group's 23.19% revenue growth to $5.94 billion in H1 2025. However, MTN Nigeria remains the group's 12th-ranked market by ARPU at $2.09, well behind Ghana's $5.60. Nigeria is a Question Mark: it is a high-growth market (Africa's largest economy) but MTN's real-terms market position has been severely weakened by currency devaluation. The strategic question is whether to invest aggressively to rebuild ARPU or to harvest cash while stabilising the business.

### Dogs (Low Growth, Low Market Share)

**Ayoba Super-App:** MTN Group is discontinuing Ayoba, which peaked at 35 million monthly active users by April 2024 but failed to reach its 100 million user target against WhatsApp. The decision to exit is strategically correct and frees resources for Star investments.

**Non-Core Market Exits:** MTN concluded the sale of its Afghanistan business for $35 million in February 2024, having previously exited Yemen and Syria — consistent with BCG logic of divesting Dogs.

**BCG Warrant:** The portfolio analysis confirms that MTN's strategic resource allocation should concentrate investment in MoMo (Star) and Bayobab (emerging Star), harvest South Africa and mature West African markets (Cash Cows), make a binary decision on Nigeria (Question Mark requiring clear investment or harvest choice), and exit Dogs (Ayoba, non-core markets). This is the logic embedded in Ambition 2030's three-platform structure — but the analysis also reveals that execution discipline in the Nigeria Question Mark decision is the most consequential near-term strategic choice, because Nigeria's size means misallocation there has group-level consequences.

## 7. COHERENT CHAIN OF ANALYSIS: SYNTHESIS

The five frameworks converge on a consistent diagnosis, each framework building on the previous:

1. **PESTEL** identifies the structural fragility of a USD-cost, local-currency-revenue model in politically volatile African markets, and the long-run demand opportunity in connectivity and financial inclusion.
2. **Porter's Five Forces** shows that the most defensible competitive positions are in fintech (network effects, regulatory moats) and digital infrastructure (high barriers to entry), not in voice connectivity (high substitution threat, buyer power amplified by regulators).
3. **RBV** confirms that MTN's VRIN resources — subscriber scale, MoMo, and Bayobab — are precisely the assets that underpin fintech and infrastructure, not legacy voice.
4. **Ansoff** shows that product development (fintech deepening) and related diversification (infrastructure monetisation) offer the best risk-adjusted growth paths, while market penetration in voice has structural limits.
5. **BCG** confirms that MoMo and Bayobab are Stars deserving investment, South Africa and mature voice markets are Cash Cows to harvest, Nigeria is a critical Question Mark, and Ayoba was a Dog correctly being exited.

The chain of reasoning is: **macro fragility (PESTEL) → limited defensibility of legacy business (Five Forces) → VRIN advantages concentrated in fintech/infrastructure (RBV) → fintech and infrastructure are the right growth vectors (Ansoff) → invest in Stars, harvest Cash Cows, resolve the Nigeria Question Mark, exit Dogs (BCG).**

## 8. RECOMMENDATIONS AND ACTION PLAN

### Recommendation 1: Accelerate MoMo's Structural Separation and Deepen Financial Services *(Priority: Immediate)*

**Rationale:** MoMo is MTN's strongest VRIN asset (RBV), operating in the highest-growth quadrant (Ansoff product development) and classified as a Star (BCG). MTN currently facilitates US$100 million worth of loans per month — but MoMo's active user growth slowed to near 1% in 2024, indicating that the platform is approaching saturation in basic payment services and must move up the value chain.

**Action:** Accelerate the rollout of credit, insurance, and savings products across all 16 markets. Prioritise markets where financial inclusion gaps are largest — countries like Niger (14% account ownership), Chad (20%), and Madagascar (24%) face the least competitive pressure from bank-led alternatives. Use the Mastercard partnership's cross-border remittance capabilities to generate USD-denominated revenue streams, directly addressing the currency mismatch identified in PESTEL. Complete fintech structural separation across all remaining markets, following the Uganda model.

**Success metric:** MoMo monthly active users growing to 80 million by 2026, with fintech revenue contribution exceeding 25% of group service revenue in constant-currency terms.

---

### Recommendation 2: Complete the IHS Towers Acquisition and Operationalise Bayobab as a Third-Party Infrastructure Platform *(Priority: 12 Months)*

**Rationale:** The Five Forces analysis identifies supplier power in the tower segment as a structural vulnerability. The RBV analysis identifies Bayobab's infrastructure as a VRIN asset. The Ansoff analysis classifies third-party infrastructure services as related diversification — the highest-potential growth quadrant alongside fintech. "This proposed transaction is a pivotal step in further strengthening MTN Group's strategic and financial position for a future where digital infrastructure will become ever more essential to Africa's growth and development," said MTN Group President and CEO Ralph Mupita.

**Action:** Prioritise regulatory approvals for the $2.2 billion IHS Towers acquisition, which will combine Africa's largest mobile network operator with one of its largest digital infrastructure platforms. Post-acquisition, integrate IHS's nearly 29,000 high-quality towers into Bayobab's open-access infrastructure platform and actively market tower and fiber capacity to rival operators (Airtel, Orange, Safaricom). This transforms a capex burden into a revenue-generating asset and creates a natural hedge: if MTN's retail business underperforms in a market, Bayobab can still generate revenue from that market's other operators.

**Success metric:** Bayobab generating 15% of group EBITDA from third-party customers by 2027, reducing MTN's net infrastructure cost by R5 billion annually.

### Recommendation 3: Resolve the Nigeria Question Mark — Invest Selectively, Hedge Aggressively *(Priority: 12–18 Months)*

**Rationale:** The BCG analysis classifies Nigeria as a Question Mark — high-growth market, weakened competitive position — requiring a clear strategic choice. The PESTEL analysis identifies the naira's structural volatility as the root cause. The naira's 70% slide since 2023 pushed MTN Nigeria's local-currency revenues higher but translated into a forecast 90% USD profit drop, highlighting the disconnect between nominal growth and hard-currency earning power.

**Action:** (a) Implement a natural hedging strategy — borrow in naira to fund naira-denominated capex, reducing USD debt exposure in Nigeria, following the approach already used by Nokia and Scania in African markets. (b) Accelerate MoMo Nigeria's cross-border remittance product, which generates dollar-equivalent flows. (c) Pursue selective capex investment in data infrastructure (4G densification in Lagos, Abuja, Kano) to capture ARPU uplift from data growth. (d) Continue the partial stake sales in MTN Nigeria, Cameroon, Côte d'Ivoire and Ghana, reducing group-level concentration risk while retaining operational control.

**Success metric:** MTN Nigeria's USD ARPU recovering to $3.50 by end-2026, with net FX losses reduced by 30% through natural hedging measures.

---

### Recommendation 4: Institutionalise Regulatory Engagement as a Core Competence *(Priority: Ongoing)*

**Rationale:** The PESTEL analysis and Five Forces analysis both identify regulatory risk as a structural feature of African telecom markets, not an exceptional event. The Nigeria tariff freeze, Guinea-Conakry licensing dispute, and Ghana merger dynamics all demonstrate that regulatory outcomes are a major determinant of MTN's financial performance — yet regulatory engagement appears reactive rather than proactive.

**Action:** Establish a dedicated Pan-African Regulatory Affairs function reporting directly to the Group CEO, with country-level teams in all 16 markets. Develop a systematic stakeholder engagement programme that builds political capital through local investment commitments, employment creation data, and financial inclusion metrics. Use the fintech structural separation model — standalone regulated entities engaging directly with central banks — as a template for proactive regulatory positioning across all business lines.

**Success metric:** Zero market exits due to regulatory failure in the 2025–2030 period; tariff review cycles shortened to 24 months or less in all major markets.

### Recommendation 5: Reallocate Capital from Dogs to Stars — Complete the Portfolio Rationalisation *(Priority: Immediate)*

**Rationale:** The BCG analysis confirms that the Ayoba shutdown and non-core market exits are freeing capital. MTN has already raised R21.7 billion of its R25 billion non-core asset sales target, and should reach its target by next year. The remaining R3.3 billion from planned further stake sales should be directed to MoMo expansion and Bayobab/IHS investment, not to defending legacy voice revenues.

**Action:** Complete the R25 billion asset sales programme by end-2025 as targeted. Establish a formal capital allocation framework that explicitly links BCG portfolio classification to investment thresholds: Stars (MoMo, Bayobab) receive growth capex; Cash Cows (South Africa, mature West Africa) receive maintenance capex only; Question Marks (Nigeria, enterprise) receive time-limited investment with clear performance gates; Dogs receive no new investment and are divested.

**Success metric:** Stars (MoMo + Bayobab) accounting for 40% of group capex by 2026, up from an estimated 20% in 2024.
