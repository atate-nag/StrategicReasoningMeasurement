# ExxonMobil at the Strategic Crossroads: Navigating the Dual Imperative of Hydrocarbon Growth and Energy Transition

## CASE STUDY — Strategic Management Analysis

---

## 1. Introduction and Strategic Situation

ExxonMobil Corporation (NYSE: XOM) is one of the world's largest publicly traded integrated oil and gas companies, operating across upstream exploration and production, downstream refining, chemicals, and — increasingly — low-carbon solutions. In 2023–2025, the company executed two of the largest acquisitions in its modern history: a ~$60 billion all-stock purchase of Pioneer Natural Resources and a $4.9 billion acquisition of Denbury Inc. These moves crystallise a fundamental strategic tension: ExxonMobil is simultaneously doubling down on fossil-fuel production and investing in decarbonisation infrastructure. This case examines whether that dual strategy is coherent, competitive, and durable — and what ExxonMobil must do to convert it into sustained advantage.

The strategic situation is defined by three interlocking pressures: (1) a favourable near-term demand environment that rewards scale and low-cost production; (2) a structurally uncertain long-run demand trajectory as the energy transition accelerates; and (3) intensifying stakeholder demands — from investors, regulators, and civil society — for credible decarbonisation. The analysis proceeds through PESTEL (macro-environment), Porter's Five Forces (industry structure), a Resource-Based View/VRIN analysis (internal capabilities), and a SWOT synthesis, culminating in a set of strategic recommendations. Each inferential step states explicitly why the evidence warrants the conclusion drawn.

## 2. PESTEL Analysis

### 2.1 Political

The geopolitical environment is simultaneously supportive and volatile for ExxonMobil. Energy security concerns have reinforced Western governments' desire for domestic production. At the same time, regulatory pressure on emissions is intensifying. California's SB 253 and SB 261 were approved by Governor Newsom in 2023 and signed into law in October 2024; disclosures of Scope 1 and 2 emissions are scheduled to begin in 2026, covering the previous fiscal year, while Scope 3 emissions reporting will begin in 2027. ExxonMobil has responded by suing California to block enforcement — a decision that signals regulatory risk is material and, as shown in Section 5, creates a governance vulnerability. The Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) in the United States creates a countervailing political incentive: it provides tax credits for carbon capture and storage that underpin the economics of ExxonMobil's Low Carbon Solutions (LCS) business. This matters for the recommendations in Section 7 because the viability of LCS investment is partly a function of sustained policy support.

### 2.2 Economic

ExxonMobil's 2024 financial report showed total annual revenues of $349.585 billion and net earnings of $33.7 billion. The company achieved earnings of $33.7 billion and cash flow from operations amounting to $55.0 billion, and its strategic focus on cost efficiency and asset optimisation resulted in leading returns on capital employed, with a notable 12.7% return for 2024. ExxonMobil maintains a strong balance sheet, with a net debt-to-capital ratio of just 7%. The company distributed $36.0 billion to shareholders in 2024 — more than all but five companies in the entire S&P 500. This financial strength is not merely a performance metric; it is a strategic resource because it gives ExxonMobil the optionality to invest counter-cyclically and to fund the dual-track strategy described below — an optionality that capital-constrained peers lack. However, oil price volatility remains a systemic risk: ExxonMobil's earnings are highly correlated with Brent crude prices, and a sustained downturn would compress cash flows and constrain the investment programme.

### 2.3 Social

Consumer and investor attitudes toward fossil fuels are shifting, though unevenly. A small activist hedge fund called Engine No. 1 won two seats on the board of ExxonMobil by convincing other investors that the company's plan to deal with climate change was not much of a plan at all. Engine No. 1 managed to get three nominees elected to the ExxonMobil board of directors in May 2021, in an open effort to prod the oil giant toward renewable energy and test whether activist investing could make a difference. This episode is strategically significant because it demonstrates that institutional investors — including large asset managers — are willing to use governance mechanisms to force strategic change, a threat that persists today. Simultaneously, around 730 million people lack electricity access, and nearly two billion rely on cooking with wood or animal waste — sustaining near-term demand for affordable hydrocarbons, particularly in emerging markets. This social duality — pressure from wealthy-country investors alongside energy-poverty demand from developing nations — is the fundamental tension that ExxonMobil's strategy must navigate.

### 2.4 Technological

Technology is both a threat and an opportunity. Accelerating sales of electric cars — which reached a record 17 million in 2024 and are on course to surpass 20 million in 2025 — have kept a peak in global oil demand on the horizon; electric vehicles are set to displace a total of 5.4 million barrels per day of global oil demand by the end of the decade. This represents a structural headwind for liquid fuels. Conversely, ExxonMobil's proprietary Direct Lithium Extraction (DLE) technology converts this threat into an opportunity: Direct Lithium Extraction is expected to be as low as two-thirds less carbon intensity than hard rock mining, and by the first half of the 2030s, ExxonMobil aims to produce enough lithium to supply the manufacturing needs of about 1 million EVs a year. ExxonMobil's move into direct lithium extraction has the potential to revolutionise the lithium industry by drawing more investment from oil and gas players to the sector; the oil giant is drilling its first lithium well in the Smackover formation in southern Arkansas, targeting first production in 2027. The inference is direct: ExxonMobil's subsurface expertise — a core VRIN resource — is transferable to DLE, meaning the EV substitution threat can be partially hedged by supplying the EV supply chain.

### 2.5 Environmental

In the IEA's Net Zero by 2050 pathway, gas demand declines by 55% to 1,750 billion cubic metres and oil declines by 75% to 24 million barrels per day, from around 90 million barrels per day in 2020. Under the IEA's Stated Policies Scenario, however, the peak in oil demand is accompanied by flattening in oil demand around 2030, due mainly to changes in US policies and lower gas prices. This scenario divergence — between a rapid net-zero transition and a more gradual policy-driven plateau — is the central environmental risk for ExxonMobil. If the world moves faster toward net zero than STEPS implies, ExxonMobil's upstream asset base faces stranded-asset risk. Research conducted by the Carbon Tracker Initiative has revealed that over $1 trillion of oil and gas assets risk becoming stranded across the global economy as a result of policy action on climate and the rise in alternative energy sources. ExxonMobil's own ambition addresses this partially: Exxon's plan targets Scope 1 and Scope 2 net-zero for its operated assets by 2050, and sets a nearer target of net-zero Scope 1 and 2 for its operated Permian assets by 2035; absolute flaring and methane emissions are expected to decrease by 60% and 70%, respectively, by 2030.

### 2.6 Legal

The Follow This investor group argued that ExxonMobil's legal action amounted to "tactics of intimidation and bullying to silence a fair ask to tackle the climate crisis," and that Scope 3 emissions "are the concern of more and more investors who want to safeguard the long-term future of the company and the global economy." These legal actions, while tactically defensible, carry reputational risk that — as the Engine No. 1 episode demonstrates — can translate into governance consequences. This legal dimension feeds directly into the governance recommendation in Section 7.

## 3. Porter's Five Forces Analysis

### 3.1 Threat of New Entrants — LOW

The oil and gas industry is characterised by enormous capital requirements, long lead times, proprietary geological knowledge, and regulatory barriers. The merger of Pioneer and ExxonMobil combines Pioneer's more than 850,000 net acres in the Midland Basin with ExxonMobil's 570,000 net acres in the Delaware and Midland Basins, creating the industry's leading high-quality undeveloped U.S. unconventional inventory position, with an estimated 16 Bboe resource in the Permian Basin. A position of this scale and geological quality cannot be replicated by a new entrant. The threat of new entrants is therefore low, which preserves industry profitability for incumbents — a structural advantage that justifies ExxonMobil's continued upstream investment.

### 3.2 Bargaining Power of Suppliers — MODERATE TO HIGH

ExxonMobil faces suppliers with considerable power due to the specialised nature of the oil and gas industry; a limited number of suppliers provide essential equipment and services, like advanced drilling technology, and in 2024, the cost of specialised drilling equipment increased by 7% globally. Providers of advanced carbon capture technologies, crucial for meeting 2024 emissions targets, can command higher prices due to the substantial investment and lack of readily available alternatives for a company of ExxonMobil's scale; the switching costs for such critical, integrated systems are often prohibitive. This supplier power constrains margins in both the upstream and LCS businesses and reinforces the strategic logic of vertical integration — a logic that the Denbury acquisition exemplifies by bringing CCS infrastructure in-house.

### 3.3 Bargaining Power of Buyers — MODERATE

Oil is a globally traded commodity, meaning individual buyers have limited price-setting power. However, ExxonMobil's Porter's Five Forces Analysis reveals intense industry rivalry and significant buyer power, while also highlighting barriers to entry and the threat of substitutes in the energy sector. In the chemicals and speciality products segments, buyers are more concentrated and can exert greater pressure. Overall, buyer power is moderate — commodity segments are competitive, while speciality and decarbonisation services segments are partially insulated by differentiation.

### 3.4 Threat of Substitutes — HIGH AND RISING

This is the most structurally threatening force. Renewable energy, battery storage, and electrification of transport collectively constitute a long-run substitute for ExxonMobil's core products. The EV displacement figure cited in Section 2.4 (5.4 million barrels per day by 2030) quantifies the near-term substitution pressure. The long-run IEA NZE scenario — a 75% decline in oil demand by 2050 — quantifies the extreme case. Because this force is high and rising, the strategic response cannot be purely defensive: ExxonMobil must either extend the economic life of hydrocarbons (through CCS) or enter the substitute markets (through DLE lithium and hydrogen). Both are elements of the LCS strategy.

### 3.5 Competitive Rivalry — HIGH

ExxonMobil faces intense rivalry from global giants like Shell, Chevron, BP, and TotalEnergies, all operating across the full oil and gas spectrum; this competition spans exploration, production, refining, and marketing. In 2024, China's oil companies took over large oil fields in the Middle East from ExxonMobil — illustrating that national oil companies (NOCs) are an additional competitive threat in the global resource race. ExxonMobil's competitive response has been to differentiate on cost leadership: relative to 2019, estimated cumulative structural cost savings totalled $14.3 billion, which included an additional $2.2 billion in the nine months of 2025. This cost discipline is the mechanism by which ExxonMobil sustains competitive advantage in a high-rivalry environment — and it is the basis for the Pioneer integration recommendation in Section 7.

## 4. Resource-Based View (RBV) and VRIN Analysis

The Resource-Based View (Barney, 1991) holds that sustainable competitive advantage derives from resources and capabilities that are Valuable, Rare, Imperfectly Imitable, and Non-Substitutable (VRIN). The resource-based view suggests that organisations must develop unique, firm-specific core competencies that will allow them to outperform competitors by doing things differently; Barney stated that for resources to hold potential as sources of sustainable competitive advantage, they should be valuable, rare, imperfectly imitable and not substitutable. Applying this framework to ExxonMobil identifies four strategic resource clusters.

### 4.1 Reservoir and Geological Knowledge Base

Exxon exercises unique geoscience capabilities and understanding of the global hydrocarbon endowment to identify and prioritise high-quality resources in a cost-effective manner. This knowledge base — accumulated over more than a century of operations — is rare (few firms possess equivalent depth), imperfectly imitable (it is embedded in human capital and proprietary data), and valuable (it drives the selection of low-cost, high-return assets). The Pioneer acquisition amplified this: the combined companies will have an estimated 16 Bboe resource in the Permian Basin, and at close, ExxonMobil's Permian production volume would more than double to 1.3 MMboed, based on 2023 volumes, and is expected to increase to approximately 2 MMboed in 2027.

**VRIN Assessment:** Valuable ✓ | Rare ✓ | Inimitable ✓ | Non-substitutable ✓ → **Source of sustained competitive advantage.** The warrant is that geological knowledge is path-dependent and accumulated over decades; it cannot be purchased or rapidly replicated by rivals.

### 4.2 Scale, Integration, and Structural Cost Leadership

ExxonMobil competes globally across the entire energy and petrochemical value chain, focusing heavily on high-growth, low-cost resource basins and expanding into lower-emission industrial solutions. Relative to 2019, estimated cumulative structural cost savings totalled $14.3 billion. This cost discipline is embedded in organisational processes and systems — the Global Projects organisation, enterprise-wide transformation programmes — that are difficult for rivals to replicate quickly. The warrant: cost savings that are structural (i.e., permanently removed from the cost base) rather than cyclical represent a durable source of margin advantage, not merely a temporary efficiency gain.

**VRIN Assessment:** Valuable ✓ | Rare ✓ (at this scale) | Inimitable ✓ (organisational routines) | Non-substitutable ✓ → **Source of sustained competitive advantage.**

### 4.3 CCS Infrastructure and Pipeline Network

ExxonMobil's CCS network is now the largest in the U.S. with over 1,500 miles of CO₂ pipelines; so far it has invested $3 billion and plans to invest another $30 billion between now and 2030. Of Denbury's 1,300 miles of CO₂ pipeline, roughly 70% are in the Gulf Coast states of Louisiana, Texas, and Mississippi — one of the largest U.S. markets for CO₂ reduction — and nine of their ten strategically located CO₂ storage sites are also in this region. This physical infrastructure is rare (no comparable network exists), imperfectly imitable (it took decades to build and requires extensive regulatory permitting), and valuable. Transaction synergies are expected to enable more than 100 MTA of emissions reductions over time, driving strong growth and returns.

**VRIN Assessment:** Valuable ✓ | Rare ✓ | Inimitable ✓ | Non-substitutable ✓ → **Emerging source of sustained competitive advantage, contingent on policy support.** The warrant: first-mover advantage in CCS infrastructure creates network effects (more emitters connected to the network increase its value) that latecomers cannot easily replicate, provided the IRA policy environment is maintained.

### 4.4 Balance Sheet Strength

The debt-to-capital ratio was 13% and the net-debt-to-capital ratio was 6%, reflecting a period-end cash balance of $23.2 billion at year-end 2024. This financial flexibility enables counter-cyclical investment and large acquisitions. However, balance sheet strength is less rare at the supermajor level — Chevron and Shell also maintain strong balance sheets — and is therefore a competitive necessity rather than a unique differentiator.

**VRIN Assessment:** Valuable ✓ | Rare ✗ (at supermajor level) → **Competitive parity, not sustained advantage.** The warrant: because multiple rivals possess comparable balance sheet strength, it cannot be the basis of differentiation, though its absence would be a disadvantage.

## 5. SWOT Synthesis

The PESTEL analysis characterises the external environment; the Five Forces analysis characterises industry structure; the RBV identifies internal capabilities. The SWOT integrates all three to surface the strategic tensions that the recommendations must resolve.

**Strengths:** World-class geological knowledge (VRIN); scale and integration; industry-leading ROCE (12.7%); the largest U.S. CO₂ pipeline network (1,500+ miles); a fortress balance sheet; $14.3 billion in cumulative structural cost savings since 2019; and the Pioneer-enhanced Permian position (16 Bboe resource).

**Weaknesses:** Heavy revenue concentration in fossil fuels with Scope 3 emissions entirely unaddressed; the LCS business is expected to account for billions of dollars in revenues by 2030 and earnings of some $2 billion — roughly 6% of 2024 group earnings, meaning the transition narrative is currently not supported by material earnings; reputational damage from legal aggression toward climate activists; and no Scope 3 target.

**Opportunities:** IRA tax credits for CCS; growing industrial demand for decarbonisation services; DLE lithium for EV supply chains; ExxonMobil is pursuing up to $30 billion of low-emission opportunities between 2025 and 2030, with almost 65% spent on reducing emissions for third-party customers. Energy security policy tailwinds and demand growth in emerging markets also represent opportunities.

**Threats:** EV-driven oil demand displacement of 5.4 million barrels per day by the end of the decade; the IEA NZE scenario's implied 75% oil demand decline by 2050; stranded-asset risk ($1 trillion global exposure); rising carbon regulation; intensifying NOC competition; and activist investor pressure.

**Key Strategic Tension:** ExxonMobil's greatest strengths — scale, geological expertise, Permian dominance — are maximally valuable in a world where oil demand remains elevated. But they become liabilities if the energy transition accelerates faster than the STEPS scenario projects. The Pioneer acquisition locks in a multi-decade hydrocarbon production profile; if the NZE scenario materialises, this represents significant stranded-asset exposure. Simultaneously, the LCS business is strategically sound but currently too small to credibly anchor a transition narrative, creating a "credibility gap" that is ExxonMobil's most acute reputational and governance vulnerability.

## 6. Strategic Options Evaluation

Three broad strategic options can be identified:

**Option A: Accelerate the Dual Track (current strategy).** Continue aggressive Permian growth while scaling LCS investment. This captures near-term cash flows and positions for the long-run transition. The risk is that if the transition accelerates, stranded assets materialise; if it slows, LCS investment may be premature.

**Option B: Full Pivot to Renewables.** Redirect capital from upstream toward wind, solar, and green hydrogen (the path partially taken by BP and TotalEnergies, and subsequently partially reversed). This aligns with the NZE scenario but destroys near-term returns and sacrifices the VRIN advantages in geological knowledge and CCS infrastructure that are ExxonMobil's only sources of sustained competitive advantage.

**Option C: Disciplined Dual Track with Explicit Transition Milestones.** Maintain Option A's logic but add binding LCS earnings milestones, a phased Scope 3 pathway, and reformed climate governance. This addresses the credibility gap without sacrificing near-term performance.

**Evaluation and Warrant:** Option B is strategically incoherent for ExxonMobil because it would destroy the VRIN resources identified in Section 4 — the basis of its competitive advantage — without guarantee of equivalent advantage in renewables, where ExxonMobil has no established position. ExxonMobil's strategy is uniquely defined by its 'dual-track' approach of aggressively pursuing both advantaged hydrocarbon growth and building a new, industrial-scale Low Carbon Solutions business, and its Low Carbon strategy focuses on CCS and hydrogen, leveraging its core competencies. This is the correct strategic logic — leveraging existing VRIN resources into adjacent markets — but it requires the credibility gap to be closed. Option A without credibility-gap remediation risks the governance disruption that Engine No. 1 demonstrated is both possible and consequential. Option C is therefore recommended.

## 7. Recommendations and Action Plan

### Recommendation 1: Accelerate LCS Commercialisation and Establish Binding Revenue Milestones

**What:** Set a binding target for LCS to contribute at least 15% of group earnings by 2030 (approximately $5 billion at current earnings levels), up from the current ~$2 billion aspiration. Prioritise the Baytown low-carbon hydrogen facility — a final investment decision for the project is expected in 2025, subject to stakeholder support, regulatory permitting, and market conditions, with start-up planned in 2029 — and scale the Gulf Coast CCS network to signed offtake agreements exceeding 10 million tonnes per annum.

**Why the evidence warrants this:** ExxonMobil's CCS infrastructure is a VRIN resource — rare, inimitable, and valuable — but the LCS business is targeting hard-to-abate sectors which together account for about 80% of global energy-related CO₂ emissions, and currently generates negligible earnings relative to the $3 billion already invested. The strategic logic is sound, but commercial scale is required to convert infrastructure into earnings. The IRA tax credit regime makes the economics viable now; delay risks losing first-mover advantage as competitors build alternative networks. Binding milestones also convert a narrative into a measurable commitment, directly addressing the credibility gap identified in the SWOT.

### Recommendation 2: Adopt a Scope 3 Emissions Pathway with Phased Targets

**What:** Publish a Scope 3 emissions trajectory — not an absolute net-zero target, but a credible, measurable intensity-reduction pathway — beginning with a 10% Scope 3 intensity reduction by 2030 relative to a 2023 baseline.

**Why the evidence warrants this:** ExxonMobil's current net-zero ambition covers only Scope 1 and 2 from operated assets. Scope 3 emissions "are the concern of more and more investors who want to safeguard the long-term future of the company and the global economy in view of the climate crisis." The California SB 253 legislation will require Scope 3 disclosure from 2027 regardless. A proactive pathway — rather than a reactive disclosure — converts a compliance obligation into a strategic asset, pre-empting activist pressure and demonstrating good-faith engagement with institutional investors. An intensity-based (rather than absolute) Scope 3 target is feasible because ExxonMobil cannot control how customers use its products, while still demonstrating directional commitment. The warrant: proactive disclosure and target-setting reduces the probability of further governance interventions of the Engine No. 1 type, which represent a material strategic risk.

### Recommendation 3: Leverage the Pioneer Integration to Embed Technology-Driven Cost Leadership

**What:** Deploy ExxonMobil's proprietary drilling and reservoir technologies across Pioneer's 850,000 net acres to drive unit costs below $35 per barrel across the combined Permian portfolio.

**Why the evidence warrants this:** ExxonMobil is acquiring Pioneer's resources at a cost below $35 per barrel, despite the acquisition's price tag exceeding $60 billion, and the integration of operations is projected to generate substantial cost synergies and revenue gains, potentially resulting in around $2 billion in "merger synergies." ExxonMobil's Permian production volume would more than double to 1.3 MMboed at close, and is expected to increase to approximately 2 MMboed in 2027. However, synergy realisation is not automatic: it requires active technology transfer and operational integration. The structural cost savings programme — $14.3 billion since 2019 — demonstrates that the organisational capability exists. The warrant: lower-cost assets remain economically viable at lower oil prices and are therefore less exposed to demand-destruction scenarios, directly mitigating the stranded-asset risk identified in the SWOT. Cost leadership is the mechanism by which ExxonMobil extends the economic life of its hydrocarbon portfolio even under moderate energy-transition scenarios.

### Recommendation 4: De-escalate Legal Confrontation with Investors; Establish a Climate Governance Council

**What:** Settle the Follow This lawsuit; establish a board-level Climate Governance Council with independent external membership to provide oversight of the LCS strategy and the Scope 3 pathway.

**Why the evidence warrants this:** Engine No. 1 managed to get three nominees elected to the ExxonMobil board of directors in 2021 in an open effort to prod the oil giant toward renewable energy and test whether activist investing could make a difference. This demonstrated that legal and governance resistance to investor climate demands is a losing long-run strategy. According to Engine Number 1, electing new board members would improve how ExxonMobil manages the risks posed by climate change, which would increase returns to ExxonMobil stock, and in turn benefit shareholders. Continued legal aggression amplifies reputational risk without resolving the underlying governance tension. The warrant: a Climate Governance Council signals genuine accountability, differentiates ExxonMobil from peers who have faced more disruptive shareholder interventions, and provides a credible oversight mechanism for the Scope 3 pathway recommended above — converting a governance threat into a governance asset.

### Recommendation 5: Commercialise DLE Lithium as a Strategic Hedge Against EV-Driven Demand Destruction

**What:** Accelerate the DLE programme in Arkansas toward first commercial production in 2027, with a goal of supplying lithium for 1 million EV batteries per year by the early 2030s, and negotiate long-term offtake agreements with EV manufacturers.

**Why the evidence warrants this:** Through the appraisal drilling programme and technology pilot using Direct Lithium Extraction technology, ExxonMobil has successfully produced lithium carbonate from the Smackover formation in southern Arkansas. Direct Lithium Extraction is expected to be as low as two-thirds less carbon intensity than hard rock mining, and by the first half of the 2030s, ExxonMobil aims to produce enough lithium to supply the manufacturing needs of about 1 million EVs a year. The warrant: EV sales are displacing 5.4 million barrels per day of oil demand by 2030 — the most direct substitution threat to ExxonMobil's core business. DLE converts this threat into an opportunity by applying the same subsurface expertise (a VRIN resource) to brine extraction. Securing offtake agreements now — before the lithium market becomes crowded — would establish a durable first-mover position in a structurally growing market, consistent with the RBV logic of leveraging existing capabilities into adjacent markets.

## 10. Conclusion

ExxonMobil's strategic situation is defined by a genuine and unresolved tension between its world-class hydrocarbon capabilities and the structural shift in the global energy system. The PESTEL analysis reveals a macro-environment simultaneously supportive of near-term oil production and threatening to the long-run business model. Porter's Five Forces confirms that industry rivalry is intense and the threat of substitutes is rising — but that barriers to entry remain high, protecting incumbents who achieve cost leadership. The RBV/VRIN analysis identifies ExxonMobil's geological knowledge, scale and integration, and CCS infrastructure as genuine sources of sustained competitive advantage — but only if actively leveraged rather than merely maintained.

The recommended strategy — a disciplined dual track with binding LCS milestones, a phased Scope 3 pathway, Pioneer-driven cost leadership, de-escalated governance confrontation, and DLE lithium commercialisation — is grounded in a coherent evidence chain: it preserves the VRIN resources that generate current returns, addresses the credibility gap that is ExxonMobil's most acute reputational vulnerability, and builds the optionality required to navigate the scenario uncertainty that defines the energy transition. The alternative — continuing the dual track without addressing the credibility gap — risks the governance disruption that Engine No. 1 demonstrated is both possible and consequential, and leaves ExxonMobil exposed to the stranded-asset risk that the Carbon Tracker Initiative has quantified at over $1 trillion across the global industry.
