## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Manchester United is one of the world's most commercially powerful sports brands, yet it has simultaneously become one of English football's most conspicuous strategic failures. Since Sir Alex Ferguson's retirement in 2013, the club has cycled through six managers in under thirteen years, recorded five consecutive years of pre-tax losses, and finished as low as 15th in the Premier League. This case study diagnoses the root causes of that paradox using four interlocking frameworks: Johnson, Scholes and Whittington's (2008) concept of **strategic drift**; Porter's (1980) **Five Forces**; Barney's (1991) **Resource-Based View (RBV)**; and Ansoff's (1957) **Growth Matrix**. A **SWOT analysis** bridges the frameworks. The central argument is that Manchester United's crisis is not primarily a footballing problem but a **governance and resource-allocation failure**: the club's extraordinary commercial assets have been systematically decoupled from the football operation that generates them, producing a vicious cycle in which poor on-pitch performance erodes the very brand it depends upon.

The sporting dimension is equally stark. United finished 15th in the Premier League in 2024–25, their worst league placement in Premier League history, breaking their previous all-time low of 8th place set the season before. ## SECTION 2: FRAMEWORK 1 — STRATEGIC DRIFT (Johnson, Scholes & Whittington, 2008)

### 2.1 Concept

Johnson, Scholes and Whittington (2008) define **strategic drift** as the tendency of an organisation's strategy to develop incrementally from its established base rather than in line with changes in its environment, resulting in a widening gap between organisational strategy and environmental demands. Over time, incremental adjustments fail to keep pace with external change until a crisis forces radical transformation.

### 2.2 Application: The Post-Ferguson Drift

Under Ferguson, Manchester United operated a coherent, manager-centric model in which a dominant coach with full control over recruitment, player development, and tactical identity provided the strategic anchor. When Ferguson retired, the club's environment changed fundamentally but the *model* did not. The club's response to each managerial failure was another managerial appointment, without diagnosing the underlying structural problem.

The competitive environment shifted dramatically: between 2018 and 2024, Manchester City won six Premier League titles in seven seasons — a rate of dominance that exceeded even Ferguson's United at their peak. United's timeline during this same period is at its thinnest since the pre-Ferguson years — after Ferguson's retirement in 2013, United cycled through David Moyes, Louis van Gaal, José Mourinho, Ole Gunnar Solskjær, Ralf Rangnick, and Erik ten Hag. Each appointment represented an incremental response that preserved the broken governance model rather than confronting its inadequacy — precisely the pattern Johnson et al. describe.

The financial environment also changed: Manchester United said they are "in danger" of breaching the Premier League's Profit and Sustainability Rules (PSR), having reported losses before tax of £312.9 million over the last three years. The incremental strategy of signing expensive players to compensate for managerial failures — Manchester United's total transfer spending reached £211.8 million in 2022–23 — was not only strategically incoherent but financially unsustainable under the new regulatory framework.

### 2.3 The Flux Phase

Johnson et al. note that strategic drift eventually produces a "flux" phase characterised by confusion and contradictory strategic moves. This is visible in the 2022–2025 period. Ratcliffe's INEOS extended Erik ten Hag's contract in 2024 and backed him heavily in the transfer market, only to sack him months later at significant cost. His successor, Ruben Amorim, arrived for a major release fee but was dismissed in January 2026 without completing a full season — only three months after Ratcliffe had said he would give Amorim three years to prove himself. Another example is the recruitment of Dan Ashworth as sporting director, pursued at considerable expense, who was gone within months, even after Ratcliffe publicly declared that "Ashworth is a 10/10 sporting director." These are not isolated decisions but symptoms of an organisation that has lost strategic coherence.

**Inferential step**: The evidence of six managers in thirteen years, contradictory contract decisions, and the club's own admission of PSR risk collectively demonstrate that incremental responses have failed. The logical warrant for the strategic drift diagnosis is that *no single managerial appointment* addresses the structural problem; the pattern of failure is itself the evidence that the model — not the individuals — requires change.

## SECTION 3: FRAMEWORK 2 — PORTER'S FIVE FORCES (Porter, 1980)

Porter (1980) argues that the competitive intensity of an industry is determined by five structural forces. Applied to Manchester United's position in elite European football, these forces reveal the structural constraints the club faces.

### 3.1 Competitive Rivalry — HIGH and Intensifying

Between 2018 and 2024, Manchester City won six Premier League titles in seven seasons — a rate of dominance that exceeded even Ferguson's United at their peak. The injection of sovereign wealth (Manchester City), private equity (Chelsea), and state-backed capital (Newcastle) has raised the resource threshold for competing at the top. United's wage bill, while large in absolute terms — Casemiro was paid more money per week than anyone else in the squad, with his £350,000-per-week contract — is not matched by squad quality, a symptom of poor resource allocation rather than insufficient resource. This force is **unfavourable** for United.

### 3.2 Bargaining Power of Suppliers (Players and Agents) — HIGH

The transfer market continues to show significant volatility with escalating player costs, with the average Premier League player transfer fee increasing by 12.3% in 2022–23. Manchester United's recruitment failures compound this: the sale of Scott McTominay to Napoli in 2024 for around $34 million was described by Marco Materazzi as "the biggest transfer market mistake we've ever seen, without a doubt," with his value estimated at €60 to €70 million or more. Manchester United have started a revamp of their recruitment department, with co-owner Sir Jim Ratcliffe having given interviews in which he described United's recruitment data as inadequate. High supplier power combined with weak internal recruitment capability is a **doubly unfavourable** condition.

### 3.3 Bargaining Power of Buyers (Fans and Broadcasters) — MODERATE but Shifting

The club's own letter to fans admitted: "We are currently making a significant loss each year totalling over £300 million in the past three years... if we do not act now we are in danger of failing to comply with PSR/FFP requirements." This remarkable admission further erodes fan confidence. The Manchester United Supporters' Trust (MUST) has demonstrated increasing organised resistance: MUST demanded the club appoint independent directors to the board "to protect the interest of the club as a football club, not its shareholders and their focus on profits over results," and demanded that the owners work with MUST over a share scheme.

### 3.4 Threat of New Entrants — LOW but Structurally Altered

The traditional barriers to entry in elite football remain high. However, state-backed ownership models represent a qualitatively new form of entrant that bypasses traditional resource constraints. Manchester United has 41.4 million followers on Instagram and 37.1 million on Twitter as of 2024, and the club's digital revenue reached £61.5 million in the 2022/2023 financial year — but these figures reflect historical brand equity that could erode under continued sporting decline.

### 3.5 Porter Synthesis

The most critical forces are competitive rivalry (intensifying) and supplier power (high). These interact: because rivals have better recruitment infrastructure and squad coherence, United must overpay in the transfer market, which inflates supplier power further. Breaking this cycle requires structural investment in recruitment capability and a coherent footballing identity — not merely another managerial appointment. This connects directly to the strategic drift diagnosis: the Five Forces analysis explains *why* the environment now punishes incremental responses.

## SECTION 4: FRAMEWORK 3 — RESOURCE-BASED VIEW (Barney, 1991)

### 4.1 Concept

Barney (1991) argues that sustained competitive advantage derives from resources that are **Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN)**. Applied to Manchester United, this framework separates genuine strategic assets from those that are depreciating.

### 4.2 The Global Brand: The Core VRIN Asset

Forbes valued Manchester United at approximately $6.5 billion in 2024, reflecting unmatched recognition and sponsor appeal. United posted record commercial revenues of £333.3 million while achieving record matchday revenues of £160.3 million in FY2025. This demonstrates that the brand retains commercial value even during sporting decline. The brand is **valuable** (it generates £333 million in commercial revenue), **rare** (built over 147 years of history), and **partially inimitable** (rivals cannot replicate Manchester United's historical narrative).

However, the RBV also reveals a critical vulnerability: brand equity in football is not permanently separable from sporting performance. The brand was built on winning. Manchester United's performance has been volatile, with significant fluctuations in league standings, and the club has 41.4 million followers on Instagram as of 2024 — but these are lagging indicators of past success, not guarantees of future relevance. **If the brand was built by winning, it can be eroded by losing.**

### 4.3 The Absent VRIN Capability: Recruitment

The most damaging resource gap is the **absence of a functioning recruitment capability**. Manchester United have struggled to land successful transfers in the post-Sir Alex Ferguson era at Old Trafford. The strategic dismantling of the "authoritarian manager" model was described as an institutional necessity to end the cycle of reactionary, relationship-based recruitment that plagued the post-Ferguson era. Under the mandate of Sir Jim Ratcliffe and INEOS, Manchester United has transitioned to an executive-driven hierarchy designed to ensure that the club's footballing identity and squad sustainability remain insulated from the volatility of coaching changes. A capability that is neither rare, inimitable, nor non-substitutable cannot generate competitive advantage; its absence, however, can destroy it.

### 4.4 Old Trafford: A Depreciating Asset

The stadium had a roof leak against Arsenal in May 2024, with similar occurrences in 2012, 2019, and 2023. In 2023, the stadium was left off the list for hosting UEFA Euro 2028 matches due to being unable to say whether it would be available for the competition. In March 2025, Manchester United finally confirmed their intention to build a new 100,000-seat stadium next to the current Old Trafford. The stadium and wider regeneration project have the potential to deliver an additional £7.3 billion per year to the UK economy, including the possible creation of 92,000 new jobs, more than 17,000 new homes, and driving an additional 1.8 million visitors annually.

**Inferential step**: The RBV analysis warrants the conclusion that the club's investment priorities have been inverted. Resources have been concentrated on short-term, non-VRIN activities (signing expensive players without a coherent recruitment framework) while the genuinely VRIN assets (brand, academy, infrastructure) have been under-invested. Correcting this inversion is the strategic imperative.

## SECTION 7: INTEGRATED DIAGNOSIS — THE VICIOUS CYCLE

The four frameworks converge on a single diagnosis. **Strategic drift** explains *how* the crisis developed: incremental managerial changes preserved a broken governance model. **Five Forces** explains *why* the competitive environment now punishes that model: rivals have built coherent sporting projects that United's reactive approach cannot match. The **RBV** explains *what* is at risk: the global brand — the club's only genuinely VRIN asset — is a lagging indicator that will erode if sporting decline continues. The **Ansoff matrix** maps *where* the strategic opportunities lie.

Together, they describe a **vicious cycle**:

> **Glazer LBO debt** → **under-investment in football operations** → **managerial instability and recruitment failure** → **sporting decline** → **loss of Champions League revenue** → **PSR constraint on transfer activity** → **further sporting decline** → **brand erosion** → **commercial revenue pressure** → **inability to service debt or invest** → *[repeat]*

This cycle has a **principal-agent dimension** (Jensen & Meckling, 1976): the Glazer family's incentives — maximising return on their equity investment — are structurally misaligned with the long-term sporting investment required to break it. This approach allows the investor to put very little of their own capital into a purchase but does lumber the club with immense debt, which can become very costly when interest payments are due. Across the almost 20 years since the takeover was completed in June 2005, Manchester United's total net interest cost on debt has crept to a staggering £1.06 billion. As the Manchester United Supporters Trust stated: "the fans will effectively be paying for someone to borrow money to own their club."

### RECOMMENDATION 1: Institutionalise a Permanent Sporting Director Model
**Horizon**: Immediate (0–12 months) | **Frameworks**: Strategic drift; RBV

**Evidence**: The strategic dismantling of the "authoritarian manager" model was described as an institutional necessity to end the cycle of reactionary, relationship-based recruitment that plagued the post-Ferguson era. Under the mandate of Ratcliffe and INEOS, Manchester United has transitioned to an executive-driven hierarchy designed to ensure that the club's footballing identity and squad sustainability remain insulated from the volatility of coaching changes. However, the dismissal of Dan Ashworth within months of appointment demonstrated that the role lacked institutional protection.

**Recommendation**: Appoint a permanent Sporting Director with a contractual mandate that *survives* individual managerial changes, responsible for: (a) defining a club-wide playing identity that all coaches must work within; (b) overseeing all recruitment decisions through a data-led process; (c) managing squad transitions between managers without wholesale rebuilds. This is the model that has enabled Liverpool (Michael Edwards, then Richard Hughes) and Manchester City (Txiki Begiristain) to maintain continuity despite coaching changes.

**Why the evidence warrants this conclusion**: The RBV shows that recruitment capability is currently neither rare, inimitable, nor non-substitutable — it is a competitive weakness. The strategic drift analysis shows that each managerial change has reset the strategic clock. A Sporting Director model decouples footballing identity from individual managerial tenure, converting an institutional weakness into a potential source of sustained competitive advantage. Without this structural fix, any new manager inherits the same broken system.

**Why the evidence warrants this conclusion**: Porter's analysis shows that high supplier power (player wages) combined with weak recruitment capability creates a double cost.
