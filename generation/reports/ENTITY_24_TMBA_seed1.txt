# 🚗 Strategic Case Study: Mercedes-Benz Group AG
## *Navigating the Electrification Crossroads — From Prestige to Peril and Back*

---

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Mercedes-Benz Group AG, one of the world's most iconic luxury automotive brands, finds itself at a defining strategic crossroads. The luxury automaker has faced a sharp decline in earnings and revenue in recent quarters, with adjusted EBIT for its Cars division falling to €8.7 billion in 2024 (from €14.3 billion in 2023) and Group revenue contracting to €145.6 billion. The simultaneous collapse of sales in its largest market, China, the stalling of its EV transition, and the rise of technologically sophisticated local competitors have compounded into a structural crisis. This case study analyses the strategic situation facing Mercedes-Benz using a suite of analytical frameworks — PESTEL, Porter's Five Forces, SWOT, the Ansoff Matrix, and the Value Chain — before proposing a coherent set of strategic recommendations.

---

## 2. COMPANY BACKGROUND

Founded in 1926 and headquartered in Stuttgart, Germany, Mercedes-Benz Group AG is one of the world's premier luxury automobile manufacturers. Mercedes-Benz operates globally, with a strong presence in Europe, North America, Asia, and emerging markets, with Fortune 500 ranking it #42 in 2024 and annual revenues of approximately US$165 billion. The brand's identity is built on a century-long legacy of engineering excellence, safety innovation, and aspirational luxury — encapsulated in the iconic three-pointed star.

Key strategic targets historically included transitioning to a fully electric vehicle lineup where the market allows, with an intention to have over 50% of sales from battery-electric vehicles by 2030, achieving carbon neutrality in its entire fleet by 2039 (Ambition 2039), and increasing the sales share of its top-end luxury segment by 60% compared to 2019 levels by 2026.

The company organises its portfolio into three tiers:
- **Top-End Luxury:** Mercedes-AMG, Maybach, G-Class, S-Class
- **Core:** C-Class, E-Class, GLC derivatives
- **Entry Luxury:** A-Class, B-Class, EQA, EQB

## 3. THE STRATEGIC SITUATION: IDENTIFYING THE PROBLEM

### 3.1 The China Crisis

The 3% decline to 1.98 million vehicles in 2024 was led by a slump in deliveries in China, Mercedes-Benz's largest market, where consumers are shunning models like the S-Class and Maybach in favour of local brands. Sales in the country dropped 7% to 683,600 cars from a year earlier.

The numbers tell a story of structural erosion in what was once Mercedes-Benz's most profitable market. China sales fell 27% year-on-year in the third quarter of 2025, the sharpest decline across any region. For the full year, China contributed just 15.9% of group revenue — a share that has been shrinking steadily as domestic competitors have surged.

BYD, now the world's largest electric vehicle manufacturer, is producing cars with comparable technology at price points Mercedes cannot match without destroying its brand positioning. Xiaomi's entry into the premium EV segment has added another front to a war that European carmakers are losing on home turf. Chinese consumers who once paid a premium for the three-pointed star are increasingly choosing technologically advanced domestic alternatives.

### 3.2 The EV Transition Stall

Mercedes-Benz's biggest single market saw sales drop seven percent in 2024. Overall, the group's sales fell four percent last year from the previous year, hit by a drop of 23% in sales of electric vehicles. It was the latest evidence that the transition to EVs is stalling, a slowdown that is weighing heavily on carmakers across Europe.

In July 2021, the Mercedes-Benz Group announced that by the end of the 2020s, all new cars sold would be "EV-only," but in February 2024 the company revised that plan. The pace of electrification and the transition to all-electric BEVs will be determined by customer and market conditions, so the company will be tactically flexible and will continue to meet the needs for internal combustion engine vehicles well into the 2030s.

### 3.3 Financial Deterioration

Earnings per share (EPS) followed a similar downward trend in 2024, decreasing by 24.3% to €10.19. The Mercedes-Benz Cars division reported an adjusted EBIT of €8.7bn, a decline from €14.3bn in 2023, primarily due to lower sales volumes, particularly in China, negative net pricing, and an unfavourable model mix.

The German auto giant's net profit fell 28% from the previous year to 10.4 billion euros ($10.8 billion), while revenues also slid about four percent to 145.6 billion euros. The group announced plans to slash production costs by a tenth by 2027 and also gave a bleak outlook, saying it expected lower sales and leaner profit margins.

## 4. ANALYTICAL FRAMEWORKS

### 4.1 PESTEL ANALYSIS

The PESTEL framework examines the macro-environmental forces shaping Mercedes-Benz's strategic landscape.

#### 🏛️ Political
The EU-China tariff spat raises duties up to 35% on electric-vehicle imports, squeezing Mercedes-Benz Group AG's margins as China accounts for approximately 30% of 2025 revenue. Tariffs hit high-margin S-Class and EQS exports, so management is scaling local China production to avoid levies while preserving 'Made in Germany' cachet via design and engineering kept in Germany.

Trade disputes between the U.S. and China have led to increased tariffs on automotive parts, impacting Mercedes-Benz's production costs and pricing strategies. The Russia-Ukraine conflict further disrupted supply chains, particularly for raw materials such as palladium and neon gas critical for semiconductor production.

#### 💰 Economic
With rising prices in the luxury segment leading to reduced demand, there's growing uncertainty about whether companies can maintain stability in pricing and performance through new model launches and tighter cost control. Inflationary pressures across Europe and North America have squeezed consumer disposable income, while China's post-pandemic economic recovery has been slower than anticipated. Currency volatility — particularly the weakening of the Chinese yuan — has further eroded Mercedes-Benz's reported earnings from its largest market.

#### 👥 Social
The US Inflation Reduction Act offers a $7,500 tax credit for EVs with localised battery assembly; Mercedes-Benz Group AG has invested $1.7B in its 2025 Alabama EV plant to capitalise on this incentive. Generational shifts in consumer values — with younger, digitally native consumers prioritising sustainability, software-defined experiences, and value-for-money — are disrupting the traditional luxury car purchase calculus. The 2024 launch of the Mercedes-Benz Modular Architecture (MMA) platform signifies a strategic pivot toward a new generation of premium buyers who are digitally-native and place a high value on sustainability, representing a significant evolution from the company's historic elite clientele.

#### 🔬 Technological
Mercedes-Benz is introducing its proprietary Mercedes-Benz Operating System (MB.OS), a comprehensive chip-to-cloud platform that will debut in the new CLA. This system integrates various vehicle domains into a unified software architecture. This positions Mercedes-Benz as a potential software-defined vehicle leader — but also exposes it to competition from technology companies (Apple, Google, Huawei) increasingly embedded in the automotive ecosystem. The company's focus on innovation, particularly in EV architecture and autonomous driving, is a key differentiator, and its extensive patent portfolio creates barriers to entry for competitors.

#### 🌍 Environmental
In 2024, carbon emissions were cut by 75% (one year ahead of the 2030 target), with the Rastatt plant using 100% green energy for electric CLA production. The EU's General Safety Regulation (GSR2) mandating advanced driver-assistance systems in new vehicles as of 2024 requires ongoing compliance investment. Non-compliance can lead to substantial financial penalties and reputational damage.

#### ⚖️ Legal
The automotive sector faces ongoing scrutiny under competition laws, with regulators examining potential anti-competitive practices. The company's commitment to intellectual property protection, covering design, engineering, and software innovations, is crucial for maintaining its competitive edge in a market with billions invested in R&D. Data privacy regulations (GDPR in Europe, evolving frameworks in China and the US) increasingly constrain how Mercedes-Benz can monetise the vast data generated by its connected vehicles.

### 4.2 PORTER'S FIVE FORCES ANALYSIS

Porter's Five Forces framework (1979) provides a structural analysis of the competitive intensity within Mercedes-Benz's industry environment.

#### ⚔️ Force 1: Competitive Rivalry — **HIGH**

The luxury and premium automotive industry is very competitive. The competitors of Mercedes-Benz include BMW, Audi, Lexus, Volvo, Jaguar, and Porsche. The vehicles made by these brands all differ significantly in design, functions, luxuries, and other technical aspects. Beyond traditional rivals, Tesla has redefined the premium EV segment globally, while Chinese newcomers — BYD, NIO, Li Auto, and Xiaomi — are rapidly ascending into price points that directly challenge Mercedes-Benz's core and entry segments. Sales declines in China (down 15% in Q4 2024) highlight the company's struggle to compete with local EV brands that offer comparable performance at lower prices.

#### 🆕 Force 2: Threat of New Entrants — **MEDIUM-HIGH**

Historically, the capital intensity of automotive manufacturing created formidable barriers to entry. However, the EV transition has lowered some of these barriers. Software-first companies like Tesla demonstrated that a new entrant could rapidly gain significant market share. In China, Xiaomi — a consumer electronics company — launched a premium EV (the SU7) in 2024 that directly targets Mercedes-Benz's core demographic. The company's focus on innovation, particularly in EV architecture and autonomous driving, is a key differentiator. Its extensive patent portfolio creates barriers to entry for competitors. Nevertheless, the convergence of automotive and technology industries means the threat from non-traditional entrants remains elevated.

#### 🔄 Force 3: Threat of Substitutes — **MEDIUM**

High-quality used Mercedes vehicles deliver similar utility at lower cost, making them a credible substitute for new sales; certified pre-owned programs add warranty and manufacturer financing that further bridge the gap. Mercedes-Benz runs an extensive CPO program to keep buyers in-brand and capture lifetime value. Beyond the second-hand market, broader mobility substitutes — ride-sharing (Uber, Didi), car subscriptions, and public transport improvements in urban centres — increasingly challenge private car ownership, particularly among younger consumers.

#### 🏭 Force 4: Bargaining Power of Suppliers — **MEDIUM-HIGH**

In 2024, major automotive suppliers like Bosch and Continental continue to hold significant sway due to their scale and broad customer base. The transition to EVs has introduced new critical dependencies: battery cell manufacturers (CATL, Samsung SDI, Panasonic) and semiconductor suppliers wield considerable pricing power. The global chip shortage of 2021–2023 exposed the vulnerability of just-in-time supply chains. The company actively diversifies its supplier base to mitigate risks associated with localised trade disputes or sudden tariff increases, aiming for greater supply chain resilience.

#### 🛒 Force 5: Bargaining Power of Buyers — **MEDIUM**

Direct sales and agency models at Mercedes-Benz increase price transparency across markets, accelerating cross-border comparability and reducing dealer price dispersion. Online configurators make cross-brand comparisons simple, shortening purchase cycles and increasing churn. In the luxury segment, brand loyalty remains a moderating factor — buyers invest in the prestige of the three-pointed star. However, the proliferation of high-quality alternatives and the growing sophistication of Chinese consumers have meaningfully increased buyer power, particularly in Asia.

**Summary Table — Porter's Five Forces:**

| Force | Intensity | Key Driver |
|---|---|---|
| Competitive Rivalry | 🔴 High | BMW, Audi, Tesla, BYD, Xiaomi |
| Threat of New Entrants | 🟠 Medium-High | Tech companies, Chinese OEMs |
| Threat of Substitutes | 🟡 Medium | CPO market, ride-sharing, MaaS |
| Supplier Power | 🟠 Medium-High | Battery/chip dependency |
| Buyer Power | 🟡 Medium | Online transparency, Chinese market shift |

### 4.3 SWOT ANALYSIS

#### ✅ Strengths

1. **Brand Equity & Heritage:** As of June 2024, Mercedes-Benz was valued at $22.8 billion USD, ranking fourth globally among automotive brands. This strong brand recognition allows for premium pricing and fosters significant customer loyalty.

2. **Top-End Segment Resilience:** Despite broader challenges, top-tier models like the G-Class and S-Class saw sales rise by 41% and 9% year-to-date, demonstrating resilience in high-margin segments.

3. **Global Manufacturing Footprint:** A global network of more than 6,500 dealerships across 130 countries ensures that Mercedes vehicles are accessible worldwide. Manufacturing plants in Europe, Asia, and the Americas allow Mercedes to localise production and adapt to regional markets.

4. **Technological Innovation Pipeline:** The development of its proprietary MB.OS operating system, set to debut in 2025, aims to enhance vehicle control and user experience.

5. **Financial Services Arm:** Mercedes-Benz Mobility, which provides financial services for the group's vehicle divisions, maintained a strong presence with a total portfolio of €138.1bn, up from €135bn in 2023.

#### ⚠️ Weaknesses

1. **Over-reliance on China:** China's contribution to group revenue has fallen from over 30% to 15.9%, yet the company's cost structure and strategic planning remain heavily exposed to this single market.

2. **EV Transition Lag:** The group's sales fell four percent, hit by a drop of 23% in sales of electric vehicles. Mercedes-Benz's EV lineup has struggled to achieve the software-first, seamlessly integrated experience offered by Tesla and Chinese rivals.

3. **High Operating Costs:** The production of luxury vehicles, especially those equipped with the latest technologies, advanced safety features, and premium materials, comes with high manufacturing costs.

4. **Declining Entry Segment:** The Entry segment, comprising A- and B-Class derivatives, EQA, and EQB, saw a decline of 14% in 2024, with sales reaching 534,800 units. A further 9% drop was observed in Q1 2025.

#### 🌱 Opportunities

1. **Software & Digital Revenue:** Daimler's strategic focus on digital services, including the MB.OS operating system and enhanced MBUX Virtual Assistant, opens avenues for new revenue generation through over-the-air updates and subscription-based features. This digital transformation is crucial as the automotive industry increasingly moves towards software-defined vehicles.

2. **Emerging Markets Growth:** India delivered double-digit growth in 2024–2025, prompting expanded assembly in Chakan, Pune to add luxury EV models and support Mercedes-Benz's expansion plans in emerging markets.

3. **Ultra-Luxury Expansion (Mythos Series):** The strategy is based on profitable growth through the introduction of luxury EVs, and as part of this strategy, Mercedes-Benz will launch the Mercedes-AMG PureSpeed, the first in the ultra-luxury brand "Mythos" series, in 2025.

4. **EV Incentive Alignment:** The US Inflation Reduction Act offers a $7,500 tax credit for EVs with localised battery assembly; Mercedes-Benz Group AG has invested $1.7B in its 2025 Alabama EV plant.

#### 🚨 Threats

1. **Chinese EV Competitors:** BYD, now the world's largest electric vehicle manufacturer, is producing cars with comparable technology at price points Mercedes cannot match without destroying its brand positioning.

2. **Geopolitical & Tariff Risk:** The EU-China tariff spat raises duties up to 35% on electric-vehicle imports, squeezing Mercedes-Benz Group AG's margins.

3. **Regulatory Tightening:** Emissions regulations across Europe continue to tighten, imposing significant compliance costs and potential fines for carmakers that fail to meet fleet CO₂ targets.

4. **Consumer Preference Shifts:** Younger luxury consumers increasingly prioritise digital experience, sustainability credentials, and value — areas where Chinese and American EV brands currently outperform.

### 4.4 ANSOFF MATRIX — GROWTH STRATEGY ANALYSIS

The Ansoff Matrix (1957) maps Mercedes-Benz's growth options across existing/new products and existing/new markets.

#### Quadrant 1: Market Penetration (Existing Products × Existing Markets)
Mercedes-Benz is pursuing aggressive cost restructuring and pricing adjustments to defend share in its established markets. The "Next Level Performance" program is centered on operational efficiency and electrification, aiming to restore double-digit adjusted Return on Sales (RoS) margins. Aggressive cost-cutting includes 10% production cost reductions by 2027 and localised manufacturing in key markets. Discounting of existing EV models and loyalty programmes are also being deployed to retain existing customers.

#### Quadrant 2: Market Development (Existing Products × New Markets)
India delivered double-digit growth in 2024–2025, prompting expanded assembly in Chakan, Pune to add luxury EV models and support Mercedes-Benz's expansion plans in emerging markets. Southeast Asia, the Middle East, and Sub-Saharan Africa represent underpenetrated markets where the Mercedes brand carries strong aspirational cachet and local EV competition is less mature.

#### Quadrant 3: Product Development (New Products × Existing Markets)
This is Mercedes-Benz's most active quadrant. Mercedes-Benz is targeting an electrified vehicle (xEV) share of more than 30% by 2027. The company plans to develop seven China-specific models and deepen its localisation efforts in this market. In the Entry Luxury segment, Mercedes-Benz will reduce the number of model variants from seven to four while significantly elevating the technological substance of these products. The new MB.OS will be launched in 2024 with the next compact MMA platform.

#### Quadrant 4: Diversification (New Products × New Markets)
Daimler's strategic focus on digital services, including the MB.OS operating system and enhanced MBUX Virtual Assistant, opens avenues for new revenue generation through over-the-air updates and subscription-based features. The company is also exploring fleet mobility services and energy management solutions (vehicle-to-grid technology), representing genuine diversification beyond the traditional car sales model. The "Mythos" ultra-luxury series targets an entirely new stratum of ultra-high-net-worth clientele.

## 5. STRATEGIC DIAGNOSIS

Synthesising the above frameworks, three core strategic tensions emerge:

| # | Strategic Tension | Nature |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Brand vs. Volume:** Pursuing ultra-luxury margins risks abandoning the volume needed to fund EV R&D | Dilemma |
| 2 | **China vs. Decoupling:** Deep China exposure is a vulnerability, yet China remains critical for scale and profit | Paradox |
| 3 | **ICE vs. EV:** Maintaining ICE competitiveness while investing in electrification creates capital allocation conflict | Trade-off |

The root cause is a **dual disruption**: Mercedes-Benz faces simultaneous disruption of its *product* (ICE → EV) and its *market* (Western-led luxury → Chinese domestic competition), without the luxury of addressing either sequentially.

## 6. STRATEGIC RECOMMENDATIONS & ACTION PLAN

Based on the multi-framework analysis, the following five strategic recommendations are proposed:

---

### 📌 Recommendation 1: Commit Fully to the "Luxury-First, Volume-Second" Strategy

**Rationale:** Mercedes-Benz cannot win a price war with BYD or a technology war with Xiaomi in the mass-premium segment without destroying its brand equity. Its sustainable competitive advantage lies in the top-end, where the G-Class and S-Class saw sales rise by 41% and 9% year-to-date, demonstrating resilience in high-margin segments.

**Action Plan:**
- **Accelerate the Mythos ultra-luxury series** — expand beyond the AMG PureSpeed to create a full portfolio of bespoke, limited-edition vehicles commanding €200,000+ price points.
- **Rationalise the entry segment** — reduce the number of entry model variants from seven to four and reposition them with significantly higher technological and design substance to justify premium pricing.
- **Divest or discontinue** models with thin margins and high competitive exposure (e.g., EQA, EQB) where Chinese rivals offer comparable technology at dramatically lower prices.
- **KPI:** Increase Top-End Luxury segment share to 25% of total revenue by 2027.

---

### 📌 Recommendation 2: Accelerate MB.OS as a Profit Centre, Not Just a Feature

**Rationale:** The automotive industry is shifting from hardware-defined to software-defined vehicles. Digital services, including the MB.OS operating system and enhanced MBUX Virtual Assistant, open avenues for new revenue generation through over-the-air updates and subscription-based features. Mercedes-Benz must monetise its software stack aggressively.

**Action Plan:**
- **Launch a tiered subscription model** for MB.OS features (advanced autonomous driving, personalised AI assistant, performance unlocks), targeting €1,500–€3,000 per vehicle per year in recurring revenue.
- **Open MB.OS to third-party developers** (analogous to Apple's App Store), creating a platform ecosystem that generates revenue, deepens user lock-in, and differentiates the ownership experience.
- **Partner with luxury lifestyle brands** (hospitality, fashion, wellness) to embed exclusive digital experiences within MB.OS, reinforcing the brand's luxury positioning.
- **KPI:** Generate €2 billion in annual digital/subscription revenue by 2028.

---

### 📌 Recommendation 3: Execute a "China for China" Deep Localisation Strategy

**Rationale:** Mercedes-Benz plans to develop seven China-specific models and deepen its localisation efforts in this market, and operates its largest R&D hub outside Germany in China. Half-measures will not suffice — the company must treat China as a quasi-independent innovation hub.

**Action Plan:**
- **Establish a China-headquartered EV sub-brand** in joint venture with a local partner (e.g., BAIC), with full authority over product design, software, and go-to-market strategy — insulated from German bureaucratic cycles.
- **Integrate Chinese tech ecosystems** (WeChat, Alipay, Baidu Maps, DeepSeek AI) natively into the China-market MB.OS stack, rather than offering European-first features as an afterthought.
- **Adopt a China-speed product cadence** — reduce model refresh cycles from 7 years to 3–4 years for China-specific models, matching the pace of domestic competitors.
- **Price competitively in Core segment in China** while protecting Maybach/G-Class pricing — accept lower margins in Core to maintain scale and brand presence.
- **KPI:** Stabilise China market share at ≥10% of the premium segment by 2026; recover to 750,000 annual units by 2027.

---

### 📌 Recommendation 4: Restructure the Supply Chain Around Battery Sovereignty

**Rationale:** Mercedes-Benz's EV competitiveness is constrained by its dependence on external battery suppliers and its exposure to geopolitical supply chain disruption. Aggressive cost-cutting, including 10% production cost reductions by 2027 and localised manufacturing in key markets, aims to mitigate supply chain risks and inflationary pressures.

**Action Plan:**
- **Invest in solid-state battery partnerships** — commit to a joint development agreement with a leading solid-state battery developer (e.g., QuantumScape, in which Mercedes already holds a stake) to secure next-generation cell technology by 2027–2028.
- **Establish regional battery assembly** in Europe (aligned with EU Battery Regulation requirements), North America (aligned with IRA incentives), and China (aligned with local content requirements).
- **Diversify critical mineral sourcing** — establish long-term offtake agreements for lithium, cobalt, and nickel with producers in Australia, Chile, and Canada to reduce Chinese mineral dependency.
- **KPI:** Achieve 40% in-house or long-term contracted battery supply by 2027; reduce battery cost per kWh by 30%.

---

### 📌 Recommendation 5: Pursue Strategic Geographic Diversification into High-Growth Emerging Markets

**Rationale:** Over-reliance on China (historically ~30% of revenue) is a systemic risk. India delivered double-digit growth in 2024–2025, prompting expanded assembly in Chakan, Pune to add luxury EV models. A deliberate rebalancing towards India, Southeast Asia, and the Middle East can reduce this concentration risk.

**Action Plan:**
- **India:** Expand the Chakan plant to full EV assembly capability; launch India-specific entry luxury models priced for the aspirational upper-middle class; target 50,000 annual units by 2027.
- **Middle East & Gulf States:** Leverage the region's high-net-worth consumer base, low EV adoption barriers, and strategic geographic position; establish a regional hub in Dubai for bespoke Maybach and AMG customisation.
- **Southeast Asia:** Enter Vietnam, Thailand, and Indonesia with CKD (completely knocked down) assembly partnerships to minimise tariff exposure and build local brand affinity.
- **KPI:** Reduce China's share of group revenue from ~30% to ≤18% by 2028; grow India + Middle East + Southeast Asia combined to ≥12% of revenue.

## 9. CONCLUSION

Mercedes-Benz stands at one of the most consequential strategic inflection points in its 139-year history. The convergence of EV disruption, Chinese competitive intensity, and macroeconomic headwinds has eroded the financial performance that once seemed impregnable. Yet the brand's core assets — its engineering heritage, iconic design language, global dealer network, and the aspirational power of the three-pointed star — remain formidable.

The strategic path forward is not to compete on every front simultaneously, but to make deliberate choices: **double down on ultra-luxury**, **monetise software aggressively**, **localise deeply in China**, **secure the battery supply chain**, and **diversify geographically**. These recommendations are grounded in the analytical frameworks applied throughout this case — PESTEL, Porter's Five Forces, SWOT, Ansoff, and Value Chain — and collectively represent a coherent strategic response to a genuinely complex situation.

The company's "Next Level Performance" program has become central to its strategy for mitigating these challenges, but operational efficiency alone will not be sufficient. What Mercedes-Benz requires is not merely a leaner company — it requires a **strategically reimagined** one, capable of defending its legacy while pioneering the future of luxury mobility.
