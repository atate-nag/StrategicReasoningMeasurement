# Bayer AG: A Strategic Case Study

## The Anatomy of a Self-Inflicted Crisis and the Road to Turnaround

---

## 1. Introduction and Case Overview

This case study examines Bayer AG's strategic situation from 2018 to the present, centred on what has been described as one of the worst corporate acquisitions in recent history: the $63 billion purchase of Monsanto. The case is analytically significant because it illustrates, in a single firm, the simultaneous operation of multiple strategic failure modes — M&A due diligence failure, portfolio misalignment, financial over-leverage, and competitive erosion — and the subsequent turnaround attempt. The analysis applies Porter's Five Forces, the BCG Growth-Share Matrix, the Resource-Based View (RBV), a SWOT analysis, and Ansoff's Growth Matrix to diagnose the situation and derive a coherent set of recommendations.

---

## 2. Company Background and Strategic Context

In 2018, Bayer AG completed its $63 billion acquisition of U.S.-based agricultural giant Monsanto Company, paying an 18.6x EV/EBITDA multiple and financing the deal partly through a $57 billion bridge-loan. The strategic rationale was clear on paper: the deal would create a company commanding more than a quarter of the combined world market for seeds and pesticides in the fast-consolidating farm supplies industry, and Bayer would become the world's largest seed company. However, the acquisition fundamentally shifted Bayer's centre of gravity: Bayer shifted from a healthcare-oriented company towards the cyclical agri-business, with Agribusiness replacing healthcare as its biggest revenue earner.

Over the five years following the acquisition, the company generated approximately 48% of its revenue from its Crop Science division, 40% from Pharmaceuticals, and 12% from Consumer Health. This revenue distribution is strategically significant because Pharmaceuticals was the most profitable segment during this period, with an operating profit margin of 22%, followed by Consumer Health at 17%, while Crop Science recorded an operating loss of 20%. In other words, the segment that now dominates revenue is also the one that has been destroying value — a structural misalignment at the heart of Bayer's strategy.

The market has rendered a harsh verdict. Bayer shares have lost close to 80% since the Monsanto deal was closed in 2018 and about 70% since it was agreed in 2016. Bayer's purchase of Monsanto was dubbed one of the "worst corporate deals" in recent history by the Wall Street Journal.

## 3. The Strategic Situation: Three Interlocking Crises

Bayer's current strategic situation is best understood as three interlocking crises — legal, financial, and competitive — each of which reinforces the others.

### 3.1 The Litigation Crisis

In 2018, Bayer AG completed its $63 billion acquisition of Monsanto. At the heart of Monsanto's product line was Roundup, a glyphosate-based herbicide that had long been the subject of health and environmental controversy. Shortly after the acquisition, Bayer found itself engulfed in a wave of litigation alleging that Roundup causes non-Hodgkin's lymphoma and other cancers. To date, Bayer has paid over $10 billion to settle a portion of the claims but faces approximately 67,000 lawsuits that remain pending as of May 2025.

The litigation burden has escalated further. Bayer announced a proposed U.S. nationwide class settlement designed to resolve current and future Roundup claims, with funding of the class totalling up to $7.25 billion U.S. dollars, with Monsanto making declining capped annual payments for up to 21 years. These resolutions will lead to an increase of the provision and liabilities for litigation from €7.8 billion to €11.8 billion, including €9.6 billion for glyphosate.

The litigation crisis is not merely a financial drain; it constitutes a strategic trap. Bayer appears to have underestimated the scale and durability of Monsanto's litigation risk — publicly available studies and ongoing lawsuits at the time of acquisition should have triggered more extensive legal scenario modelling and financial forecasting. This failure of M&A due diligence is the root cause of Bayer's current distress: by acquiring Monsanto's liabilities alongside its assets, Bayer imported a contingent obligation that has proved to exceed any plausible synergy gain.

The legal situation does carry one potential escape route. A favourable ruling by the U.S. Supreme Court on the federal preemption question could largely end the Roundup litigation. The main legal question at issue is whether state-based failure-to-warn claims are pre-empted when federal regulators have not required such a warning. In January 2026, the U.S. Supreme Court announced it will review the Durnell Roundup case, addressing the split in authority among federal circuit courts. This represents a pivotal binary risk for the firm's strategic planning horizon.

### 3.2 The Financial Crisis

Net financial debt amounted to €32.626 billion as of December 31, 2024, down 5.4% from year-end 2023. This debt load — the direct product of the Monsanto acquisition financing — constrains every strategic option available to Bayer. Bayer's priority is to reduce its debt level to boost financial flexibility; accordingly, it has proposed a dividend of only €0.11 per share. The near-elimination of the dividend signals the severity of the financial constraint: capital that would ordinarily flow to shareholders is being retained to service obligations incurred by the acquisition.

Bayer reported a net loss of €2.6 billion for the full year 2024, primarily due to impairment losses in the Crop Science division. In 2024, Bayer Crop Science recorded significant impairment charges totalling €3.78 billion, primarily in crop protection, including a €3.27 billion impairment on goodwill reflecting weaker-than-expected agricultural market conditions. These goodwill write-downs are the accounting recognition of a strategic truth: the assets acquired from Monsanto are worth substantially less than what Bayer paid for them. This is not merely a balance-sheet event; it confirms that the acquisition's strategic thesis — that agricultural scale would generate sustained premium returns — has failed.

### 3.3 The Competitive Crisis

Bayer faces simultaneous competitive deterioration in both of its major divisions.

**In Crop Science**, the glyphosate business experienced a 13% drop in pricing, though volume increased by 7%, and fungicides faced challenges as generic competition and industry-wide inventory destocking put downward pressure on sales. This pricing erosion reflects a structural, not cyclical, problem: the commoditisation of glyphosate as patents expire and Chinese generic manufacturers enter the market. U.S. agrichemicals competitor Corteva and the agriculture unit of Germany's BASF have also been hit by lower prices, but without the additional burden of litigation. The competitive disadvantage is therefore asymmetric: Bayer bears litigation costs that its rivals do not.

**In Pharmaceuticals**, Bayer faces a patent cliff of significant magnitude. Bayer lost its U.S. patent on Xarelto, its factor Xa inhibitor, in 2025, which alone accounted for an estimated €1–1.5 billion of lost 2025 revenues. The company is set to lose its patent on Eylea in 2027. Sales of Xarelto dropped by 33% to €2.34 billion in 2025 as generics flooded the market following patent expiry. Together, Xarelto and Eylea have historically represented the pharmaceutical division's two largest revenue sources, meaning their erosion creates a structural revenue gap that must be filled by pipeline products.

## 4. Strategic Analysis

### 4.1 Porter's Five Forces Analysis

**Threat of New Entrants (Moderate–High in generics; Low in innovative pharma/seeds):** In crop protection chemicals, the expiry of glyphosate patents has lowered barriers, enabling Chinese generic manufacturers to enter at scale and compress margins — as evidenced by the 13% glyphosate price decline. In innovative pharmaceuticals and proprietary seed technologies, barriers remain high due to R&D costs, regulatory approval timescales, and intellectual property. The implication is that Bayer's competitive moat exists only in its innovative segments, not in its commodity-facing ones.

**Bargaining Power of Buyers (High in crop science; Moderate in pharma):** Competitive advantages are durable in brand equity and scale but face sector-specific pressures: patent cliffs in pharma, private-label pressure in OTC, and commodity/litigation cycles in crop science. Farmers purchasing crop protection products increasingly have generic substitutes, raising their switching power. Insurers and pharmacy benefit managers in pharmaceuticals also exert pricing pressure, particularly post-patent expiry.

**Bargaining Power of Suppliers (Low–Moderate):** Bayer's scale gives it negotiating leverage over raw material suppliers. However, its dependence on contract research organisations and specialised biotech partners for pipeline development creates pockets of supplier power in the innovation chain.

**Threat of Substitutes (High in crop science; Rising in pharma):** In crop science, biological crop protection products represent an emerging substitute for traditional chemical inputs. Ag biotech startups in biologicals, gene-editing and digital platforms challenge traditional input models and distribution. In pharmaceuticals, biosimilar entry has already begun eroding Eylea revenues.

**Competitive Rivalry (Intense):** Bayer competes with Roche, Pfizer, Novartis, Syngenta, and Corteva across overlapping markets. In crop science, Syngenta, a subsidiary of ChemChina, offers a wide range of crop protection products and seeds, supported by significant investments in R&D, and Bayer's acquisition of Monsanto has further strengthened its position in the seeds and traits segment — but this strength is offset by the litigation overhang that rivals do not carry.

**Five Forces Conclusion:** The analysis reveals that Bayer's strongest competitive position lies in innovative pharmaceuticals (high barriers, differentiated products, patent protection) and proprietary seed genetics. Its weakest position is in commodity crop chemicals, where it is exposed to generic competition without a litigation-free cost structure. This warrants a strategic reorientation toward the former and away from the latter.

### 4.2 BCG Growth-Share Matrix

Applying the BCG framework to Bayer's three divisions:

- **Pharmaceuticals — "Star" transitioning to "Question Mark":** High market growth in cardiovascular, oncology, and women's health, but declining relative market share as Xarelto and Eylea face generic erosion. The pipeline (acoramidis, elinzanetant, asundexian) represents the investment needed to restore Star status. Bayer is relying on five assets to drive growth and reclaim near-term profits, including Beyonttra (acoramidis) for ATTR-CM, elinzanetant for menopause management, and asundexian for secondary stroke prevention.

- **Crop Science — "Dog":** Crop Science recorded an operating loss of 20% over the 2020–2024 period, with declining pricing, impairment charges, and litigation liabilities. Despite being the largest revenue segment, it is the worst performer by profitability. BCG logic would suggest divesting or restructuring this unit — yet doing so is constrained by the litigation, which travels with the asset.

- **Consumer Health — "Cash Cow":** Bayer considered a potential sale of consumer health, but although such a deal would immediately give Bayer some cash to pay down debt, the tax losses would be significant, and Bayer would lose a business that's reliably generating cash. This assessment aligns with BCG logic: Consumer Health generates steady cash flows (17% operating margin) in a low-growth market, making it a classic Cash Cow whose cash should fund the Star (Pharmaceuticals pipeline).

**BCG Conclusion:** The matrix points to a portfolio strategy of investing heavily in Pharmaceuticals innovation, milking Consumer Health for cash, and rationalising — rather than growing — Crop Science. This conclusion connects directly to the financial constraint: with €32.6 billion in net debt, Bayer cannot afford to invest across all three divisions simultaneously.

### 4.3 Resource-Based View (RBV)

The RBV asks: which of Bayer's resources and capabilities are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN)?

- **R&D capability in pharmaceuticals:** In 2024, Bayer's R&D expenses were approximately €5.5 billion, reflecting its commitment to innovation. This sustained investment has built a pipeline of differentiated assets (acoramidis, elinzanetant, darolutamide) that competitors cannot easily replicate. This is Bayer's clearest VRIN resource.

- **Seed genetics and trait technology (Crop Science):** Monsanto's seed intellectual property — the original rationale for the acquisition — retains value as a rare, inimitable asset in GMO crops. However, its value is being eroded by the litigation overhang, regulatory restrictions on GMO crops in key markets (particularly Europe), and pricing pressure in commodity herbicides.

- **Brand equity in Consumer Health:** Brands such as Aspirin, Bepanthen, and Aleve carry decades of consumer trust. These are valuable and moderately rare, but increasingly subject to private-label competition, making them imperfectly inimitable.

- **The DSO operating model:** Bayer has cut organisational layers from 12–13 to 6–7, reduced management positions by roughly 50%, eliminated approximately 12,000 positions, and created some 2,000 autonomous teams of 6–10 people with authority to make decisions previously escalated through layers of approval. If successfully embedded, this constitutes an organisational capability that is hard to replicate quickly. Bayer's Dynamic Shared Ownership (DSO) operating model is driving efficiency, with €500 million saved in 2024 and €800 million projected for 2025, targeting €2 billion in annual savings from 2026.

**RBV Conclusion:** Bayer's most durable competitive advantage lies in pharmaceutical R&D capability and its emerging DSO-driven organisational efficiency. Its seed genetics IP retains strategic value but is impaired by litigation. Consumer Health brands are valuable but not uniquely defensible. The implication is that the firm should protect and invest in pharmaceutical R&D above all other resource commitments.

### 4.4 SWOT Analysis

**Strengths:**

- €5.5 billion R&D investment in 2024
- Proprietary seed genetics and trait technology in Crop Science

**Weaknesses:**
- Net financial debt of €32.6 billion as of December 31, 2024, severely limiting strategic flexibility
- Ongoing Roundup litigation with total provisions now at €11.8 billion
- Net loss of €2.6 billion in 2024 due to Crop Science impairments
- Patent cliff threatening Xarelto and Eylea revenues
- Reputational damage from the Monsanto acquisition, reducing investor confidence

**Opportunities:**

- A favourable U.S. Supreme Court ruling on federal preemption could largely end the Roundup litigation, removing the single largest strategic overhang
- Growing global demand for food security driving long-term agricultural input demand
- Digital farming and precision agriculture as a new competitive frontier

**Threats:**
- Continued adverse jury verdicts in Roundup cases before Supreme Court resolution
- Accelerating generic and biosimilar competition in pharmaceuticals
- Commoditisation of crop protection chemicals through Chinese generic entry
- Potential credit rating downgrade if debt reduction targets are missed, raising borrowing costs

### 4.5 Ansoff's Growth Matrix

Bayer's current strategic options can be mapped onto Ansoff's matrix:

- **Market Penetration (existing products, existing markets):** Grow Nubeqa (darolutamide) in prostate cancer. Bayer continues to strengthen its leadership position in prostate cancer with the anticipated launch of a third indication in 2025 for darolutamide, supported by data from the ARANOTE trial. This is the lowest-risk growth path and should be prioritised given the financial constraint.

- **Product Development (new products, existing markets):** Launch acoramidis, elinzanetant, and asundexian into established therapeutic areas (cardiology, women's health, neurology). Bayer acquired EU and UK rights for acoramidis from BridgeBio in March 2024 for $130 million — a targeted, capital-efficient move to add a near-term product without a full internal R&D programme.

- **Market Development (existing products, new markets):** Expand Kerendia (finerenone) into heart failure. The robust data from Kerendia's clinical development programme underscore its potential to become a pillar of therapy for patients with chronic kidney disease associated with Type 2 Diabetes and patients with heart failure.

- **Diversification:** Given Bayer's debt burden, diversification into entirely new businesses is not feasible and would be strategically inadvisable. The DSO restructuring and debt repayment must precede any diversification move.

**Ansoff Conclusion:** Bayer's optimal growth path lies in market penetration and product development — leveraging existing capabilities in existing or adjacent therapeutic markets — rather than market development or diversification. This is consistent with the financial constraint: lower-risk growth options preserve cash for debt repayment while building the pharmaceutical revenue base needed to replace Xarelto and Eylea.

## 5. Synthesis: The Strategic Logic Chain

The analytical frameworks converge on a coherent diagnosis. The Monsanto acquisition created a three-part trap: (1) it loaded the balance sheet with debt that constrains all subsequent strategic action; (2) it imported litigation liabilities that were not adequately priced or modelled in due diligence; and (3) it shifted the portfolio's centre of gravity toward Crop Science, which is the least profitable and most litigated segment. The RBV analysis shows that Bayer's genuine VRIN resources lie in pharmaceutical R&D, not in commodity crop chemicals. The BCG matrix confirms that Crop Science is a "Dog" while Pharmaceuticals is a "Star" that needs investment. Porter's Five Forces reveals that competitive advantage is defensible in innovative pharma but not in generic-exposed crop protection. The SWOT analysis shows that the most transformative opportunity — Supreme Court preemption — is binary and not fully within management's control, while the most actionable opportunities lie in the pharmaceutical pipeline.

The logical chain is therefore: *the acquisition destroyed value by misallocating capital to a low-return, high-liability business; the resulting debt and litigation constrain strategic flexibility; the path to recovery requires concentrating resources on the pharmaceutical pipeline, managing Crop Science for cash rather than growth, and resolving the litigation overhang as a precondition for financial normalisation.*

## 6. Recommendations and Action Plan

The following recommendations are presented in order of strategic priority, reflecting the constraint that financial repair must precede strategic expansion.

### Recommendation 1: Pursue Litigation Resolution as the Overriding Priority (Immediate, 0–24 months)

**Rationale:** Every other strategic option is constrained by the litigation overhang. The proposed $7.25 billion class settlement and the pending Supreme Court review represent a convergence of legal and legislative strategies that could, if successful, cap and contain the liability. The class settlement and the U.S. Supreme Court case are independently necessary and mutually reinforcing elements in a multi-pronged strategy to significantly contain the litigation, with Monsanto making declining capped annual payments for up to 21 years, giving it greater financial certainty. Management should pursue both tracks simultaneously: supporting the class settlement process and preparing the strongest possible Supreme Court brief on federal preemption.

**Action:** Dedicate legal and financial resources to securing court approval of the class settlement. Engage proactively with U.S. legislators on the Modern Ag Alliance initiative to establish statutory preemption as a complementary legislative backstop.

**Warrant:** Until the litigation is capped, Bayer's credit profile remains impaired, its cost of capital remains elevated, and its ability to invest in the pharmaceutical pipeline is constrained. Resolution is therefore not merely a legal objective but the enabling condition for every other strategic recommendation.

### Recommendation 2: Accelerate Pharmaceutical Pipeline Launches (Immediate–Medium Term, 12–36 months)

**Rationale:** The pharmaceutical patent cliff — Xarelto's U.S. patent expiry in 2025 costing an estimated €1–1.5 billion in revenues, and Eylea's expected patent loss in 2027 — creates a revenue gap that must be filled by pipeline products. The pipeline is credible: Bayer is relying on five assets to drive growth, including Beyonttra (acoramidis), elinzanetant, and asundexian. The DSO model's agility advantage is directly applicable here: early DSO experiments moved launch dates forward by 5–9 months, shortening internal processes by 60% and resulting in a 30% increase in value.

**Action:** Prioritise regulatory submissions and commercial launch preparation for acoramidis (ATTR-CM), elinzanetant (menopause), and asundexian (secondary stroke). Allocate DSO teams specifically to each launch with 90-day cycle targets for regulatory milestones. Protect the R&D budget (approximately €5.5 billion) from debt-driven cuts.

**Warrant:** The BCG and RBV analyses both identify pharmaceutical innovation as Bayer's core value-creating activity. Allowing the pipeline to be underfunded in order to service acquisition debt would compound the original strategic error of the Monsanto acquisition.

### Recommendation 3: Restructure Crop Science Around Profitable Sub-segments (Medium Term, 12–48 months)

**Rationale:** Crop Science as currently configured is not a strategic asset — it is a value destroyer. Crop Science recorded an operating loss of 20% over 2020–2024. However, within Crop Science, the seeds and traits business retains genuine competitive advantage (VRIN characteristics) while commodity crop protection chemicals do not. Bayer should therefore restructure Crop Science to concentrate on seeds, traits, and digital farming, while managing or divesting commodity herbicide and fungicide exposure.

**Action:** Conduct a formal portfolio review of Crop Science sub-segments. Prioritise investment in proprietary seed genetics and digital farming tools. Consider licensing or divesting the commodity glyphosate business to a partner better positioned to manage its litigation and pricing risks. Bayer's comprehensive plan to boost profitability at Crop Science should be accelerated, with explicit profitability targets by sub-segment.

**Warrant:** The Ansoff matrix shows that market penetration in existing seeds and traits markets is the lowest-risk growth path in agriculture. The Five Forces analysis shows that competitive advantage is defensible in proprietary genetics but not in commodity chemicals. Concentration on the former and retreat from the latter is therefore the strategically coherent position.

### Recommendation 4: Retain Consumer Health as a Cash-Generating Platform (Medium Term)

**Rationale:** Although a sale of Consumer Health would immediately give Bayer cash to pay down debt, the tax losses would be significant, and Bayer would lose a business that's reliably generating cash. The BCG Cash Cow logic applies: Consumer Health should be managed for cash efficiency, not growth investment. Bayer's DSO model is driving €500 million in savings in 2024, with €800 million projected for 2025 and a target of €2 billion in annual savings from 2026. These savings, applied to Consumer Health's cost base, can increase the cash available for debt repayment without requiring a value-destructive sale.

**Action:** Apply DSO efficiency measures to Consumer Health operations. Focus on volume growth in emerging markets and Rx-to-OTC switches to extract additional value from existing brand equity without requiring large capital investment. Retain the division unless litigation settlement costs force an emergency monetisation.

**Warrant:** The financial constraint analysis shows that retaining a cash-generative business is superior to a tax-inefficient sale when the proceeds are needed for debt repayment. The DSO efficiency programme provides a mechanism to increase Consumer Health's cash contribution without a sale.

### Recommendation 5: Deleverage Systematically to Restore Strategic Flexibility (Ongoing)

**Rationale:** Net financial debt of €32.6 billion as of December 31, 2024 represents the binding constraint on Bayer's strategic options. Until this is reduced to a level consistent with an investment-grade credit profile, Bayer cannot make the acquisitions, partnerships, or R&D investments needed to sustain long-term competitiveness. Bayer's priority is to reduce its debt level to boost financial flexibility.

**Action:** Maintain the minimum dividend policy until net debt falls below €25 billion. Use DSO cost savings (targeting €2 billion annually from 2026) and Consumer Health cash flows to accelerate debt repayment. Explore non-core asset disposals — such as minority stakes in platform companies — that do not impair the pharmaceutical or core agricultural pipeline.

**Warrant:** The RBV analysis shows that financial flexibility is itself a strategic resource: it enables Bayer to invest in R&D, pursue bolt-on acquisitions (as it did with the acoramidis rights acquisition), and withstand adverse litigation outcomes. Without deleveraging, the debt burden will continue to crowd out strategic investment, perpetuating the cycle of underperformance.

## 7. Conclusion

Bayer's strategic situation is the product of a single, catastrophically misjudged acquisition. Bayer appears to have underestimated the scale and durability of Monsanto's litigation risk; publicly available studies and ongoing lawsuits at the time of acquisition should have triggered more extensive legal scenario modelling and financial forecasting. The consequence is a firm simultaneously facing litigation provisions of €11.8 billion, net debt of €32.6 billion, a pharmaceutical patent cliff, and commodity pricing erosion in its largest division.

Yet the analytical frameworks reveal that Bayer retains genuine strategic assets: a credible pharmaceutical pipeline, an emerging organisational capability in DSO, stable Consumer Health cash flows, and proprietary seed genetics. The path to recovery is coherent, if demanding: resolve the litigation overhang, invest in pharmaceutical pipeline launches, restructure Crop Science around its defensible sub-segments, retain Consumer Health as a cash engine, and systematically deleverage. Each recommendation flows from the diagnostic analysis, and each is warranted by the evidence of where Bayer's competitive advantage genuinely lies.

The deepest lesson of this case is not operational but strategic: the Monsanto acquisition illustrates what happens when M&A strategy overweights revenue scale and underweights contingent liability risk. The recovery strategy is, in essence, a systematic unwinding of those errors — redirecting resources toward the businesses where Bayer's VRIN capabilities are strongest, and away from those where it is structurally disadvantaged.
