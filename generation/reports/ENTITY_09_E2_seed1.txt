# Strategic Case Study: Charoen Pokphand Foods (CPF)
## *Navigating the Sustainability–Legitimacy Tension in a Vertically Integrated Global Agribusiness*

---

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Charoen Pokphand Foods Public Company Limited (CPF), headquartered in Bangkok, Thailand, is one of Asia Pacific's largest agro-industrial and food conglomerates. After achieving a net profit of THB 19,558 million on total sales of THB 580,747 million in 2024 — a 476% increase from a net loss in 2023 — the company demonstrated a dramatic financial recovery driven by overseas operations, cost management, and improved livestock market conditions. Yet beneath this financial resurgence lies a structural strategic tension: CPF's vertically integrated, protein-intensive business model generates the very environmental and social externalities — deforestation risk, supply chain labour abuses, and carbon emissions — that increasingly threaten its market access, investor confidence, and long-term licence to operate in regulated markets, particularly the European Union.

This case study analyses that tension using **PESTEL**, **Porter's Five Forces**, the **Resource-Based View (RBV)**, **Value Chain Analysis**, and a **SWOT/TOWS synthesis**. The analysis leads to a coherent set of strategic recommendations designed to convert CPF's sustainability commitments from reputational defences into genuine competitive advantages.

## 2. COMPANY BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### 2.1 Origins and Corporate Structure

CPF was established in 1978 with operations in animal feed production, livestock breeding, further processing and trade. Currently, CPF invests overseas in nine countries, has subsidiaries in 17 countries, and exports to over 40 countries. CPF reported sales revenue of approximately US$17.8 billion in 2024, with a market capitalisation of over $5.9 billion.

CPF's business model is defined by deep **vertical integration**. CPF's primary competitive advantage lies in its full vertical integration, spanning raw material sourcing, feed milling, animal husbandry, slaughtering, processing, and branded food distribution, which minimises intermediary costs and ensures end-to-end traceability. Its operations span livestock (swine, broiler chickens, layer hens, ducks) and aquaculture (shrimp and fish).

### 2.2 The "Kitchen of the World" Vision

CPF operates under the vision "Kitchen of the World" with a commitment to creating sustainable food security. This vision is underpinned by nine commitments shaped around three pillars of sustainability — "Food Security", "Self-Sufficient Society" and "Balance of Nature."

### 2.3 Recent Financial Performance

Of the total sales for 2024, 63% was generated from overseas operations across 13 countries, while 31% came from domestic operations in Thailand, and 6% from export activities. Revenues were primarily generated from farming operations (55%), animal feed (23%), and food products (22%). In Q1 2025, CPF reported a record-breaking net profit of THB 8.549 billion — an increase of 642% or six times higher than the same period last year, reflecting the company's strong adaptability to economic and geopolitical challenges.

## 3. THE STRATEGIC SITUATION: DEFINING THE PROBLEM

### 3.1 The Central Tension

CPF's core strategic situation can be characterised as a **sustainability–legitimacy tension**: the company's competitive advantage is built on a vertically integrated, high-volume protein production model that is simultaneously its greatest strength and its most significant source of strategic risk. This tension operates on three interconnected levels.

**Level 1 — Regulatory Legitimacy.** The EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR), Regulation (EU) 2023/1115, aims to bring down greenhouse gas emissions and biodiversity loss by boosting the consumption of deforestation-free products. From 30 December 2026, products placed on, sold within, or exported from the EU must be free from deforestation. The regulation covers commodities most associated with deforestation, including cattle, cocoa, coffee, palm oil, rubber, soy, and wood. CPF's soy-based animal feed supply chain creates direct compliance risk for its European market access. Bangkok Produce Merchandising (BKP), a subsidiary of CPF, and Louis Dreyfus Company have signed a Memorandum of Understanding to develop satellite mapping solutions for soy traceability, committing to a deforestation-free supply chain by 2025.

**Level 2 — Reputational Legitimacy.** CPF admitted that slave labour was part of its supply chain, stating: "The issue of endemic slavery in Thailand's seafood supply chain affects all producers in Thailand as they all use by-catch to produce their fishmeal in the same way." Although CPF has since taken remedial action — including paying premium prices to purchase fishmeal to compensate producers for the higher costs related to certifying the supply chain back to fishing boats, and engaging IFFO to audit and certify its seafood supply chain — the reputational damage created a persistent trust deficit with Western institutional investors and retail buyers.

**Level 3 — Competitive Legitimacy.** CPF is expanding into halal markets and new areas in Europe, confident that global food demand will stay strong despite economic challenges. However, this expansion ambition is conditional on demonstrating supply chain integrity to demanding European buyers. The failure to resolve the sustainability–legitimacy tension risks locking CPF into lower-margin, commoditised markets while competitors capture high-value, sustainability-premium segments.

### 3.2 Why This Is a Strategic, Not Merely Operational, Problem

The tension is strategic — not merely operational — because it affects *which markets* CPF can access, *which investors* will hold CPF stock, and *which customers* will specify CPF as a preferred supplier. These are not problems solvable through a single policy or certification; they require a coherent, multi-year strategic response that realigns CPF's business model with its operating environment.

## 4. EXTERNAL ANALYSIS

### 4.1 PESTEL Analysis

#### Political

The most significant political development is the EUDR, which creates a hard compliance deadline of December 2026 that cannot be deferred. For CPF, which depends on soy as a primary animal feed ingredient, non-compliance means loss of EU market access.

On trade policy, CPF's CEO has stated that "CP Foods remains unaffected by concerns surrounding US tariff policies as we focus on affordable food products that are produced locally." The company depends on exports for only 4–5% of its sales, primarily to some Asian markets such as Japan, as well as European countries. CPF adopts a localised production model in all markets outside its home base Thailand, producing and selling locally within each market to ensure food security and meet local consumer needs. This structural hedge against trade policy volatility is a genuine political strength.

#### Economic

The outstanding performance in 2024 surpassed targets largely due to a strong recovery in overseas operations, resulting from successful adjustments in supply and demand within the swine industry, which had faced oversupply and depressed prices in 2023. Notably in Vietnam, rising pork prices — driven by ASF outbreaks late in the year — contributed to better-than-anticipated results. This illustrates a recurring economic vulnerability: CPF's profitability is structurally exposed to commodity price cycles driven by disease outbreaks, feed cost inflation, and supply-demand imbalances across multiple national markets.

#### Social

The global plant-based protein market was valued at USD 24.08 billion in 2024 and is estimated to grow at a CAGR of about 14.1% from 2025 to 2033, reaching USD 78.94 billion. While CPF's core business remains conventional animal protein, this trend signals a medium-term threat to volume growth in premium markets and an opportunity for product portfolio diversification.

#### Technological

CP Foods launched "Feed Sustainovation 2024," an initiative dedicated to enhancing workforce capabilities through AI and innovative technologies to drive sustainable business practices. As part of its commitment to advancing Agri Tech, CP Foods is integrating AI and cutting-edge innovations across all production processes. The satellite mapping collaboration with Louis Dreyfus for soy traceability is a concrete example of technology serving regulatory compliance simultaneously.

#### Environmental

CPF is strengthening its commitment to Net-Zero emissions across the entire value chain by 2050 through the development of climate-responsible food products. Within 2030, CP Foods targets to raise revenue from green products by 40%, as current revenue from green products accounted for around 34% of total revenue in 2022, helping to reduce greenhouse gas emissions by 1.483 million tons of carbon. CPF's Biodiversity and Zero Deforestation Commitment is on target to be achieved by 2030, affirming the company's social responsibility through its "Balance of Nature" strategy.

#### Legal

The 2014 forced labour allegations resulted in a US lawsuit claiming damages related to alleged human rights abuses in the Thai shrimp supply chain. In January 2017, the United States brought a case against CPF and others claiming damages related to the alleged presence of human rights abuses in the supply chain for Thai shrimp. Although CPF has since implemented remediation measures, this precedent illustrates that supply chain opacity creates material legal liability. The EUDR adds a new layer: companies that fail to demonstrate deforestation-free supply chains face penalties and market exclusion.

### 4.2 Porter's Five Forces Analysis

**Force 1: Threat of New Entrants — LOW to MODERATE**

CPF's vertically integrated businesses incorporate the manufacturing of animal feed, animal breeding, animal farming, meat processing, and food processing. This integration requires enormous capital investment and relationship capital that cannot be quickly replicated. Since 1975, CP Foods has pioneered an integrated contract farming model in Thailand through the "Promoting Animal Husbandry for Small-Scale Farmers Program" involving over 5,500 smallholders. These embedded farmer relationships represent decades of social capital that constitute a structural barrier to entry. However, well-capitalised alternative protein companies represent a new form of entrant that bypasses the conventional supply chain entirely.

**Force 2: Bargaining Power of Suppliers — MODERATE**

CPF's vertical integration substantially reduces supplier power for most inputs. However, upstream raw material suppliers — particularly soy and fishmeal — retain meaningful leverage. The fishmeal supply chain scandal illustrated that CPF's dependence on third-party fishmeal suppliers created both reputational and legal exposure. The EUDR compliance requirement for soy traceability further elevates the strategic importance of supplier relationships, since CPF cannot simply switch suppliers without risking supply continuity.

**Force 3: Bargaining Power of Buyers — HIGH**

Tesco Lotus publicly committed to continuing to work on ensuring shrimp feed was not produced by forced labour, and stated it is determined to work with prawn suppliers in Thailand on developing trials that limit the use of fishmeal. Major global retailers have the leverage to delist suppliers who fail to meet sustainability standards, and the EUDR gives European buyers a legal basis to exclude non-compliant suppliers. This makes buyer power a high-intensity competitive force.

**Force 4: Threat of Substitutes — MODERATE and RISING**

Plant-based meat and seafood were 4% more expensive in 2024, versus just a 1% price hike for their conventional counterparts. CPF's strength in the value-for-money segment provides a near-term buffer. However, plant-based foods now reach 59% of US households, with health identified as the primary driver, indicating that the substitute threat intensifies as alternative protein technology matures and cost curves decline.

**Force 5: Competitive Rivalry — HIGH**

CP Group competitive analysis shows dominance in feed and integrated poultry, sustained R&D investment, and market positions across Southeast Asia and China. The group leverages retail data and telecom assets to outpace rivals on price and targeted distribution. However, rivalry is intensifying on the sustainability dimension: competitors are also investing in ESG credentials, and CPF cannot assume its DJSI ranking provides durable differentiation unless backed by verified, audited outcomes.

**Structural Conclusion:** The Five Forces analysis reveals that CPF operates in a structurally challenging industry where buyer power is high, substitutes are rising, and competitive rivalry on sustainability credentials is intensifying. CPF's vertical integration is its primary structural defence, but it does not insulate the company from the sustainability compliance pressures that increasingly determine market access.

That vision is achievable — but only if CPF treats sustainability not as a reputational shield, but as the structural foundation of its next competitive advantage.
