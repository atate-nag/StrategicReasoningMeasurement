# Polestar at the Crossroads: A Strategic Case Study

## The Challenger Brand Facing an Existential Test

---

## 1. Introduction and Case Overview

This case study examines Polestar Automotive Holding UK PLC (Nasdaq: PSNY) — a Swedish premium electric vehicle (EV) brand — as it confronts a compound strategic crisis in the 2023–2025 period. Polestar was carved out of Volvo Cars in 2017 as a standalone performance-EV brand and listed on Nasdaq in June 2022 via a SPAC merger. Its strategic situation is defined by three interlocking tensions: (1) a severe financial fragility that threatens going-concern status; (2) an intensifying competitive environment in which the brand's differentiation is being eroded from multiple directions simultaneously; and (3) a geopolitical ownership structure that creates both resource advantages and profound market-access risks.

The case uses four analytical frameworks — Porter's Five Forces, the Resource-Based View (RBV), SWOT analysis, and the Ansoff Growth Matrix — applied in sequence so that each framework's output feeds the next, building a coherent chain from industry structure → internal capability → strategic position → growth options → recommendations.

## 2. Company Background and Context

Polestar CEO Michael Lohscheller describes the brand's competitive posture as that of a challenger: "We are challenging the industry. We are challenging the competition, and we try out new things. Over and above that, we do beautiful cars." This framing is strategically significant: it signals a deliberate differentiation strategy rather than a cost-leadership or scale-volume approach. Understanding whether Polestar's resources actually support that challenger identity is the central question of this analysis.

Polestar CEO Michael Lohscheller has stated that Scandinavian minimalist design and the brand's sustainability framing remain the two differentiators that allow Polestar to coexist with Volvo Cars and Zeekr under the broader Geely Holding umbrella. These claimed differentiators, however, must be tested against the realities of the firm's financial position and competitive environment before they can be accepted as genuine strategic assets.

## 3. External Environment: Porter's Five Forces

Porter's Five Forces analysis establishes the structural attractiveness of the industry in which Polestar competes, providing the external baseline against which internal capabilities are subsequently assessed.

### 3.1 Competitive Rivalry — **Very High**

Rivalry among existing competitors is fierce, especially with established automakers and new EV entrants. In 2024, Tesla still led the US EV market with 50% share. Polestar's primary direct competitor is Tesla, particularly with models like the Model 3 and Model Y. Tesla's strong brand presence and extensive Supercharger network provide a significant advantage. In 2024, Tesla delivered approximately 1.8 million vehicles globally, showcasing its dominance in the EV market. Beyond Tesla, BMW, Mercedes-Benz, and Audi are rapidly electrifying their lineups, leveraging their brand loyalty and extensive dealer networks; in 2024, BMW's EV sales increased by 100% year-over-year.

The implication for Polestar is direct: it is a sub-50,000-unit brand competing against incumbents with decades of brand equity, far larger dealer networks, and vastly superior capital resources. High rivalry structurally compresses margins and forces Polestar into heavy discounting — a dynamic that, as Section 5 will show, has already destroyed gross profitability.

### 3.2 Threat of New Entrants — **High**

The threat of new entrants remains high, given the EV market's growth potential. The luxury EV segment specifically is attracting capital: the luxury electric vehicle market was valued at USD 205.10 billion in 2025 and is forecast to expand to USD 478.22 billion by 2036 at an 8.00% CAGR. Large and growing markets attract entrants, which means the competitive intensity Polestar faces today is likely to increase, not diminish. This reinforces the need for Polestar to establish durable, hard-to-replicate competitive advantages rather than relying on first-mover or niche positioning.

### 3.3 Bargaining Power of Suppliers — **High**

The EV sector's dependence on a few specialised suppliers, particularly for batteries and semiconductors, grants these suppliers substantial bargaining power. In 2024, companies like CATL and LG Energy Solution controlled a significant portion of the global battery market. Polestar's reliance on key tech suppliers, like those for battery tech and ADAS, elevates supplier power. This is a structural cost pressure that Polestar, unlike Tesla (which is vertically integrating into battery production), cannot easily escape given its asset-light model. High supplier power therefore constrains Polestar's ability to improve gross margins through input-cost reduction alone.

### 3.4 Bargaining Power of Buyers — **Moderate to High**

Customer bargaining power significantly impacts Polestar. However, the EV market is competitive, and loyalty can be tested. Premium car buyers are sophisticated, have multiple credible alternatives (Tesla, BMW i-series, Mercedes EQ, Audi e-tron), and are increasingly price-sensitive as the EV market matures and discounting becomes widespread. The practical consequence — evidenced in Polestar's financial results — is that Polestar has been forced to offer greater dealer discounts to move inventory, directly eroding margins.

### 3.5 Threat of Substitutes — **Moderate**

Plug-in hybrid vehicles (PHEVs) and premium internal combustion engine (ICE) vehicles remain viable substitutes for consumers not yet ready to commit to full electrification. However, the structural direction is clear: battery electric vehicles (BEVs) accounted for 13.6% of all new car registrations in the EU in 2024 and rebounded significantly in 2025, with BEVs reaching a 17.4% market share. The substitute threat is therefore declining over the medium term as EV adoption accelerates, which is a moderately positive structural signal for Polestar.

**Five Forces Summary:** The overall industry structure is unattractive for a subscale challenger. High rivalry, high new entrant threat, and high supplier power create a "structural trap" in which only firms with either massive scale (Tesla, legacy OEMs) or genuinely inimitable differentiation can sustain profitability. This finding directly motivates the RBV analysis: does Polestar possess the internal resources to escape this structural trap?

## 4. Internal Analysis: Resource-Based View (RBV)

The RBV (Barney, 1991) holds that sustainable competitive advantage derives from resources that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN). This section evaluates Polestar's key resource bundles against VRIN criteria.

### 4.1 Brand Identity and Design Capability — **Partially VRIN**

All Polestar models are fully electric, with a clear aim to be climate-neutral by 2030. The brand emphasises sustainability — using recycled, vegan, and traceable materials throughout interiors and manufacturing. Clean, minimalist design — Scandinavian cool, with crisp lines, uncluttered cabins, and dramatic lighting. This combination of aesthetic and ethical positioning is *valuable* (it commands premium pricing) and *somewhat rare* (few rivals combine both so explicitly). However, its *inimitability* is being eroded.

Geely Holding controls one of the broadest premium-to-luxury portfolios in the global auto industry — Zeekr, Lynk & Co, Polestar, Lotus, Volvo Cars, Smart, and the Geely Galaxy sub-brand — most of which share the SEA (Sustainable Experience Architecture) platform family for their EVs. The Zeekr design centre is also based in Sweden, at the Zeekr Gothenburg design studio led by former Volvo design head Stefan Sielaff — meaning that the "Scandinavian design" identity Lohscheller invoked is not exclusive to Polestar within the Geely portfolio. This is a critical finding: Polestar's primary stated differentiator is being replicated by a sibling brand within the same corporate family, undermining its inimitability and therefore its VRIN status.

### 4.2 Sustainability Credentials and Transparency — **Stronger VRIN**

Polestar launched a Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) report alongside the Polestar 2 to show transparency about how much CO2 is released in producing and operating the vehicle. Polestar has set an ambitious roadmap to reach climate neutrality by 2040, meaning eliminating all greenhouse gas emissions across operations and all phases of a car's life cycle, including supply chain and manufacturing. This degree of supply-chain transparency and the Polestar 0 Project (targeting a truly climate-neutral car by 2030 with no offsets) is more genuinely rare and harder to replicate quickly, as it requires deep supplier-relationship investments and third-party verification systems. This resource is *valuable, rare, and moderately inimitable*, making it Polestar's strongest VRIN asset.

### 4.3 Platform and Technology Access via Geely/Volvo — **Valuable but Not Rare**

The Volvo relationship provides practical infrastructure advantages that would be difficult for a standalone startup to replicate. Polestar vehicles are sold and serviced through Volvo's extensive dealer network, giving the brand physical presence at scale without the capital expenditure of building its own retail footprint from scratch. On the technology side, access to Geely's rapidly advancing Chinese automotive capabilities gives Polestar a competitive edge in areas such as battery technology, cost engineering, and manufacturing efficiency.

Polestar's asset-light manufacturing approach allows it to maximise efficiency and scalability by working closely with Volvo, who bring decades of expertise to the table. The Polestar 3 shares the SPA2 platform with the Volvo EX90, reducing R&D cost. However, because this platform access is shared across multiple Geely brands, it is *valuable but not rare or inimitable* — it does not, by itself, generate sustainable competitive advantage.

### 4.4 Financial Resources — **Critical Weakness**

The RBV requires that resources be *sustained*. Polestar's financial resources are severely depleted. Polestar reported revenue of $2.38 billion for fiscal year 2023, down 3% from $2.45 billion from 2022, citing higher discounts and lower sales of carbon credits. The company reported a gross loss of $414.7 million for the year, compared with a gross profit of $98.4 million the previous year. Gross profit/(loss) for the six months ended June 30, 2024 was a gross loss of $23.3 million, a decrease of $44.9 million, compared to a gross profit of $21.6 million for the six months ended June 30, 2023. This decrease is attributed to lower net revenue primarily due to lower volumes of Polestar 2 sales and greater dealer discounts.

Management's liquidity and funding plan casts significant doubt upon Polestar's ability to continue as a going concern. This is the most consequential finding of the RBV analysis: financial fragility threatens the firm's ability to *sustain* any of its other resources, because continued investment in brand, technology, and sustainability programmes requires cash that Polestar does not reliably generate internally.

**RBV Summary:** Polestar's most defensible resource is its sustainability transparency programme. Its design identity is being eroded by intra-Geely competition. Its platform access is shared and therefore non-rare. Its financial position is a critical liability that threatens the sustainability of all other resources. The strategic implication is that Polestar must *urgently restore financial viability* while *doubling down on sustainability transparency* as its most defensible differentiator.

## 6. Growth Options: Ansoff Matrix

Having established the external environment (Five Forces), internal capabilities (RBV), and strategic position (SWOT), the Ansoff Matrix is used to evaluate which growth directions are appropriate given Polestar's resource constraints and competitive context.

The Ansoff Matrix (Ansoff, 1957) maps four growth strategies along two dimensions — products (existing/new) and markets (existing/new) — with risk increasing as both dimensions become "new."

### Quadrant 1: Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets)

This is the *lowest-risk* strategy and is already underway. The updated business plan targets compound annual retail sales volume growth of 30–35% for 2025 to 2027 and a positive adjusted EBITDA in 2025. The established direct-to-consumer online sales channel will remain, giving customers a choice in how they want to buy their Polestar. Given Polestar's financial fragility, this quadrant should be the primary near-term focus: driving more sales of Polestar 3 and Polestar 4 in Europe and North America, where the brand already has infrastructure.

**Warrant:** Market penetration requires the least incremental capital, generates volume to spread fixed costs, and is consistent with the going-concern constraint identified in the RBV. The 37.2% increase in order intake in Q4 2024 versus Q4 2023 demonstrates that this strategy is already yielding results.

### Quadrant 2: Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets)

Polestar aspires to a sales breakdown of 40% in Europe, 30% in the United States, and 30% in the Asia-Pacific region. Expanding in Asia-Pacific (South Korea, Australia) with existing models represents moderate-risk market development. Polestar plans to start sales in France this year, adding a major European market where it has not previously had a retail presence. This is appropriate given the brand's European identity and the lower geopolitical risk of European sales versus US or Chinese markets.

**Warrant:** New markets add revenue without the R&D cost of new products, and the European market specifically reduces the tariff and Chinese-ownership-perception risks that afflict US sales. However, market development still requires distribution investment, so it must be sequenced *after* the financial stabilisation achieved through market penetration.

### Quadrant 3: Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets)

The second half of 2025 will see the planned start of sales of Polestar 5, the performance four-seat grand tourer based on Polestar's in-house developed bonded-aluminium platform. It will also be the first Polestar to use 800-volt technology. The Polestar 7 compact SUV, targeting the premium compact segment and planned for European manufacture, represents further product development. Polestar 7 targets the world's fastest growing and most profitable premium segment.

**Warrant:** The Polestar 5's proprietary bonded-aluminium platform and 800V architecture represent genuine technological differentiation that, unlike the shared SEA platform, is harder for Geely siblings to replicate — directly addressing the RBV finding that platform-sharing undermines inimitability. Product development is therefore strategically necessary but capital-intensive; it must be financed through the profitability gains from Quadrants 1 and 2 rather than treated as a near-term cash solution.

### Quadrant 4: Diversification (New Products, New Markets)

This is the highest-risk quadrant and is not recommended for Polestar in its current financial state. Ongoing cash burn and need for additional funding mean that diversification would stretch resources beyond breaking point. The going-concern language in Polestar's own filings makes this quadrant off-limits until financial stability is achieved.

**Ansoff Summary:** The recommended strategic sequence is: **Penetration → Market Development → Product Development**, with diversification deferred indefinitely. This sequence matches Polestar's resource constraints (prioritising cash generation) with its competitive imperatives (expanding geographic footprint to reduce tariff exposure, then building proprietary technology to restore inimitability).

## 7. The Central Strategic Problem: The Geopolitical-Ownership Trap

The frameworks above converge on a structural problem that no single analytical tool fully captures in isolation but which emerges clearly when they are read together: Polestar is caught in a *geopolitical-ownership trap*. Its Chinese parentage provides essential resources (platform technology, manufacturing capacity, capital) but simultaneously generates existential market-access risks in its two most important growth markets.

The U.S. tariffs on Chinese goods surged to 145% in April 2025, applying to nearly all Chinese-manufactured products including Polestar's EVs, which are currently produced in China. Polestar said it would need to introduce "mitigating measures" to achieve cashflow breakeven for 2025. The EU has imposed parallel tariffs on Chinese-manufactured EVs, and a Biden Administration rule effectively bans the import or sale of vehicles containing Chinese-connected vehicle system components, even if assembled in the U.S., if owned or controlled by entities from China or Russia.

The ownership restructuring has not resolved this trap. Geely Sweden Holding became the company's second-largest shareholder, while Volvo retained an 18 percent stake in Polestar, down from around 48 percent. While this broadens the shareholder base, it does not change the fundamental reality of Chinese ultimate control, which regulators and consumers in Western markets increasingly scrutinise.

This trap is the reason why manufacturing geography has become a strategic priority. Polestar 4 production is set to begin in South Korea in mid-2025, further diversifying the company's manufacturing footprint. Polestar's asset-light manufacturing approach allows for flexibility and scalability through its partnerships. The Ridgeville, South Carolina plant, built by Volvo, has been adapted to meet Polestar's specific needs, underscoring the strength of the collaboration. The factory produces cars for customers in the U.S. and Europe, complementing existing production in Chengdu, China.

The strategic logic is explicit: by manufacturing in the US and South Korea, Polestar can avoid tariffs and potentially satisfy rules-of-origin requirements, converting a geopolitical liability into a competitive asset. However, success hinges on execution — specifically whether Polestar can ramp up South Carolina/South Korea output without delays — and on whether U.S. buyers accept Polestar's $60,000–$80,000 price points amid economic uncertainty.

## 10. Conclusion

Polestar's strategic situation is one of genuine potential imperilled by near-term existential risk. The luxury EV market is growing rapidly, the brand has a credible identity and a growing product portfolio, and early signs of margin recovery in 2025 suggest the turnaround plan is working. But the going-concern language in Polestar's own filings, the 145% US tariff on Chinese-manufactured EVs, and the erosion of design differentiation by Geely siblings mean that the margin for error is extremely thin.

The analytical chain developed in this case — Five Forces → RBV → SWOT → Ansoff — converges on a single strategic imperative: Polestar must *stabilise before it scales*. The sequence of recommendations (margin discipline, manufacturing diversification, sustainability leadership, European market development, proprietary platform investment) is ordered precisely to match the firm's resource constraints to its strategic opportunities. Executing this sequence coherently, rather than pursuing all options simultaneously, is the difference between Polestar becoming a durable premium EV brand and becoming another cautionary tale of ambition exceeding resources.
