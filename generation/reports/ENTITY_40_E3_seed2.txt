# Strategic Case Study: Vattenfall AB and the Dual-Mandate Dilemma — Navigating Sweden's Energy Transition as a State-Owned Enterprise

---

## 1. Introduction and Case Framing

This case study examines the strategic situation of **Vattenfall AB**, Sweden's wholly state-owned energy company, as it attempts simultaneously to serve a commercial mandate (profitability, return on capital) and a public-policy mandate (enabling Sweden's fossil-free energy transition). The central strategic problem is a **dual-mandate tension**: the Swedish state, as sole owner, demands both financial returns and the delivery of a national energy transformation — objectives that are increasingly pulling in opposite directions as investment costs rise, policy signals become contradictory, and the demand for electricity threatens to double by 2045. The analysis applies the Resource-Based View (RBV), Dynamic Capabilities theory, Stakeholder theory, and principal–agent analysis to diagnose the situation, and concludes with a set of strategic recommendations.

---

## 2. Situational Context

### 2.1 Ownership and Governance Structure

Vattenfall AB is wholly owned by the Swedish state, with its headquarters in Solna, Sweden. This single-shareholder structure gives the Swedish government both ultimate authority over strategy and the responsibility for defining what the company is for. Vattenfall's dividend policy and financial targets are resolved by the general meeting according to the Swedish state's ownership policy; the targets include a return on capital employed of 8 per cent and a dividend amounting to 40–70 per cent of profit after tax. These financial targets matter for the analysis because they establish that the state expects a commercial return, not merely a public-service subsidy — a point that creates structural tension when investment in the energy transition is commercially unattractive without policy support.

The OECD's 2024 revised Guidelines on Corporate Governance of State-Owned Enterprises emphasise the need for the state to act as an active, informed and professional owner, including clearly defining the rationales and expectations for SOEs, especially with regard to their public policy objectives. As will be shown below, Sweden has not consistently met this standard in its direction of Vattenfall, and that governance gap is a root cause of several strategic failures.

### 2.2 Financial Position

The Swedish state-owned power utility Vattenfall posted 2024 revenue of SEK 246 billion (€22 billion, -15% compared to 2023) and profit of SEK 33 billion (€3 billion, ×3.3 compared to the prior year). The divergence — falling revenues but dramatically rising profit — reflects higher achieved prices in the Nordic market rather than volume growth. The underlying operating profit for the first nine months of 2024 increased by SEK 3.7 billion to SEK 19.3 billion, primarily driven by higher achieved prices in the Nordics and increased hydro, nuclear and wind power generation. This context is strategically significant: the profit improvement is partly cyclical (price-driven) rather than structural, meaning it cannot be assumed to persist as electricity prices normalise.

### 2.3 The Demand Imperative

Annual power demand in Sweden is expected to double from 140–150 TWh to 300 TWh in 2045 as the country transitions into the next phase of industrial electrification. More specifically, the IEA finds that electrification of end-use sectors will drive electricity demand from 140 TWh in 2023 to 160–210 TWh in 2030 and 200–340 TWh in 2045, with industry underpinning the lion's share of this growth. This demand trajectory is the strategic backdrop for every investment decision Vattenfall must now make: the question is not whether more generation is needed, but how to finance it, at what risk, and under what ownership model.

### 2.4 The Policy Environment: The Tidö Agreement

Sweden's 2022 centre-right coalition government adopted a positive stance towards nuclear energy; the Tidö Agreement changed the energy policy goal from "100% renewable to 100% fossil-free" and assumes electricity demand of at least 300 TWh in 2045, double current demand. This policy shift has profound strategic consequences for Vattenfall. By reframing the goal as "fossil-free" rather than "renewable," the government legitimised nuclear investment and simultaneously de-prioritised offshore wind — a technology in which Vattenfall had been making large bets. The shift thus created a strategic misalignment between Vattenfall's existing investment pipeline and its owner's newly stated preferences.

## 3. Strategic Analysis

### 3.1 Principal–Agent Analysis: The Governance Problem

The foundational analytical lens for a state-owned enterprise is **principal–agent theory** (Jensen & Meckling, 1976): the state (principal) delegates operational authority to Vattenfall's management (agent), but their interests may diverge. In Vattenfall's case, the problem is compounded because the principal itself is internally inconsistent — different arms of the Swedish government pursue different objectives.

Evidence of this dysfunction is visible in Vattenfall's history. Critics, including politicians, environmentalists and journalists, have argued that the state, as Vattenfall's owner, is "the owner without principles," having allowed the company to transform from a national jewel with high social responsibility into a profit-driven company — with Vattenfall's CO2 emissions in Germany alone exceeding those of the whole of Sweden. This critique directly illustrates the principal–agent failure: when the state failed to define clear public-policy objectives for Vattenfall's European expansion, management pursued commercial growth in lignite-intensive German markets, producing outcomes — massive CO2 emissions — that contradicted Sweden's own environmental values.

The consequences were severe. When Vattenfall sold its German lignite operations in 2016, the result in Vattenfall's income statement was a loss in the range of minus SEK 22–27 billion; the assets included cash of SEK 15 billion but liabilities and provisions, including re-cultivation obligations, amounted to SEK 18 billion. This loss, warranted by the earlier principal–agent failure, demonstrates that ambiguous ownership objectives impose real financial costs — not merely reputational ones.

**Analytical warrant:** The principal–agent framework explains why strategic incoherence in a state-owned firm is not merely a governance abstraction but a source of measurable value destruction. Unless the state resolves its dual mandate explicitly, Vattenfall's management will continue to face contradictory signals, leading to suboptimal capital allocation.

### 3.2 PESTEL Analysis: The External Environment

**Political:** The Tidö Agreement represents a decisive political shift toward nuclear power. A bill to amend Sweden's legislation on nuclear power was introduced to parliament, aiming to remove the current law limiting the number of reactors in operation to ten and allowing reactors to be built on new sites rather than just existing ones. This removes a legislative barrier to Vattenfall's nuclear ambitions but simultaneously signals that wind energy is no longer the political priority — creating a risk that Vattenfall's substantial wind investment pipeline (SEK 59 billion planned) lacks political backing.

**Economic:** Falling electricity prices in 2024 squeezed revenues even as profits rose through hedging. On the continent, the price decline was driven by lower gas and coal prices along with increased generation from wind and solar power, which also put downward pressure on electricity prices in southern Sweden. This price environment makes new capital-intensive investments (nuclear, offshore wind) harder to justify on merchant terms alone.

**Social/Geographic:** Northern Sweden (pricing zones SE1 and SE2) is currently a surplus area, while the south is in a deficit in terms of production relative to consumption, especially pricing zone SE4. This geographic imbalance means that new generation capacity and grid investment are not interchangeable: building more hydro in the north does not solve the supply deficit in the south where industrial demand is growing fastest.

**Technological:** Small modular reactors (SMRs) represent a potentially transformative technology. Vattenfall has shortlisted British Rolls-Royce SMR and American GE Hitachi Nuclear Energy for ongoing evaluation for new nuclear reactors at Ringhals; the company is also investigating the conditions for building large-scale reactors on the site. SMRs are relevant because their smaller capital commitment and modular deployment reduce the "all-or-nothing" risk of conventional large-scale nuclear, but they remain commercially unproven at scale.

**Environmental:** Vattenfall's CO2 emissions from its own operations have been reduced by 53% between 2017 and 2024. This progress supports the company's stated mission but also raises the bar: the remaining emissions are harder to eliminate, and the transition to fully fossil-free operations requires the very capital investments whose economics are currently challenged.

**Legal/Regulatory:** The development of an offshore wind sector in Sweden has been stifled by vetoes from competing interests, notably the Swedish Armed Forces. This regulatory barrier is directly responsible for Vattenfall's decision to pause its most advanced Swedish offshore wind project, as shown in Section 3.3 below.

### 3.3 Porter's Five Forces: Industry Attractiveness

**Threat of new entrants (moderate–high):** The liberalisation of European energy markets and falling costs of solar and wind have lowered entry barriers for new renewable generators, increasing competitive pressure on Vattenfall's power sales business.

**Bargaining power of buyers (increasing):** Large industrial customers — steelmakers, battery manufacturers, data centres — are increasingly able to negotiate long-term Power Purchase Agreements (PPAs) directly, bypassing spot markets and squeezing margins.

**Bargaining power of suppliers (high in offshore wind):** Vattenfall decided not to take an investment decision on Norfolk Boreas due to higher inflation and capital costs; the geopolitical situation has made offshore wind and its supply chain particularly vulnerable, with overall costs for Norfolk Boreas increasing by up to 40%. Supplier concentration in the offshore wind turbine and installation vessel market gives equipment manufacturers and contractors substantial pricing power, directly eroding project economics.

**Threat of substitutes (moderate):** For baseload generation, nuclear and hydro face limited substitutes in Sweden's specific context. For distributed energy services (heat pumps, EV charging), substitutes from third-party providers are growing.

**Competitive rivalry (moderate):** In the Nordic wholesale electricity market, Vattenfall competes with Fortum, Statkraft, and others. Its scale and diversified portfolio provide some insulation, but the market is price-competitive and increasingly commoditised.

**Analytical warrant:** The Five Forces analysis reveals that the most strategically threatening force is supplier power in offshore wind, which has already forced two major project cancellations or divestments. This finding connects directly to the investment strategy analysis in Section 3.5: Vattenfall cannot solve its wind problem through commercial negotiation alone; it requires either structural market reform or a pivot in technology mix.

### 3.4 Resource-Based View: Core Assets and Competitive Advantage

Barney's (1991) VRIN framework asks whether a firm's resources are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable. Applied to Vattenfall:

**Hydro and nuclear assets (VRIN-positive):** Vattenfall's total installed hydro power capacity of 8,800 MW generated 36.1 TWh of electricity in 2023; the combined installed capacity of nuclear power was 5,500 MW. These assets are rare (Sweden's geography is not replicable), inimitable (new large-scale hydro is essentially impossible to build in Sweden), and non-substitutable for dispatchable low-carbon baseload. They are Vattenfall's most durable source of competitive advantage.

**Operational expertise in nuclear and hydro:** Vattenfall's ambition is to be a world leader in the operation of both nuclear and hydro power with high safety and sustainability standards as well as cost-efficient fossil-free electricity production with high availability. This operational knowledge base — accumulated over decades — is tacit, path-dependent, and difficult for new entrants to replicate, satisfying the inimitability criterion.

**Wind development pipeline (partially VRIN-negative):** Wind development capabilities are valuable but not rare — competitors including Ørsted, RWE, and Iberdrola possess comparable capabilities. The recent project cancellations suggest these capabilities are not currently generating above-normal returns, which weakens their strategic value.

**Analytical warrant:** The RBV analysis identifies a strategic asymmetry: Vattenfall's strongest competitive advantages lie in its legacy hydro and nuclear assets, not in its offshore wind pipeline. This has direct implications for capital allocation — it suggests that deepening the nuclear advantage (through new build at Ringhals) is more strategically defensible than competing in a commoditised offshore wind market against better-capitalised rivals.

### 3.5 Dynamic Capabilities: Sensing, Seizing, and Reconfiguring

Teece, Pisano & Shuen (1997) argue that in rapidly changing environments, sustained competitive advantage requires dynamic capabilities — the ability to sense opportunities, seize them, and reconfigure the asset base accordingly. Vattenfall's recent strategic moves can be assessed against this framework.

**Sensing:** Vattenfall has correctly identified the demand opportunity. Vattenfall and Industrikraft have signed an agreement that allows the parties to progress in enabling new nuclear power on the Värö Peninsula at Ringhals, covering responsibilities, influence, and financing. This partnership demonstrates that Vattenfall has sensed the shift in political and market signals toward nuclear and is beginning to position accordingly.

**Seizing:** The seizing capability is constrained by the investment economics. Vattenfall has decided on an investment plan for 2025–2029 amounting to SEK 170 billion; growth investments total SEK 104 billion, with the largest share, SEK 59 billion, planned for investment in new wind power. The paradox here is striking: despite the political pivot toward nuclear and the demonstrated failure of offshore wind economics, the largest single growth investment category remains wind. This suggests a **seizing gap** — the company's formal investment plan has not yet fully reconfigured to match its own sensing of the changed environment.

**Reconfiguring:** The most significant reconfiguration signal is the pausing of offshore wind. Vattenfall has decided to pause the development of the offshore wind power project Swedish Kriegers Flak until further notice, due to unviable investment prerequisites in Sweden; the wind farm was estimated to generate 2.7 TWh of fossil-free electricity per year. While this is a necessary reconfiguration, it is reactive rather than proactive — the project was paused only after investment prerequisites became clearly unviable, not as part of a deliberate portfolio repositioning.

**Analytical warrant:** The dynamic capabilities lens reveals that Vattenfall is sensing change but seizing and reconfiguring too slowly. The investment plan's continued heavy weighting toward wind, even as wind project economics deteriorate and political support weakens, indicates organisational inertia. This connects to the principal–agent problem: management may be reluctant to write down the wind pipeline because doing so would require acknowledging a costly strategic error to the state owner.

### 3.6 Stakeholder Analysis

Using Mendelow's (1991) power–interest matrix, Vattenfall's key stakeholders can be mapped as follows:

| Stakeholder | Power | Interest | Implication |
|---|---|---|---|
| Swedish Government (owner) | Very High | Very High | Must be managed as co-strategist |
| Swedish Armed Forces | High | Moderate | Veto player on offshore wind |
| Industrial energy users (Industrikraft) | High | High | Potential co-investors in nuclear |
| Environmental NGOs | Moderate | High | Legitimacy risk around nuclear |
| Employees (esp. Germany) | Moderate | High | Just transition obligations |
| EU regulators | High | Moderate | Nuclear taxonomy, state aid rules |

The most analytically significant stakeholder conflict is between the Swedish Government and the Swedish Armed Forces. The development of Sweden's offshore wind sector has been stifled by vetoes from competing interests, notably the Swedish Armed Forces. This is a within-state conflict that Vattenfall cannot resolve unilaterally — it requires the government, as owner, to adjudicate between its defence priorities and its energy objectives. The IEA is explicit that a perceived rivalry between nuclear and wind is counterproductive to the overarching objective to achieve a diversified and secure fossil-free electricity system, where all clean energy sources will have a role to play in achieving net zero targets.

**Analytical warrant:** The stakeholder analysis reveals that Vattenfall's strategic room for manoeuvre in offshore wind is constrained not by commercial factors alone but by a regulatory veto that is ultimately a political problem. This means that commercial investment decisions (pause Kriegers Flak) are being driven by a governance failure (the state has not resolved the military–energy conflict), which loops back to the principal–agent diagnosis in Section 3.1.

### 3.7 Ansoff Matrix: Growth Strategy Assessment

Applying the Ansoff (1957) growth matrix to Vattenfall's current strategic choices:

- **Market penetration (existing products, existing markets):** Vattenfall is deepening its position in Swedish nuclear and hydro — its existing core assets in its home market. This is the lowest-risk strategic direction and aligns with its RBV strengths.

- **Product development (new products, existing markets):** SMR technology at Ringhals represents product development — a new nuclear technology deployed in Vattenfall's home market. Vattenfall and the Industrikraft consortium signed an agreement for joint investment and collaboration to promote development of new nuclear generating capacity in Sweden; Industrikraft will invest SEK 400 million as a first step. The risk is high given SMR technology immaturity, but the co-investment with industrial partners partially mitigates financial exposure.

- **Market development (existing products, new markets):** Vattenfall's European wind expansion (Netherlands, Germany, UK) was a market development strategy. The results were mixed: the UK Norfolk Boreas project was sold, and the German lignite operations produced a multi-billion-krona write-down. This historical evidence warrants caution about further geographic diversification.

- **Diversification (new products, new markets):** Vattenfall's expansion into EV charging infrastructure and heat pump services represents related diversification, leveraging its energy distribution networks. This is strategically coherent as a hedge against commoditisation of electricity generation.

**Analytical warrant:** The Ansoff analysis, combined with the RBV findings, supports a strategic recommendation to concentrate on market penetration and domestic product development (nuclear new build) rather than continued market development through offshore wind in foreign markets — the quadrant with the highest historical failure rate for Vattenfall.

## 4. Synthesis: The Core Strategic Problem

The analysis above converges on a single core problem: **Vattenfall is caught between a commercially rational portfolio pivot (toward nuclear, away from expensive offshore wind) and an investment plan that has not yet fully made that pivot, partly because the state owner has not resolved the policy contradictions that make the pivot necessary.**

Specifically:
1. The principal–agent analysis shows that ambiguous state objectives led to the German lignite disaster and continue to produce investment incoherence.
2. The PESTEL and Five Forces analyses show that offshore wind economics have deteriorated sharply while nuclear has received new political legitimacy.
3. The RBV analysis shows that Vattenfall's durable competitive advantages lie in nuclear and hydro, not wind.
4. The dynamic capabilities analysis shows that reconfiguration is happening too slowly.
5. The stakeholder analysis shows that the state has not resolved the military–energy veto that blocks offshore wind, yet has also not provided the risk-sharing model that would make nuclear economically viable.

A key policy priority is to ensure that Sweden's goal of 2.5 gigawatts of new nuclear power capacity by 2035 is accompanied by an efficient licensing and financial risk-sharing model. Without this, Vattenfall faces a situation in which its most strategically defensible investment (nuclear new build) is also commercially unviable without state support — and the state has not yet committed that support clearly enough to unlock the investment.

## 5. Recommendations

The following recommendations address the dual-mandate tension at both the corporate and ownership levels.

### Recommendation 1: The Swedish Government Must Formalise a Risk-Sharing Model for Nuclear — Immediately

**What:** The government should legislate a Contract for Difference (CfD) or equivalent risk-sharing mechanism for new nuclear capacity at Ringhals by 2026, providing revenue certainty over a 35-year horizon.

**Why:** Vattenfall has stated that a future investment decision for new nuclear will, among other things, require a reasonable risk-sharing model with the state; the company agrees that all fossil-free sources of energy will be needed for Sweden to reach 300 TWh by 2045, as set by the Swedish Parliament. Without a CfD, the capital cost and merchant price risk of new nuclear are too large for a single company to bear — even a state-owned one with an 8% ROCE target. The warrant is straightforward: you cannot ask a commercially mandated SOE to make a 35-year, multi-billion-krona investment on the basis of unpriced merchant risk. The state must either accept a lower return target or provide the price floor that makes the return achievable.

### Recommendation 2: Resolve the Military Veto on Offshore Wind Through an Inter-Ministerial Protocol

**What:** The Swedish government should establish a formal inter-ministerial arbitration process — involving the Ministry of Energy, the Ministry of Defence, and Svenska Kraftnät — to resolve military objections to offshore wind projects within a defined 12-month window.

**Why:** The IEA notes that the development of Sweden's offshore wind sector has been stifled by vetoes from competing interests, notably the Swedish Armed Forces. Vattenfall cannot invest in offshore wind if the regulatory pathway is blocked by an unresolved inter-agency conflict. The warrant: the military veto is a within-government problem that is being displaced onto Vattenfall's balance sheet in the form of stranded development costs and paused projects. The OECD's guidance that the state should act as a professional, active owner implies it must resolve internal contradictions before directing the SOE to act.

### Recommendation 3: Rebalance the 2025–2029 Investment Plan — Reduce Wind, Accelerate Nuclear and Grid

**What:** Vattenfall should reduce the SEK 59 billion wind allocation in its 2025–2029 plan by at least SEK 15–20 billion and redirect capital toward: (a) accelerating the Ringhals SMR feasibility and permitting process; (b) grid and transmission investment in southern Sweden (SE4) to address the supply deficit; and (c) increasing the hydro modernisation programme.

**Why:** The current plan's heavy wind weighting contradicts the RBV finding that wind is not a VRIN resource for Vattenfall, the Five Forces finding that offshore wind supply chains give contractors excessive pricing power, and the dynamic capabilities finding that the company is reconfiguring too slowly. Vattenfall is investing over SEK 700 million in Harsprånget, Sweden's largest hydro power plant by capacity with an annual production of about 2 TWh — this type of investment in existing, VRIN-positive assets should be scaled up, not crowded out by a wind programme whose economics have been demonstrated to be fragile.

### Recommendation 4: Adopt a Formal Dual-Mandate Reporting Framework

**What:** Vattenfall and its state owner should adopt a formal dual-mandate scorecard — reported annually to the Riksdag — that tracks both commercial KPIs (ROCE, FFO/net debt) and public-policy KPIs (TWh of fossil-free generation added, contribution to Sweden's 300 TWh target, CO2 reduction per SEK invested).

**Why:** The OECD's 2024 revised Guidelines emphasise the need for the state to clearly define the rationales and expectations for SOEs, especially with regard to their public policy objectives. Currently, Vattenfall is held accountable primarily through commercial financial targets, while its public-policy contribution is measured only in sustainability reports. A dual-mandate scorecard would make the trade-offs between commercial and public objectives explicit and auditable, reducing the principal–agent information asymmetry and enabling better-informed ownership decisions.

### Recommendation 5: Establish a Co-Investment Model with Industrial Partners for Nuclear

**What:** Vattenfall should expand the Industrikraft co-investment model — currently focused on SMR development — into a broader consortium that includes pension funds, the Swedish National Pension Fund (AP-fonden), and EU Green Deal financing instruments.

**Why:** Industrikraft was formed in June 2024 to support the expansion of Swedish electricity supply; as a first step, Industrikraft will invest SEK 400 million in the project, contributing resources and expertise in project management and technology selection. SEK 400 million is a meaningful signal of industrial commitment but is orders of magnitude below the eventual investment required for new nuclear capacity. Broadening the consortium to include institutional investors would de-risk the project for Vattenfall's balance sheet, align the interests of energy consumers (who need the electricity) with those of investors, and reduce the state's direct financial exposure — making the investment politically sustainable over the long parliamentary cycles that nuclear construction requires.

## 7. Conclusion

Vattenfall stands at a strategic inflection point. Its legacy hydro and nuclear assets are its most defensible competitive advantages; its offshore wind ambitions have been repeatedly undermined by supply-chain economics and regulatory vetoes; and its owner — the Swedish state — has sent contradictory signals by demanding both commercial returns and a fossil-free energy transformation, without providing the policy instruments (risk-sharing, military–energy arbitration, clear mandate definition) that would make both objectives simultaneously achievable. The path forward requires the state to act as the professional, active owner that the OECD Guidelines demand: resolving internal policy contradictions, providing a nuclear risk-sharing model, and holding Vattenfall accountable against a dual-mandate scorecard that makes the trade-offs visible. Without these governance reforms at the ownership level, Vattenfall's management will continue to face an unsolvable optimisation problem — and Sweden's 300 TWh ambition will remain a target without a credible delivery vehicle.
