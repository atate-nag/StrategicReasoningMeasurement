# Diageo plc: Navigating the Crossroads — A Strategic Case Study

## Strategic Situation, Analysis, and Recommendations

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Diageo plc, the world's largest premium spirits company by revenue, faces a defining strategic inflection point. After a decade of near-uninterrupted growth, the company has entered a period of simultaneous demand-side disruption (structural decline in alcohol consumption among younger cohorts), supply-side cost pressure (tariffs, agave price volatility), and regional concentration risk (over-dependence on a slowing North American market). Diageo delivered 1.7% organic net sales growth in fiscal 2025 — in a challenging year that was described as "in line with guidance." Yet that modest top-line figure masks a deteriorating profit picture: reported operating profit declined 27.8% and reported operating profit margin declined 819 basis points in fiscal 2025. This case study diagnoses the strategic situation using PESTEL analysis, Porter's Five Forces, a SWOT analysis, and the Ansoff Matrix, before developing a coherent set of strategic recommendations.

---

## 1. COMPANY BACKGROUND

Diageo was formed in 1997 through the merger of Grand Metropolitan and Guinness. Today it is a FTSE 100 company headquartered in London, with operations in over 180 countries. Its portfolio includes Johnnie Walker in Scotch whisky, Smirnoff in vodka, Baileys in liqueurs, Tanqueray in gin, Captain Morgan in rum, and Guinness in beer — brands that benefit from wide distribution, marketing support and consumer recognition. Don Julio and Casamigos have outgrown the US super-premium segment; tequila is among the highest-margin categories and rising as a share of group profit — US tequila value overtook US whiskey in 2023.

## 2. THE STRATEGIC SITUATION

The strategic problem is not cyclical; it is structural. Three interlocking forces define Diageo's challenge:

**First**, its most important market is weakening. North America is Diageo's largest division, representing more than a third of net sales. Yet solid organic net sales growth in Europe, LAC and Africa was offset by weakness in CWS impacting Asia Pacific results and softer performance in North America as US Spirits declined, reflecting weak consumer confidence.

**Second**, a structural demographic shift threatens the long-run demand base. The percentage of Millennials and Gen Z who consume alcohol is down 10% and 9%, respectively, since 2023; Gallup found that the overall US drinking rate is at a new low amid rising concerns over the health consequences of alcohol. From 1997 to 2023, at least 60% of Americans reported drinking alcohol; the figure fell to 62% in 2023 and to 58% in 2024, before reaching 54% in the most recent poll.

**Third**, external shocks — tariffs and currency volatility — amplify these structural headwinds. Assuming the current 10% tariff and no other changes, the unmitigated impact of these tariffs is estimated to be approximately $200 million on an annualised basis.

## 3. PESTEL ANALYSIS

PESTEL identifies the macro-environmental forces shaping Diageo's strategic context. The framework's value here lies not in listing factors but in tracing the *causal chain* from each force to Diageo's strategic position.

### 3.1 Political

Diageo has anticipated and planned for a number of potential scenarios regarding tariffs in recent months. The political risk is acute because Diageo's two highest-margin growth categories — tequila (produced in Mexico) and Canadian whisky (Crown Royal) — are precisely the products caught by US tariffs on Mexican and Canadian imports. This means the political environment directly erodes the margin contribution of Diageo's fastest-growing brands, compounding the demand-side problem. The geopolitical dimension extends to China: weakness in Chinese white spirits (CWS) in China negatively impacted group net sales by approximately 2.5% in Q1 of fiscal 2026.

### 3.2 Economic

The macroeconomic environment has produced a "cautious consumer" in Diageo's core developed markets. Organic net sales growth in fiscal 2024 was driven by resilient growth in Africa, Asia Pacific, and Europe, which offset the decline in North America, attributable to a cautious consumer environment and the impact of lapping inventory replenishment in the prior year. The economic squeeze is not uniform: Diageo's fiscal 2025 results underscored the diverging fortunes of its portfolio across developed and emerging markets, with overall organic sales rising 1.7%, growth disproportionately supported by emerging economies wherein both volume and price/mix trends were positive. This economic divergence is strategically significant: it implies that Diageo's growth engine is shifting geographically, but its capital allocation and operational infrastructure remain weighted toward mature markets.

### 3.3 Social

The sober-curious movement is the most consequential social trend facing Diageo. Thirty percent of Americans participated in Dry January in 2025, representing a 36% increase from 2024; purchases of spirits dropped 39%, wine fell 36%, and beer/cider/hard seltzer decreased 21% in January 2024 compared to the previous month. Crucially, this is not purely a cultural moment — it reflects a durable generational shift. A 2023 Gallup survey found that the share of adults under age 35 who say they ever drink dropped ten percentage points over two decades, to 62% in 2021–2023 from 72% in 2001–2003. The strategic implication is that Diageo cannot simply wait for the cycle to turn: the pool of future core consumers is structurally smaller than it was a decade ago.

### 3.4 Technological

Technology shapes Diageo's competitive position in two ways. First, digital marketing and data analytics are changing how brands recruit consumers — Diageo's investment in digital capabilities is part of its US route-to-market transformation. Diageo's investments in digital capabilities, supply chain, and its transformational route-to-market changes will be supportive in driving long-term sustainable growth, and early benefits from changes in the US route-to-market transformation are already being seen. Second, technology enables the no/low alcohol segment to produce more credible alternatives. According to an IWSR Drinks Market Analysis survey, non-alcoholic drinks are becoming mainstream in bars, supermarkets, and even at home, with 31% projected growth by 2024 across significant markets. Diageo has responded by extending its Ritual Zero Proof brand into RTDs, launching three Ritual Zero Proof RTD cocktails — Margarita, G&T, and Spritz — under the rationale that consumers should not have to compromise on flavour or experience.

### 3.5 Environmental

Climate change is a material operational risk for Diageo, not merely a reputational one. Climate-driven yield variability affected agave and barley prices in 2024–2025; tequila production costs rose materially amid significant agave price swings. Given that tequila is among Diageo's highest-margin categories, agave price volatility directly compresses the profit contribution of its most strategically important growth brands.

### 3.6 Legal

Regulatory risk operates at multiple levels. Alcohol advertising restrictions, minimum unit pricing legislation, and health labelling requirements all constrain Diageo's marketing freedom. Risk factors include changes in consumer preferences and tastes, as well as economic, political, social or other developments in countries and markets in which Diageo operates, including elevated geopolitical instability as a result of conflict in the Middle East. In India, the regulatory environment around alcohol advertising and state-level licensing creates barriers to growth even as the demographic opportunity is immense.

**PESTEL Summary Assessment:** The macro-environment is structurally adverse in Diageo's core developed markets (political tariff risk, social abstinence trend, economic consumer caution) but structurally favourable in emerging markets (economic growth, demographic expansion, rising middle class). This asymmetry is the central strategic tension that all subsequent analysis must resolve.

## 4. PORTER'S FIVE FORCES ANALYSIS

Porter's Five Forces identifies the structural profitability of the premium spirits industry and locates Diageo's competitive position within it. The framework is applied not as a static snapshot but to explain *why* industry profitability is under pressure and *where* Diageo is most exposed.

### 4.1 Threat of New Entrants — LOW to MODERATE

The premium spirits industry has high barriers to entry: aged inventory (Scotch requires minimum 3 years, often 12+), brand equity built over decades, and complex global distribution networks. Strong intangible assets and a cost advantage are at the heart of Diageo's wide economic moat; its portfolio contains 14 of the top 100 global premium distilled spirits brands. However, the craft spirits movement has lowered barriers in the sub-premium and artisanal segments, and the no/low alcohol category has near-zero production barriers. The threat is therefore low in Diageo's core premium-and-above segments, but moderate in the adjacent categories it needs to enter to address the sober-curious consumer.

### 4.2 Bargaining Power of Suppliers — LOW to MODERATE

Diageo's scale gives it significant purchasing power over most inputs. However, the agave supply chain is a specific vulnerability: tequila production costs rose materially amid significant agave price swings in 2024–2025, and Diageo cannot easily substitute away from agave in its tequila brands (Don Julio, Casamigos) without destroying brand integrity. Barley and grain suppliers are more fragmented and substitutable, keeping supplier power low in Scotch and vodka. The overall assessment is moderate, with a specific concentration risk in agave.

### 4.3 Bargaining Power of Buyers — MODERATE to HIGH

In the on-trade (bars, restaurants), large hospitality chains exercise meaningful leverage over listing decisions. In the off-trade (supermarkets, liquor stores), major retailers — particularly in the US, where three-tier distribution creates additional intermediary layers — can demand promotional spend and shelf-space fees. Diageo's US route-to-market transformation is partly a response to this: by building more direct commercial relationships and improving execution at the point of sale, Diageo reduces its dependence on distributor relationships. Early benefits from changes in the US route-to-market transformation are already being seen. The end-consumer's bargaining power is also rising as the no/low alcohol category provides genuine substitutes.

### 4.4 Threat of Substitutes — HIGH and RISING

This is the most strategically urgent force. Substitutes operate at two levels:

- *Within-alcohol substitution*: The sober-curious movement has gained unprecedented momentum, with spirits purchases dropping 39% in January 2024 compared to the previous month. Consumers are substituting spirits with beer, wine, hard seltzers, and RTDs — categories where Diageo has partial but not dominant coverage.

- *Out-of-category substitution*: Non-alcoholic beverages — from premium sparkling waters to functional drinks — are capturing the "occasion" that spirits previously owned. Non-alcoholic drinks are becoming mainstream in bars, supermarkets, and even at home.

The threat of substitutes is therefore not merely about losing share within spirits; it is about the entire category losing occasions to non-alcoholic alternatives. This elevates the strategic urgency of Diageo's no/low investments.

### 4.5 Competitive Rivalry — HIGH

The premium spirits industry is an oligopoly of scale players with intense brand-level rivalry. Pernod Ricard competes head-to-head with Diageo, Beam Suntory, and Bacardi across whiskies, vodka, cognac, and agave spirits, leveraging M&A, route-to-market depth, and heavy brand-building to sustain premium momentum. In specific categories, rivalry is intensifying: competition intensified in India's premium whisky segment in 2025 as both Diageo and Pernod Ricard competed aggressively. The rivalry dynamic is particularly dangerous for Diageo in the current environment because competitors are also investing heavily in marketing at a time when Diageo has been under pressure to cut costs.

**Five Forces Summary:** The industry is structurally attractive in its core premium-and-above segments (high barriers, low supplier power, strong brand moats) but is facing a structural deterioration in the threat of substitutes and intensifying rivalry. The implication is that Diageo must defend its premium positioning while simultaneously building credible positions in adjacent categories — a classic "defend and extend" strategic challenge.

## 5. SWOT ANALYSIS

The SWOT synthesises the internal and external analyses into a matrix that connects Diageo's capabilities to its strategic environment.

### 5.1 Strengths

**Brand portfolio depth and pricing power:** Diageo's brands benefit from wide distribution, marketing support, and consumer recognition, which help sustain volumes and pricing across different economic environments. Protected trademarks across the reserve portfolio and multiple industry awards in 2023–2025 for Johnnie Walker special releases, Don Julio, and Guinness strengthen pricing power and trade support.

**Scale and distribution:** Diageo's portfolio contains 14 of the top 100 global premium distilled spirits brands, giving it unparalleled leverage with global distributors and retailers.

**Emerging market footprint:** With nearly 40% of sales from emerging markets, Diageo has a diversified geographic base that is increasingly providing the growth offset to developed-market weakness.

**Cash generation:** Diageo generated strong free cash flow of $1.5 billion in fiscal 2025, up $0.5 billion year-on-year, providing strategic flexibility.

### 5.2 Weaknesses

**North American concentration and distribution inefficiency:** North America represents more than a third of net sales, and the US three-tier distribution system has historically limited Diageo's ability to respond quickly to market shifts. The ongoing route-to-market transformation acknowledges this structural weakness.

**Leverage:** Net debt as at 30 June 2025 was $21.9 billion, with a leverage ratio of 3.4x net debt to adjusted EBITDA, constraining Diageo's ability to make large acquisitions or weather a prolonged downturn.

**Scotch over-dependence in a declining premium tier:** In Diageo's results for the year ending 30 June 2024, Scotch sales declined by 10%, with blended whisky Johnnie Walker reporting a 6% drop and single malts slipping by 14%. Scotch remains Diageo's largest category by value, so this decline has an outsized impact on group performance.

**Marketing spend under pressure:** Diageo's marketing spending declined nearly 3% in 2024, compared to the previous year, at precisely the moment when brand investment is most needed to defend share and recruit new consumers.

### 5.3 Opportunities

**India's demographic dividend:** Within the next decade, some 150 million Indian consumers will enter the legal purchasing age for alcohol, and the Scotch market has seen rapid premiumisation, with lower price segments slowly shrinking. Diageo has already begun responding: recent capital deployment includes expanded manufacturing in Rajasthan to support luxury and premium demand.

**No/low alcohol and RTD growth:** Global scaling of Guinness, RTD expansion (Crown Royal RTD, Ketel One Botanical), and experiential taps aim to capture RTD growth projected to outpace total beverage alcohol.

**Premiumisation resilience:** Premiumisation is driving value growth as consumers reduce their drinking frequency yet willingly pay more for super-premium and ultra-premium tiers, allowing supplier revenue to rise even as total case volumes soften. This trend is particularly pronounced among consumers aged 18–34, with 54% actively choosing premium spirits.

**Africa's long-run growth:** Africa delivered organic net sales growth across all markets in fiscal 2025, led by Ghana, while Latin America and the Caribbean saw improving conditions in Brazil and premiumisation-led growth in Mexico.

### 5.4 Threats

**Structural decline in alcohol consumption:** The overall US drinking rate has fallen to 54% in the most recent poll, its lowest level in decades, representing a structural rather than cyclical threat to volume growth.

**Tariff exposure:** The unmitigated impact of current tariffs is estimated to be approximately $200 million on an annualised basis, directly eroding profitability in Diageo's highest-margin tequila and Canadian whisky categories.

**China CWS headwinds:** Weakness in Chinese white spirits in China negatively impacted group net sales by approximately 2.5% in Q1 fiscal 2026, and there is no near-term catalyst for recovery in that segment.

**Intensifying competition:** In a period where Pernod Ricard saw a 4% organic sales decline in H1 FY25, Diageo reported a 1% rise in organic sales, underscoring competitive pressures and differing market performances — but both companies are competing aggressively for the same shrinking pool of premium spirits occasions.

## 6. STRATEGIC ANALYSIS: THE CORE TENSION

The PESTEL, Five Forces, and SWOT analyses converge on a single core strategic tension: **Diageo's business model was optimised for a world of expanding alcohol consumption and premiumisation in developed markets. That world is changing faster than Diageo is adapting.**

The causal chain is as follows:

1. **Structural demand decline** in Diageo's core markets (US, UK) reduces the total addressable market for spirits, meaning volume growth must come from either market share gains or geographic expansion.

2. **Market share gains** are expensive in an oligopoly where rivals (Pernod Ricard, Beam Suntory, Bacardi) are equally well-capitalised and brand-invested — and Diageo has *reduced* marketing spend at precisely the wrong moment.

3. **Geographic expansion** into emerging markets (India, Africa) is the logical response, but Diageo's capital structure (leverage of 3.4x) and operational infrastructure are not yet fully optimised for this pivot.

4. **Tariffs and agave costs** compress the margin on the brands (Don Julio, Casamigos, Crown Royal) that were supposed to fund the geographic diversification.

5. **The no/low alcohol opportunity** is real but requires Diageo to cannibalise its own category — a classic innovator's dilemma — and its current investments (Ritual Zero Proof) are nascent relative to the scale of the opportunity.

This chain of reasoning frames the recommendations below.

## 7. ANSOFF MATRIX: GROWTH VECTOR ANALYSIS

The Ansoff Matrix provides a framework for evaluating Diageo's growth options across four quadrants: market penetration, product development, market development, and diversification.

### 7.1 Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets)

In North America, Diageo's primary near-term lever is execution improvement rather than strategic reinvention. Operational restructuring — creating standalone market teams to better capture regional growth opportunities — aims to boost responsiveness to consumer trends, with savings earmarked for "feet on the street" expansion in the US. This is the right short-term move: improving route-to-market execution in the US can recover market share lost to distribution inefficiency without requiring new capital. However, market penetration alone cannot solve the structural demand decline — it is necessary but insufficient.

### 7.2 Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets)

The RTD and no/low alcohol categories represent Diageo's most important product development opportunity. Diageo's spirits RTD portfolio grew net sales 17% organically in fiscal 2026 H1, demonstrating that this is not a speculative bet but a proven growth vector. Ritual Zero Proof is responding to growing demand for "convenient, flavourful non-alcoholic cocktails," though Diageo declined to provide sales data to support the claim, indicating that this segment is still in an early, investment phase. The strategic logic is clear: by owning the no/low occasion, Diageo captures consumer wallet share even as total alcohol consumption declines.

### 7.3 Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets)

India is the most compelling market development opportunity. Scotch accounts for just 13% of total whisky value in India, and penetration is low at 10%; Diageo claims to have recruited 10 million new consumers to Scotch, with some 150 million Indian consumers set to enter the legal purchasing age within the next decade. Diageo's 2025 expansion prioritises Asia-Pacific and Africa, with a major scale-up in India to capture premium Scotch and local craft spirit demand. This is the highest-return strategic option available, combining existing brand assets (Johnnie Walker, Black & White) with a structurally growing market.

### 7.4 Diversification (New Products, New Markets)

Diageo's foray into non-alcoholic RTDs in new consumer segments represents the highest-risk quadrant. The Ritual Zero Proof launch is a form of related diversification — leveraging existing brand-building capabilities in a new product category targeting a new (sober-curious) consumer. This is appropriate as a portfolio hedge but should not consume disproportionate capital relative to the more proven India and Africa opportunities.

## 8. RECOMMENDATIONS AND ACTION PLAN

The following recommendations are derived directly from the analytical chain above. Each recommendation states the problem it addresses, the evidence warranting it, and the expected outcome.

---

### Recommendation 1: Accelerate the US Route-to-Market Transformation and Reinstate Marketing Investment

**Problem addressed:** North American underperformance due to distribution inefficiency and declining brand salience among younger consumers.

**Evidence:** Diageo's marketing spending declined nearly 3% in 2024, yet marketing is the primary mechanism through which premium spirits brands recruit new consumers and defend pricing power. Simultaneously, early benefits from changes in the US route-to-market transformation are already being seen, confirming that execution improvement yields measurable results.

**Reasoning:** In a market where volume is under structural pressure, the only path to value growth is through price/mix improvement — i.e., premiumisation. Premiumisation requires brand investment. Cutting marketing spend to protect short-term margins destroys the brand equity that is Diageo's primary competitive asset. The Accelerate programme's cost savings should therefore be ring-fenced and reinvested in marketing, not distributed as margin improvement. Savings are earmarked for "feet on the street" expansion in the US, a market where Diageo's premium tequila (Don Julio) and other brands need stronger execution — this logic should be extended to brand-building spend.

**Action:** Redirect at least 60% of Accelerate programme savings toward US marketing and digital consumer engagement. Set a target of recovering marketing spend to 2022 levels (as a percentage of net sales) within 18 months.

---

### Recommendation 2: Pursue Aggressive Capital Reallocation Toward India and Sub-Saharan Africa

**Problem addressed:** Over-concentration in structurally declining developed markets; under-investment in structurally growing emerging markets.

**Evidence:** Diageo's fiscal 2025 results showed growth disproportionately supported by emerging economies, with both volume and price/mix trends positive in regions such as Africa. India's opportunity is structural: within the next decade, some 150 million Indian consumers will enter the legal purchasing age for alcohol, and the Scotch market has seen rapid premiumisation.

**Reasoning:** The Ansoff matrix analysis identifies market development (existing products in new markets) as the highest-return, moderate-risk growth vector available to Diageo. India combines three favourable conditions simultaneously: a growing middle class, a cultural shift toward Scotch as a status symbol, and low current penetration (10%). Full-year organic net sales rose by 8% in India in fiscal 2024, with Scotch boosted by Black & White (up 24%) and Johnnie Walker Blonde, confirming that the demand is real and the brands are resonating. Africa provides a parallel opportunity: Diageo's strategy in Africa is to grow its beer portfolio fast and its spirits brands faster, with smart investments through local production.

**Action:** Increase capital expenditure allocation to India and Africa by 25% over the next three years, funded by the proceeds of ongoing portfolio disposals (Pampero, Safari, Cacique, and the EABL transaction). Set explicit market share targets for India's premium Scotch segment.

---

### Recommendation 3: Build a Credible No/Low Alcohol Platform Through Acquisitions and Organic Investment

**Problem addressed:** Structural decline in alcohol consumption among Gen Z and Millennials; threat of substitution from non-alcoholic beverages.

**Evidence:** Thirty percent of Americans participated in Dry January in 2025, a 36% increase from 2024, with spirits purchases dropping 39% in that month. Non-alcoholic drinks are becoming mainstream in bars, supermarkets, and even at home, with 31% projected growth by 2024 across significant markets. Diageo's current response (Ritual Zero Proof RTDs) is directionally correct but insufficient in scale.

**Reasoning:** The Five Forces analysis identifies the threat of substitutes as "high and rising." The appropriate strategic response to a rising substitute threat is not to cede the category but to participate in it — thereby retaining the consumer relationship even as their drinking habits evolve. Diageo's spirits RTD portfolio grew net sales 17% organically in H1 fiscal 2026, demonstrating that consumers are receptive to Diageo-branded convenience formats. The same brand equity that drives premium spirits purchases can be leveraged in the no/low category. Failure to build this platform risks losing an entire generation of consumers to competitors (Athletic Brewing, Seedlip) who are building brand loyalty in the no/low space now.

**Action:** Acquire one or two established no/low alcohol brands with demonstrated Gen Z consumer traction (analogous to the Casamigos acquisition model in tequila). Allocate dedicated innovation budget to extend existing brands (Tanqueray 0.0, Gordon's 0.0) with premium positioning. Target 5% of group net sales from no/low alcohol within five years.

---

### Recommendation 4: Implement a Tariff Mitigation and Supply Chain Resilience Strategy

**Problem addressed:** US tariffs on Mexican and Canadian imports threatening the profitability of Don Julio, Casamigos, and Crown Royal.

**Evidence:** The unmitigated impact of current tariffs is estimated to be approximately $200 million on an annualised basis. Tequila production costs rose materially amid significant agave price swings in 2024–2025, compounding the tariff pressure on the same category.

**Reasoning:** Tariffs are a political risk that Diageo cannot eliminate, but it can mitigate their impact through supply chain restructuring and pricing strategy. The $200 million unmitigated impact represents approximately 1% of net sales — material but manageable if addressed proactively. The risk of inaction is that tariff costs are absorbed into margins, reversing the operating leverage that the Accelerate programme is designed to generate.

**Action:** Pursue a three-pronged mitigation strategy: (1) accelerate agave inventory hedging and long-term supply contracts to reduce cost volatility; (2) evaluate limited US-based production or bottling for Crown Royal to reduce Canadian import exposure; (3) implement selective price increases on Don Julio and Casamigos to pass through a portion of tariff costs, leveraging the brands' strong pricing power in the super-premium segment where consumers demonstrate a clear preference for premium products by reducing consumption frequency rather than compromising on quality.

---

### Recommendation 5: Pursue Disciplined Portfolio Rationalisation to Reduce Leverage and Fund Growth

**Problem addressed:** High leverage (3.4x net debt/EBITDA) constraining strategic flexibility; sub-scale brands consuming management attention without contributing proportionate growth.

**Evidence:** Net debt as at 30 June 2025 was $21.9 billion, with a leverage ratio of 3.4x net debt to adjusted EBITDA. Diageo has already begun this process: the disposals of Pampero, Safari, Cacique, and the Santa Vittoria production facility were completed in fiscal 2025.

**Reasoning:** The Accelerate programme's cost savings target of $625 million over three years is necessary but not sufficient to restore Diageo's financial flexibility. Reducing leverage to below 3.0x would provide the balance sheet headroom to pursue the India/Africa capital reallocation and no/low alcohol acquisitions recommended above. Portfolio rationalisation also sharpens management focus: a leaner portfolio of fewer, bigger brands is more consistent with Diageo's "premiumisation" strategy than a long tail of regional brands consuming disproportionate overhead.

**Action:** Identify and divest a further 8–10 sub-scale brands (targeting $1–1.5 billion in disposal proceeds) over the next 24 months. Apply proceeds to debt reduction, targeting a leverage ratio of 2.8–3.0x, which would restore M&A optionality.

## 9. COHERENCE CHECK: THE STRATEGIC LOGIC

The five recommendations form a coherent strategic programme, not a disconnected list. The logic chain is:

- **Stabilise** the core (Recommendation 1: US route-to-market and marketing investment) to stop the share erosion that is undermining confidence and cash generation.
- **Fund** the transformation (Recommendation 5: portfolio rationalisation) by converting sub-scale assets into financial flexibility.
- **Pivot** the growth engine (Recommendation 2: India and Africa capital reallocation) toward markets where the structural demand tailwind is favourable, leveraging existing brand assets in new geographies.
- **Hedge** the demand risk (Recommendation 3: no/low alcohol platform) by capturing the consumer relationship even as drinking habits evolve, addressing the structural threat identified in the social dimension of PESTEL and the substitutes force in Five Forces.
- **Protect** the margin (Recommendation 4: tariff mitigation) on the highest-margin categories to ensure that the above investments are funded by sustainable operating cash flow rather than debt.

## 10. CONCLUSION

Diageo's strategic situation in 2025 is serious but not terminal. The company believes in the attractive long-term fundamentals of its industry and in its ability to continue to outperform as the total beverage alcohol landscape evolves. That confidence is warranted in the long run: total beverage alcohol has grown at a 4.4% CAGR over the 10 years through to 2023, and international spirits has grown at 5.1% over the same period. Diageo possesses the brand assets, distribution scale, and emerging-market footprint to benefit from those structural tailwinds. What it lacks is the strategic clarity to act decisively across all five fronts simultaneously.

The analytical chain presented here leads to an unambiguous conclusion: Diageo must stop treating its North American decline as a temporary cyclical problem and start treating it as the leading indicator of a structural shift that requires a fundamental reorientation of capital, talent, and brand investment. The companies that win the next decade of the spirits industry will be those that successfully navigate the demographic transition — retaining the premium-seeking consumer while capturing the sober-curious one, and doing so in the markets where population growth and rising incomes create the most durable demand. Diageo has all the assets required to be that company. The question is whether it acts with sufficient urgency and strategic coherence to deploy them.
