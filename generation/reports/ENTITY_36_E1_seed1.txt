# SLB (Schlumberger): Navigating the Strategic Crossroads of Energy Transition and Digital Disruption

## A Strategic Case Study

---

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

SLB (formerly Schlumberger) occupies a paradoxical strategic position in the mid-2020s: it is simultaneously the world's largest oilfield services company and an organisation in the midst of a profound identity transformation. Having rebranded in October 2022, SLB articulated a "three engines of growth" strategy — Core Oil & Gas, Digital, and New Energy — designed to future-proof the business against the structural decline of fossil fuels while capturing near-term cash flows from a still-robust upstream investment cycle.

This case study argues that SLB's strategic situation is defined by a **dual-horizon dilemma**: the need to maximise returns from its dominant Core business in a cyclically uncertain environment, while simultaneously building credible, scaled positions in Digital and New Energy before the competitive window narrows. The analysis — drawing on PESTEL, Porter's Five Forces, a Resource-Based View (VRIN), SWOT synthesis, and the Ansoff Matrix — reveals that SLB possesses genuinely rare and inimitable capabilities in subsurface science and global project delivery, but faces mounting threats from oil price volatility, customer capex discipline, and the risk that its New Energy pivot remains too small and too slow to matter at scale.

The recommendations centre on four strategic imperatives: (1) deepening the digital platform moat through ecosystem lock-in; (2) accelerating production-phase service integration via the ChampionX acquisition; (3) making disciplined, focused bets in New Energy that leverage existing subsurface capabilities; and (4) restructuring the geographic revenue mix to reduce North American cyclicality.

## 2. COMPANY BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### 2.1 Origins and Evolution

Founded in 1926 by brothers Conrad and Marcel Schlumberger, the company pioneered electrical well-logging — the technique of measuring subsurface rock properties to locate hydrocarbons. With $36.3 billion in revenue for 2024 and 111,000 employees globally, the company's scale is immense.

The company's history is one of continuous technological reinvention: from wireline logging to seismic acquisition, from directional drilling to formation evaluation, and from hardware provision to software-defined services. SLB's vision is a strategic roadmap built on three core, actionable engines of growth: Core Oil and Gas, Digital, and New Energy — mandating market leadership in advanced digital and AI-powered solutions.

### 2.2 The 2022 Rebrand: A Strategic Signal

In October 2022, the company officially rebranded from "Schlumberger" to "SLB," retiring a 96-year-old name. The rebrand signalled a deliberate strategic repositioning — from a company defined by its oil and gas heritage to "a global technology company focused on driving energy innovation for a balanced planet." The new brand strategy positions SLB as a business leading the energy transition, building recognition that SLB goes beyond oil and gas services and is a technology company, clarifying their focus to drive energy innovation.

### 2.3 Current Financial Profile (FY2024)

In full-year 2024, year-on-year revenue increased by 10% and adjusted EBITDA grew by 12%, while SLB generated $3.99 billion in free cash flow, enabling it to return $3.27 billion to shareholders and reduce net debt by $571 million. Digital revenue of $2.44 billion grew 20% year on year, supported by close to 35% growth from Cloud, AI, and Edge technology.

The four reporting segments are: **Well Construction** (the largest by revenue at approximately 34%), **Production Systems** (~33%), **Reservoir Performance** (~20%), and **Digital & Integration** (~12%) — the fastest-growing by margin quality.

### 2.4 The ChampionX Acquisition (2024–2025)

In April 2024, SLB announced an all-stock agreement to acquire ChampionX Corporation. The acquisition strengthens SLB as a leader in the production space, with world-class production chemicals and artificial lift technologies, and combined portfolios will drive customer value through deep industry expertise and digital integration. The transaction closed on 16 July 2025, with former ChampionX shareholders now owning approximately 9% of SLB's outstanding shares.

## 3. THE STRATEGIC SITUATION

### 3.1 The Core Dilemma

SLB's central strategic challenge can be framed as a **dual-horizon dilemma** (Baghai, Coley & White, 1999). On Horizon 1, the Core Oil & Gas business generates the overwhelming majority of revenue and cash flow, but faces structural uncertainty as the energy transition accelerates. On Horizon 3, the New Energy business holds transformational promise but currently generates a fraction of total revenue. On Horizon 2, Digital represents the most immediately actionable growth engine but requires sustained investment and platform ecosystem development to achieve durable competitive advantage.

The strategic question is: **How should SLB allocate resources, capabilities, and management attention across three engines that operate on radically different time horizons, risk profiles, and competitive dynamics — while maintaining the financial returns necessary to satisfy investors in the near term?**

### 3.2 Compounding Pressures

The macroeconomic environment remains uncertain, with global upstream investment expected to decline compared to 2024. Tariff-related uncertainties pose potential risks to margins, particularly affecting imports of raw materials into the US and exports subject to retaliatory tariffs. Despite a challenging macro environment, SLB demonstrated resilience, with earnings estimates revised downward for 12 consecutive months through early 2025, reflecting market uncertainty about the pace and scale of the energy transition pivot.

## 4. EXTERNAL ANALYSIS: PESTEL

### 4.1 Political

**Geopolitical fragmentation** is the defining political reality for SLB. SLB faces significant challenges from international sanctions, particularly in Russia, where it was impacted by US and EU sanctions since 2022. Meanwhile, resource nationalism — with royalty hikes of 2–5 percentage points and local content requirements of 30–60% — compresses margins across key producing regions.

**Energy policy divergence** creates further complexity. New Energy relies on over $100 billion in CCS and hydrogen subsidies between 2020 and 2024, with SLB New Energy generating approximately $1.2 billion in revenue in 2024. The return of a pro-fossil-fuel administration in the US in 2025 introduced policy uncertainty for domestic clean energy investment.

### 4.2 Economic

**Oil price volatility** is the most direct economic risk. Price volatility, especially in global crude markets, is likely to increase as markets are buffeted by bearish risks of an economic slowdown or dwindling demand growth from China. The IEA projected 2024 oil demand at 102.4 million barrels per day, up 1.3% year-on-year, while the IMF trimmed 2024 global growth to 3.0%, pressuring capital expenditure decisions.

**Interest rates** matter for SLB both as a borrower and through their effect on customer investment decisions. Elevated rates in 2024 increased the cost of capital for E&P companies, contributing to capex discipline and moderating the pace of new project sanctioning.

### 4.3 Social

**Talent competition** intensifies as the technology sector competes with energy companies for data scientists, AI engineers, and software developers. SLB's ambition to become a technology company requires attracting talent that may not traditionally consider an energy services firm as an employer of choice.

**ESG investor pressure** is reshaping capital allocation. The global shift to renewables and decarbonisation targets threatens Schlumberger's oilfield service revenue and long-term growth, while rising costs from R&D, compliance, and digital transformation risk compressing margins.

### 4.4 Technological

Technology is the most dynamic dimension of SLB's environment. The global Artificial Intelligence in Energy Market is projected to reach $46.2 billion by 2031, growing at a CAGR of 17.18% from 2024 to 2031.

SLB and NVIDIA are building on their long-standing collaboration to develop generative AI solutions for the energy industry, building and optimising models to the specific needs and requirements of the data-intensive energy industry, including subsurface exploration, production operations, and data management.

The Lumi data and AI platform integrates advanced AI capabilities — including generative AI — with workflows across the energy value chain. The open, secure, and modular platform unlocks access to high-quality data across subsurface, surface, planning, and operations, increasing cross-domain collaboration and improving the quality and speed of decision-making.

### 4.5 Environmental

**Climate regulation** is tightening globally. SLB reported a 15% Scope 1 & 2 emissions intensity reduction versus 2022 and a 13% Scope 3 emissions intensity reduction, demonstrating operational progress.

**CCS and geothermal** are maturing rapidly. The global carbon capture and storage market is projected to reach $10 billion by 2025, while the market for hydrogen energy solutions is expected to exceed $200 billion.

### 4.6 Legal

SLB designed the Lumi platform to adhere to industry-leading standards, including the Open Group's OSDU Technical Standard for data liberation and the National Institute of Standards and Technology cybersecurity guidelines — addressing both legal compliance and competitive differentiation in data security.

## 5. INDUSTRY ANALYSIS: PORTER'S FIVE FORCES

Porter's Five Forces (1980) provides a structural analysis of the OFS industry's competitive dynamics.

### 5.1 Threat of New Entrants — LOW to MODERATE

The global scale of SLB forces complex logistics to move 200+ tonne rigs and hazardous materials to remote offshore and onshore sites, raising supplier importance. Specialist heavy-lift and hazardous-capable carriers in volatile regions wield higher bargaining power despite many providers. Beyond logistics, decades of R&D investment have produced deep intellectual property portfolios. The company invested $731 million in R&D for the twelve months ending June 30, 2025, creating a formidable knowledge barrier.

However, the **digital dimension** partially lowers entry barriers: technology companies can potentially offer software-defined services without the physical infrastructure of traditional OFS companies, representing a new category of entrant.

### 5.2 Bargaining Power of Buyers — MODERATE to HIGH

SLB's digital solutions, such as the Delfi™ digital platform and Lumi™ data and AI platform, are driving efficiency and enabling AI-driven decision-making across the energy industry — creating switching costs that partially offset buyer power. Nevertheless, the concentration of large NOC and IOC customers provides significant negotiating leverage, and customers can multi-source services across SLB, Halliburton, and Baker Hughes.

### 5.3 Bargaining Power of Suppliers — MODERATE

SLB's $36.3 billion revenue and volume gives it leverage over most commodity suppliers, but specialist heavy-lift and hazardous-capable carriers in volatile regions wield higher bargaining power. The ChampionX acquisition partially internalises the supply of production chemicals, reducing supplier dependence in this category.

### 5.4 Threat of Substitutes — MODERATE (rising)

In the Core business, direct substitution is limited. However, the global shift to renewables and decarbonisation targets constitutes a structural substitution threat to Schlumberger's oilfield service revenue and long-term growth. In the Digital segment, E&P companies can use general-purpose cloud platforms (AWS, Microsoft Azure) rather than SLB's proprietary Delfi/Lumi ecosystem.

### 5.5 Competitive Rivalry — HIGH

Key rivals include Halliburton and Baker Hughes, which offer a similar breadth of services across the oil and gas lifecycle, from exploration to production. These companies also invest heavily in technology and digital solutions to enhance their competitive positioning. Halliburton has a strong focus on North America and a significant market share, continuing to invest in technological innovations.

**Industry assessment**: The structural attractiveness of the OFS industry is **moderate and declining** in the Core business, but **potentially high** in digital and new energy adjacencies where competitive positions are still being established. This reinforces the strategic logic of SLB's three-engine model.

## 6. INTERNAL ANALYSIS: RESOURCE-BASED VIEW (VRIN) AND VALUE CHAIN

### 6.1 Resource-Based View: VRIN Analysis

The Resource-Based View (Barney, 1991) holds that sustained competitive advantage derives from resources and capabilities that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN).

| Resource / Capability | Valuable? | Rare? | Inimitable? | Non-substitutable? | Competitive Implication |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Proprietary subsurface data & IP** | ✓ High | ✓ Decades of accumulation | ✓ Path-dependent; cannot be replicated | ✓ No substitute for formation knowledge | **Sustained competitive advantage** |
| **Global operational network (100+ countries)** | ✓ High | ✓ Only 2–3 peers at this scale | ✓ Costly to build; regulatory complexity | Partial — local players substitute regionally | **Sustained competitive advantage** |
| **Digital platforms (Delfi, Lumi)** | ✓ Growing | Moderate — rivals building own | Moderate — switching costs rising | Partial — cloud providers could substitute | **Temporary → developing advantage** |
| **Domain expertise in AI/subsurface** | ✓ High | ✓ Scarce talent pool | ✓ Tacit knowledge; hard to codify | Partial — tech companies entering | **Temporary → developing advantage** |
| **Brand and NOC relationships** | ✓ High | ✓ Decades of trust | ✓ Relationship-specific; non-transferable | ✓ No substitute for trusted incumbent | **Sustained competitive advantage** |
| **New Energy capabilities** | Emerging | Low currently | Low | Low | **Option value only** |

SLB's robust international presence and digital innovation drive its market leadership. Strategic focus on digital, core, and new energy businesses positions SLB for long-term growth, with commitment to decarbonisation and new energy as key differentiators.

**Key insight**: SLB's most durable competitive advantages reside in its proprietary subsurface knowledge base, global operational reach, and long-standing NOC relationships — all deeply path-dependent and genuinely inimitable. Its digital capabilities are real but not yet fully inimitable; the strategic imperative is to deepen platform lock-in before rivals or technology entrants erode this position.

### 6.2 Dynamic Capabilities

Teece, Pisano & Shuen (1997) argue that in rapidly changing environments, competitive advantage comes from **dynamic capabilities** — the ability to sense, seize, and reconfigure resources. In early 2025, SLB completed its $7.8 billion ChampionX takeover, shifting from drilling services toward production chemistry and AI-driven recovery — demonstrating an ability to identify and capture strategic assets through M&A.

The 2022 rebrand and the restructuring of the business around three engines represents a deliberate **reconfiguration** of organisational identity and resource allocation. However, reconfiguration risk is significant: the transition from a services company to a technology company requires cultural and structural changes that are difficult to execute at scale.

### 6.3 Value Chain Analysis

Porter's Value Chain (1985) analysis identifies where SLB creates and captures value. SLB's value chain is undergoing a structural shift — from a **capital-intensive, labour-intensive field services model** toward a **data-intensive, platform-based technology model**. Over the past few years, customers have made investments to modernise their digital infrastructures, recognising the pivotal role that digital plays in increasing efficiency, enhancing production, and elevating performance. This is leading to visibly accretive revenue growth throughout SLB's Digital business, which remains on track to reach approximately $3 billion in Digital revenue annually by 2025.

The strategic challenge is managing this transition without destroying value in the Core business that funds it.

## 7. STRATEGIC POSITION: SWOT SYNTHESIS

### Opportunities

**SWOT strategic logic**: The analysis points toward a **SO (Strengths-Opportunities) strategy** of leveraging digital and subsurface capabilities to capture platform ecosystem and production-phase growth opportunities, combined with an **ST (Strengths-Threats) strategy** of using financial resilience and NOC relationships to weather cyclical and geopolitical volatility.

## 8. STRATEGIC OPTIONS: ANSOFF MATRIX

The Ansoff Matrix (1957) provides a framework for evaluating SLB's growth options across the dimensions of markets (existing vs. new) and products/services (existing vs. new).

### 8.1 Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets)

**Option**: Deepen market share in Core OFS services within the existing E&P customer base.

SLB's Production Systems segment is proud to see that most business lines contributed to strong performance, as the company continued to secure sizable bookings while also increasing its backlog for the future. The ChampionX acquisition deepens penetration of the production chemicals market within existing customer relationships.

**Assessment**: This is SLB's lowest-risk growth path and the primary source of near-term cash generation. However, it does not address the structural threat of energy transition and is subject to cyclical demand volatility. The Ansoff framework categorises this as the **lowest-risk strategy** — appropriate for cash generation but insufficient as the sole growth engine.

### 8.2 Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets)

**Option**: Extend Core OFS capabilities into geographies or customer segments not yet fully penetrated — particularly NOC markets in Africa, Southeast Asia, and Latin America.

International revenue grew 18% year on year in Q1 2024, with remarkable year-on-year growth of 29% in the Middle East & Asia, in addition to growth of 18% in Europe & Africa. SLB is well positioned to benefit from a recovery led by international markets, supported by sustained investment for oil capacity, gas expansion projects, and a constructive outlook for deepwater.

**Assessment**: Geographic market development is a **moderate-risk, moderate-return** strategy that leverages SLB's existing capabilities.

### 8.3 Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets)

**Option**: Develop and scale new digital and AI-powered products (Delfi, Lumi, generative AI models) for existing E&P customers.

Digital revenue is on track to reach approximately $3 billion in Digital revenue annually by 2025, led by new technology platforms comprising Cloud, AI, and Edge, which have grown at a high rate. SLB and NVIDIA announced they will build on their long-standing collaboration to develop generative AI solutions for the energy industry, building and optimising models to the specific needs and requirements of the data-intensive energy industry, including subsurface exploration, production operations, and data management — accelerating the development and deployment of industry-specific generative AI foundation models.

**Assessment**: This is SLB's **highest-priority near-term growth option**. It leverages existing customer relationships and domain expertise while building a new, higher-margin revenue stream. The risk is that rivals and technology companies develop competing platforms — making speed of ecosystem development critical.

### 8.4 Diversification (New Products, New Markets)

**Option**: Enter new energy markets (CCS, geothermal, hydrogen, energy storage, critical minerals) with adapted capabilities.

SLB's New Energy unit focuses on five areas: carbon capture and storage, geothermal and geoenergy, critical minerals, energy storage, and hydrogen. The unit is expected to bring in around $3 billion by the end of this decade and at least $10 billion by the end of the next decade by focusing on impact and scale.

CCS is one of the fastest-growing and most-immediate opportunities to reduce carbon emissions, and SLB is leveraging its domain expertise and deep knowledge of the subsurface to respond to increasing demand in storage solutions, while also expanding its carbon capture offering with the addition of Aker Carbon Capture, combining subsurface expertise with surface process engineering capabilities.

**Assessment**: Diversification carries the **highest risk** in the Ansoff framework. However, SLB's subsurface expertise provides a genuine **related diversification** advantage in CCS and geothermal — reducing the risk profile relative to pure conglomerate diversification. The strategic logic is sound; the execution challenge is scale and speed.

**Ansoff synthesis**: SLB's three-engine strategy maps directly onto the Ansoff matrix — Core (market penetration + market development), Digital (product development), and New Energy (related diversification). The coherence of this mapping validates the strategic logic, but also highlights the resource allocation tension: all four quadrants require investment simultaneously.

## 9. EVALUATING STRATEGY: SAFe CRITERIA

Johnson, Scholes & Whittington's SAFe criteria — **Suitability, Acceptability, and Feasibility** — provide a rigorous evaluation framework for SLB's strategic choices.

### 9.1 Suitability

*Does the strategy address the key opportunities and threats identified in the strategic analysis?*

The three-engine strategy is **highly suitable** in its overall architecture:
- **Core** addresses the near-term opportunity of sustained international upstream investment while managing cyclical risk through geographic diversification
- **Digital** addresses the technological disruption opportunity and the threat of disintermediation by building a proprietary platform ecosystem
- **New Energy** addresses the long-run structural threat of energy transition by building option value in adjacent markets

However, the balance of resource allocation is a suitability concern: with New Energy revenues still a small fraction of total, the strategy may be insufficiently weighted toward the long-run structural threat.

### 9.2 Acceptability

*Are the returns acceptable to key stakeholders, and are the risks manageable?*

- **Financial returns**: FY2024 results — 10% revenue growth, 12% EBITDA growth, $3.99B FCF, and $3.27 billion returned to shareholders — demonstrate that the current strategy is generating acceptable financial returns.
- **Investor acceptability**: The downward revision of earnings estimates for 12 consecutive months and a projected 14.7% decline in EPS for 2025 suggest investors are not yet fully convinced by the transition narrative, creating a credibility gap between digital/New Energy ambition and current revenue contribution.
- **Customer acceptability**: Customers have made investments to modernise their digital infrastructures, recognising the pivotal role that digital plays in increasing efficiency, enhancing production, and elevating performance, indicating strong customer receptivity to SLB's digital offerings.

### 9.3 Feasibility

*Does SLB have the resources and capabilities to execute the strategy?*

- **Financial feasibility**: With $3.99B in annual FCF and a strong balance sheet, SLB has the financial resources to fund both the ChampionX integration and continued Digital/New Energy investment. The all-stock structure of the ChampionX deal preserves cash, with SLB expecting to return $4 billion to shareholders in 2025 and anticipating realising approximately $400 million in annual pretax synergies.
- **Capability feasibility**: The Digital strategy is feasible given existing platform assets and the NVIDIA partnership. The New Energy strategy is partially feasible (CCS, geothermal leverage existing subsurface capabilities) but less so in hydrogen and energy storage, which require genuinely new competencies.
- **Organisational feasibility**: Managing three engines simultaneously across 100+ countries with 111,000+ employees is an enormous organisational challenge. The risk of strategic dilution — spreading management attention too thin — is real.

### Recommendation 1: Deepen the Digital Platform Moat Through Ecosystem Lock-In

**Strategic logic**: The VRIN analysis identifies SLB's digital platforms as currently providing a *temporary to developing* competitive advantage — valuable and increasingly rare, but not yet fully inimitable. The Five Forces analysis shows that the threat of technology disintermediation is real and growing. The Ansoff analysis identifies product development (Digital) as SLB's highest-priority near-term growth option.

**Recommended actions**:
1. **Accelerate the Lumi ecosystem**: Prioritise development of an open, interoperable API ecosystem that attracts third-party developers and creates network effects — making Lumi the "energy industry operating system." The Aker BP partnership — to co-develop a digital platform to digitally transform Aker BP's subsurface workflows, reducing costs, shortening planning cycles, and increasing production — is a model to replicate at scale.
2. **Deepen the NVIDIA partnership**: By combining NVIDIA Omniverse libraries and NVIDIA Nemotron open models with SLB digital and AI platforms, the collaboration aims to accelerate the transformation of data into actionable insights, spanning traditional machine learning, generative AI, and emerging agentic AI technologies designed to improve performance and support reliable, efficient, and lower-carbon energy systems. Expand this to co-create energy-specific AI agents embedded in customer workflows — creating behavioural switching costs.
3. **Target digital revenue of $5 billion by 2027**: The current trajectory (20% YoY growth, $2.44B in 2024) supports this ambition. Achieving it requires accelerating cloud migration among international NOC customers, where adoption lags IOCs.

**KPIs**: Digital revenue growth rate (target: sustained 20%+); number of active Delfi/Lumi platform users; recurring software revenue as % of Digital segment.

**Strategic logic**: The Five Forces analysis shows that buyer power is partially offset by integrated contract models that increase switching costs. The SWOT analysis identifies production and recovery growth as a key opportunity as E&P companies shift capex toward maximising recovery from existing assets.

**Recommended actions**:
1. **Execute ChampionX integration with speed and discipline**: Capture the approximately $400 million in targeted annual pretax synergies, while SLB expects to return $4 billion to shareholders in 2025, demonstrating that integration does not compromise financial returns.

Develop integrated "production optimisation as a service" offerings that combine chemicals, artificial lift, and Lumi AI-powered monitoring.
3. **Leverage North American footprint**: ChampionX's combination of production-focused solutions and customer relationships, particularly in North America, with SLB's international presence, is intended to create value for customers and stakeholders globally, providing a platform for revenue recovery when North American activity rebounds.

**KPIs**: ChampionX integration synergy realisation; production-phase revenue as % of total; North America revenue growth rate.

### Recommendation 3: Make Focused, Capability-Leveraging Bets in New Energy

**Strategic logic**: The Ansoff analysis identifies New Energy as a related diversification strategy — higher risk than market penetration or product development, but with genuine strategic logic given SLB's subsurface capabilities. The PESTEL analysis confirms that CCS and geothermal are the fastest-growing low-carbon markets most directly aligned with SLB's existing competencies.

**Recommended actions**:
1. **Concentrate New Energy investment on CCS and geothermal**: These are the two areas where SLB's subsurface expertise (formation evaluation, well construction, reservoir modelling) provides a genuine competitive advantage. The Jubail CCS hub partnership in Saudi Arabia — one of the world's largest, with a capacity to store up to nine million metric tons of CO2 annually — exemplifies the scale of opportunity.
2. **Apply the "billion-dollar opportunities only" discipline**: As stated by SLB New Energy President Gavin Rennick, the unit looks for two things: impact and scale. "If the scale is not there, it is not really an energy transition business." This discipline should be rigorously maintained to avoid diluting resources across too many nascent markets.
3. **Partner on hydrogen and energy storage**: Rather than building genuinely new competencies from scratch, SLB is developing solutions like low-carbon hydrogen through investments and collaborations with companies like John Cockerill Hydrogen and Genvia, a joint venture with the CEA (French Alternative Energy and Atomic Energy Commission), providing exposure without full capability investment.
4. **Set a credible New Energy revenue milestone**: A publicly committed target of $1 billion in New Energy revenue by 2027 would provide investor accountability and organisational focus — bridging the credibility gap identified in the SAFe acceptability analysis.

**KPIs**: New Energy revenue (target: $1B by 2027, $3B by 2030); number of CCS projects in operation; CO2 capture capacity under contract.

### Recommendation 4: Restructure the Geographic Revenue Mix to Reduce Cyclicality

**Strategic logic**: The PESTEL analysis identifies oil price volatility and North American land market cyclicality as key threats. The SWOT analysis identifies North American underperformance as a structural weakness. The Five Forces analysis confirms that international markets (particularly Middle East, deepwater) offer more structurally attractive competitive dynamics.

**Recommended actions**:
1. **Prioritise Middle East and Asia-Pacific**: Remarkable year-on-year growth of 29% in the Middle East & Asia in Q1 2024 demonstrates the momentum available from sustained NOC investment cycles and the deepwater complexity that favours SLB's integrated capabilities.
2. **Increase deepwater exposure**: SLB is well positioned to benefit from an activity rebound led by international markets, supported by sustained investment for oil capacity, gas expansion projects, and a constructive outlook for deepwater. Deepwater projects are structurally more complex, require more integrated service delivery, and generate higher margins than land drilling.
3. **Manage North American exposure actively**: Rather than attempting to grow North American market share in a cyclically challenged market, focus on maintaining margin discipline and using ChampionX's North American production chemicals business as a more stable, less rig-count-correlated revenue base.
4. **Pursue long-term integrated contracts**: Pursue multi-year performance-based contracts where SLB is compensated for production outcomes rather than activity levels, to reduce revenue cyclicality and align incentives with customer value creation.

**KPIs**: International revenue as % of total (target: 85%+); Middle East & Asia-Pacific revenue growth rate; deepwater revenue as % of Well Construction segment.

The VRIN analysis confirms that SLB's subsurface IP and NOC relationships constitute genuine, sustained competitive advantages that rivals cannot easily replicate. The Ansoff mapping validates the strategic logic of each engine. The SAFe evaluation confirms that the strategy is suitable and financially feasible — but highlights acceptability risks arising from the investor credibility gap and execution complexity.

The four recommendations — deepening the digital moat, maximising ChampionX integration, focusing New Energy on CCS and geothermal, and rebalancing toward international markets — form a coherent, sequenced action plan that addresses these execution risks while staying true to the three-engine model. Critically, they are **sequenced by time horizon**: the digital and ChampionX priorities are executable within 24 months and generate near-term returns; the New Energy and geographic rebalancing priorities are 12–48 month strategic plays that build long-run resilience.

SLB's strategic pivot from a legacy oilfield services giant to a diversified energy technology leader is becoming increasingly clear, but the transition is incomplete. The energy transition will not wait for the perfect strategy; it rewards those who act with conviction and speed. SLB has the capabilities, the financial resources, and the market position to be a defining company of the energy transition. The question is whether it can move fast enough.
