# AbbVie Inc.: Strategic Case Study
## *Navigating the Post-Humira Landscape — From Dependency to Diversification*

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

AbbVie Inc. stands at one of the most consequential inflection points in modern pharmaceutical history. Having built its identity around a single blockbuster drug — Humira (adalimumab) — the company now faces the full force of biosimilar competition, a $3.5 billion pipeline write-down, regulatory pricing pressure from the U.S. Inflation Reduction Act (IRA), and the imperative to transform into a genuinely diversified biopharmaceutical enterprise. Yet simultaneously, AbbVie has demonstrated remarkable strategic resilience, with successor drugs growing at extraordinary rates and management reaffirming high single-digit compound annual revenue growth through 2029.

---

## SECTION 1: COMPANY BACKGROUND

AbbVie was spun off from Abbott Laboratories in January 2013 with a focused mission: discover, develop, and commercialise advanced therapies. Its flagship product, Humira, generated a peak of **$21.2 billion** in global revenue in 2022, before biosimilar competition led to a significant decline. This singular commercial success, however, masked a dangerous strategic vulnerability — an over-reliance on a single product.

In 2020, AbbVie completed the landmark **$63 billion acquisition of Allergan**, and in 2024 alone, AbbVie significantly bolstered its portfolio through strategic acquisitions, including ImmunoGen for $10.1 billion, Cerevel Therapeutics for $8.7 billion, and Aliada Therapeutics for $1.4 billion.

In 2024, total net revenues were $56.334 billion, up 3.7%. The company employs approximately 50,000 people globally and is headquartered in North Chicago, Illinois.

---

## SECTION 2: THE STRATEGIC SITUATION — DEFINING THE PROBLEM

### 2.1 The Humira Patent Cliff

Humira's revenue dropped to $14.4 billion in 2023, down from the record $21.2 billion it made the year before, with an even steeper decline coming in 2024. U.S. Humira net revenues fell 39.9 percent in Q1 2024 alone, while internationally Humira net revenues were $499 million, a decrease of 15.8 percent. Ten biosimilars are now on the market, making this not merely a financial shock but an identity crisis for the company.

### 2.2 Pipeline Setback: The Emraclidine Failure

On November 11, 2024, AbbVie announced that its two Phase 2 EMPOWER trials investigating emraclidine as a once-daily, oral monotherapy treatment for adults with schizophrenia did not meet their primary endpoint. This resulted in a partial after-tax impairment charge of $3.5 billion related to the emraclidine intangible asset acquired as part of the Cerevel Therapeutics acquisition.

### 2.3 Regulatory and Pricing Pressure

The IRA introduced Medicare drug price negotiation, with Imbruvica's list price reduced from $14,934 to $9,319 per month — a 38% reduction effective 2026. This sets a precedent that could affect other AbbVie products in subsequent negotiation rounds.

### 2.4 Aesthetics Underperformance

Full-year global net revenues from the aesthetics portfolio were $5.176 billion, a decrease of 2.2 percent on a reported basis, with global Botox Cosmetic net revenues at $2.720 billion.

### 2.5 The Strategic Opportunity

Global Skyrizi net revenues grew 50.8 percent to $3.205 billion in Q3 2024, while global Rinvoq net revenues grew 45.3 percent to $1.614 billion in the same period. The company now expects combined Skyrizi and Rinvoq 2027 revenues of more than $31 billion, an increase of approximately $4 billion compared to previous guidance.

## SECTION 3: PESTEL ANALYSIS

### Political
AbbVie is subject to increasing public and legislative pressure with respect to pharmaceutical pricing, with practices of managed care organisations and governmental purchasers contributing to pricing pressures. Executive Order 14297, issued on May 12, 2025, directs the Secretary of Health to take further action on drug pricing, adding to regulatory uncertainty. Furthermore, any new or additional tariffs, particularly those targeting the pharmaceuticals industry, may increase uncertainties and could adversely impact AbbVie's business.

### Economic
Rising interest rates and macroeconomic uncertainty have dampened consumer spending on elective aesthetics procedures, directly impacting Botox Cosmetic and Juvederm revenues. The strong U.S. dollar has created headwinds for international revenues, with "operational" growth rates consistently outpacing reported rates.

### Socio-Cultural
An ageing global population drives increasing demand for immunology, oncology, and neuroscience therapies — all core AbbVie therapeutic areas. Growing awareness of mental health conditions creates significant long-term market opportunities in neuroscience. In 2024, AbbVie reported global Botox revenues of approximately $2.7 billion in aesthetics and $3.2 billion in therapeutic use — totalling about $6 billion, or roughly 11 percent of the company's overall revenue.

### Technological
The ADC platform brought in via the ImmunoGen acquisition represents a significant oncology opportunity. The AbbVie pipeline strategy sees the company at an inflection point, with the Cerevel portfolio including multiple CNS programmes. However, the $3.5 billion impairment from the failed schizophrenia drug emraclidine underscores R&D risks.

### Legal
Litigation matters resulted in $737 million in charges in full-year 2024. Patent protection remains the cornerstone of AbbVie's competitive moat, yet the Humira case illustrates the limits of even the most aggressive patent strategy.

## SECTION 4: PORTER'S FIVE FORCES ANALYSIS

### Force 1: Competitive Rivalry — HIGH
The top 10 pharmaceutical companies control approximately 55% of global pharmaceutical market share, with intense rivalry among top players. Johnson & Johnson's Stelara generated $10.4 billion in 2024, while Novartis's Cosentyx is a significant challenger. AbbVie's oncology drugs, such as Imbruvica and Venclexta, compete with offerings from Roche, AstraZeneca, and Merck. In the BTK inhibitor space, AbbVie executives acknowledged that Calquence had taken away market share in leukemia, particularly for treating patients who had not previously received other drugs.

### Force 2: Threat of New Entrants — MODERATE
High barriers to entry in branded pharmaceuticals limit the threat from entirely new players. However, the biosimilar segment represents a form of "new entry" that is structurally enabled by patent expiry — and AbbVie has experienced this acutely.

### Force 3: Threat of Substitutes — HIGH
Biosimilars are the most immediate substitution threat, as demonstrated by Humira's revenue collapse. In oncology, the rapid evolution of treatment protocols means that today's standard of care can quickly be displaced.

### Force 4: Bargaining Power of Buyers — HIGH AND INCREASING
The IRA has fundamentally altered the bargaining dynamic in the U.S. market. Imbruvica's negotiated price of $9,319 represents a significant reduction from the $14,934 list price. Pharmacy benefit managers, managed care organisations, and hospital systems all exert significant additional pricing pressure.

### Force 5: Bargaining Power of Suppliers — LOW TO MODERATE
AbbVie's scale and manufacturing capabilities mitigate supplier power in most areas, though talent in R&D and clinical development represents a critical "supplier" input where competition is intense.

**Key Insight:** AbbVie's most durable competitive advantages lie in its next-generation immunology drugs and its global commercial platform. The emraclidine failure reveals that CNS R&D — a domain entered primarily through acquisition — is not yet a proven core competence.
