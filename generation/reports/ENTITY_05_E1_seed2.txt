# **Bayer AG: A Harvest of Consequences**
### *Strategic Case Study: The Monsanto Acquisition and the Road to Recovery*

---

## **Executive Summary**

Bayer AG, the 160-year-old German life sciences conglomerate, finds itself at one of the most consequential strategic crossroads in its history. The 2018 acquisition of Monsanto — conceived as a bold leap to create the world's preeminent integrated agriculture company — has instead produced a cascade of financial, legal, and reputational crises that have destroyed tens of billions of euros in shareholder value. Compounded by a pharmaceutical patent cliff and an unsustainable debt burden, Bayer's strategic situation demands rigorous analysis. This case study applies Porter's Five Forces, the Resource-Based View (RBV), the Ansoff Matrix, and McKinsey's 7-S Framework to diagnose the situation, before arriving at a coherent set of strategic recommendations.

---

## **1. Background: The Company and the Deal**

Bayer AG, headquartered in Leverkusen, Germany, operates across three divisions: **Pharmaceuticals**, **Consumer Health**, and **Crop Science**. The acquisition of Monsanto increased Bayer's leverage, and the combination of litigation, restructuring, and investment needs has required careful financial management ever since.

Bayer completed its $63 billion acquisition of Monsanto in June 2018 — the largest in Bayer's history. Bayer had formally announced its intention to acquire Monsanto in May 2016. The strategic rationale was unambiguous at the time: adjusted for divestments, Bayer expected synergies to deliver annual contributions of $1.2 billion to EBITDA before special items as of 2022. Total projected synergies were even higher in the original deal documents: total synergies of approximately $1.5 billion after three years of merger were expected, along with additional integrated benefits.

The premium paid was extraordinary. Bayer paid an 18.6x EV/EBITDA multiple, and Monsanto shareholders were paid $128 per share, representing a 44 percent premium to the pre-announcement price. To finance the deal, Bayer aimed to raise $19 billion by issuing convertible bonds and new shares to existing shareholders, as well as a $57 billion bridge loan.

The strategic logic rested on three pillars: scale in seeds and crop protection, cross-selling between Monsanto's seed genetics and Bayer's chemical crop protection, and the integration of digital farming (Climate FieldView) to create an end-to-end precision agriculture platform. On paper, this was a textbook example of Ansoff's **diversification into related markets** — using Bayer's existing crop protection capabilities to enter the adjacent seeds and traits market at scale.

What followed was not the synergy story management had promised.

---

## **2. The Strategic Situation: A Multi-Front Crisis**

### 2.1 Legal and Financial Fallout

Shortly after the acquisition, Bayer found itself engulfed in a wave of litigation alleging that Roundup causes non-Hodgkin's lymphoma and other cancers. To date, Bayer has paid over $10 billion to settle a portion of these claims. As of October 2024, claimants in more than 100,000 Roundup lawsuits had reached settlements equalling approximately $11 billion, with more trials and settlements expected.

The financial drain is stark. Bayer's CFO Wolfgang Nickl stated that without litigation costs, the company would have generated about €5 billion of cash flow on average per year. The litigation has not merely cost cash — it has destroyed optionality. The deal has led to the company fighting myriad legal challenges in various U.S. courts over alleged negative health effects, including cancer, from exposure to Monsanto products including Roundup weed killer.

The market's verdict on the acquisition has been damning. Bayer shares fell by almost half since the buyout. At a 30% loss of share value since it closed, the deal stands as one of the worst, sitting alongside AOL's merger with Time Warner and Bank of America's acquisition of Countrywide. This situation brought to light two major problems for the German giant: a collapse in investor confidence, who now see the Monsanto acquisition as one of the worst strategic blunders in Bayer's history, and persistent legal and financial headwinds.

The goodwill write-downs have been relentless. Bayer recognised a goodwill impairment of approximately €2.5 billion based on anticipated market developments, particularly with respect to the glyphosate business. In Q3 2024, further impairment losses were recorded: Bayer wrote down goodwill of the Crop Science division in the amount of almost €3.3 billion in the third quarter of 2024 due to continued agricultural weakness.

By year-end 2024, net financial debt amounted to €32.626 billion as of December 31, 2024, down 5.4 percent from year-end 2023. The debt burden is so severe that the company intends to pay out the legal minimum in dividends in order to further reduce debt and gain greater flexibility.

### 2.2 The Pharmaceutical Patent Cliff

Bayer's pharmaceutical troubles compound the agricultural crisis. Bayer lost its US patent on Xarelto, its factor Xa inhibitor, in 2025, which alone accounted for an estimated €1 billion–€1.5 billion of lost 2025 revenues. The company is set to lose its patent on Eylea in 2027. The German group has been racing to offset a decline in revenue as blood thinner Xarelto and eye medicine Eylea lose patent protection, opening the company up to competition from cheaper generic versions.

The revenue gap from these expirations is acknowledged to be unfillable in the short term. Bayer's own pharma chief admitted that "No company in the world would be able to fill such a gap just at once."

### 2.3 Crop Science Competitive Pressure

Beyond litigation, Bayer's Crop Science division faces structural headwinds. Sales at Crop Science fell slightly compared to the prior year and earnings declined significantly in 2024. Pharmaceuticals generated sales growth on a currency- and portfolio-adjusted basis, but earnings were mainly held back by product mix and currency effects.

EBITDA before special items declined by 13.5 percent to around €10 billion in 2024. Core earnings per share, at €5.05, were significantly below the prior-year figure. Total group sales for 2024 came in at €46.6 billion, representing an increase of approximately 1 percent on a currency- and portfolio-adjusted basis.

## **3. Strategic Analysis**

### 3.1 Porter's Five Forces: Industry-Level Diagnosis

**Force 1 – Competitive Rivalry (HIGH)**

Bayer faces intense rivalry across all three divisions. In Crop Science, Bayer holds about 23 percent of the global seeds, traits and crop protection market, but is challenged by pure-play rivals such as Corteva, Syngenta, and BASF. The agricultural inputs industry underwent massive consolidation in the 2010s (Dow-DuPont, ChemChina-Syngenta), meaning all remaining players are large, well-capitalised, and fighting for share in a commoditising market. In Pharmaceuticals, rivalry is intensified by patent expiry: once Xarelto and Eylea lose exclusivity, generic and biosimilar entrants will structurally erode margins. In the Consumer Health segment, Bayer holds top-3 global OTC player status with strong No.1/No.2 category shares in EMEA and LatAm, but faces competition from Haleon (spun out of GSK/Pfizer), Johnson & Johnson, and private-label retailers.

**Force 2 – Threat of New Entrants (LOW–MODERATE)**

The barriers to entry in both pharmaceuticals and crop science are formidable — R&D costs, regulatory hurdles, and the need for global distribution networks are significant deterrents. Due to its differentiated product offerings as well as breakthrough innovation in consumer health, pharmaceuticals, and crop science, Bayer is well-versed in the industry where a new entrant might not be able to compete. However, the threat is evolving: AgTech start-ups and biotech firms increasingly challenge incumbents in precision agriculture and novel therapeutics. AgTech investments hit $10.5 billion in 2024, pressuring Bayer to scale digital spend to protect margins.

**Force 3 – Threat of Substitutes (MODERATE)**

In crop science, biological pest controls and gene-edited crop varieties represent emerging substitutes for conventional chemical herbicides — a direct threat to Roundup's franchise. In pharmaceuticals, the threat of substitutes is drug-specific: the threat of substitutes is low to moderate and is dependent on individual drugs. For drugs under patent protection, substitutes are limited; for off-patent products, generic substitution is near-total.

**Force 4 – Bargaining Power of Buyers (INCREASING)**

In 2024, online agricultural marketplaces and health information portals provided farmers with data on crop protection products, enabling them to benchmark Bayer's offerings against competitors. Similarly, healthcare providers and pharmacy benefit managers (PBMs) in the US wield growing negotiating power over drug pricing. The Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) in the US further constrains pharmaceutical pricing power for Medicare-covered products.

**Force 5 – Bargaining Power of Suppliers (MODERATE)**

Consolidation in global shipping and cold-chain logistics has cut Bayer's carrier options, raising supplier power; top 10 ocean carriers now control approximately 85% of capacity, enabling price hikes for temperature-controlled pharma and specialty chemical supply chains. Raw material suppliers for active pharmaceutical ingredients (APIs) also hold moderate power, particularly given geopolitical concentration of API manufacturing in Asia.

**Five Forces Synthesis:** The overall industry attractiveness for Bayer is **moderate to low** across its core segments. Competitive rivalry and buyer power are intensifying, while new entrant and substitute threats are rising in structural importance. This reinforces the need for Bayer to compete on differentiated, proprietary assets — precisely what the RBV analysis below examines.

### 3.2 Resource-Based View (RBV): What Does Bayer Actually Own?

The RBV (Barney, 1991) holds that sustainable competitive advantage derives from resources and capabilities that are **Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN)**. The Monsanto acquisition was, in RBV terms, an attempt to acquire a new bundle of VRIN resources — Monsanto's seed germplasm library, its Roundup Ready trait technology, and its FieldView digital platform. The question is whether those resources retain their VRIN properties.

| Resource/Capability | Valuable? | Rare? | Inimitable? | Non-substitutable? | Assessment |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Seed germplasm library (Monsanto) | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | Partially | Strong, but rivals have comparable libraries |
| Roundup / glyphosate franchise | ✓ (declining) | ✗ (generic) | ✗ | ✗ | Eroding; litigation undermines brand |
| Pharmaceutical R&D pipeline | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | Partially | Key asset; Nubeqa/Kerendia promising |
| Climate FieldView platform | ✓ | ✓ | Partially | Partially | Differentiating but not yet monetised at scale |
| Consumer Health brand portfolio (Aspirin, Claritin, Aleve) | ✓ | Partially | ✓ | Partially | Strong cash generation; undervalued in conglomerate |
| Global regulatory/distribution network | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | Partially | True VRIN; hard for entrants to replicate |
| R&D investment capability | ✓ | Partially | Partially | Partially | €5.5bn R&D spend (2024); constrained by debt |

The critical RBV insight is that **the Monsanto acquisition destroyed value not because the resources were not VRIN, but because the price paid exceeded the present value of those resources, and because a hidden liability (Roundup litigation) was catastrophically mispriced in due diligence.** Several critical lessons emerge from this high-profile case: Bayer appears to have underestimated the scale and durability of Monsanto's litigation risk.

Bayer's genuinely strong VRIN resources today are its **pharmaceutical R&D pipeline**, its **consumer health brand portfolio**, and its **seed germplasm assets**. These are the foundations on which any recovery strategy must be built.

In 2024, Bayer's R&D expenses were approximately €5.5 billion, reflecting its commitment to innovation. This focus supports future revenue and market leadership, especially in pharmaceuticals and crop science.

### 3.3 Ansoff Matrix: Mapping Bayer's Strategic Options

The Ansoff Matrix (Ansoff, 1957) maps growth strategies across existing/new products and existing/new markets. Bayer's current position and forward options can be mapped as follows:

**Current Position — Diversification (High Risk, Already Executed)**
The Monsanto acquisition was a classic diversification move — entering the seeds and traits market (new product category) while partially leveraging existing crop protection market relationships. As the matrix predicts, diversification carries the highest risk. Bayer is now living with the consequences of that risk materialising.

**Option A — Market Penetration (Low Risk)**
Bayer can deepen its position in existing markets with existing products. In Crop Science, this means growing market share in corn and soybean seeds in the Americas. In Pharmaceuticals, it means accelerating uptake of Nubeqa (darolutamide for prostate cancer) and Kerendia (finerenone for chronic kidney disease). Growth in the pharmaceutical unit should return in 2027, with margins starting to expand in 2028, led by Nubeqa and Kerendia, which together should bring in sales of significant scale. Nubeqa is on track to reach blockbuster status.

**Option B — Product Development (Moderate Risk)**
Bayer is pursuing product development most aggressively in pharmaceuticals. Bayer submitted regulatory filings for a third indication for darolutamide in prostate cancer and a new indication for finerenone in patients with heart failure, reinforcing Bayer's leadership in these therapeutic areas. In Crop Science, the development of biological alternatives to chemical herbicides represents a product development strategy to address regulatory and reputational headwinds around glyphosate.

**Option C — Market Development (Moderate Risk)**
Expanding Consumer Health into high-growth emerging markets (India, Southeast Asia, Sub-Saharan Africa) represents a market development opportunity. Opportunities include expanding into emerging markets where OTC self-medication is growing as healthcare access improves and middle classes expand. Bayer's brand equity in Aspirin, Aleve, and Claritin translates across geographies.

**Option D — Diversification (High Risk — to be avoided)**
Given the financial constraints imposed by debt and litigation, further diversification is strategically contraindicated. Bayer must consolidate, not expand its scope.

**Ansoff Synthesis:** The matrix points clearly toward **market penetration and product development** as the priority strategies, with selective market development in Consumer Health. Diversification should be avoided until the balance sheet is repaired. This is consistent with CEO Bill Anderson's stated strategic priorities.

### 3.4 McKinsey 7-S Framework: Internal Alignment Analysis

The 7-S Framework (Peters & Waterman, 1982) examines whether an organisation's hard elements (Strategy, Structure, Systems) are aligned with its soft elements (Shared Values, Skills, Staff, Style). Misalignment between these elements is a leading cause of strategic failure.

| Element | Current State | Alignment Assessment |
|---|---|---|
| **Strategy** | "Enhance Performance, Regain Flexibility" — focus on pipeline, debt reduction, DSO | Partially clear; split-up question unresolved creates ambiguity |
| **Structure** | Transitioning to Dynamic Shared Ownership (DSO); flattening hierarchy | Improving; legacy bureaucracy being dismantled |
| **Systems** | Legacy ERP and divisional reporting systems; FieldView digital platform | Fragmented; integration between divisions remains weak |
| **Shared Values** | "Health for all, Hunger for none" mission | Strong mission statement; credibility damaged by Roundup litigation |
| **Skills** | Deep R&D expertise in pharma and crop science; digital farming capabilities | Strong in science; weaker in litigation management and M&A integration |
| **Staff** | ~100,000 employees; ~12,000 job cuts underway | Talent uncertainty during restructuring; risk of losing key R&D staff |
| **Style** | Moving from hierarchical German corporate culture to more agile, empowered model | Significant cultural transition underway; execution risk |

The most critical misalignment is between **Strategy and Structure**. For years, Bayer operated with what CEO Anderson described as 12 layers of management between the CEO and customers. Anderson said that the 12 layers of management between himself and customers were "simply too much" and added that by the end of 2024, the company would remove multiple layers of management and coordination.

The response has been the **Dynamic Shared Ownership (DSO)** model. The Bayer Group introduced DSO worldwide in January 2024, which reduces hierarchies, eliminates bureaucracy, streamlines structures, and accelerates decision-making processes. DSO was introduced by CEO Bill Anderson in January 2024, though the groundwork began in mid-2023. Anderson's diagnosis was blunt: Bayer had become too slow, too bureaucratic, and too far removed from its customers.

The financial results of DSO are beginning to materialise. The Dynamic Shared Ownership (DSO) operating model has already delivered €500 million in savings in 2024, with €800 million expected in 2025 and a goal of €2 billion by 2026.

However, the 7-S analysis reveals a risk: **restructuring creates staff uncertainty that can damage the very skills and capabilities that constitute Bayer's VRIN resources.** Bayer's restructuring is rooted in reducing overhead to free capital for high-impact initiatives. By eliminating approximately 12,000 roles — primarily in management and coordination — the company is betting heavily on its DSO model. If this restructuring inadvertently removes critical scientific or regulatory talent, it could undermine the pharmaceutical pipeline that is central to the recovery strategy.

### 3.5 Strategic Logic Chain: How the Crises Connect

The three crises — litigation, patent cliff, and debt — are not independent; they form a **mutually reinforcing negative feedback loop**:

```
Monsanto acquisition (2018)
        ↓
Roundup litigation → Cash outflows (~$11bn+ settlements)
        ↓
Debt burden (€32.6bn net debt) → Constrained R&D/investment capacity
        ↓
Weaker pharma pipeline investment → Patent cliff impact amplified
        ↓
Earnings pressure → Goodwill impairments → Share price decline
        ↓
Reduced strategic flexibility → Inability to pursue M&A or split-up
        ↓
[Loop reinforces itself]
```

Breaking this loop requires **sequencing** — addressing debt and litigation first to restore strategic flexibility, then deploying that flexibility toward pipeline investment and, ultimately, portfolio restructuring.

## **4. Strategic Recommendations**

The following recommendations are derived directly from the analytical frameworks above. They are sequenced in order of priority and time horizon.

### **Recommendation 1: Resolve the Litigation Overhang (Immediate Priority)**

**Rationale (RBV + Five Forces):** The litigation liability is the single largest destroyer of Bayer's strategic capacity. It consumes cash, management attention, and investor confidence. Until it is resolved, Bayer's VRIN resources cannot be deployed effectively, and the company's cost of capital remains elevated.

**Action:** Bayer should pursue a **comprehensive legislative or judicial resolution** of the Roundup litigation. Bayer has been lobbying Congress to pass legislation protecting the company from lawsuits claiming glyphosate in Roundup causes cancer and has attempted to change state laws to avoid further litigation losses. The proposed class settlement represents the most viable path. Bayer's proposed $7.25 billion class settlement for US claims received preliminary approval from the 22nd Judicial Circuit Court of Missouri, representing a significant step toward resolution. Management should prioritise final approval and fund the settlement through asset disposals rather than operating cash flow, to preserve investment capacity.

**Caveat:** This recommendation must be balanced against Bayer's stated belief in the scientific safety of glyphosate. Bayer has maintained that "glyphosate is safe" and has named ongoing Roundup-related litigation a top priority. Settling does not require conceding the science — it reflects a rational financial calculation.

---

### **Recommendation 2: Accelerate Pharmaceutical Pipeline to Bridge the Patent Cliff (Short-Term, 1–3 years)**

**Rationale (Ansoff — Product Development; RBV):** Bayer's pharmaceutical R&D pipeline is its most valuable VRIN resource. The patent cliff on Xarelto and Eylea creates a revenue gap that cannot be filled overnight, but the emerging portfolio of Nubeqa, Kerendia, elinzanetant, and acoramidis provides a credible bridge — if investment is sustained.

Regulatory filings are underway for a third indication for darolutamide in prostate cancer and a new indication for finerenone in patients with heart failure, reinforcing Bayer's leadership in these therapeutic areas. Bayer expects a low- to mid-single-digit percentage sales decline for its pharma division in 2024, followed by an immediate return to growth in 2025.

**Action:** Ring-fence the pharmaceutical R&D budget from cost-cutting pressures. The DSO restructuring savings (€2 billion target by 2026) should be **explicitly reinvested** in late-stage clinical programmes and regulatory submissions, not distributed to shareholders. Bayer should also deepen its partnership with Recursion Pharmaceuticals: Recursion delivered multiple data packages to Bayer and initiated the first joint oncology project, expected to advance rapidly towards Lead Series nomination, with Bayer becoming the first external beta-user of Recursion's AI-powered drug discovery engine. This AI-augmented discovery capability could accelerate pipeline replenishment at lower cost than traditional in-house R&D.

---

### **Recommendation 3: Monetise the Consumer Health Division (Medium-Term, 2–4 years)**

**Rationale (Ansoff — Portfolio Rationalisation; 7-S — Strategy/Structure alignment):** Consumer Health is a profitable, cash-generative business with strong brand equity. However, it is strategically misaligned with Bayer's core life sciences identity. As to separating pharma and consumer health from agriculture, CEO Anderson said "our answer is 'not now' — and this shouldn't be misunderstood as 'never.'"

The strategic logic for separation is compelling. Consumer Health competes in a different value chain from Pharmaceuticals and Crop Science — it requires mass-market brand management, retail distribution, and consumer marketing capabilities, not R&D-intensive drug development. Keeping it within Bayer creates a conglomerate discount: the market values diversified conglomerates below the sum of their parts.

At least one activist investor has been pushing for the company to split up. Executives considered this option during a broader strategic review but ultimately concluded it would take up too many resources without fixing the underlying problems. This reasoning is sound *for the immediate term* — executing a split while managing litigation and a patent cliff would be operationally overwhelming. However, once litigation is resolved and the pharma pipeline is stabilised (2026–2027), a **partial IPO or trade sale of Consumer Health** would unlock significant value and reduce debt.

**Action:** Commission a formal separation readiness assessment for Consumer Health in 2025, targeting execution in 2026–2027 once the balance sheet has improved. Potential acquirers include Haleon, Reckitt, or a private equity consortium. Proceeds should be directed entirely to debt reduction, targeting net debt below €25 billion.

---

### **Recommendation 4: Rebuild Crop Science Around Digital and Biological Assets (Medium-Term)**

**Rationale (RBV; Ansoff — Product Development):** The glyphosate-centric model is structurally challenged — by litigation, by regulatory tightening in the EU, and by the long-term shift toward biological and regenerative agriculture. Bayer's competitive advantage in Crop Science must be rebuilt around assets that are genuinely VRIN: seed germplasm, the FieldView digital platform, and biological crop protection.

Climate FieldView exceeded 220 million subscribed acres in 2024, enabling cross-sell of seeds and crop protection and supporting agtech monetisation. This platform is a genuine differentiator — no competitor has a comparable integrated digital farming ecosystem at this scale. The strategic imperative is to **deepen the FieldView moat** by integrating AI-driven agronomic recommendations, expanding internationally, and converting free users to paid subscriptions.

Bayer's mission includes developing sustainable agricultural solutions that support farmers and contribute to global food security, with regenerative agriculture forming a core pillar. Biological crop protection (microbials, biopesticides) is the fastest-growing segment of the agricultural inputs market and directly addresses the reputational and regulatory risks created by Roundup. Bayer should accelerate its biologicals portfolio through targeted bolt-on acquisitions — small enough not to strain the balance sheet, but strategically meaningful.

---

### **Recommendation 5: Complete the Organisational Transformation (Ongoing)**

**Rationale (7-S Framework):** The DSO model is the right structural response to Bayer's bureaucratic legacy, but cultural transformation is the hardest and slowest element of the 7-S model to change. The risk is that the organisation reverts to hierarchical behaviours once the immediate pressure of restructuring subsides.

DSO shifts decision making closer to the work, reduces bureaucracy, accelerates innovation through cross-functional, mission-led teams, driving higher impact and performance. To embed this permanently, Bayer must align its **performance management systems** (the "Systems" element of 7-S) with the new model — rewarding speed, cross-functional collaboration, and customer outcomes rather than hierarchical compliance.

Since rolling out the new operating model, the company has significantly reduced the number of hierarchy layers in the organisation, with the number of management positions having been roughly halved. This is meaningful progress, but the cultural shift — from a German industrial conglomerate to an agile, mission-driven life sciences company — will take years to complete and requires consistent leadership commitment.

## **5. The Coherent Strategic Logic**

The five recommendations above are not independent; they form a **sequenced recovery chain**:

| Phase | Timeline | Priority | Outcome |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Phase 1: Stabilise** | 2024–2026 | Litigation resolution + DSO savings | Cash flow normalisation; debt reduction |
| **Phase 2: Rebuild** | 2025–2027 | Pharma pipeline acceleration + FieldView expansion | Revenue bridge across patent cliff |
| **Phase 3: Restructure** | 2026–2028 | Consumer Health separation | Debt elimination; strategic focus |
| **Phase 4: Grow** | 2027+ | Biologicals M&A + pharma launches | Return to profitable growth |

The logic chain is explicit: **you cannot restructure the portfolio until the balance sheet is repaired; you cannot repair the balance sheet until litigation is resolved; you cannot resolve litigation without cash; and you cannot generate cash without protecting the pharmaceutical pipeline.** Everything flows from litigation resolution.

## **6. Conclusion**

Bayer's predicament is a textbook study in the consequences of strategic overreach. The Monsanto acquisition — conceived as a diversification masterstroke — violated three fundamental strategic principles: it paid an excessive premium (destroying value from day one), it failed to price a known but underestimated liability (Roundup litigation), and it created a debt burden that constrained the company's ability to respond to subsequent shocks (the pharmaceutical patent cliff).

The frameworks applied here converge on a consistent diagnosis. Porter's Five Forces reveals intensifying competitive pressures that demand differentiated, proprietary assets — not scale for its own sake. The RBV shows that Bayer's genuine VRIN resources lie in its pharmaceutical pipeline, its seed germplasm library, and its FieldView digital platform — not in the commoditising glyphosate franchise. The Ansoff Matrix prescribes market penetration and product development over further diversification. And the 7-S analysis reveals that structural and cultural misalignment has compounded the financial crisis.

CEO Bill Anderson is asking investors to bear with the German conglomerate through one more challenging year before a turnaround effort starts showing significant results. That turnaround is achievable — but only if the company executes the recommendations above with discipline and in the right sequence. Bayer has the assets, the science, and the global reach to recover. What it needs now is strategic clarity, financial discipline, and the courage to make the portfolio decisions it has so far deferred.
