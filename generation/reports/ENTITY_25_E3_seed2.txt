# MTN Group: From Connectivity Titan to Platform Business — Navigating Currency Risk, Competitive Pressure, and the Fintech Pivot in Pan-African Telecoms

## A Strategic Case Study

---

## PART I — CASE OVERVIEW AND CONTEXT

### 1.1 The Strategic Situation

MTN Group is Africa's largest mobile network operator by subscriber base. MTN Group, which has 288 million customers across 18 markets in Africa, has been navigating a period of considerable economic turbulence. More precisely, by end-2024, MTN ended FY 2024 with a subscriber base of 291 million, excluding the markets it exited in the year, representing net additions of 6.2 million customers amid ongoing SIM registration regulations.

The company's strategic situation is defined by a fundamental paradox: MTN is simultaneously experiencing robust operational growth and severe reported financial losses. Data revenue decreased by 12% on a reported basis, but increased by almost 22% in constant-currency terms. Fintech revenue increased by 11% on a reported basis, but by almost 29% in constant currency. This divergence between reported and constant-currency performance is not a minor accounting technicality — it is the central strategic problem. The reason for the divergence is the catastrophic depreciation of the Nigerian naira, MTN's single largest market.

MTN Nigeria reported a loss before tax of R9 billion (US$508 million) in the six-month period ended June 30, 2024, compared with a restated profit before tax of R8.3 billion ($464 million) a year earlier. For the full year, MTN Nigeria reported a N400.44bn loss after tax for the year ended December 31, 2024, as the devaluation of the naira drove up foreign exchange losses and weighed on the company's earnings. The loss represents a 192 per cent increase from the N137.02bn loss recorded in 2023.

This case study analyses the strategic choices MTN faces as it attempts to transition from a traditional mobile network operator (MNO) into a diversified digital platform business — and asks whether that transition can adequately insulate it from the macroeconomic volatility that has come to define operating in Africa.

---

### 1.2 Industry Context: The African Telecoms Market

The African telecoms sector presents a paradox of scale and constraint. On the demand side, growth is structural and compelling. Mobile internet penetration in Sub-Saharan Africa reached 27% by the end of 2023, yet a substantial usage gap of 60% remains. The mobile industry's $140 billion contribution to GDP could unlock $170 billion by 2030 if the digital divide is bridged. Mobile internet penetration is forecast to reach 33% by the end of the decade (576 million users), up from 28% in 2024 (416 million users).

On the supply side, structural constraints are severe. Infrastructure remains a challenge for African telcos, with profitability limited by high costs and low levels of infrastructure sharing. Unlike their global counterparts that have embraced cost-sharing agreements to improve efficiency and reduce costs, African telcos operate in fragmented markets with high costs. Energy costs compound this: a base station in a remote rural area costs, on average, 35 to 40% more for an operator to run than in a city, with the increased cost relating to higher energy costs that come from having to use more diesel where power supply and grid access is unavailable.

The competitive landscape is concentrated. Five telecom giants are battling it out in this market: Orange, MTN, Maroc Telecom, Vodafone, and Airtel. In Nigeria specifically, MTN led with 84.6 million subscribers, representing a 51.4% market share, and approximately ₦3.94 trillion ($2.63 billion) in revenue, while Airtel followed with 56.6 million subscribers (34.4%) and ₦2.64 trillion ($1.76 billion).

Mobile money has become the sector's defining growth vector. Africa processed 65% of global mobile money transaction value in 2024, or $1.1 trillion. The continent also led in volume, accounting for nearly 82 billion of the 108 billion global transactions. Approximately 108 billion transactions totalling over $1.68 trillion were processed through mobile money accounts in 2024.

## PART II — ANALYTICAL FRAMEWORKS

### 2.1 Porter's Five Forces: Industry Structure Analysis

**Force 1 — Threat of New Entrants: LOW**

The barriers to entry in Pan-African telecoms are formidable. Spectrum licensing, tower infrastructure, regulatory approvals, and the capital required to build national networks create near-insurmountable entry costs. Operators are expected to invest US$77 billion in networks between 2024 and 2030 — a figure that effectively prices out all but the largest global players. The incumbency of MTN, Airtel, and Orange across multiple markets, each with established agent networks and brand recognition, further raises the barrier. This low threat of new entry is strategically significant: it means MTN's competitive threat comes primarily from existing rivals rather than disruptive newcomers, which shapes the nature of rivalry below.

**Force 2 — Bargaining Power of Suppliers: MODERATE-HIGH**

MTN's key suppliers include tower companies, equipment vendors (Ericsson, Huawei), and energy providers. The proposed acquisition of IHS Towers illustrates how supplier power has become strategically problematic. IHS has faced challenges in diversifying its revenue base, with MTN continuing to account for a significant majority (~70%) of its African revenues. This mutual dependency — MTN is IHS's dominant customer, yet depends on IHS for critical infrastructure — created a supplier relationship that MTN sought to resolve through vertical integration. By integrating tower ownership, MTN expects to improve cost predictability, eliminate tower rental expenses, and increase third-party tenancy across sites. The logic is clear: converting a supplier relationship into an owned asset reduces supplier bargaining power and improves long-run cost structure.

**Force 3 — Bargaining Power of Buyers: MODERATE**

African telecom subscribers have moderate but growing bargaining power. Switching costs are low in markets with number portability, and price sensitivity is high given low average revenues per user. Duopolies in Kenya and Egypt intensify competition, compressing ARPU as operators use loyalty programs to retain customers. However, buyers' power is constrained by network quality differences and by the ecosystem lock-in that mobile money creates — once a subscriber's financial life is embedded in MoMo, switching to a rival network carries significant friction costs. This is why MTN's fintech strategy is not only a revenue diversification play but also a customer retention mechanism.

**Force 4 — Threat of Substitutes: MODERATE AND RISING**

The primary substitutes for traditional voice and SMS revenue — WhatsApp, over-the-top (OTT) messaging apps — have already structurally eroded that revenue stream. This is why data and fintech have become the strategic priorities. In fintech, the substitute threat comes from standalone mobile money providers (M-Pesa), neobanks, and increasingly from global payment platforms. With approximately 835 million registered mobile money accounts in SSA in 2023, telco operators are leveraging their infrastructure to offer financial services — but this same growth attracts non-telco fintech entrants. The threat of substitution thus creates urgency around MTN's fintech differentiation strategy.

**Force 5 — Competitive Rivalry: HIGH**

Rivalry among incumbents is intense. On the continent, there has been a natural consolidation of telecoms. Between the operators, their lobby, and institutions like the African Union, no one is calling for major sector consolidation. The result is sustained multi-player competition. In Nigeria, MTN and Airtel are engaged in a direct battle for market share, with both companies investing heavily in network quality and fintech ecosystem development. Active sharing agreements announced by MTN, Airtel, Orange and Vodacom all suggest that further action needs to be taken beyond utilisation of tower companies and satellite operators. The willingness of rivals to share infrastructure signals that competition has shifted from network coverage (a hygiene factor) to digital services and fintech ecosystems (the new battleground).

**Five Forces Summary:** The industry structure is moderately attractive — high entry barriers protect incumbents, but intense rivalry, rising OTT substitutes, and high supplier costs compress margins. The strategic implication is that MTN cannot compete on connectivity alone; it must build switching costs through ecosystem depth, which points directly to the fintech pivot.

### 2.2 Resource-Based View (RBV) and VRIN Analysis

The RBV framework, originating with Barney (1991), holds that sustained competitive advantage derives from resources that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN). RBV posits that a firm's sustained competitive advantage is derived from possessing valuable, rare, inimitable, and non-substitutable (VRIN) resources.

Applying VRIN to MTN's resource base reveals the following:

**Network Infrastructure — Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, but Partially Substitutable**

MTN's 16-country network, built over three decades, is genuinely rare and difficult to replicate quickly. MTN's continued capital investment of R30 billion to strengthen the quality and capacity of its networks enabled it to capture the opportunities in data and fintech across its markets, with data traffic accelerating by a third as data subscribers grew by 8% to 158 million active users. However, infrastructure is increasingly becoming a shared commodity through tower-sharing agreements. This means the inimitability of network assets is eroding, and competitive advantage must be sought elsewhere.

**Brand — Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, Non-substitutable**

MTN is described as having "Africa's most valuable brand" in its own investor communications. MTN's competitive advantage stems from its scaled fintech platform, GSM customer base, and Africa's most valuable brand. Brand trust is particularly critical in mobile money, where users must trust the platform with their financial lives. This is a genuinely inimitable resource: Airtel and Orange cannot replicate thirty years of MTN brand-building.

**MoMo Platform and Agent Network — Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, Non-substitutable**

This is MTN's most strategically significant VRIN resource. MTN has an ambitious plan to grow to 100 million active MoMo users, 2 million agents, 3 million merchants, and US$750 billion transaction value in 2025, realising this vision by rapidly expanding its partner ecosystem through integration. The combination of 66 million active MoMo users (as of H1 2024), a vast agent network, and a Mastercard partnership creates a platform that is genuinely difficult for rivals to replicate quickly. In 2023, external validation arrived: Mastercard agreed to invest $200 million for a minority stake in MTN Group Fintech at a $5.2 billion valuation. This valuation signals that the market recognises MoMo as a VRIN resource — the $5.2 billion fintech valuation is embedded within a company whose total market capitalisation has been under severe pressure from forex losses, suggesting the market believes the fintech asset is worth more than the current stock price implies.

**Data Assets and Customer Relationships — Valuable, Rare, Inimitable**

Combining data wealth and a scaled mobile wallet, MTN currently facilitates US$100 million worth of loans per month. The proprietary data on 291 million subscribers' transaction behaviour, location patterns, and financial habits is a resource that cannot be replicated by a new entrant. This data underpins MTN's ability to offer credit scoring, micro-loans, and personalised digital services — the basis for the platform business model it is building.

**RBV Conclusion:** MTN's sustainable competitive advantage rests primarily on its MoMo platform, brand, and proprietary data — not on its network infrastructure, which is becoming commoditised. This conclusion directly motivates the fintech pivot: the company is, rationally, attempting to extract more value from its genuinely inimitable resources.

### 2.3 Dynamic Capabilities Framework

The static RBV is insufficient to explain how MTN should navigate a volatile, fast-changing environment. A significant criticism of the RBV was its static nature, as it struggled to explain how firms could sustain competitive advantage in dynamic environments. Dynamic Capability Theory was initially introduced by Teece and Pisano (1994) and further elaborated by Teece, Pisano and Shuen (1997).

Dynamic capabilities — the ability to sense opportunities, seize them, and reconfigure assets — explain MTN's strategic evolution more fully. MTN has demonstrated three dynamic capabilities:

1. **Sensing:** Identifying mobile money as the defining growth opportunity before rivals fully committed. MTN launched MoMo in 2009 and has continuously evolved it from P2P transfers to a full financial platform. For MTN, the pandemic was a turning point. The company leveraged the opportunity to expand its offerings, moving beyond simple peer-to-peer transfers. Today, MoMo supports bill payments, microloans, cross-border remittances, and increasingly commerce.

2. **Seizing:** Structurally separating the fintech business to unlock independent valuation and attract strategic investors. MTN undertook a deliberate restructuring by licensing standalone MoMo entities like MobileMoney Ltd in Ghana (2022) and MTN Mobile Money Uganda Ltd (2021), preparing for regulatory independence and future financial innovation.

3. **Reconfiguring:** Pivoting from a pure connectivity business to a three-platform model. MTN Group announced Ambition 2030, which streamlines its execution approach into three principal platforms of choice for consumers, homes and businesses: Connectivity; Fintech; and Digital Infrastructure.

The dynamic capabilities lens reveals that MTN's strategic challenge is not simply to exploit existing resources but to continuously reconfigure them in response to macroeconomic shocks — most critically, the forex crisis in Nigeria.

### 2.4 Porter's Generic Strategies

Porter's framework identifies three generic competitive strategies: cost leadership, differentiation, and focus. Porter's model posits that businesses should concentrate on one strategy to maintain a clear identity and competitive edge.

MTN's historical strategy was broad-scope differentiation: competing across all segments in all markets on the basis of network quality, brand, and service breadth. The fintech pivot represents a deepening of differentiation — building a financial services ecosystem that rivals cannot easily replicate.

However, MTN faces a structural tension. In connectivity (its core), it must compete on cost efficiency to maintain margins in low-ARPU markets. In fintech, it must compete on innovation and ecosystem depth. These are not naturally complementary strategies, and managing them simultaneously risks the "stuck in the middle" trap Porter warned against.

The resolution of this tension is visible in MTN's Ambition 2030 architecture: treat connectivity as a scale-efficiency platform (approaching cost leadership through infrastructure sharing and capex discipline) while pursuing differentiation through the fintech and digital infrastructure platforms. Ambition 2030 targets high-teens revenue growth, margin expansion, and disciplined capital allocation across Connectivity, Fintech, and Digital Infrastructure. This bifurcated strategy — cost discipline in the base, differentiation at the platform layer — is strategically coherent, but only if the forex problem is resolved.

## PART III — THE CORE STRATEGIC PROBLEM: FOREX EXPOSURE AS EXISTENTIAL RISK

The analytical frameworks above converge on a single, urgent problem: MTN's operational strengths are being systematically negated by currency risk concentrated in Nigeria.

**The Mechanism:** MTN Nigeria's costs — tower leases, equipment, and debt — are denominated in or indexed to US dollars, while revenues are earned in naira. When the naira depreciates, dollar-denominated costs balloon in naira terms, creating forex losses that overwhelm operating profits.

MTN Nigeria disclosed that forex losses surged to N925 billion, up from N740 billion in 2023, as the naira depreciated from N907/$1 in December 2023 to N1,535/$1 by the end of 2024. Despite achieving record naira revenues, MTN Nigeria's record revenue of N3.36 trillion for 2024 was overshadowed by a loss after tax of N400.44 billion.

**Why this is a strategic, not merely financial, problem:** The forex loss is not a one-off shock. MTN's ability to adapt to regulatory changes while managing the economic pressures of currency devaluation and inflation is pivotal to its strategy for stabilising and eventually increasing its revenue streams. If forex losses recur, they will:

- Constrain MTN's ability to invest in network capex, slowing its 5G rollout and data capacity expansion — precisely the capabilities it needs to grow data revenue.
- Undermine the fintech business model in Nigeria, where MoMo PSB has already struggled (losing 2.5 million active wallets in 2024 as part of a strategic reset).
- Damage investor confidence in the Group, raising the cost of capital and making the MoMo spinoff valuation harder to defend.

The forex problem thus creates a vicious cycle: losses reduce investment capacity, which reduces competitive position, which reduces future revenues, which worsens the loss position. Breaking this cycle is MTN's most urgent strategic priority.

**Regulatory Dimension:** The Nigerian Communications Commission's approval of a 50% tariff hike in early 2025 was a partial response. The NCC recognised the financial pressures faced by Nigerian households and businesses and mandated that operators implement adjustments, with the approved adjustment aimed at addressing the significant gap between operational costs and current tariffs. Tariff rates in Nigeria had remained constant since 2013, despite increased operating costs for telecom companies. While the tariff hike improves naira revenue, it does not address the underlying dollar-cost problem — it merely reduces the gap. Telecom executives noted that the 50% tariff hike will not fully address challenges, as operational costs have surged by 120%.

## PART V — RECOMMENDATIONS AND ACTION PLAN

The following recommendations are derived directly from the analytical chain above. Each recommendation addresses a specific finding from the frameworks and is warranted by the evidence.

### Recommendation 1: Accelerate Naira Revenue Localisation and Dollar-Cost Reduction (Urgency: Immediate)

**The Problem (from Section III):** MTN's forex losses are structural, not cyclical. The naira has depreciated from N461/$ in 2022 to N1,535/$ by end-2024 — a 233% depreciation in two years. The US dollar appreciated by 97% against the naira in 2023 alone, resulting in a closing rate of N907/$ at the end of the year. The trajectory is unlikely to reverse fully.

**The Recommendation:** MTN should pursue a two-track localisation strategy:

*Track A — Revenue-side:* Accelerate the tariff normalisation process in Nigeria, using the NCC's 2025 approval as a foundation for further adjustments indexed to inflation. The 50% hike is a start, but operational costs have surged by 120%, meaning further adjustments are commercially justified and regulatorily precedented.

*Track B — Cost-side:* Renegotiate tower lease contracts with IHS (and, post-acquisition, as the tower owner) to introduce naira-denominated or hybrid pricing. The proposed IHS acquisition is strategically sound precisely because it converts a dollar-indexed operating cost into a controllable asset. MTN expects to improve cost predictability and eliminate tower rental expenses — this cost localisation is the most direct hedge against forex risk available.

**The Warrant:** The RBV analysis shows that MTN's fintech and brand assets are genuinely valuable. The forex losses are destroying the financial returns from those assets without destroying the assets themselves. Reducing dollar-cost exposure is therefore the highest-priority action to allow MTN's real competitive strengths to generate reported financial returns, not just constant-currency gains.

---

### Recommendation 2: Accelerate MoMo Spinoff and Use Proceeds to Deleverage and Fund Capex (Urgency: High)

**The Problem (from Section II.2):** MTN's MoMo platform is valued at $5.2 billion by Mastercard's investment, but this value is trapped inside a listed entity whose share price is depressed by forex losses. The market is not pricing the fintech asset correctly because it is obscured by the connectivity business's problems.

**The Recommendation:** MTN should proceed with the MoMo spinoff across all viable markets (building on the Ghana and Uganda precedents) and sell up to the 30% stake that management has indicated it is willing to divest. MTN Group remains open to selling up to 30% of its thriving fintech arm amid soaring transaction volumes.

The proceeds should be allocated to: (a) reducing dollar-denominated Group debt to lower forex exposure; (b) funding the IHS tower acquisition; and (c) accelerating 4G/5G capex in high-growth markets where the usage gap remains large.

**The Warrant:** The dynamic capabilities framework shows that MTN has already demonstrated the "seizing" capability — it has successfully restructured MoMo into standalone entities. The next step is to monetise that restructuring. The Mastercard partnership provides both capital and commercial validation. The partnership is set to launch a new era of collaboration to connect millions of people and small businesses across Africa with digital tools to transact through secure mobile payments. Selling a minority stake now, while the $5.2 billion valuation is established, is preferable to waiting for a full IPO in a volatile market.

---

### Recommendation 3: Deepen Infrastructure Sharing to Reduce the Cost of Connectivity (Urgency: Medium-High)

**The Problem (from Section II.1, Force 2):** High infrastructure costs are a structural drag on margins across all African markets, not just Nigeria. Rural deployment is particularly expensive. A base station in a remote rural area costs 35 to 40% more to run than in a city.

**The Recommendation:** MTN should expand its active network sharing agreements with Airtel Africa beyond the current Rwanda, Congo-Brazzaville, and Zambia pilots. The prospective agreements are expected to cover passive sharing (tower structures, power systems, security infrastructure) and active sharing (antennae, baseband units, and transport backhaul links). Active sharing — sharing radio access network (RAN) equipment — delivers far greater cost savings than passive sharing alone.

Simultaneously, MTN should accelerate the transition from diesel to solar power at rural sites. Renewable energy systems enable faster and more cost-effective expansion into underserved areas. This addresses both the cost problem and the coverage gap opportunity simultaneously.

**The Warrant:** The Five Forces analysis identifies high supplier costs (energy, tower leases) as a key profitability constraint. Infrastructure sharing reduces these costs without sacrificing competitive position in the areas that matter most — fintech and digital services. The usage gap of 60% represents millions of potential MoMo users who cannot be reached without affordable network coverage. Reducing the cost of rural coverage therefore directly expands the addressable market for MTN's highest-margin, most inimitable asset: the MoMo platform.

---

### Recommendation 4: Implement a Structured Forex Risk Management Programme (Urgency: Immediate)

**The Problem:** MTN currently has no effective hedge against naira depreciation. The forex losses of N925 billion in 2024 are not offset by any financial instrument, making reported earnings hostage to Central Bank of Nigeria policy.

**The Recommendation:** MTN should implement a three-layer forex risk management programme:

- *Layer 1 — Natural hedging:* Increase the proportion of local-currency debt in Nigeria and other high-risk markets. Local-currency borrowing means interest and principal payments are made in naira, matching the currency of revenue.
- *Layer 2 — Operational hedging:* Accelerate the localisation of supply chains — sourcing equipment from local or regional suppliers where possible, and denominating contracts in local currencies.
- *Layer 3 — Financial hedging:* For residual dollar exposures (unavoidable equipment imports, for example), use forward contracts and options to cap downside exposure. While hedging facilities across much of the continent are either not available or prohibitively expensive, the scale of MTN's balance sheet means it can access instruments unavailable to smaller operators.

**The Warrant:** The core strategic problem (Section III) is that forex losses are destroying the financial returns from MTN's genuinely valuable operational assets. The RBV analysis shows the assets are strong; the problem is purely financial. A structured hedging programme does not eliminate forex risk but reduces its magnitude to a manageable level, allowing reported earnings to better reflect operational performance. This is essential to restore investor confidence and support the share price needed to execute the MoMo spinoff at a favourable valuation.

---

### Recommendation 5: Deepen the Fintech Ecosystem to Build Switching Costs and ARPU (Urgency: Medium)

**The Problem (from Section II.1, Force 3):** Buyer bargaining power is moderate and rising. Low switching costs in connectivity mean subscribers can and do move between operators. MTN's long-run competitive moat depends on creating financial ecosystem lock-in.

**The Recommendation:** MTN should accelerate the build-out of MoMo's financial services stack — specifically credit, insurance, and savings products — to deepen user engagement and increase ARPU. MTN has an ambitious plan to grow to 100 million active MoMo users, 2 million agents, 3 million merchants and US$750 billion transaction value in 2025. The key lever is merchant acquisition: the Mastercard partnership will empower MoMo merchants to seamlessly accept card payments, enhancing the platform's instant cross-border money remittance services.

MTN should also expand the open API ecosystem, enabling third-party fintech developers to build on the MoMo platform, creating an app marketplace that increases platform stickiness. MTN has leveraged open APIs to organise MoMo API Hackathons, boosting the fintech innovation ecosystem.

**The Warrant:** The Porter's Generic Strategies analysis (Section II.4) identifies differentiation through ecosystem depth as MTN's most defensible competitive position. The GSMA data confirms that mobile money is the growth engine of African telecoms: approximately 108 billion transactions totalling over $1.68 trillion were processed through mobile money accounts in 2024. Each additional financial service embedded in MoMo raises switching costs, increases transaction frequency, and generates fee income that is not subject to the same forex risk as connectivity revenue (since MoMo transactions are denominated in local currency). Deepening the fintech ecosystem is therefore both a competitive differentiation strategy and a forex risk mitigation strategy.

---

### Recommendation 6: Pursue Selective Market Rationalisation to Concentrate Resources on High-Value Markets (Urgency: Medium)

**The Problem:** Operating across 16 markets creates organisational complexity and dilutes management attention. Some markets are too small or too politically unstable to generate adequate returns on capital.

**The Recommendation:** MTN should continue the portfolio rationalisation it has already begun (it confirmed the sale of its Guinea-Bissau operation in 2024) and focus capital and management attention on its five to seven highest-value markets: Nigeria, South Africa, Ghana, Uganda, Cameroon, and Côte d'Ivoire. The Group confirmed the sale of its operation in Guinea-Bissau to Telecel Group, following the receipt of all regulatory approvals, as part of a process to simplify the portfolio and reduce complexity.

In retained markets, MTN should pursue the full Ambition 2030 platform strategy. In smaller markets, it should consider a "connectivity-only" model — providing basic mobile services without the full fintech platform investment — or seek joint ventures to share costs.

**The Warrant:** The dynamic capabilities framework (Section II.3) emphasises the importance of reconfiguring assets in response to environmental change. Spreading R30 billion of annual capex across 16 markets means each market receives insufficient investment to achieve the network quality and fintech ecosystem depth needed to win. Concentrating resources on high-value markets allows MTN to achieve the scale economies in fintech that make the MoMo platform genuinely inimitable — a platform with 5 million users in a small market is far less valuable than one with 20 million users in a large market.

## CONCLUSION

MTN Group's strategic situation is one of genuine paradox: a company with Africa's most valuable brand, the continent's largest mobile money platform, and 291 million subscribers is reporting billion-dollar losses. The analytical chain developed here resolves this paradox: the losses are financial artefacts of currency mismatch, not evidence of operational failure. MTN's VRIN resources — MoMo, brand, data — remain intact and are growing in constant-currency terms.

The strategic prescription that follows is therefore not to rebuild the business but to protect the financial returns from a business that is operationally sound. This requires urgent action on forex risk (Recommendations 1, 2, 4), medium-term action on cost structure (Recommendation 3), and sustained investment in the differentiation strategy that will define MTN's long-run competitive position (Recommendations 5, 6).

MTN's Ambition 2030 strategy aims to expand internet access across Africa, deepen financial inclusion, and scale digital services to meet growing demand, with millions of Africans remaining underserved, presenting a significant long-term opportunity. The structural opportunity is real. The strategic task is to ensure that the financial architecture of the Group is robust enough to pursue it.
