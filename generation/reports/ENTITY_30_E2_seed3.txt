# Polestar: A Strategic Case Study
## *The Challenger at the Crossroads — Navigating Identity, Liquidity, and Competitive Positioning in the Premium EV Market*

---

## 1. Introduction and Case Overview

Polestar Automotive Holding UK PLC (Nasdaq: PSNY) presents one of the most instructive strategic paradoxes in contemporary business: a brand with genuine design distinction, a credible sustainability narrative, and access to world-class manufacturing infrastructure, yet one that has repeatedly missed financial targets, triggered going-concern disclosures, and watched its share price collapse. The central strategic question is not whether Polestar makes compelling cars — the evidence suggests it does — but whether its business model, competitive positioning, and ownership structure can be made to generate sustainable value before the company exhausts its liquidity.

This case study argues that Polestar suffers from a **compound strategic problem** with three interlocking dimensions: (1) an identity ambiguity that undermines its differentiation in a crowded premium EV segment; (2) a structural cost disadvantage rooted in low volume and platform dependency; and (3) a geopolitical exposure that threatens its primary manufacturing and market-access arrangements. These three problems are not independent — they reinforce one another in a feedback loop that the firm's current turnaround plan has only partially addressed. The analysis proceeds through a PESTEL scan, Porter's Five Forces, a Resource-Based View (VRIN) assessment, and a SWOT synthesis, before deriving a coherent set of strategic recommendations grounded in the Ansoff Matrix and generic strategy logic.

---

## 2. Company Background

Polestar was founded as a racing and performance division of Volvo Cars before being spun off in 2017 as a standalone electric performance brand under the joint ownership of Volvo Cars and Geely Holding. The turning point came in 2017 when Volvo Cars announced the spin-off of Polestar into a standalone brand focused exclusively on electric performance vehicles, as part of Volvo's broader electrification strategy and in response to growing global demand. The brand listed on Nasdaq via a SPAC merger in 2022 at an implied valuation of approximately $20 billion — a valuation that has since been dramatically eroded.

Polestar operates within the premium electric vehicle segment, competing with both established luxury brands transitioning to EVs and dedicated EV manufacturers, with a positioning that targets affluent, environmentally conscious consumers who prioritise design, performance, and advanced technology. Its product portfolio spans the Polestar 2 (compact executive sedan), Polestar 3 (electric SUV), and Polestar 4 (SUV coupé), with the Polestar 5 (four-door GT) and Polestar 6 (roadster) announced for future launch.

The company's ownership structure is central to understanding its strategic situation. Polestar's brand identity is built around Scandinavian design, performance, and sustainability, yet Geely's influence extends beyond financial backing to include technology, manufacturing capabilities, and operational resources that have been crucial to Polestar's expansion.

## 3. PESTEL Analysis

### 3.1 Political

The most acute political threat facing Polestar is the escalating trade war between Western economies and China. Polestar has stated that new hefty EU and US import tariffs on its Chinese-made cars are becoming a significant issue, and that it will need to "take steps to offset" these new tariffs. This is structurally damaging because Polestar's manufacturing is concentrated in China via Geely's facilities. Volvo and Polestar are brands majority-owned and controlled by Chinese automaker Geely, putting the Swedish marques in a vortex of difficulty in the US, where legislative proposals such as the "Protecting American Autoworkers from China Act" would increase base tariffs on China-built vehicles to 125%. The inference is direct: a tariff at that level would render Chinese-manufactured Polestars economically unviable in the US market, removing a key growth geography and undermining the asset-light manufacturing model that underpins Polestar's cost structure.

### 3.2 Economic

The macroeconomic environment has been doubly challenging. First, rising interest rates through 2023–24 increased the cost of capital precisely when Polestar required sustained external financing. Second, the premium automotive segment faces demand softness as consumers defer large-ticket purchases. Revenue decreased by USD 57.9 million or 10% in Q3 2024, mainly due to lower global vehicle sales of Polestar 2, higher discounts in a competitive market, and a delay in the sales ramp-up of new car lines. The economic significance is that discounting erodes the gross margin of a firm that has never yet achieved sustained positive gross profit — meaning that volume-through-discount strategies are self-defeating for Polestar in a way they are not for Tesla, which operates at far higher scale.

### 3.3 Social

Consumer attitudes toward EVs are maturing, with environmental credentials becoming a baseline expectation rather than a differentiator. However, consumer environmental consciousness is driving market growth, with 73% of global consumers willing to pay a premium for sustainable products. This is favourable for Polestar's sustainability positioning, but only if that positioning is credibly and distinctively communicated — a challenge given the proliferation of "green" claims across the industry.

### 3.4 Technological

The technology landscape is simultaneously an opportunity and a threat. Battery costs continue to fall, which should improve gross margins over time. However, the rapid pace of software-defined vehicle development means that firms without proprietary platforms risk falling behind on the features that increasingly determine premium purchase decisions. Polestar's CEO acknowledged the strategic risk inherent in the Geely platform-sharing model, noting that "the business model with shared architectures and joining synergies can only work once you very well differentiate." This admission by the CEO himself confirms that technology dependency is a recognised strategic liability, not merely an analytical inference.

### 3.5 Environmental

Environmental regulation is a structural tailwind for the EV sector broadly, but creates specific execution pressure for Polestar. EV adoption in Europe slowed in 2024 as automakers delayed sales and model launch plans to coincide with tightening vehicle CO₂ regulations in 2025, though EV sales across the region are now rising again. Polestar has invested heavily in environmental credibility: it publishes Life Cycle Assessments (LCAs) for every model, making the climate impact of each vehicle publicly available, on the basis that real progress starts with transparency. This is a genuine differentiator in a segment where competitors make sustainability claims but few submit to the same level of third-party-auditable disclosure.

### 3.6 Legal

Beyond tariffs, Polestar faces legal and regulatory complexity arising from its dual-listed, multi-jurisdictional structure — incorporated in the UK, listed in the US, operationally headquartered in Sweden, and manufacturing in China. Covenant compliance on its debt facilities represents a live legal risk. Polestar's own financial disclosures state there is "material uncertainty as to whether Polestar will be able to comply with all its covenants in future periods," and that "management cannot guarantee that Polestar Group will be successful in securing the funds necessary."

## 4. Porter's Five Forces Analysis

### 4.1 Competitive Rivalry — **Very High**

The premium EV segment is experiencing rapid competitive intensification. Polestar's primary direct competitor is Tesla, particularly with models like the Model 3 and Model Y, whose strong brand presence and extensive Supercharger network provide a significant advantage. Beyond Tesla, legacy premium OEMs have committed enormous capital to electrification: in 2024, BMW reportedly sold 368,000+ EVs, outselling both Audi (164,000) and Mercedes-Benz (185,000) combined in the EV segment. The strategic implication is that Polestar — with approximately 45,000 annual retail sales in 2024 — is competing against firms with 4–8× its EV volume in the same price band, creating a severe scale disadvantage on cost absorption.

Bernstein analyst Daniel Roeska characterised Polestar as being "on a road to nowhere," primarily due to its inability to match the aggressive price cuts made by Tesla and BYD. While this is an extreme formulation, it captures the structural reality: in a rivalry characterised by price competition, Polestar's cost structure makes it a price-taker rather than a price-setter.

### 4.2 Threat of New Entrants — **Moderate**

The capital intensity of automotive manufacturing creates natural barriers to entry. However, the Geely group itself represents an unusual form of internal competitive threat: Polestar's CEO acknowledged that Polestar, Volvo Cars, and Zeekr — all under the Geely umbrella — share platform architectures and increasingly compete in overlapping European price bands. This is a form of intra-group rivalry that is structurally unusual and strategically damaging: Polestar must differentiate not only from external rivals but from sister brands sharing its own platforms and distribution infrastructure.

### 4.3 Threat of Substitutes — **High**

The substitutes for Polestar are both direct (other premium EVs) and indirect (premium ICE vehicles and hybrids for consumers not yet committed to full electrification). The proliferation of SUV-format EVs from BMW (iX, iX3), Mercedes (EQC, EQS), and Audi (Q4 e-tron, e-tron GT) means that Polestar's Polestar 3 and 4 face well-funded, brand-established alternatives in their core price bracket. The threat of substitutes is therefore high and rising.

### 4.4 Bargaining Power of Buyers — **High**

Buyers of premium EVs are sophisticated, have access to extensive comparative information, and face a growing choice set. Higher discounts required in a competitive market contributed directly to Polestar's revenue decline and gross margin compression in 2024. This evidences buyer power in practice: customers extracted price concessions that materially harmed Polestar's economics. The direct-to-consumer sales model, while reducing channel costs, also removes the buffer of dealer negotiation and places Polestar's pricing directly in front of market pressure.

### 4.5 Bargaining Power of Suppliers — **Moderate to High**

Polestar's asset-light model means it is heavily dependent on Geely's manufacturing facilities and Volvo's dealer and service network. Polestar vehicles are sold and serviced through Volvo's extensive dealer network, giving the brand physical presence at scale without the capital expenditure of building its own retail footprint. While this reduces fixed costs, it creates a structural dependency: Polestar's operational continuity is contingent on the goodwill and strategic alignment of its parent companies. If Geely's priorities shift — as they might under intensifying geopolitical pressure — Polestar's supply and distribution arrangements could be disrupted. This is a form of supplier power that is not captured in traditional analysis but is central to Polestar's risk profile.

## 5. Resource-Based View (VRIN) Analysis

The VRIN framework (Barney, 1991) evaluates whether a firm's resources are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable — the conditions necessary for sustained competitive advantage.

### 5.1 Brand and Design Aesthetic — *Valuable, Rare, Partially Inimitable*

Polestar's CEO identified Scandinavian minimalist design and sustainability framing as the two differentiators that allow Polestar to coexist with Volvo Cars and Zeekr under the broader Geely Holding umbrella. The design language — characterised by clean surfaces, ethical material sourcing, and a performance-without-ostentation philosophy — is genuinely rare in the premium EV segment, where rivals tend toward either technological maximalism (Tesla) or traditional luxury signalling (BMW, Mercedes). However, design can be imitated over time, and the inimitability condition is only partially satisfied. The brand's long-term advantage depends on whether the aesthetic becomes associated with a coherent lifestyle identity, which requires sustained investment in brand-building that the current financial situation makes difficult.

### 5.2 Sustainability Transparency — *Valuable, Rare, Partially Inimitable*

Polestar's commitment to publishing Life Cycle Assessments for every model represents a level of environmental transparency that is rare in the automotive industry. This is a genuinely inimitable capability in the short term — not because competitors cannot produce LCAs, but because the institutional commitment to radical transparency, and the reputational capital built around it, cannot be replicated quickly. The "Polestar 0" project (targeting a climate-neutral car by 2030) reinforces this. However, for this resource to generate competitive advantage, it must be monetised through premium pricing — which requires buyers to value transparency enough to pay for it, an assumption that has not yet been validated at scale.

### 5.3 Geely/Volvo Platform Access — *Valuable, Not Rare, Substitutable*

Access to Geely's manufacturing and Volvo's dealer network reduces Polestar's capital requirements substantially. Geely provides resources including technology, manufacturing capabilities, and financial backing that have been crucial in Polestar's rapid expansion. However, this resource is not rare (other Geely brands access the same platforms), is not inimitable (competitors build their own platforms), and is substitutable (Polestar could theoretically contract with other manufacturers). Crucially, it is a **borrowed** resource rather than an owned one, meaning it confers no durable competitive advantage and exposes Polestar to dependency risk. The VRIN analysis therefore suggests that platform access is operationally necessary but strategically insufficient.

### 5.4 Financial Resources — *Not Valuable in Current Form*

Since inception, Polestar Group has generated recurring net losses and negative operating and investing cash flows, with net losses for the six months ended June 30, 2025 amounting to $1.19 billion, and net current liabilities of approximately $2.99 billion as of the same date. The financial resource base fails the "valuable" test: rather than enabling competitive action, it constrains it. The company struggles to fund research and development as well as operational expansion due to its high debt levels, and faces a disadvantage in terms of economies of scale because its production levels remain lower than those of major EV manufacturers. This creates a vicious cycle: insufficient scale → insufficient gross profit → insufficient R&D investment → insufficient product differentiation → insufficient pricing power → insufficient scale.

## 7. The Core Strategic Problem: A Compound Trap

The analysis above reveals that Polestar is caught in a **compound strategic trap** with three interlocking mechanisms:

**Mechanism 1 — The Identity Trap:** Polestar's positioning between mass-market EV and established luxury creates a "squeezed middle" problem. It cannot compete on price with Tesla/BYD, nor on brand heritage with BMW/Mercedes/Porsche. In theory, Polestar can take risks that Volvo cannot — it is an experimental brand that can make radical changes to styling, material, and features — yet Volvo is still behind the EV curve nearly eight years after the split, and now the EV spin-off is in trouble on its own. The original strategic logic of the spin-off — Polestar as a risk-taking EV laboratory — has not translated into a clear consumer value proposition.

**Mechanism 2 — The Scale Trap:** Annual retail sales dropped to 44,851 vehicles in 2024, down from 52,796 in 2023. At this volume, fixed costs per unit are extremely high, preventing positive gross margins. Without positive gross margins, the company cannot self-fund growth. Without growth, it cannot achieve the scale needed for positive gross margins. This is a classic scale trap, and it is compounded by the fact that Polestar's asset-light model — while reducing fixed costs — also limits its ability to capture manufacturing efficiencies.

**Mechanism 3 — The Dependency Trap:** Polestar's reliance on Geely for platforms, manufacturing, and capital means that its strategic options are constrained by a parent whose own interests may diverge. The tariff threat makes this dependency acutely dangerous: if Chinese-manufactured vehicles face prohibitive tariffs in the US and EU — Polestar's two most important markets — the company must either move manufacturing (requiring capital it does not have) or exit those markets (destroying revenue).

These three traps interact: identity ambiguity prevents premium pricing → premium pricing is needed to cover high per-unit costs at low volume → low volume prevents scale economies → scale economies are needed to fund the brand investment that would resolve identity ambiguity.

## 8. Strategic Options: Ansoff Matrix Assessment

The Ansoff Matrix (Ansoff, 1957) provides a framework for evaluating Polestar's growth options across four quadrants:

**Market Penetration (existing products, existing markets):** Increasing share in current segments through pricing, marketing, or distribution. This option is strategically inadvisable for Polestar in its current state. Deeper discounting to gain share would further compress already-negative gross margins, worsening the financial position. Polestar's strengthened business plan explicitly reorients toward profitability by prioritising margin progression over volume — confirming that management has correctly rejected pure market penetration as a path forward.

**Market Development (existing products, new markets):** Entering new geographies with the current product range. This is partially viable — expanding in markets where Chinese-manufactured vehicles face lower tariff exposure (e.g., Southeast Asia, Middle East) — but is insufficient as a primary strategy given the scale of the financial challenge.

**Product Development (new products, existing markets):** Launching higher-margin models for existing customers. This is Polestar's most promising near-term option. In Q1 2025, gross margin improved by 15 percentage points to 7% versus Q1 2024, driven by a growing share of higher-margin models in the sales mix and continuing cost reduction measures. The Polestar 5, as a flagship GT at a higher price point, represents the critical product development bet — if it can be brought to market with sufficient differentiation to command a genuine luxury premium, it could break the scale trap by improving margin per unit rather than requiring volume growth.

**Diversification:** Entering new markets with new products. This is the highest-risk quadrant and is inappropriate given Polestar's financial constraints. The company cannot afford to dilute management attention or capital allocation.

The Ansoff analysis therefore points to a **focused product development strategy** anchored on the Polestar 5 flagship, combined with selective market development in tariff-advantaged geographies.

## 9. Generic Strategy Assessment (Porter, 1980)

Porter's generic strategies framework identifies three viable positions: cost leadership, differentiation, and focus. Polestar's current position is strategically ambiguous — it attempts broad differentiation but at a scale insufficient to support the cost base, and without the brand heritage to command the price premium that broad differentiation requires.

The correct generic strategy for Polestar is **focused differentiation**: competing in a defined premium segment (design-conscious, sustainability-committed, urban affluent buyers) with a product and brand experience that cannot be replicated by volume players. This is consistent with the CEO's stated direction: "Our game plan to win is that we are a challenger. We are challenging the industry. We are challenging the competition, and we try out new things. Over and above that, we do beautiful cars," says Lohscheller.

However, the focused differentiation strategy is only viable if Polestar resolves the brand identity ambiguity identified above. Currently, the differentiation is real but insufficiently communicated and insufficiently premium in pricing. The Polestar 5 must be the vehicle (literally and figuratively) through which Polestar establishes a clear identity as a design and sustainability leader — not merely a well-designed Volvo variant.

## 10. Recommendations and Action Plan

The following recommendations are derived directly from the analytical chain above. Each recommendation addresses a specific strategic problem identified in the analysis and is sequenced to reflect the urgency of the financial situation.

---

### Recommendation 1: Stabilise the Financial Position Through Covenant Management and Selective Asset Monetisation *(Immediate: 0–12 months)*

**Rationale:** The going-concern risk is the most urgent problem because it constrains all other strategic options. Polestar is facing a critical period in which its survival will be dependent on its ability to continually raise new capital and renegotiate terms with its lenders to fund its high cash burn and bridge a significant liquidity gap. Without financial stability, no medium-term strategy can be executed.

**Action:** Management should prioritise covenant renegotiation with lenders, leveraging the improving Q1 2025 gross margin trajectory as evidence of operational progress. Gross margin improved by 15 percentage points to 7% in Q1 2025, net loss was reduced by 31%, and Adjusted EBITDA loss improved by 46% year-on-year — these are credible data points for a covenant renegotiation conversation. Additionally, Polestar should explore whether IP assets (particularly its LCA methodology and sustainability reporting infrastructure) could be licensed to third parties, generating non-dilutive revenue.

**Why the evidence warrants this conclusion:** A firm cannot execute a differentiation strategy if it is simultaneously managing a liquidity crisis. The financial stabilisation must precede, not accompany, the strategic repositioning.

---

### Recommendation 2: Resolve the Manufacturing Geography Problem Through a Phased Production Diversification *(Short-Medium Term: 12–36 months)*

**Rationale:** The tariff threat is structural and unlikely to abate. New hefty EU and US import tariffs on Chinese-made Polestar cars are becoming an issue, requiring the company to "take steps to offset" them. The current manufacturing concentration in China is therefore a strategic liability that compounds with every escalation of trade tensions.

**Action:** Polestar should negotiate with Geely and Volvo to expand production of the Polestar 3 and Polestar 5 at Volvo's Charleston, South Carolina facility (which already manufactures the Volvo EX90 for the US market). This would reduce US tariff exposure without requiring Polestar to build its own manufacturing capacity — consistent with the asset-light model. For the European market, the Volvo Ghent facility in Belgium represents an analogous option.

**Why the evidence warrants this conclusion:** The asset-light model is only an advantage if the assets Polestar accesses are geopolitically viable. Volvo's existing Western manufacturing footprint provides a ready solution that does not require capital Polestar does not have. The cost of not acting — potential exclusion from the US market — is existential.

---

### Recommendation 3: Sharpen Brand Identity Through the Polestar 5 as a Flagship Anchor *(Medium Term: 18–36 months)*

**Rationale:** The VRIN analysis identified Polestar's design aesthetic and sustainability transparency as its only genuinely rare and partially inimitable resources. The focused differentiation strategy requires these resources to be leveraged into a clear, premium brand identity. The Polestar 5 — a four-door GT at a higher price point than the current range — is the critical vehicle for this repositioning.

**Action:** The Polestar 5 should be positioned explicitly as a competitor to the Porsche Taycan and Audi e-tron GT, not the Tesla Model S. This requires: (a) pricing the Polestar 5 at or above €100,000 to signal genuine luxury intent; (b) making the full LCA and supply chain transparency report a standard part of the purchase experience, not a supplementary document; (c) investing in a small number of flagship brand experiences (not volume retail) in key cities — London, Munich, New York, Shanghai — to build brand equity with the target demographic.

**Why the evidence warrants this conclusion:** Polestar sits in a narrow but valuable space in the premium EV market, operating between mass-scale EV leaders and low-volume luxury startups — and in Q4 2025, Polestar avoided aggressive discounting, with buyers paying for the product rather than rebates. This suggests the brand can command premium pricing when the product justifies it. The Polestar 5 must be the proof point that the brand deserves a luxury price, not a premium-mass price.

---

### Recommendation 4: Operationalise Sustainability as a Commercial Differentiator, Not a Reporting Exercise *(Medium Term: 12–24 months)*

**Rationale:** Polestar's sustainability transparency is its most inimitable resource, yet it is currently positioned as a reporting and PR activity rather than a commercial differentiator. 73% of global consumers are willing to pay a premium for sustainable products — but this willingness to pay is only accessible if sustainability is made tangible and verifiable at the point of purchase.

**Action:** Polestar should develop a "Transparency Score" — a simple, consumer-facing metric derived from its existing LCA data — displayed prominently in all marketing materials and on the vehicle itself (e.g., on the infotainment welcome screen). This transforms a back-office capability into a front-of-house differentiator. Additionally, Polestar should pursue B Corp certification or equivalent third-party validation to make its sustainability claims externally auditable, increasing credibility with the target demographic of design-conscious, environmentally committed affluent buyers.

**Why the evidence warrants this conclusion:** Polestar's CEO identified sustainability framing as one of only two differentiators that allow Polestar to coexist with Volvo Cars and Zeekr under the broader Geely umbrella. If sustainability is genuinely a differentiator, it must be made commercially legible — not buried in technical reports that only specialists read. The conversion of a genuine capability into a perceived and priced advantage is the strategic gap this recommendation addresses.

---

### Recommendation 5: Manage the Intra-Group Rivalry Through a Formal Brand Architecture Agreement with Geely *(Ongoing)*

**Rationale:** The intra-group competition between Polestar, Volvo, and Zeekr is a structural problem that will intensify as Geely's portfolio expands in Europe. The three premium brands share platform architectures and increasingly compete in overlapping European price bands. Without a formal brand architecture agreement, Polestar risks being cannibalised by its own parent.

**Action:** Polestar's management should negotiate a formal brand positioning agreement with Geely that defines clear price-band and segment boundaries across the portfolio — analogous to the Volkswagen Group's architecture that separates Skoda, VW, Audi, and Porsche. Polestar's defined territory should be the €45,000–€120,000+ design-and-sustainability-led EV segment, with Volvo occupying the safety-and-family segment and Zeekr the technology-and-performance segment. This agreement should be disclosed to investors as evidence of strategic clarity within the group.

**Why the evidence warrants this conclusion:** Polestar's CEO himself acknowledged that "the business model with shared architectures and joining synergies can only work once you very well differentiate." Differentiation from sibling brands requires not just marketing decisions but structural commitments about where each brand competes. Without such a commitment, Polestar's differentiation investments will be partially offset by intra-group overlap.

## 11. Conclusion

Polestar's strategic situation is serious but not irreversible. The analytical chain constructed in this case study leads to a clear conclusion: Polestar possesses genuinely rare resources — its design identity and sustainability transparency — but has failed to translate them into competitive advantage because of three compounding structural problems: identity ambiguity, a scale trap, and a dependency trap. The compound trap is self-reinforcing, but it has a logical entry point for intervention: the Polestar 5 flagship, which can simultaneously improve gross margins (breaking the scale trap), establish a clear luxury identity (resolving the identity trap), and reduce reliance on high-volume Chinese manufacturing (beginning to address the dependency trap).

The updated business plan envisions a compound annual retail sales volume growth of 30–35% for 2025 to 2027 and a positive adjusted EBITDA in 2025, with improvements in margin, fixed costs, and working capital expected from 2026. This plan is directionally correct in prioritising margin over volume, but it will only succeed if the brand architecture, manufacturing geography, and sustainability commercialisation recommendations above are executed in parallel. Financial improvement without strategic repositioning merely delays the compound trap's reassertion; strategic repositioning without financial stabilisation cannot be funded.

The five recommendations above are therefore not independent initiatives — they form a coherent programme in which financial stabilisation creates the runway for strategic repositioning, manufacturing diversification removes the geopolitical constraint, the Polestar 5 provides the commercial proof point, sustainability operationalisation converts a capability into a price premium, and the brand architecture agreement prevents intra-group erosion of the differentiation investment. Executed together, they give Polestar a credible path from its current precarious position to a sustainable competitive advantage in the premium EV segment. Executed piecemeal, any one of them is insufficient.
