## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Shell plc finds itself at one of the most consequential strategic crossroads in its 115-year history. Once a straightforward integrated oil and gas major, Shell must now simultaneously defend its profitable fossil fuel core, manage mounting legal and regulatory climate pressure, and build credible low-carbon businesses — all while delivering shareholder returns. This case study examines the tension between Shell's legacy hydrocarbon model and its stated ambition to become a net-zero emissions energy business by 2050. Using PESTEL, Porter's Five Forces, SWOT, Value Chain Analysis, and the VRIO framework, it identifies the root causes of Shell's strategic dilemma and concludes with a prioritised set of actionable recommendations.

---

## 2. COMPANY BACKGROUND

Shell plc (formerly Royal Dutch Shell) is one of the world's largest energy companies by revenue, headquartered in London, UK. Founded in 1907, Shell operates across more than 70 countries and employs approximately 93,000 people. Its business is organised into five segments:

| Segment | Description |
|---|---|
| **Integrated Gas** | LNG production, marketing, trading, and gas-to-liquids |
| **Upstream** | Conventional oil and gas exploration and production |
| **Marketing** | Retail fuel, lubricants, and mobility services |
| **Chemicals & Products** | Refining, petrochemicals, and trading |
| **Renewables & Energy Solutions** | Power, hydrogen, EV charging, and carbon credits |

Shell launched its "Powering Progress" strategy in 2021, and its target to become a net-zero emissions energy business by 2050 remains at the heart of its strategy. However, in 2024, Shell refreshed its renewable generation, energy marketing, and gas and power trading strategy — a notable pivot back toward its most profitable core — raising fundamental questions about the coherence and credibility of its long-term strategic direction.

## 3. THE STRATEGIC SITUATION

### 3.1 Defining the Problem

Shell faces a **dual legitimacy crisis**: it must demonstrate commercial viability to shareholders while demonstrating environmental credibility to regulators, courts, civil society, and future talent. Three interrelated tensions define the situation:

**Tension 1 — Profitability vs. Decarbonisation**

Shell's most profitable businesses (Integrated Gas and Upstream) are also its highest-emitting. In an uncertain environment, Shell planned to reduce overall capital spending to $22–25 billion for 2024 and 2025, while raising its dividend per share by about 15% starting in the second half of 2023. Critics argue this represents a strategic retreat from the energy transition.

**Tension 2 — Legal Pressure vs. Strategic Autonomy**

On 12 November 2024, the Court of Appeal in The Hague ruled in the appeal proceedings between Shell plc and Milieudefensie. Shell appealed a district court ruling that had required Shell to reduce its net CO₂ emissions by 45% by 2030 compared with 2019 levels. The Court of Appeal concluded that, notwithstanding Shell's responsibility to reduce greenhouse gas emissions, a civil court cannot impose specific emissions reduction targets on the company. However, the Court of Appeal's ruling affirmed that companies have a duty of care to align their business models with the goals of the Paris Agreement.

**Tension 3 — Short-Term Returns vs. Long-Term Resilience**

By 2024, global investment in clean energy technologies was projected to exceed $2 trillion, signalling a rapid shift away from traditional fossil fuels. Shell's failure to develop a coherent and credible transition strategy risks stranded assets, regulatory penalties, reputational damage, and ultimately, competitive irrelevance.

## 4. EXTERNAL ANALYSIS

### 4.1 PESTEL Analysis

---

#### 🔴 Political

- **Energy security**: Following Russia's invasion of Ukraine, European governments prioritised energy security, creating strong demand for LNG as a bridge fuel — a direct tailwind for Shell's Integrated Gas business.
- **Carbon regulation**: The European Union's Emissions Trading System (EU ETS) set a carbon price at €80.66 per tonne of CO₂ in January 2024. The United Kingdom's Carbon Reduction Commitment requires Shell to purchase carbon allowances at £75.40 per tonne.
- **Climate litigation risk**: Environmental activists continue to bring litigation against governments and companies for the effects of climate change on individuals and communities around the world. The Milieudefensie case sets a precedent for future litigation globally.

#### 🟡 Economic

- **Oil price volatility**: Shell's revenues are highly sensitive to global commodity prices. The post-pandemic oil price surge (2021–2022) delivered record profits; subsequent normalisation compressed margins.
- **Shareholder return expectations**: Capital markets continue to reward short-term cash generation. Shell's 15% dividend increase in 2024 reflects this pressure.
- **Emerging market demand**: Developing economies in Asia and Africa continue to increase fossil fuel consumption, providing a longer demand runway for Shell's core products than many Western forecasts suggest.

#### 🟠 Social

- **Climate consciousness**: Public and consumer awareness of climate change is intensifying, creating reputational risk for oil majors.
- **Energy poverty**: Approximately 800 million people globally lack access to electricity. The world must achieve an orderly transition away from fossil fuels to low-carbon energy to achieve net-zero emissions, while today fossil fuels meet around 80% of global energy demand, with an even greater reliance in many developing countries.
- **Talent**: Younger employees increasingly prefer employers with credible sustainability credentials, creating a talent acquisition challenge for fossil fuel companies.

#### 🔵 Technological

- **Renewable cost deflation**: The levelised cost of solar and wind power has fallen dramatically, making renewables cost-competitive with fossil fuels in many markets — threatening Shell's core product economics.
- **AI and digitalisation**: Shell's AI-driven predictive maintenance programme actively monitors over 10,000 pieces of critical equipment — including control valves, pumps, and compressors — across its global portfolio, running nearly 11,000 distinct models.
- **SAF**: Shell became one of the world's largest traders and suppliers of sustainable aviation fuel in 2024, achieving this because of its long-term agreements with producers, the strength of its customer relationships, and strategic investments in logistics around key terminals and airports.

#### 🟢 Environmental

- **Net-zero commitments**: The Paris Agreement's 1.5°C pathway requires rapid and deep decarbonisation. Shell's Scope 3 emissions (from the use of products it sells) are by far its largest emissions category and the hardest to reduce.
- **Stranded asset risk**: Tightening carbon budgets mean that a significant proportion of known fossil fuel reserves may never be economically extractable, threatening the long-term value of Shell's upstream portfolio.

#### ⚫ Legal

- **Climate litigation proliferation**: The Court of Appeal's ruling in Milieudefensie v. Shell sets a precedent for corporate responsibility in addressing climate change. While the court did not impose specific reduction targets on Shell, it affirmed that companies have a duty of care to align their business models with the goals of the Paris Agreement.
- **Mandatory disclosure**: Regulators in the UK, EU, and US are mandating detailed climate-related financial disclosures (TCFD, CSRD), increasing compliance costs and the visibility of Shell's transition risks.

### 4.2 Porter's Five Forces

| Force | Intensity | Key Driver |
|---|---|---|
| Threat of New Entrants | Low–Moderate | High capital barriers in O&G; lower in new energy (EVs, renewables) |
| Supplier Power | Moderate | Scale leverage vs. concentrated new-energy supply chains |
| Buyer Power | Moderate–High | Commodity markets; EV substitution eroding retail customer base |
| **Threat of Substitutes** | **High ↑** | Renewables, EVs, green hydrogen — cost-competitive and accelerating |
| **Competitive Rivalry** | **High** | Global IOCs, NOCs, and new-energy entrants |

**Force 1 — Threat of New Entrants (Low–Moderate)**: The oil and gas industry has extraordinarily high barriers to entry. However, in Shell's emerging businesses (EV charging, renewable power, hydrogen), barriers are lower. Technology companies, utilities, and automotive manufacturers represent a new and unconventional competitive threat.

**Force 2 — Bargaining Power of Suppliers (Moderate)**: Shell's scale and long-term contracting relationships provide leverage. However, in new energy supply chains (e.g., electrolysers, solar panels), supply is concentrated among a small number of manufacturers — many of them Chinese — creating dependency risk.

**Force 3 — Bargaining Power of Buyers (Moderate–High)**: In commodity markets (crude oil, LNG spot), buyers have significant power because products are largely undifferentiated. The long-term shift to EVs threatens to erode Shell's retail fuel customer base, increasing buyer power as alternatives multiply.

**Force 4 — Threat of Substitutes (High and Rising)**: This is Shell's most significant competitive threat. There are around 40 million battery electric and plug-in hybrid vehicles on roads today, with up to 275 million expected by 2030. Renewable energy, EVs, heat pumps, and green hydrogen all represent substitutes for Shell's core products, and their costs are falling rapidly.

**Force 5 — Competitive Rivalry (High)**: The integrated oil and gas sector is intensely competitive. Shell's primary rivals — ExxonMobil, BP, TotalEnergies, Chevron, and Saudi Aramco — all possess comparable financial resources, technical capabilities, and global reach. In new energy markets, Shell faces competition from pure-play renewable companies and technology firms with lower legacy costs.

**Implication**: The structural attractiveness of Shell's core industry is declining. The threat of substitutes and competitive rivalry are the dominant forces, reinforcing the urgency of strategic transformation.

## 7. RECOMMENDATIONS AND ACTION PLAN

Five prioritised recommendations are proposed for Shell's Board and Executive Committee, sequenced to address immediate threats while building long-term competitive advantage.

---

### 🔵 Recommendation 1: Reaffirm and Operationalise the LNG-Led Transition Strategy
**Priority: Immediate | Timeframe: 0–2 Years**

Shell's world-leading LNG franchise is its most powerful strategic asset for the next decade. LNG serves as a critical bridge fuel — displacing coal in Asia and providing energy security in Europe — while renewable infrastructure is built at scale.

**Actions:**
- **Accelerate LNG capacity expansion** through the North Field (Qatar) and LNG Canada projects, targeting the 20–30% increase in liquefaction volumes by 2030.
- **Lock in long-term Asian supply contracts** with utilities in Japan, South Korea, China, and India — markets where the transition away from coal will sustain LNG demand for 15–20 years.
- **Ring-fence LNG cash flows** and commit a defined percentage (recommended: 25–30% of free cash flow) to funding the low-carbon portfolio, creating a transparent and auditable link between legacy profits and transition investment.
- **Reframe LNG commercially as a "transition fuel with a sunset clause"** — explicitly communicating a credible, time-bound pathway from LNG to hydrogen and renewable gas.

*Strategic Rationale*: Exploits Shell's sustained competitive advantage in LNG (VRIO) while embedding a credible transition narrative that satisfies the suitability criterion.

---

### 🟢 Recommendation 2: Build a Focused, Profitable Clean Energy Portfolio
**Priority: High | Timeframe: 1–3 Years**

Shell's 2024 pivot away from broad renewable power generation was strategically rational — Shell does not have a competitive advantage in commodity renewable electricity. However, the retreat must not be misread as abandoning the energy transition. Shell should concentrate clean energy investments where its existing capabilities create genuine competitive differentiation:

- **Sustainable Aviation Fuel (SAF)**: Shell aims to produce around 2 million tonnes of SAF annually by 2025. Invest aggressively in SAF production capacity and long-term airline supply agreements. Aviation is a hard-to-abate sector with no viable electric alternative at scale — this is a 30-year opportunity.
- **Industrial Hydrogen**: Focus hydrogen investment on industrial customers (steel, chemicals, refining) where Shell has existing B2B relationships. Target the Holland Hydrogen I project as a proof-of-concept and scale from there. Shell has earmarked approximately $0.5 billion for cash capex in EV charging and up to $1 billion annually in hydrogen and CCS in 2024 and 2025.
- **EV Charging**: Leverage the existing retail network to deploy EV charging at scale. EV charging monetises Shell's retail real estate and brand in a post-petrol world.
- **Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS)**: Invest in CCS at Shell's own industrial facilities and develop CCS-as-a-service for industrial customers — a natural extension of Shell's engineering expertise.

*Strategic Rationale*: Applies the VRIO logic — invest where Shell's capabilities are valuable, rare, and inimitable — rather than competing in commodity renewable markets where Shell has no structural advantage.

---

### 🟡 Recommendation 3: Develop a Credible, Quantified Scope 3 Emissions Reduction Roadmap
**Priority: High | Timeframe: 0–18 Months**

The Court of Appeal's ruling affirmed that companies have a duty of care to align their business models with the goals of the Paris Agreement. The absence of a court-imposed specific target does not eliminate legal, regulatory, and reputational risk — it simply defers it. Shell should proactively:

- **Publish a sector-by-sector Scope 3 decarbonisation roadmap** — showing how Shell's customers in transport, aviation, and industry will transition to lower-carbon products, and what Shell's role in enabling that transition will be.
- **Set interim Scope 3 targets for 2030 and 2035**, aligned with the IEA's Net Zero Emissions scenario, with independent third-party verification.
- **Reform the carbon credit strategy**: Phase out reliance on nature-based carbon credits for offsetting operational emissions by 2030 and replace with genuine emissions reductions. Reserve carbon credits for genuinely residual emissions only.
- **Engage proactively with the European Commission** on a voluntary sectoral emissions reduction framework — shaping regulation rather than reacting to it.

*Strategic Rationale*: Converts the most significant legal and reputational threat (PESTEL/SWOT) into a proactive strategic asset that differentiates Shell from less-transparent competitors.

---

### 🟠 Recommendation 4: Accelerate Organisational and Cultural Transformation
**Priority: Medium | Timeframe: 2–5 Years**

Strategy without organisational capability is merely aspiration. Shell's ability to execute the Disciplined Transition strategy depends on transforming its culture, talent base, and incentive structures.

- **Redesign executive incentives**: Increase the weighting of energy transition metrics in the annual bonus scorecard. Shell introduced new metrics in the annual bonus scorecard to reflect its role in the energy transition. These should be extended to tie long-term incentive plans to absolute Scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions reductions — not just intensity improvements.
- **Invest in talent transformation**: Launch a large-scale internal reskilling programme to transition engineers and technical staff from fossil fuel operations to hydrogen, CCS, and digital energy management.
- **Create dedicated transition business units** with separate P&L accountability, distinct culture, and faster decision-making processes — insulated from the bureaucratic inertia of the legacy business.
- **Embed a "just transition" commitment**: Ensure communities and workers dependent on Shell's fossil fuel operations are supported through the transition — both as an ethical obligation and as a social licence strategy.

*Strategic Rationale*: Addresses the organisational inertia weakness (W5) and the talent threat (T5) identified in the SWOT analysis. Consistent with the McKinsey 7-S model principle that strategy and structure must be aligned.

---

### 🔴 Recommendation 5: Proactively Shape the Regulatory and Policy Environment
**Priority: Medium | Timeframe: Ongoing**

Shell is a large enough actor to influence — not merely react to — the regulatory environment.

- **Advocate publicly for a global carbon price**: A well-designed carbon pricing mechanism would level the competitive playing field and reward Shell's investments in lower-carbon energy.
- **Engage constructively with the EU Taxonomy Regulation** to ensure that LNG and CCS qualify as transition-eligible investments, securing Shell's access to green capital markets.
- **Lead the development of international SAF and hydrogen standards**: First-mover advantage in standard-setting creates durable competitive advantages and shapes market structure in Shell's favour.
- **Strengthen transparency and disclosure**: Go beyond mandatory TCFD requirements and publish comprehensive, independently verified climate transition plans.

*Strategic Rationale*: Addresses the regulatory threat (T3) and litigation risk (T2) identified in the PESTEL and SWOT analyses, while creating first-mover advantages in emerging regulatory frameworks.

## 8. CONCLUSION

Shell plc is not facing a simple strategic choice between oil and renewables. It is navigating a far more complex challenge: managing the decline of one energy system while building the foundations of another — at pace, at scale, and under intense legal, regulatory, and social scrutiny.

The strategic analysis reveals that Shell's greatest competitive assets — its LNG trading franchise, its global brand, its integrated value chain, and its engineering expertise — are genuinely transferable to a lower-carbon energy system. The risk is not that Shell lacks the resources or capabilities to transition; it is that short-term financial pressures, organisational inertia, and strategic ambiguity will cause it to squander the window of opportunity that its current profitability provides.

The recommended "Disciplined Transition" strategy — anchored in LNG, focused on select clean energy markets where Shell has genuine competitive advantage (SAF, industrial hydrogen, EV charging, and CCS), and underpinned by a credible Scope 3 roadmap and organisational transformation — offers the most viable path to long-term value creation.

The Court of Appeal confirmed that Shell has an obligation to combat dangerous climate change, and unlike the District Court in 2021, the Court of Appeal ruled that a court cannot impose a specific CO₂ reduction target. That strategic space — between legal obligation and commercial opportunity — is exactly where Shell's leadership must operate. The companies that will thrive in the energy transition are not those that resist it the longest, nor those that abandon their competitive advantages in its name, but those that use what they have built to shape what comes next. Shell has the assets, the scale, and the talent to be that company. The question is whether it has the strategic will.
