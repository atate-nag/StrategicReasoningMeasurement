# Mercedes-Benz at the Crossroads: Navigating the Dual Disruption of Electrification and China Market Erosion

## A Strategic Case Study

---

## 1. Introduction and Strategic Situation

Mercedes-Benz Group AG, one of the world's most storied automotive brands, faces a strategic crisis that is simultaneously financial, technological, and geographical. For 2024, the Mercedes-Benz Group reported revenue of €145.6 billion (down 4.5% year-on-year), EBIT of €13.6 billion (down 30.8% YoY), and net profit of €10.4 billion (down 28.4% YoY). These numbers are not a temporary blip but the measurable outcome of two converging structural disruptions: a premature and now-retreating commitment to battery-electric vehicles (BEVs), and an accelerating loss of position in China — historically the company's single most important growth market.

This case study analyses the strategic situation through four interlocking frameworks — Porter's Five Forces, the Resource-Based View (RBV), Porter's Generic Strategies, and the Ansoff Growth Matrix — to diagnose the root causes of Mercedes-Benz's difficulties and to construct a coherent, evidence-grounded set of strategic recommendations.

---

## 2. Background: The "Top-End Vehicle" Strategy and Its Vulnerabilities

Before examining the disruptions, it is important to understand the strategic logic Mercedes-Benz was pursuing. Mercedes-Benz Group aims to lead the automotive industry through a robust commitment to electrification, luxury, and sustainability, with a key target of increasing the sales share of its top-end luxury segment by 60% compared to 2019 levels by 2026, focusing on high-margin vehicles. In practice, this meant concentrating investment on brands such as AMG, Maybach, and the G-Class — vehicles that command premium pricing and protect margin even when volume falls.

Top-End Vehicle (TEV) mix, including AMG, Maybach, G-Class, S-Class, and EQS, reached an estimated 22 percent of 2024 sales, improving margins. This share represents genuine progress toward the stated target. However, the strategy's vulnerability lies in its dependence on a stable luxury consumer base, particularly in China — a dependence that has been severely tested.

Mercedes-Benz held a brand value of $22.8 billion as of June 2024, placing it fourth globally among automotive brands, behind Tesla, Toyota, and BMW, and ahead of Porsche, Ferrari, and Ford. This brand equity is the foundation of the TEV strategy, yet brand value alone cannot insulate Mercedes from structural market shifts.

## 3. External Environment Analysis: Porter's Five Forces

Porter's Five Forces framework (Porter, 1980) provides a systematic lens through which to assess the competitive intensity and structural attractiveness of the industry Mercedes-Benz inhabits. Each force is evaluated in turn, with the analysis demonstrating how external pressures are directly eroding the financial results documented above.

### 3.1 Competitive Rivalry (HIGH)

The luxury automotive segment is intensely rivalrous, and the rivalry has intensified in 2024. BMW led the luxury car sales chart in the first half of 2024, outselling its main rivals Mercedes-Benz, Audi, and Porsche. This is strategically significant because it signals that Mercedes is losing ground not only to Chinese entrants but to its traditional German peers. The rivalry is no longer purely about product quality; it has expanded to encompass software capability, EV range, and digital services — dimensions on which Mercedes has invested heavily but not yet monetised.

With the rapid rise of Chinese domestic new energy brands, the German Big Three — BMW, Mercedes-Benz, and Audi — are gradually losing market share in the first two of their three main market segments from low to high. This analysis is critical: the competitive threat is not confined to the entry-level segment. Chinese brands are ascending the value chain, making the TEV strategy's "retreat upmarket" less of a safe haven than management anticipated.

### 3.2 Threat of New Entrants (MEDIUM-HIGH)

The shift to electric vehicles has structurally lowered entry barriers by reducing the mechanical complexity that historically protected incumbents. Chinese manufacturers such as BYD, NIO, and Li Auto have entered the luxury-adjacent space with technology-forward products. Mercedes-Benz has lost market share in the world's largest car market alongside rivals BMW and Audi, as local brands including BYD, NIO, and Li Auto gain ground with technology-focused premium offerings. This matters because the traditional source of entry barriers — decades of engineering heritage and dealer networks — is less decisive when consumers prioritise software integration and charging infrastructure over mechanical refinement.

### 3.3 Bargaining Power of Buyers (INCREASING)

Buyer power in the luxury segment has historically been suppressed by brand prestige and limited substitutability. However, two forces are increasing buyer leverage. First, the proliferation of credible alternatives (Chinese brands, Tesla) gives high-net-worth consumers genuine choice. Second, Mercedes itself has been forced into price concessions in China: the guide price of the 2025 Mercedes-Benz C260L Sport is 356,800 yuan, but the naked car price at a Beijing dealership has dropped to 250,000 yuan — and price concessions have not effectively boosted sales. This price erosion is self-defeating: it damages the brand positioning on which the TEV strategy depends, while failing to recover volume.

### 3.4 Bargaining Power of Suppliers (MEDIUM)

Mercedes-Benz spent around €10.5 billion on R&D in 2024, focusing on autonomous driving, electrification, and digital ecosystems. This level of investment, combined with a global production network of over 30 manufacturing facilities, gives Mercedes significant scale leverage over most suppliers. However, the transition to EVs has created new dependencies — particularly on battery cell manufacturers and semiconductor suppliers — where supplier concentration is high and switching costs are substantial. This represents a structural increase in supplier power relative to the ICE era.

### 3.5 Threat of Substitutes (LOW-MEDIUM)

In the ultra-luxury segment, direct substitutes (private aviation, bespoke coachbuilders) remain niche. However, the broader mobility-as-a-service trend represents a longer-term substitution threat, particularly in urban China where ride-hailing and autonomous taxis are advancing rapidly. For the purposes of this case study, this threat is noted but is not the primary strategic pressure.

**Five Forces Summary**: The dominant forces are competitive rivalry (intensified by Chinese entrants ascending the value chain) and increasing buyer power (manifested in forced price concessions in China). These two forces are the proximate external causes of Mercedes-Benz's financial deterioration, and they directly undermine the TEV strategy.

## 4. Internal Analysis: Resource-Based View

The Resource-Based View (Barney, 1991; Wernerfelt, 1984) holds that sustainable competitive advantage derives from resources and capabilities that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN). Applying this framework to Mercedes-Benz reveals a portfolio of strong but increasingly contested resources.

### 4.1 Brand Equity and Heritage (VRIN — Strong)

Mercedes-Benz's brand is its most durable strategic asset. With over a century of manufacturing excellence, the Mercedes-Benz brand is synonymous with quality and luxury. This heritage is genuinely rare and inimitable in the short run — no competitor can manufacture a 130-year history. The strategic implication is that brand equity provides a floor of pricing power that pure-technology entrants cannot easily undercut, which is the logical foundation of the TEV strategy.

### 4.2 Engineering and R&D Capability (VRIN — Strong but Under Pressure)

Mercedes-Benz pioneered several firsts in automotive safety, such as the crumple zone (1959) and the anti-lock braking system, and continues to invest heavily in next-generation technologies. The company's engineering capability is valuable, rare, and has historically been inimitable. However, in the software-defined vehicle era, this capability is being challenged: Chinese competitors have demonstrated that software and AI integration can be developed faster outside the traditional automotive R&D model.

### 4.3 MB.OS Digital Platform (VRIN — Emerging)

With the transformation to "Digital First," Mercedes-Benz is setting new standards and will introduce its own data-supported and flexibly updateable operating system, MB.OS, from 2025. Already in 2022, Mercedes-Benz generated more than €1 billion in software-enabled revenues with products and services such as navigation, Live Traffic, and online map updates. This capability is strategically important because it creates a recurring revenue stream that is not dependent on vehicle volume — a critical hedge against cyclical sales downturns. However, it is not yet fully deployed, and its inimitability is uncertain given the pace of software development by competitors.

### 4.4 Global Manufacturing and Distribution Network (Valuable, but Not Rare)

Mercedes-Benz operates over 30 manufacturing facilities worldwide, including plants in Germany, the US, China, Hungary, and South Africa. This network provides scale and geographic reach but is not rare — BMW and Volkswagen Group possess comparable networks. It is therefore a threshold competence rather than a source of competitive advantage.

### 4.5 RBV Summary and Strategic Gap

The RBV analysis reveals that Mercedes-Benz's most durable advantages lie in brand equity and engineering heritage — both of which support the TEV strategy. The critical gap is in digital/software capability, where the MB.OS platform is promising but not yet proven at scale. This gap is consequential because Chinese luxury entrants are competing primarily on software and connectivity, the very dimensions where Mercedes remains most vulnerable.

## 5. Strategic Position: Porter's Generic Strategies

Porter's Generic Strategies framework (Porter, 1985) classifies competitive strategies as Cost Leadership, Differentiation, or Focus. Mercedes-Benz has historically pursued **Differentiation** — competing on the basis of superior product attributes (engineering, safety, prestige) rather than price. The TEV strategy represents an evolution toward **Focused Differentiation**, concentrating resources on the highest-margin luxury sub-segments.

The focused differentiation strategy is intended to aid the organisation's strategic goal of achieving superior customer experience. This is logically coherent: if Mercedes cannot win on cost against Chinese manufacturers, and if broad differentiation is being eroded by technology commoditisation, then concentrating on the ultra-luxury niche — where brand heritage and craftsmanship remain decisive — is the rational response.

However, the 2024 results reveal a critical execution failure: sales of top-end cars like the S-Class and Maybach sedans and sport utility vehicles fell 14% in 2024, undermining the company's strategy to push further upmarket by selling more of its most luxurious vehicles to boost profit. This is the central strategic paradox of Mercedes-Benz's current situation: the strategy is directionally sound but is being undermined by the very market conditions (China slowdown, EV demand collapse) that made the upmarket pivot necessary.

The inference is that the Generic Strategy is correct but insufficiently differentiated from what competitors are also pursuing. BMW is following a nearly identical playbook. The strategic imperative is therefore not to abandon focused differentiation but to identify dimensions of differentiation that are genuinely inimitable — specifically, the combination of brand heritage *and* proprietary software/digital services.

## 6. Growth Strategy Analysis: Ansoff Matrix

The Ansoff Matrix (Ansoff, 1957) maps growth options across two dimensions: markets (existing vs. new) and products (existing vs. new). Applying this to Mercedes-Benz's current situation clarifies the trade-offs in its strategic options.

| | Existing Products | New Products |
|---|---|---|
| **Existing Markets** | Market Penetration | Product Development |
| **New Markets** | Market Development | Diversification |

**Current Position**: Mercedes is simultaneously pursuing *Product Development* (new EV platforms, MB.OS) and *Market Penetration* (defending its European and North American base). The China situation represents a *Market Penetration* failure that is forcing a partial retreat.

**EV Strategy as Product Development**: In 2025, Mercedes is doing away with its EQ brand as a standalone entity; going forward, there will be less of a divide between electric and combustion models, both in terms of styling and nomenclature. This is a strategically significant shift: rather than positioning EVs as a separate product category (which failed to generate the expected demand), Mercedes is integrating electrification into its core brand identity. The new CLA is described as "the first in a completely new family of vehicles from Mercedes-Benz," with each new model available with both all-electric and high-tech hybrid powertrains. This multi-powertrain product development strategy reduces the binary risk of betting entirely on BEV adoption rates.

**China as a Market Penetration Challenge**: Sales in China, the premium automaker's biggest single market, fell by 7% in 2024, while EV sales fell 22%. This is not simply a cyclical downturn but a structural shift in competitive dynamics. The Ansoff implication is that Mercedes cannot grow by penetrating China further with its existing product and marketing formula — it requires genuine product adaptation (localised software, China-specific features) to recover ground.

**Diversification via Digital Services**: The MB.OS platform represents a nascent move toward diversification — generating revenue from software and services rather than purely from vehicle sales. An important lever identified by Mercedes is to increase net revenue per unit by raising the proportion of high-end electric vehicles such as Mercedes-Maybach and Mercedes-AMG models, while at the same time taking more direct control over pricing and sales, with rising revenue from digital services further supporting results. This is the most strategically novel element of Mercedes's current plan, and it aligns with the RBV finding that the MB.OS platform is the company's most important emerging capability.

## 7. Synthesis: The Coherent Strategic Diagnosis

Bringing the four frameworks together produces a coherent diagnosis:

1. **Five Forces** shows that competitive rivalry and buyer power are the dominant external threats, driven by Chinese brand ascent and forced price concessions in China.
2. **RBV** shows that Mercedes's strongest assets (brand equity, engineering heritage) are durable but insufficient in a software-defined competitive environment; the MB.OS platform is the critical emerging capability.
3. **Generic Strategies** confirms that Focused Differentiation is the correct strategic posture but that it is being undermined by execution failures (China price erosion, EV misjudgement) and insufficient differentiation from rivals pursuing the same strategy.
4. **Ansoff** shows that the product development path (multi-powertrain CLA family, MB.OS) is the most promising growth vector, while market penetration in China requires product and service localisation rather than price concession.

The root cause of Mercedes-Benz's crisis is a **strategy-execution gap**: the TEV strategy is logically sound given the company's resources and competitive environment, but it has been undermined by (a) premature abandonment of ICE and hybrid products that customers still want, (b) failure to localise sufficiently for China's software-driven luxury market, and (c) a cost structure that has not yet adapted to lower volumes.

The Mercedes-Benz Cars division reported an adjusted EBIT of €8.7bn, a decline from €14.3bn in 2023, primarily due to lower sales volumes — particularly in China — negative net pricing, and an unfavourable model mix. Each of these three causes (volume, pricing, mix) maps directly onto the strategic failures identified above: volume reflects the EV misjudgement; pricing reflects the China buyer-power problem; mix reflects the underperformance of the TEV strategy.

## 8. Recommendations and Action Plan

The following recommendations are derived directly from the strategic analysis above. Each recommendation is warranted by the evidence and connected to a specific framework finding.

### Recommendation 1: Fully Execute the Multi-Powertrain Product Strategy — Do Not Reverse Course

**Warrant**: The RBV analysis shows that brand equity is Mercedes's most durable asset, and the Ansoff analysis shows that product development (not market development or diversification) is the primary growth path. The decision to integrate EVs into the core brand rather than siloing them under "EQ" is strategically correct because it leverages brand equity across all powertrain types.

Mercedes-Benz CEO Ola Källenius stated: "A Mercedes-Benz is a Mercedes-Benz — no matter how it's powered. Every Mercedes is extraordinary. Its status derives from exceptional design, advanced technology, superb refinement and craftsmanship." This framing correctly positions the brand as the constant and the powertrain as a variable — the opposite of the failed EQ strategy, which positioned the powertrain as the brand.

**Action**: Accelerate the rollout of the CLA family across all powertrain variants. Mercedes-Benz is ready for the largest product offensive in its history and plans more than 40 vehicles across its network over the next three years. This pipeline must be executed without the pricing concessions that have damaged brand equity in China — volume recovery should come from product desirability, not discounting.

### Recommendation 2: Invest in China-Specific Software and Digital Services — Not Price Cuts

**Warrant**: The Five Forces analysis shows that buyer power in China is increasing and that price concessions are self-defeating — they erode the brand positioning on which the TEV strategy depends without recovering volume. The RBV analysis identifies MB.OS as the emerging capability that can differentiate Mercedes from Chinese competitors on their own terms (software and connectivity).

Mercedes-Benz's cost-cutting measures include a direct-to-consumer model and digital transformation as key focus areas. In China specifically, the direct-to-consumer model allows Mercedes to control the customer experience and pricing, preventing the dealer-level discounting that has damaged brand equity.

**Action**: Deploy MB.OS with China-specific AI features, local app integrations (WeChat, Baidu Maps, Alipay), and over-the-air update capabilities that match or exceed what Chinese domestic brands offer. This transforms the competitive battle from hardware (where Chinese brands are closing the gap) to software-plus-heritage (where Mercedes has a durable advantage).

### Recommendation 3: Protect the Top-End Vehicle Share Through Scarcity, Not Volume

**Warrant**: The Generic Strategies analysis shows that Focused Differentiation requires genuine scarcity to sustain pricing power. The current situation — where even S-Class and Maybach volumes fell 14% in 2024 — suggests that the TEV strategy is being pursued through volume targets rather than through the scarcity and exclusivity logic that makes luxury brands defensible.

Mercedes-Benz's target of increasing the sales share of its top-end luxury segment by 60% compared to 2019 levels by 2026 should be measured as a *share* of total sales (which protects margin) rather than as an absolute volume target (which encourages discounting to hit numbers). The distinction matters: a 22% TEV share of 2 million units is strategically superior to a 15% TEV share of 2.4 million units, even if the latter generates more absolute revenue.

**Action**: Implement strict allocation policies for Maybach and AMG models in China, explicitly refusing to participate in price wars. Use waiting lists and bespoke customisation (the "individualisation" programmes already available) to reinforce scarcity. This is how Ferrari and Rolls-Royce have maintained pricing power through economic cycles — a model Mercedes should emulate in its top-end sub-brands.

### Recommendation 4: Achieve the Cost Reduction Targets as a Strategic Imperative, Not Merely a Financial One

**Warrant**: The financial analysis shows that margin compression is the immediate threat to Mercedes's ability to fund the product development pipeline that underpins the long-term strategy. Without cost reduction, the company cannot sustain the €10.5 billion annual R&D investment that maintains its engineering edge.

Mercedes-Benz's cost-cutting efforts include new goals: reduce production costs by 10% by 2027, achieve an additional 10% reduction by 2030 (totalling a 20% cut), outsource non-core departments to external service providers, and focus investments on EV production while optimising combustion engine manufacturing. These targets are necessary but not sufficient — they must be achieved without compromising the quality and craftsmanship that constitute the brand's core differentiation.

**Action**: Prioritise cost reduction in supply chain and manufacturing operations (where savings do not affect the customer-facing product) over reductions in R&D and design. The plan complements a strategy already underway since 2020, which aims to reduce costs by 20 percent between 2019 and 2025. The 2025 baseline should be used to reset the cost structure before the major product launch wave peaks.

### Recommendation 5: Monetise MB.OS as a Revenue Stream Independent of Vehicle Volume

**Warrant**: The Ansoff analysis identifies digital services as a diversification opportunity that reduces Mercedes's dependence on vehicle sales volume — which is cyclical and increasingly contested. The RBV analysis identifies MB.OS as a potentially VRIN capability if it can generate proprietary data and recurring revenues.

Mercedes-Benz is confident that its strategic approach to software and hardware development will be the basis for lifetime revenues as well as additional contributions; already in 2022, the company generated more than €1 billion in software-enabled revenues. Rising revenue from digital services will further support results, with Mercedes also working on further reducing variable and fixed costs and cutting the capex share of investments.

**Action**: Establish a separate P&L reporting line for MB.OS-enabled services to make the revenue contribution visible to investors and to create internal accountability. Develop a tiered subscription model (analogous to Apple's services ecosystem) that generates recurring revenue from the existing installed base of Mercedes vehicles — not only from new purchases.

## 10. Conclusion

Mercedes-Benz's strategic crisis is real but not existential. The company possesses the brand equity, engineering capability, and financial scale to navigate the dual disruption of EV demand uncertainty and China market erosion — provided it executes the strategy it has articulated with greater discipline than it has shown in recent years. The central lesson of this analysis is that **the TEV strategy is correct in direction but has been undermined by execution failures**: premature EV commitment, price concessions in China, and a cost structure misaligned with a lower-volume, higher-value business model.

The global luxury car market was valued at $593.8 billion in 2025 and is expected to grow at a CAGR of 6.3% between 2026 and 2035, driven by increasing consumer preference for premium comfort and features. This structural tailwind means that the market Mercedes is targeting will grow significantly over the next decade. The strategic question is not whether the luxury automotive market will be attractive, but whether Mercedes-Benz will be positioned to capture that growth — or whether it will cede it to BMW, Chinese entrants, and software-native competitors.

The five recommendations above — multi-powertrain execution, China software localisation, TEV scarcity management, cost discipline, and MB.OS monetisation — constitute a coherent action plan that addresses each identified failure while preserving and leveraging the durable assets that make Mercedes-Benz one of the world's great brands.
