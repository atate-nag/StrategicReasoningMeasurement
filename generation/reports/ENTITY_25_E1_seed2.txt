# **FROM CONNECTIVITY TO PLATFORM: MTN GROUP'S STRATEGIC PIVOT IN AN ERA OF CURRENCY SHOCK AND FINTECH DISRUPTION**

## A Strategic Case Study of Pan-African Telecoms

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

MTN Group — Africa's largest mobile network operator by subscribers — stands at a strategic inflection point. After three decades of geographic expansion across the continent, the company's core business model faces simultaneous pressure from three directions: macroeconomic shocks (principally naira devaluation in Nigeria), intensifying competitive rivalry from both incumbent telcos and nimble fintech disruptors, and a structural industry shift from voice-and-data connectivity toward platform-based digital services. This case study examines MTN's strategic situation using PESTEL analysis, Porter's Five Forces, the Resource-Based View (RBV), and the Ansoff Growth Matrix. The analysis reveals a coherent but precarious strategic logic: MTN is attempting to escape the commodity trap of telecoms by repositioning as a technology platform company, but the execution of that pivot is unevenly distributed across its 16-market footprint. A set of prioritised recommendations follows.

## 1. CASE BACKGROUND: MTN GROUP IN CONTEXT

MTN Group, headquartered in Johannesburg, South Africa, was founded in 1994 and has grown into the continent's pre-eminent telecoms operator. The group connected 290.9 million subscribers across 16 markets by end-2024, enabled internet access for 157.8 million active data users, and extended broadband coverage to 93% of the population in its markets. Its geographic spread — from South Africa and Nigeria in the south and west, to Uganda and Ghana in the east and centre — gives it unmatched continental scale. MTN competes with Vodacom (205 million subscribers), Airtel Africa (163.1 million), and Orange (160 million).

The company's strategic direction is formalised in its "Ambition 2025" plan, which has now evolved into "Ambition 2030." MTN Group's 'Ambition 2025' strategy is a blueprint for significant expansion, focusing on five key growth areas: connectivity, fintech, digital infrastructure, Ayoba (its super-app), and enterprise services. The overarching aim is a transformation from a traditional mobile network operator (MNO) into what MTN calls a "technology company." MTN's Ambition 2030 streamlines execution into three principal platforms of choice for consumers, homes, and businesses: Connectivity; Fintech; and Digital Infrastructure — with AI leveraged for growth and shared value creation.

Yet this transformation is unfolding against a backdrop of severe financial turbulence. In the year to 31 December 2024, MTN's service revenue of R178 billion was down by some 15% in reported terms, and reported EBITDA (before once-off items) of R60 billion was a third lower than it was in 2023. The primary culprit was currency: basic earnings per share swung by 758 cents in 2024 to a loss of 531 cents, impacted mainly by the sharp devaluation in the naira. Understanding how MTN arrived here — and how it should proceed — requires a structured analysis of the external environment, competitive forces, and internal capabilities.

## 2. EXTERNAL ENVIRONMENT ANALYSIS: PESTEL

### 2.1 Political

The African political landscape presents both opportunity and acute risk for MTN. The company operates across 16 sovereign states, each with its own regulatory regime, licensing authority, and political risk profile. In Nigeria — MTN's single largest market — political decisions around currency management have had catastrophic near-term financial consequences. MTN Nigeria's record revenue of N3.36 trillion for 2024 was overshadowed by a loss after tax of N400.44 billion. The naira depreciated to N1,535/$ by the end of 2024 from N907.1/$ as of 31 December 2023 — a political-economic decision by the Nigerian government to float the currency that directly translated into billions of rands of forex losses at the group level.

Beyond Nigeria, MTN has had to navigate outright political hostility. MTN's offices in Guinea-Conakry were sealed by the local post and telecommunications regulator over a licensing payment issue in January 2024, and the company ultimately agreed to sell its Guinea-Conakry and Guinea-Bissau operations for a consideration of $1 each, as both businesses were in net liability positions. Conflict in Sudan has also materially affected results, with MTN Sudan excluded from group guidance calculations. These episodes illustrate a core political risk: multi-market exposure amplifies idiosyncratic country risk, and no portfolio diversification fully eliminates it.

Regulatory fragmentation also shapes the fintech pivot. Previously, MTN Nigeria's fintech operations sat between telecom and financial regulation. The new restructuring clearly separates responsibilities — telecom under the Nigerian Communications Commission (NCC), fintech under the Central Bank of Nigeria (CBN) — reducing compliance complexity and potential policy friction. This dual-regulator problem is replicated across MTN's footprint, making pan-African fintech scaling a regulatory marathon as much as a commercial one.

### 2.2 Economic

Africa's macroeconomic environment is characterised by high growth potential but significant volatility. In 2024, mobile technologies and services generated 7.7% of Africa's GDP, amounting to $220 billion in economic value, and the mobile industry's economic contribution is projected to reach $270 billion by 2030. This trajectory represents a structural growth tailwind for telecoms.

However, the near-term economic reality is punishing. Further financial risks result from regulatory complexities, including high taxation and spectrum licensing fees, as well as fluctuating foreign exchange rates. The tax burden on operators is particularly severe: the Average Effective Tax Rate (AETR) for African MNOs varies significantly across countries, ranging from 33% in Ethiopia to 118% in Niger, with special taxes and fees representing a large share — illustrating taxation by regulation. This fiscal squeeze compresses the margins available to fund the very infrastructure investment that would drive future growth.

For MTN specifically, currency risk is the dominant economic variable. Nigeria is MTN's biggest market, and the naira slump had a significant impact on the group's service revenue, which dropped by 20.8% year-on-year to R85.3 billion in H1 2024. The group's reported results thus diverge sharply from its underlying operational performance: in constant currency terms, Group service revenue increased by 13.8%, with MTN Nigeria growing by 35.6% and MTN Ghana by 34.3%. This divergence between constant-currency operational health and reported financial distress is the central economic tension MTN must manage.

### 2.3 Social

Africa's demographic and social profile is uniquely favourable for digital services growth. The continent has the world's youngest and fastest-growing population, with hundreds of millions of people entering the formal economy and digital ecosystem for the first time. While 416 million people are now using mobile internet in Africa, almost 75% of the population remain unconnected, and 960 million people in Africa — 64% of the population — are not using mobile internet despite living in an area where coverage is available.

This "usage gap" is socially determined: barriers include device affordability, digital literacy, and the relevance of available content. Despite increasing mobile coverage, Africa faces a 60% usage gap, with many people and micro-businesses not using mobile internet even when network availability exists. For MTN, this is both a risk (existing infrastructure is under-monetised) and an opportunity (a vast addressable market awaits if affordability and relevance barriers can be lowered).

The social dimension of financial inclusion is equally important. Over 350 million adults in sub-Saharan Africa are still unbanked, and mobile money represents the primary mechanism for their financial inclusion. This creates a social licence for telco-led fintech that governments are broadly supportive of, even as they impose regulatory burdens.

### 2.4 Technological

The technological environment presents African telcos with a paradox: the opportunity to leapfrog legacy infrastructure is real, but the capital requirements are enormous. With 4G adoption expected to reach 50% by 2030, telcos have a significant opportunity to upgrade networks and increase smartphone adoption. In rural and underserved areas, Fixed Wireless Access (FWA), particularly 5G FWA, is emerging as a cost-effective alternative to fibre, offering fibre-like speeds without the high cost and logistical challenges of deploying terrestrial networks in remote regions.

MTN has responded with significant infrastructure investment. Through its Bayobab subsidiary, MTN partnered with Africa50 to invest up to US$320 million connecting ten African countries between 2023 and 2025, contributing to a target of 135,000km of proprietary fibre. On the 5G front, Egypt conducted a major 5G spectrum auction in October 2024, generating $550 million, illustrating the capital intensity of spectrum acquisition. The technological arms race is expensive, and the returns are long-dated.

Artificial intelligence is emerging as the next technological frontier. Africa's telecommunications sector is poised for growth as demand for connectivity and data services increases, and this environment creates fertile ground for leveraging generative AI to drive innovation and improve service delivery. MTN has begun embedding AI into its fintech operations: MTN has established a dedicated fintech AI lab in Kigali, Rwanda, while Airtel has partnered with various fintech startups to incorporate machine learning into its services.

### 2.5 Environmental

Infrastructure deployment in Africa faces significant environmental constraints. Unreliable national power grids force operators to rely on diesel generators, raising operating costs and carbon footprints. MTN's tower-sharing strategy — having sold tower portfolios to IHS Towers and American Tower Corporation — partially addresses this by spreading infrastructure costs. Airtel Africa's lease extension with American Tower Corporation (ATC), covering over 7,100 sites, marks a commitment to renewable energy and reduced greenhouse gas emissions — a model MTN has pursued similarly. Subsea cable resilience is another environmental concern: on 14 March 2024, several undersea cables were cut, disrupting service for the Africa Coast to Europe; Bayobab repaired the disabled ACE and WASC lines during the following week. Climate-related infrastructure vulnerability is a growing operational risk.

### 2.6 Legal

The legal environment is characterised by regulatory multiplicity and unpredictability. Deep concern over compliance burdens comes across clearly in the latest EY CEO Outlook Pulse, with 61% of telco leaders believing regulatory risks will significantly impact their business's performance over the coming year. Recent merger reviews have been contentious in some markets, and regulatory sentiment toward consolidation remains unpredictable. Tax frameworks are also in flux, with base erosion and profit shifting (BEPS) 2.0 rules coming into force in 2024.

For MTN specifically, the legal challenge of separating fintech from telecoms across 16 jurisdictions — each with different licensing frameworks, capital requirements, and ownership rules — is formidable. MTN Group is accelerating the separation of its mobile money business in Nigeria and Uganda as part of a broader strategy to attract fresh investor capital and unlock the full value of one of Africa's fastest-growing fintech operations, finalising the spin-off of its fintech subsidiaries in both countries to create standalone entities.

**PESTEL Summary:** The macro-environment presents a high-opportunity, high-volatility profile. Structural tailwinds (demographics, financial inclusion, digital growth) are powerful but offset by acute currency risk, regulatory fragmentation, and fiscal extraction. The net strategic implication is that MTN must build resilience against short-term shocks while simultaneously investing in long-term platform positioning.

## 3. INDUSTRY ANALYSIS: PORTER'S FIVE FORCES

### Force 1: Threat of New Entrants — *Moderate to Low*

The capital intensity of telecoms infrastructure — spectrum licences, base stations, fibre networks — creates substantial barriers to entry for traditional MNO competition. However, the fintech layer of the industry faces a very different entry dynamic. Startups such as Moniepoint, PalmPay, and OPay are taking advantage of fintechs' narrower licences to blitzscale in Nigeria, with their large customer bases and revenues growing at high cumulative annual rates. These entrants do not need to build a network — they ride on existing infrastructure while competing for the high-margin financial services revenue that MTN is trying to capture. The threat of new entrants is therefore *low* for core connectivity but *high* for adjacent digital services.

### Force 2: Bargaining Power of Suppliers — *Moderate*

MTN's key suppliers include network equipment vendors (Ericsson, Huawei, Nokia), tower companies (IHS Towers, ATC), and subsea cable consortia. The concentration of global network equipment suppliers gives vendors some leverage, particularly for specialised 5G equipment. Tower companies, having acquired MTN's passive infrastructure, now effectively act as landlords: MTN Nigeria recently concluded the renegotiation of its tower contracts with IHS and ATC, with a revised allocation of approximately 3,500 sites between the two companies. This renegotiation illustrates supplier power — but also MTN's countervailing ability to play suppliers against each other.

### Force 3: Bargaining Power of Buyers — *Increasing*

Consumer switching costs in telecoms have historically been moderate, anchored by number portability friction and network quality differentials. However, as data becomes commoditised and services converge across operators, buyer power is rising. Price competition in data is intensifying: African telcos operate in fragmented markets with high costs and fewer opportunities to consolidate infrastructure, and many operators, even those with a substantial market share, struggle to cover their capital costs. In fintech specifically, buyer power is high: customers can hold mobile money accounts with multiple providers simultaneously, and switching between wallets is frictionless.

### Force 4: Threat of Substitutes — *High and Rising*

The most significant substitution threat comes from over-the-top (OTT) platforms (WhatsApp, Meta, Google) that erode voice and SMS revenue by offering free communication over data pipes. This is already reflected in MTN's results: group data service revenue increased by 17.2% in H1 2024; fintech service revenue climbed by 7.3%; while voice revenue decreased by 33.7%. Voice — historically the revenue backbone — is in structural decline. In fintech, substitutes include bank-issued digital wallets, international remittance platforms (Western Union, Wave), and crypto-adjacent payment solutions.

### Force 5: Competitive Rivalry — *Intense*

Unlike M-Pesa, which is strongest in a small number of markets, MTN's strategy has focused on scale across multiple jurisdictions, supported by regulatory separation of fintech operations in countries such as Ghana, Nigeria, and Uganda. This multi-market rivalry pits MTN against Airtel Africa in overlapping geographies across East and West Africa, against Orange in Francophone markets, and against Safaricom (via M-Pesa) in East Africa. Airtel Africa's financial inclusion strategy, focused on underserved markets, led to a transaction volume rise to $128 billion annually, with mobile money now contributing 19.6% of the company's total revenue — demonstrating that the fintech prize is already being actively contested.

**Five Forces Summary:** The industry structure is unfavourable for pure-play MNOs but creates strategic space for operators capable of building defensible platform ecosystems. The key strategic imperative is to shift competitive battleground from commoditised connectivity (where rivalry is intense and margins thin) to platform services (where network effects and data assets can create durable advantage).

## 4. INTERNAL CAPABILITY ANALYSIS: THE RESOURCE-BASED VIEW (RBV)

The RBV framework (Barney, 1991) holds that sustainable competitive advantage derives from resources and capabilities that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-Substitutable (VRIN). MTN's strategic assets can be evaluated against these criteria:

### 4.1 Subscriber Scale and Network Infrastructure

MTN's 291 million subscriber base across 16 markets is its most foundational resource. MTN hopes to solidify its position as "Africa's largest mobile network operator," competing with Vodacom (205 million), Airtel Africa (163.1 million), and Orange (160 million). This scale is *valuable* (it generates revenue and creates a platform for adjacent services), *rare* (no other operator matches its pan-African footprint), and *difficult to imitate* (decades of licence acquisition, infrastructure build, and brand investment). The Bayobab fibre network — targeting 135,000km of proprietary fibre — deepens this infrastructure moat.

### 4.2 MoMo Fintech Platform

The MoMo mobile money platform is MTN's most strategically significant emerging asset. MTN Group's MoMo has been swiftly gaining traction as a pioneering fintech player across the African continent, and Mastercard's $200 million investment values the fintech segment at $5.2 billion on a cash and debt-free basis. The platform processes enormous transaction volumes: the volume of fintech transactions on MTN's networks rose by 15% to more than 20 billion, valued at over US$320 billion, in 2024. MoMo's reach — 60 million active wallets across 24 African nations — constitutes a *rare* and *inimitable* asset because it combines network infrastructure access with a trusted consumer brand and an established agent network that would take years and billions to replicate.

However, MoMo's VRIN status is *contingent* on market structure. In Nigeria, the platform's competitive advantage is significantly eroded by regulatory neutrality that allows fintech startups to compete on equal terms. Six years after MTN launched its financial services push in Nigeria, adoption remains sluggish. Despite strong brand equity and a large agent network, revenue from mobile money remains modest. This illustrates a critical RBV nuance: resources that are VRIN in one context (Uganda, Ghana) may not be in another (Nigeria), depending on the institutional environment.

### 4.3 Brand and Trust Capital

MTN's brand is Africa's most valuable telecommunications brand, built over three decades of operation across diverse and challenging markets. This *intangible* resource is *valuable* (it lowers customer acquisition costs and supports premium pricing), *rare* (no competitor has equivalent pan-African brand recognition), and *inimitable* (brand trust is a function of accumulated experience and cannot be purchased). Central to MTN's digital-first strategy is the rollout of three dedicated apps for consumers, businesses, and agents, with mini-app ecosystems offering services from credit and savings to business tools and education — a strategy that leverages brand trust to drive adoption of new digital services.

### 4.4 Dynamic Capabilities: Strategic Reconfiguration

Beyond static VRIN resources, MTN's most important capability may be its *dynamic capability* — its ability to reconfigure its asset base in response to environmental change (Teece, Pisano & Shuen, 1997). The structural separation of Bayobab (digital infrastructure), MoMo (fintech), and core telecoms represents exactly this kind of deliberate reconfiguration: MTN is unbundling its vertically integrated model to unlock trapped value, attract specialist investors, and create focused management teams. The EY CEO Outlook Pulse survey underlines the continued focus on infrastructure carve-outs, with 41% of telco CEOs saying they'll pursue divestments, spin-offs, and IPOs in the coming year and 61% seeking to form joint ventures or strategic alliances — a global trend that MTN is executing at scale.

**RBV Summary:** MTN possesses genuine VRIN resources in subscriber scale, infrastructure, brand, and the MoMo platform. Its dynamic capability in strategic reconfiguration is a further source of advantage. The critical vulnerability is the *market-specificity* of these advantages: they are strongest in markets where MTN has regulatory incumbency and weakest where platform neutrality enables fintech competition.

## 5. GROWTH STRATEGY ANALYSIS: THE ANSOFF MATRIX

The Ansoff Product/Market Growth Matrix provides a structured lens for evaluating MTN's strategic options across its four quadrants:

### 5.1 Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets)

MTN's primary penetration opportunity lies in closing the usage gap — converting covered but unconnected populations into active data and fintech users. To encourage adoption, telcos can invest in digital-literacy programmes, localised content, and online safety initiatives. Because smartphone affordability remains a barrier, operators can also consider supporting uptake through device financing, instalment payment plans, and sourcing of low-cost smartphones. MTN's Ambition 2025 targets — including a 15% increase in mobile money users in key markets — reflect a penetration-first logic in established geographies.

This is the *lowest-risk* quadrant of the Ansoff matrix and should be the primary near-term focus given the financial constraints imposed by naira devaluation. MTN has simplified its balance sheet, reducing net-debt-to-EBITDA leverage to 0.5x from 0.7x in 2024, while maintaining robust liquidity headroom of R39.1 billion — a financial position that supports disciplined penetration investment without excessive leverage.

### 5.2 Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets)

MTN has historically pursued market development aggressively, expanding its MNO footprint across Africa. However, the current strategic direction is *rationalisation rather than expansion*: the disposals of Guinea-Bissau and Guinea-Conakry signal a deliberate retreat from sub-scale, high-risk markets. The market development opportunity now lies in *fintech geography* rather than *telecoms geography*. In April 2024, MTN's MoMo service expanded to 10 new countries, establishing 25 new wallet corridors, facilitating international remittances and connecting over 200 million wallets across 24 African nations. This is market development through the fintech product, piggybacking on existing telecoms infrastructure.

### 5.3 Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets)

Product development is MTN's most active strategic quadrant. The evolution from basic mobile money (wallet) to advanced fintech services (credit, insurance, savings, merchant payments) represents product development within the existing subscriber base. Fintech advanced services revenue — including banktech, remittance, and payments — grew by 52% in 2024. The Mastercard partnership accelerates this: Mastercard's involvement brings global infrastructure, regulatory relationships, and merchant acceptance capabilities that MoMo would struggle to build on its own timeline.

The enterprise segment represents another product development vector. MTN's B2B cloud, IoT, and connectivity services target existing corporate customers with new digital infrastructure products — a logical adjacency from its core network assets.

### 5.4 Diversification (New Products, New Markets)

Diversification is the highest-risk quadrant. MTN's Ayoba super-app — a messaging and content platform — represents a genuine diversification attempt, entering a market dominated by Meta's WhatsApp. The risk here is significant: competing with global platform giants on their home turf (social communication and content) requires product excellence and network effects that MTN's subscriber base alone cannot guarantee. This quadrant should be approached with caution and clear milestones for continued investment.

**Ansoff Summary:** MTN's strategic portfolio spans all four quadrants, but the risk-adjusted priority should be: (1) Market Penetration — deepening usage in existing markets; (2) Product Development — scaling fintech services within the subscriber base; (3) Market Development — extending fintech reach via wallet corridors; (4) Diversification — Ayoba and enterprise services, pursued selectively with clear performance gates.

## 6. THE CENTRAL STRATEGIC TENSION: PLATFORM AMBITION VERSUS MARKET FRAGMENTATION

The analysis above converges on a central strategic tension that MTN must resolve. The company's "platform business" ambition — becoming Africa's leading integrated digital services provider — requires *scale*, *standardisation*, and *network effects*. But MTN operates in 16 sovereign markets, each with different regulatory frameworks, currency risks, competitive dynamics, and consumer behaviours. This is the fundamental contradiction between the platform logic (which demands integration) and the African operating reality (which demands localisation).

This tension is most sharply visible in Nigeria. In Nigeria, telcos face tougher competition and operate under rules that promote platform neutrality. Meanwhile, fintechs have surged — startups such as Moniepoint, PalmPay, and OPay are blitzscaling, with their revenues growing at high cumulative annual rates. Nigeria is MTN's largest revenue market, yet it is also the market where MTN's platform advantage is most contested. The structural MoMo separation — targeting full completion by December 31, 2026, with MTN Nigeria emerging as a more streamlined telecom operator and MoMo as a standalone financial holding company — is the right response, but it is a multi-year regulatory process that leaves MTN exposed to competitive erosion in the interim.

The continent's growing population, expanding digital landscape, rising demand for mobile services, and emerging appetite for new technologies allow the telco sector to leapfrog outdated infrastructure and create new value. But in a classic case of "What got you here won't get you there," telco strategies of today may not be sufficient to win the future. MTN has correctly diagnosed this challenge. The question is whether it can execute the transformation fast enough, across enough markets, before the window closes.

## 7. RECOMMENDATIONS AND ACTION PLAN

The following recommendations are organised by strategic priority and time horizon. They follow directly from the analytical chain established above: the PESTEL analysis identifies the macro constraints and opportunities; Porter's Five Forces identifies the competitive dynamics; the RBV identifies the assets to be leveraged; and the Ansoff Matrix identifies the growth pathways. Together, they point to a coherent set of actions.

### Recommendation 1: Implement a Systematic Currency Hedging and Local-Currency Debt Strategy (Immediate — 0–12 months)

**Rationale:** The naira devaluation of 2023–2024 was the single largest destroyer of MTN Group value in recent history. MTN Nigeria's record revenue of N3.36 trillion for 2024 was overshadowed by a loss after tax of N400.44 billion, driven by forex losses that overwhelmed exceptional operational performance. This is a structural vulnerability, not a one-off event — fluctuating foreign exchange rates are a persistent financial risk for African telcos.

**Action:** MTN should accelerate the localisation of its capital structure in high-risk currency markets (Nigeria, Ghana, Ethiopia), replacing USD-denominated debt with local-currency instruments wherever feasible. Revenue from these markets should be reinvested locally in naira/cedi/birr-denominated capital expenditure to reduce the translation exposure at group level. Where hedging instruments are available, they should be systematically employed for 12–24 month rolling horizons. This will not eliminate currency risk but will reduce the magnitude of single-period shocks.

**Metric:** Target reduction of USD-denominated debt in Nigeria to below 20% of total Nigerian balance sheet within 18 months.

---

### Recommendation 2: Accelerate MoMo Structural Separation and Pursue an African-Listed IPO (12–24 months)

**Rationale:** The MoMo platform is MTN's most valuable strategic asset, yet it is currently under-valued within the group's reported financials because currency losses obscure the fintech segment's underlying performance. Mastercard's $200 million investment values the fintech segment at $5.2 billion on a cash and debt-free basis — a valuation that is invisible in MTN's depressed group market capitalisation. Structural separation unlocks this value.

MTN Group is finalising the spin-off of its fintech subsidiaries in Nigeria and Uganda, creating standalone entities to attract fresh investor capital. This process should be accelerated, and the resulting entity should be listed on both the Johannesburg Stock Exchange and a major African exchange (Lagos, Nairobi, or Accra) to maximise local investor participation and regulatory goodwill.

**Action:** Complete Nigeria and Uganda separations by the stated December 2026 deadline. Initiate Ghana and Rwanda separations concurrently. Commission a pan-African fintech holding structure that can serve as the vehicle for a dual-listed IPO by 2027. The Mastercard partnership provides the global payments rails; a local listing provides the African legitimacy and retail investor base.

**Metric:** MoMo standalone entity valued at ≥$7 billion at IPO, reflecting organic growth from the $5.2 billion Mastercard benchmark.

---

### Recommendation 3: Adopt a Differentiated Fintech Strategy by Market Tier (12–36 months)

**Rationale:** MTN's attempt to apply a uniform fintech model across 16 markets ignores the profound differences in competitive structure and regulatory environment. The reasons for MTN's mobile money underperformance in Nigeria are less about consumer demand or product quality and more about regulation and market structure — a finding that should reshape how MTN allocates its fintech investment.

**Action:** MTN should segment its markets into three tiers for fintech strategy:

- **Tier 1 — Dominant Markets** (Uganda, Rwanda, Ghana): Where MoMo has regulatory incumbency and market leadership, invest aggressively in product depth — credit, savings, insurance, merchant payments. The rollout of three dedicated apps for consumers, businesses, and agents, with mini-app ecosystems offering services from credit to education, should be prioritised here first.

- **Tier 2 — Contested Markets** (Nigeria): Where fintech competition is intense and regulatory neutrality prevails, adopt a partnership-first model rather than a direct-to-consumer build. Partner with leading fintechs (Moniepoint, Flutterwave) to provide infrastructure-as-a-service — leveraging MTN's network and agent distribution without competing head-to-head on product. This converts a competitive threat into a revenue stream.

- **Tier 3 — Emerging Markets** (DRC, Ethiopia, smaller markets): Focus on basic mobile money penetration and agent network expansion before attempting product sophistication.

**Metric:** Tier 1 MoMo ARPU (Average Revenue Per User) to increase by 30% within 24 months through advanced services; Tier 2 infrastructure-as-a-service revenue to reach $50 million within 36 months.

### Recommendation 4: Double Down on Infrastructure as a Competitive Moat via Open-Access Bayobab (24–48 months)

**Rationale:** By sharing towers, fibre networks, and energy resources, operators can reduce deployment costs and accelerate coverage expansion. MTN's Bayobab subsidiary — with its target of 135,000km of proprietary fibre and its role in the 2Africa subsea cable consortium — positions MTN as Africa's backbone infrastructure provider. This is a *genuinely inimitable* asset that competitors cannot quickly replicate.

**Action:** MTN should transition Bayobab to an explicit open-access wholesale model, selling capacity to competitors, hyperscalers (AWS, Google, Microsoft), and content delivery networks. Introducing wholesale open-access networks (WOANs) could foster competition and drive down broadband costs, making connectivity more accessible — and MTN should position itself as the provider of that open-access infrastructure rather than a reluctant participant. This transforms a potential regulatory imposition into a commercial strategy: MTN earns infrastructure revenue regardless of which retail operator wins the customer.

The $10 billion investment commitment through 2030 — MTN plans to inject $2 billion annually as part of its expansion initiative — should be explicitly ring-fenced for Bayobab, with a target of third-party revenue representing at least 30% of Bayobab's total revenue by 2028.

**Metric:** Bayobab third-party revenue at 30% of total by 2028; 135,000km fibre target met by 2026.

---

### Recommendation 5: Establish a Portfolio Rationalisation Discipline — Exit Sub-Scale, High-Risk Markets (Ongoing)

**Rationale:** MTN's multi-market exposure amplifies idiosyncratic risk without proportionate reward in its smallest markets. The Guinea-Bissau and Guinea-Conakry disposals demonstrate the willingness to exit — but the discipline should be formalised. MTN has exited certain markets as part of its cost optimisation strategy, and this should continue systematically.

**Action:** Establish a formal portfolio review framework that evaluates each market on three dimensions: (1) scale (minimum subscriber threshold of 5 million to justify standalone operations); (2) political risk (regulatory stability score); (3) strategic fit (does the market contribute to the fintech or infrastructure platform?). Markets failing on two or more dimensions should be prioritised for disposal or merger with a regional partner. Proceeds from disposals should be redeployed into Tier 1 fintech markets and Bayobab infrastructure.

**Metric:** Reduce active market count from 16 to 12 by 2028, concentrating capital in highest-return geographies.

---

### Recommendation 6: Build AI-Driven Network and Customer Intelligence as a Cross-Cutting Capability (24–48 months)

**Rationale:** Globally, telco operators have shown a measured interest in AI, primarily using it for basic applications such as customer-service chatbots and predictive maintenance — but the opportunity is far larger. MTN's data asset — behavioural, transactional, and network data from 291 million subscribers — is a *non-substitutable* resource that could power credit scoring, churn prediction, network optimisation, and personalised service delivery.

**Action:** Expand the Kigali AI lab into a Group-level AI Centre of Excellence with a mandate across three domains: (1) *Network AI* — predictive maintenance and traffic optimisation to reduce capex waste; (2) *Fintech AI* — alternative credit scoring using mobile usage data to expand MoMo lending to the unbanked (a key financial inclusion lever); (3) *Commercial AI* — personalised offers and churn reduction. African governments have begun developing national AI strategies to capitalise on the technology's potential as a tool for national development — MTN should actively engage with these strategies to position itself as a preferred national AI infrastructure partner.

**Metric:** AI-driven credit scoring deployed in three Tier 1 markets by 2026; network capex efficiency improved by 15% through AI-driven planning by 2027.

## 8. COHERENCE CHECK: THE STRATEGIC LOGIC

The six recommendations form a coherent strategic logic, not a disconnected list:

1. **Currency hedging** (Rec. 1) addresses the immediate financial vulnerability that constrains investment capacity.
2. **MoMo separation and IPO** (Rec. 2) unlocks the capital and investor attention needed to fund the fintech platform at scale.
3. **Differentiated fintech strategy** (Rec. 3) ensures that capital is deployed where MTN's VRIN advantages are strongest, not dissipated in markets where they are weakest.
4. **Open-access Bayobab** (Rec. 4) monetises the infrastructure moat regardless of retail competitive outcomes, providing a stable revenue floor.
5. **Portfolio rationalisation** (Rec. 5) concentrates management attention and capital on the markets that matter, reducing the complexity that has historically slowed execution.
6. **AI capability** (Rec. 6) builds the dynamic capability that will sustain competitive advantage across all platforms as the industry evolves.

Together, these recommendations operationalise MTN's stated ambition to become a technology platform company — but with a discipline that the current "Ambition 2025" strategy, with its five simultaneous growth vectors, has sometimes lacked. The chain runs from external environment analysis → competitive positioning → internal capability leveraging → growth strategy → concrete, measurable actions.

## 9. CONCLUSION

MTN Group's strategic situation is one of genuine complexity: a company with exceptional continental assets, a compelling platform vision, and a track record of operational execution, undermined by macroeconomic forces largely beyond its control. MTN Group posted excellent operational and financial results for 2025, delivering significantly on its Ambition 2025 strategy, serving more than 307 million voice, 172 million data, and 70 million Mobile Money customers — evidence that the underlying business is sound. The challenge is to insulate that operational performance from currency shocks, regulatory fragmentation, and the accelerating competitive threat from fintech disruptors.

The analysis presented here — grounded in PESTEL, Porter's Five Forces, the Resource-Based View, and the Ansoff Matrix — converges on a single strategic imperative: MTN must complete its transformation from a multi-market mobile network operator into a focused platform business, faster and more selectively than its current strategy implies. The platform model — with MoMo as the fintech engine, Bayobab as the infrastructure backbone, and AI as the intelligence layer — is the right destination. The recommendations above chart a credible route to get there.

To reach the next 500 million users on the continent and unlock an estimated $170 billion in GDP in 2030, African telcos will likely need to make new strategic choices. For MTN, those choices are now.
