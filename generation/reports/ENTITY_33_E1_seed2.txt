## 1. INTRODUCTION & CASE OVERVIEW

Rocket Lab Corporation (Nasdaq: RKLB) is a New Zealand-founded, California-headquartered aerospace company that has grown from a small satellite launch specialist into one of the most strategically complex actors in the commercial space economy. Founded in 2006 by Sir Peter Beck, the company went public via SPAC merger in 2021 and has since pursued an ambitious strategy of vertical integration. "What we are really focused on here is building an end-to-end space company," says Founder and CEO Sir Peter Beck.

The **strategic situation** this case examines is the tension between two simultaneous strategic imperatives: (1) **defending and monetising an established niche** in dedicated small-satellite launch while (2) **executing a high-risk, capital-intensive transition** into medium-lift launch and space systems at scale — all while remaining unprofitable and operating in a market dominated by SpaceX. The central question is: *How should Rocket Lab allocate its finite resources and manage strategic risk across these competing horizons to achieve sustainable competitive advantage?*

## 2. INDUSTRY & MACRO-ENVIRONMENTAL CONTEXT: PESTEL ANALYSIS

A PESTEL analysis establishes the external forces shaping Rocket Lab's strategic environment. These forces are not merely background — they directly determine which strategic moves are viable and which are foreclosed.

### Political

The commercial space sector is deeply shaped by government policy and procurement. Rocket Lab was awarded an $816 million prime contract by the Space Development Agency (SDA) to design and manufacture 18 satellites for the Tracking Layer Tranche 3 (TRKT3) programme under the Proliferated Warfighter Space Architecture. The $816 million contract follows a separate $515 million contract, both awarded by the U.S. Space Development Agency. These contracts reflect a deliberate U.S. government policy to diversify space industrial capacity away from legacy primes toward agile "New Space" companies. Rocket Lab currently operates a private launch complex in Mahia, New Zealand, supported by a bilateral treaty between the United States and New Zealand governments that enables use of U.S. launch and spacecraft technology for launches at LC-1 that otherwise would not be permitted for launches from foreign soil. This treaty provides a competitive advantage over other companies launching rockets from outside the U.S. that do not have the benefit of such a treaty.

### Economic

The global space launch services market size was estimated at USD 14.94 billion in 2023 and is projected to reach USD 41.31 billion by 2030, growing at a CAGR of 14.6% from 2024 to 2030. The commercial space launch market was valued at USD 8.2 billion in 2024 and is estimated to grow at a CAGR of over 14.6% from 2025 to 2034, driven by growth in small satellite launch demand. This sustained macro-tailwind means Rocket Lab is operating in a structurally expanding addressable market. However, as will be shown in the financial analysis, the company posted significant operating losses, reflecting the cost of simultaneously operating a launch business and funding the Neutron programme — a dual-burn challenge that creates ongoing capital risk.

### Societal & Technological

The commercial segment of the small satellite market is expected to record the highest CAGR of 31.0% during the forecast period. This structural shift is the foundation of Rocket Lab's original market thesis: as satellites shrink, demand for dedicated, schedule-certain small-satellite launch grows. Technologically, the shift toward reusable launch vehicles (pioneered by SpaceX) has reset cost expectations across the industry. The space industry is experiencing rapid evolution, with small satellites dominating launches.

### Legal

Export control regulations (ITAR/EAR) are a critical constraint. The New Zealand–U.S. bilateral treaty is a legal asset that competitors without equivalent arrangements cannot easily replicate. By operating its own private launch complex, Rocket Lab does not have to share the launch complex with other launch providers and, subject to obtaining required regulatory clearances, has complete control over launch schedule and availability. LC-1 serves as the high-volume launch complex, with two launch pads capable of supporting up to 120 missions every year.

## 3. INDUSTRY STRUCTURE: PORTER'S FIVE FORCES

Porter's Five Forces (1980) reveals that the commercial launch industry has an unusual competitive structure: high barriers to entry coexist with an overwhelming incumbent advantage, creating a market simultaneously protected from most new entrants yet dominated by a single player.

### Force 1: Threat of New Entrants — *Moderate but Declining*

Developing an orbital launch vehicle requires billions in capital, years of development time, specialist engineering talent, and regulatory licences across multiple jurisdictions. The proliferation of venture capital into "New Space" between 2015 and 2022 temporarily lowered the effective capital barrier, producing a wave of entrants (Relativity Space, ABL Space, Astra). Most have since failed or pivoted. Rocket Lab's private launch complexes with LC-1 capable of supporting up to 120 missions per year represent infrastructure barriers that new entrants cannot quickly replicate. The threat of new entrants is therefore moderate in the near term but structurally declining as the "venture-funded rocket startup" wave recedes.

### Force 2: Bargaining Power of Suppliers — *Moderate, Actively Managed*

Rocket Lab has strategically reduced supplier power through vertical integration. By vertically integrating these capabilities, Rocket Lab aims to reduce external dependencies, lower costs, accelerate production timelines, and provide greater control over quality and performance for both its own programmes and its customers. The Motiv acquisition brings in-house the design and manufacturing of critical spacecraft mechanisms such as SADAs, antenna and propulsion gimbals, filter wheels, focus mechanisms, and precision drive electronics, completing a key element of Rocket Lab's strategy to manufacture satellites at constellation scale. This systematic internalisation directly weakens supplier leverage.

### Force 3: Bargaining Power of Buyers — *Moderate, Offset by Pricing Power*

Buyers fall into two categories: government and commercial. Government customers offer large, sticky contracts but exercise monopsony-like pricing power in negotiations. Commercial satellite operators can, in principle, choose between Rocket Lab's dedicated Electron launches or SpaceX's Transporter rideshare missions. However, dedicated launch offers schedule certainty and orbital precision that rideshare cannot match. Rocket Lab signed several new Electron launch contracts in Q3 2024 with a total value of $55 million, demonstrating an increased average selling price of $8.4 million and showcasing a 67% pricing increase since the rocket's debut launch. This demonstrated pricing power significantly offsets buyer leverage.

### Force 4: Threat of Substitutes — *High*

This is the most acute competitive force Rocket Lab faces. SpaceX is a primary competitor, particularly in the medium-lift market with its Falcon 9. In 2023, SpaceX accounted for approximately 90% of total launches by U.S.-based companies. For cost-sensitive operators, SpaceX's rideshare programme is a powerful substitute. As SpaceX's Starship potentially enables even cheaper rideshare, the substitute threat will intensify. The strategic response — dedicated launch with schedule certainty, precise orbital insertion, and no co-manifesting risk — is real but serves a specific, premium segment.

### Force 5: Competitive Rivalry — *High and Asymmetric*

Rocket Lab's Electron rocket has established itself as the Western world's second most frequently launched orbital rocket, behind only SpaceX's Falcon 9. However, the gap to SpaceX remains vast. With 50+ successful Electron launches, a Neutron medium-lift vehicle in development, and a thriving space systems division, Rocket Lab has quietly become the most credible commercial competitor to SpaceX. Among peers, Firefly Aerospace and others compete in overlapping segments. Rivalry is therefore intense at the top (SpaceX) and fragmented at the bottom (small launch peers).

**Summary**: The Five Forces analysis reveals that Rocket Lab operates in a market with structurally attractive long-run dynamics (declining new entrant threat, growing demand) but faces an asymmetric competitive threat from SpaceX that no purely organic strategy can neutralise. This finding is critical to the strategic recommendations.

## 4. INTERNAL ANALYSIS: RESOURCES, CAPABILITIES & THE VRIO FRAMEWORK

The Resource-Based View (Barney, 1991) holds that sustainable competitive advantage derives from resources and capabilities that are **Valuable, Rare, Costly to Imitate, and Organised** to capture value (VRIO). Applying this framework to Rocket Lab reveals a hierarchy of competitive assets.

### Tangible Resources

- **Launch Infrastructure**: Two private launch complexes (LC-1 New Zealand with two pads; LC-2 Virginia). LC-1 is treaty-protected and capable of up to 120 annual missions.
- **Contract Backlog**: Rocket Lab has accumulated over $1 billion in contracts, split evenly between government and commercial customers.

### Intangible Resources & Capabilities — VRIO Table

| Resource/Capability | Valuable? | Rare? | Costly to Imitate? | Organised? | Competitive Implication |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Electron track record (50+ successful orbital launches) | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | **Sustained Advantage** |
| End-to-end vertical integration (components → spacecraft → launch) | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | **Sustained Advantage** |
| U.S.–NZ bilateral treaty (foreign launch with U.S. tech) | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | **Sustained Advantage** |
| Rapid-response launch capability (24-hour notice for DoD) | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | **Sustained Advantage** |
| Electron pricing power (67% price increase since debut) | ✓ | Moderate | Moderate | ✓ | **Temporary Advantage** |
| Neutron medium-lift vehicle (in development) | ✓ | ✓ | Moderate | Partial | **Potential Advantage (unproven)** |
| Government relationships (SDA, Space Force) | ✓ | Moderate | Moderate | ✓ | **Competitive Parity with advantage** |

The VRIO analysis highlights that Rocket Lab's deepest moat lies in its **operational track record** and **vertically integrated platform** — not in any single technology. This is a crucial insight: the company's advantage is systemic, not point-specific, making it difficult for rivals to replicate by acquiring a single capability.

### The Dynamic Capability Dimension

Teece, Pisano & Shuen (1997) argue that in fast-moving environments, the ability to *reconfigure* resources matters as much as the resources themselves. Rocket Lab has demonstrated this through serial acquisitions (SolarFlux, Sinclair Interplanetary, Advanced Solutions Inc., PSC, and Motiv Space Systems), each adding a capability that reinforced vertical integration. Rocket Lab Robotics brings in-house the design and manufacturing of critical spacecraft mechanisms such as solar array drive assemblies (SADAs), antenna and propulsion gimbals, filter wheels, focus mechanisms, and precision drive electronics, completing a key element of Rocket Lab's strategy to manufacture satellites at constellation scale and support emerging high-power orbital infrastructure. This pattern of purposeful capability-building is itself a dynamic capability.

## 5. BUSINESS MODEL & VALUE CHAIN ANALYSIS

Rocket Lab operates a **dual-segment business model** with a third emerging layer:

**Segment 1 — Launch Services**: Dedicated small satellite launch via the Electron rocket (~300 kg to LEO). Launch Services offers dedicated launch missions for satellites using the Electron rocket, with pricing around $7.5 million to $8.4 million per mission. Rocket Lab executed 16 successful launches in 2024, a 60% increase from 10 launches in 2023.

**Segment 2 — Space Systems**: Design and manufacture of satellites, spacecraft components, and mission services. All major components — from solar arrays, reaction wheels and star trackers to propulsion systems, avionics, payloads, and launch dispensers — are designed and produced in-house. This end-to-end approach enables Rocket Lab to rapidly scale production while maintaining control over cost and schedule, a critical advantage in meeting the urgent demands of national security missions.

**Segment 3 (Emerging) — Space Applications**: Rocket Lab's strong performance across launch and space systems is foundational to Rocket Lab's momentum toward delivering its own satellite service as a truly end-to-end space company, bolstered in Q1 2025 with the introduction of a new constellation-class satellite platform designed for mass manufacture.

**Full Year 2024 Financial Performance**:

Rocket Lab posted its highest annual revenue ever of $436.2 million and a record Q4 2024 revenue of $132.4 million — a 382% increase compared to Q4 2021, the company's first full quarter following its debut on the Nasdaq. Top achievements across launch and space systems include a record number of 16 launches for Electron in 2024 (a 60% increase in launch cadence compared to 2023) and more than $450 million in newly-secured launch and space systems contracts.

However, the company posted an operating loss of approximately **$228.8 million** in FY2025 (per the Annual Report to Shareholders), reflecting heavy R&D investment in Neutron. Rocket Lab's total operating expenses were $436 million in FY2025, representing 72.5% of revenue, with an operating loss of $228.8 million (38.1% of revenue).

The value chain logic is explicit: Space Systems revenue provides the stable, high-value revenue base that funds Neutron development, while Electron launches provide operational credibility and cash flow. The emerging Applications layer is intended to provide the high-margin, recurring revenue that transforms Rocket Lab's financial profile.

Diversification (space applications/data services) is the long-term vision.

Rocket Lab CEO Peter Beck explains how the company is transitioning to a new phase of scaling up operations to get its new Neutron rockets in orbit, break launch supply bottlenecks, and become the world's premier end-to-end space company. The risk is that pursuing all four quadrants simultaneously stretches finite management attention and capital — a classic "spreading too thin" trap that the recommendations must address.

## 7. THE CENTRAL STRATEGIC PROBLEM: THE DUAL-HORIZON DILEMMA

The analysis above converges on a single, well-defined strategic problem: **Rocket Lab must simultaneously defend its Horizon 1 business (Electron/Space Systems) and build its Horizon 2 business (Neutron/Flatellite), while operating at a structural loss and facing an asymmetric competitive threat from SpaceX.**

This is best understood through the **Three Horizons Framework** (Baghai, Coley & White, 1999):

- **Horizon 1** (Defend & Extend): Electron launch cadence, Space Systems constellation contracts, component supply. *Currently generating revenue and growing rapidly.*
- **Horizon 2** (Emerging Opportunities): Neutron medium-lift rocket, Flatellite platform. *Capital-intensive, unproven, and — critically — delayed.*
- **Horizon 3** (Visionary Options): End-to-end space services, own constellation operations. *Speculative but strategically logical.*

The Neutron delay is the most acute near-term manifestation of this dilemma. The failure of a propellant tank during testing will delay the first launch of Rocket Lab's Neutron rocket to at least the fourth quarter of 2026. Rocket Lab updated the Neutron development schedule following the stage 1 tank test failure, with Neutron's first launch now targeted for Q4 2026. This delay extends the period of cash burn and gives SpaceX more time to consolidate the medium-lift market.

In September 2025, Rocket Lab planned to launch Neutron three times in 2026, and five times in 2027. According to Rocket Lab, Neutron's expected debut launch in 2025 also puts the launch vehicle in a strong position to on-ramp onto the U.S. government's National Security Space Launch (NSSL) programme. NSSL eligibility is a gateway to the highest-value government launch contracts — making Neutron's first successful launch the most consequential single event in Rocket Lab's strategic trajectory.

Meanwhile, the Space Systems business has become so large ($515M + $816M SDA contracts) that it is itself a Horizon 1 anchor — one that demands operational execution and could crowd out Neutron development resources if not carefully managed.

## 9. STRATEGIC OPTIONS & EVALUATION

Three strategic options present themselves, each reflecting a different resolution of the Dual-Horizon Dilemma.

### Option A: "Focused Niche" — Prioritise Electron & Space Systems; Slow Neutron

**Logic**: Abandon or significantly delay Neutron. Concentrate all resources on scaling Electron cadence toward the 120-mission/year LC-1 capacity ceiling, growing Space Systems constellation contracts, and reaching operating profitability.

**Evaluation**: Reduces capital risk and accelerates the path to profitability. However, this permanently cedes the medium-lift market to SpaceX, forfeits NSSL eligibility, and limits total addressable market. The Space Systems business will eventually need a larger launch vehicle to serve its own constellation customers. This option trades long-term competitive position for short-term financial stability — a strategic retreat that may be irreversible.

**Assessment**: ❌ Not recommended. The opportunity cost is too high and the retreat may be permanent.

### Option B: "Full Throttle" — Accelerate All Horizons Simultaneously

**Logic**: Continue pursuing all four Ansoff quadrants at pace. Raise additional equity capital to fund Neutron, Flatellite development, and Space Systems scaling in parallel.

**Evaluation**: Maximises optionality and first-mover advantage in multiple segments. However, it stretches management bandwidth and capital to breaking point. The Neutron tank failure demonstrates that accelerating complex development programmes creates quality risks. Equity dilution is already a concern. The "full throttle" approach risks execution failure across multiple fronts simultaneously.

**Assessment**: ❌ Not recommended in its pure form. The current pace of parallel development is already producing delays and quality incidents.

### Option C: "Sequenced Ambition" — Stage the Horizons with Disciplined Capital Allocation ✅ RECOMMENDED

**Logic**: Maintain the end-to-end strategic vision but impose a *sequenced* execution discipline. Horizon 1 (Electron + Space Systems) must generate sufficient cash flow and backlog to fund Horizon 2 (Neutron) without excessive dilution. Horizon 3 (Flatellite/data services) is incubated but not funded at scale until Neutron is operational. Each horizon must "earn" the right to resource allocation by hitting defined milestones.

**Evaluation**: Preserves the strategic logic of end-to-end ambition while imposing the financial discipline that persistent losses demand. Reduces execution risk on Neutron by ensuring it receives focused attention after the tank failure. Aligns with the Three Horizons framework's prescription for managing parallel development. The primary cost is that delaying Flatellite may cede ground to emerging data-services competitors — a manageable risk given the early stage of that market.

**Assessment**: ✅ Recommended. It is the only option that is simultaneously strategically coherent and financially viable.

## 10. RECOMMENDATIONS & ACTION PLAN

The following five recommendations operationalise Option C, structured around the Three Horizons framework with explicit milestones and resource logic.

---

### Recommendation 1: Fix Neutron — Make It the Single Strategic Priority for 2026

**Rationale**: The Neutron tank failure and resulting delay to Q4 2026 is the most material near-term strategic risk. Every quarter of delay extends cash burn, delays NSSL eligibility, and gives SpaceX more time to consolidate the medium-lift market. Neutron is the keystone of the entire end-to-end strategy: without it, Rocket Lab cannot self-launch its own large constellations, cannot access NSSL contracts, and cannot credibly compete for the largest government programmes.

**Actions**:
- Assign a dedicated "Neutron Tiger Team" with ring-fenced budget reporting directly to the CEO — insulated from Space Systems programme demands.
- Implement a systematic risk-reduction testing protocol that deliberately tests to failure but with longer qualification schedules and redundant test articles.
- Establish a formal "no-launch" gate: Neutron does not fly until all qualification tests are passed with margin. The cost of a failed first launch (reputational, financial, schedule) vastly exceeds the cost of a further delay. As Beck stated: "The priority will always be to bringing a reliable rocket to market, even if it means taking a few extra months."

**KPIs**: Archimedes engine qualification completion; Stage 1 tank replacement and re-qualification; launch vehicle integration milestone dates.

---

### Recommendation 2: Maximise Electron Cadence and Pricing Power to Fund the Transition

**Rationale**: Electron is the cash engine of the transition. LC-1 has two launch pads capable of supporting up to 120 missions every year — Rocket Lab is currently far below this capacity ceiling, meaning significant revenue upside is available without additional infrastructure investment. The 67% pricing increase since the rocket's debut launch demonstrates that the market will bear higher prices for dedicated, reliable launch.

**Actions**:
- Target 20+ Electron launches in 2026 and 24+ in 2027, progressively scaling toward LC-1's 120-mission capacity ceiling.
- Continue the reusability programme (helicopter booster recovery) to reduce per-launch marginal cost and improve gross margins. Even partial reusability (engine refurbishment) could meaningfully improve economics.
- Raise Electron pricing toward $9–10 million per launch over the next two years, justified by the demonstrated reliability record and the premium of dedicated vs. rideshare.
- Expand LC-2 (Virginia) operational tempo to serve U.S. government customers requiring launches from domestic soil.

**KPIs**: Annual Electron launch count; average selling price per mission; gross margin improvement; LC-2 launch frequency.

---

### Recommendation 3: Consolidate and Execute Space Systems — Don't Over-Promise

**Rationale**: Rocket Lab's largest contract to date solidifies its position as a disruptive force in national security space, redefining the speed and efficiency of satellite production and challenging legacy aerospace primes. The combined $1.33 billion in SDA contracts is transformative but also represents significant execution risk. The risk is that Space Systems complexity crowds out Neutron attention.

**Actions**:
- Establish a dedicated Space Systems Programme Management Office (PMO) with clear separation from Launch and Neutron organisations — preventing resource conflicts.
- Invest in production line automation at the Long Beach facility to scale constellation manufacturing without proportional headcount growth, improving margins.
- Leverage the Motiv acquisition to reduce schedule risk on SADA and mechanism procurement — historically a long-lead item in satellite manufacturing.
- Resist the temptation to bid on new Space Systems contracts that cannot be executed within current capacity. Revenue backlog of $1+ billion is already substantial; over-committing creates delivery risk that could damage the government relationships central to the strategy.

**KPIs**: SDA programme milestone adherence; Space Systems gross margin; production throughput (satellites per quarter); on-time delivery rate.

---

### Recommendation 4: Incubate Flatellite/Applications — But Don't Fund It at Scale Yet

**Rationale**: The Flatellite platform and space applications vision is strategically correct — recurring revenue from data services would transform Rocket Lab's financial profile. But it is a Horizon 3 initiative that requires Neutron (to self-launch at scale) and a mature Space Systems manufacturing base. Funding it at scale now would dilute resources from more immediate priorities.

**Actions**:
- Maintain a small (10–15 person) Flatellite product team focused on platform design, customer discovery, and regulatory preparation — but do not commit to a constellation deployment timeline until Neutron is operational.
- Use the Pioneer spacecraft programme (already in orbit) as a technology demonstrator and learning platform.
- Identify 2–3 anchor customers (government or commercial) willing to pre-commit to data services contracts, de-risking the commercial model before large capital deployment.
- Formally link Flatellite deployment to Neutron's first successful launch as a milestone gate. This creates internal accountability and prevents premature capital allocation.

**KPIs**: Flatellite platform design completion; anchor customer LOIs; Pioneer spacecraft operational data; Neutron-linked deployment gate.

---

### Recommendation 5: Manage Capital Structure to Reduce Dilution Risk

**Rationale**: Rocket Lab's shares outstanding grew from approximately 494 million (Q1 2024) to 605 million (Q1 2026) — a 22% increase in two years driven by equity raises to fund operations and development. Continued operating losses require ongoing capital raises that erode shareholder value.

**Actions**:
- Set a clear public target for Adjusted EBITDA breakeven — ideally tied to Neutron's first commercial launch — to provide investors with a credible financial roadmap.
- Explore non-dilutive financing for Neutron development: U.S. government development contracts (similar to NASA's Commercial Crew programme model), DoD Other Transaction Authority (OTA) agreements, or Export-Import Bank financing for international launch customers.
- Prioritise gross margin improvement in Space Systems (target 35%+ non-GAAP by end of 2026) to reduce the cash burn rate from operations. Rocket Lab guided to GAAP gross margins of 34–36% and non-GAAP gross margins of 39–41% for Q1 2026, suggesting this trajectory is achievable.
- Consider a structured equity raise tied to a specific Neutron milestone (e.g., successful engine qualification), allowing investors to price the risk more accurately and reducing the cost of capital.

**KPIs**: Adjusted EBITDA trajectory; shares outstanding growth rate; non-dilutive funding secured; Space Systems non-GAAP gross margin.

## 11. CONCLUSION

Rocket Lab presents one of the most instructive strategic cases in contemporary aerospace: a company that has built genuine, VRIO-validated competitive advantages in a structurally growing market, yet faces an existential asymmetry against SpaceX and the continuous capital demands of its own ambition.

The PESTEL analysis confirms a favourable macro-environment: the space launch services market is projected to reach $41.31 billion by 2030, growing at a CAGR of 14.6%, with aggressive U.S. government diversification toward "New Space" companies creating structural tailwinds. The Five Forces analysis reveals that Rocket Lab's deepest threat is not new entrants (who have largely failed) but the substitute threat from SpaceX's rideshare capability and the competitive intensity of the medium-lift segment Neutron must enter. The VRIO analysis identifies Rocket Lab's systemic vertical integration, operational track record, and treaty-protected infrastructure as genuinely inimitable advantages — but advantages that are only valuable if the company survives the transition to Neutron.

The Ansoff matrix exposes the core strategic risk: Rocket Lab is pursuing all four growth quadrants simultaneously, creating execution overload. The Three Horizons framework provides the resolution: the company must *sequence* its ambitions, using Horizon 1 (Electron + Space Systems) to fund Horizon 2 (Neutron), and gating Horizon 3 (Flatellite/data services) on Neutron's successful debut.

The recommended "Sequenced Ambition" strategy — fix Neutron as the singular 2026 priority, scale Electron cadence and pricing, execute Space Systems with discipline, incubate but don't over-fund Flatellite, and manage capital structure — is the only path that is simultaneously strategically coherent and financially viable. It preserves Rocket Lab's end-to-end vision while imposing the discipline that persistent losses demand.

The priority will always be to bring a reliable rocket to market, even if it means taking a few extra months. That principle, articulated by Beck in the context of Neutron's delay, is not just a statement about one rocket. It is the correct strategic logic for the entire company: build each horizon reliably before funding the next. The stakes are high. Neutron's first successful launch will be the most consequential single event in Rocket Lab's history — not because it proves a rocket works, but because it proves the entire strategic thesis: that a vertically integrated, end-to-end space company can challenge SpaceX not by being cheaper, but by being *complete*.
