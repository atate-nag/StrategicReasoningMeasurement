## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Commercial Aircraft Corporation of China (COMAC) is a state-owned enterprise (SOE) that sits at the intersection of industrial policy, geopolitical rivalry, and commercial aviation competition. Founded in 2008 by direct mandate of the Chinese State Council, COMAC is a state-owned limited liability company that reports directly to the State Council, rather than to AVIC — a structural signal of its national-strategic significance. Its flagship product — the C919 narrowbody jet — entered commercial service in May 2023, marking China's first domestically developed mainline commercial airliner. Yet despite enormous state support, COMAC delivered just 13 of its flagship C919 aircraft in 2024, against a backdrop of US sanctions, Western technology dependencies, and the entrenched duopoly of Airbus and Boeing, who together supply more than 93% of single-aisle aircraft globally.

This case study analyses the strategic situation facing COMAC using **PESTEL**, **Porter's Five Forces**, the **Resource-Based View (RBV)/VRIN framework**, a **SWOT Analysis**, and the **Ansoff Matrix**. The analysis reveals a firm trapped in a structural paradox: simultaneously protected by state power and constrained by it. The recommendations chart a coherent path toward supply chain autonomy, phased international market entry, and institutional credibility building.

## 2. CASE BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### 2.1 Origins and Mandate

COMAC was founded in May 2008 when the Aviation Industry Corporation of China (AVIC) split off two of its Shanghai subsidiaries into a new standalone company and transferred with them the intellectual property for the ARJ21, a regional jet which was still in its early days of development. Comac received nearly $7 billion in seed capital from a combination of central and local governments, state-owned banks, and other state-owned enterprises.

COMAC is a state-owned enterprise approved by the State Council and composed of seven shareholder units. It primarily engages in the design, research, and production of civil airplanes and related products. Its mission is explicitly tied to China's industrial sovereignty: to develop civil aircraft with indigenous intellectual property and become a globally competitive manufacturer.

### 2.2 The C919 Programme

The C919 is a single-aisle, narrowbody commercial jet designed to seat 158–192 passengers and compete directly with the Airbus A320neo family and Boeing 737 MAX. Repeated schedule slippage pushed the maiden flight from 2014 to 2017 and commercial entry from 2016 to 2023 — a total development period of roughly 15 years.

The core issue lies in COMAC's reliance on foreign suppliers for 50–60% of the C919's components. The aircraft depends on Western technology, including CFM International's LEAP-1C turbofan engines, produced by a GE Aerospace and Safran joint venture.

### 2.3 State Financing and Industrial Policy Context

Up to the end of 2020, it is estimated that COMAC was granted access to nearly $72 billion of state subsidies. This includes RMB 50 billion from the China Export-Import Bank to support R&D, manufacturing and sales. COMAC sits at the heart of China's "Made in China 2025" industrial strategy, which set explicit targets for 70% domestic content in core aviation components by 2025, positioning high-bypass turbofans as pivotal to reducing import vulnerabilities and fostering indigenous innovation in strategic sectors.

### 2.4 The Competitive Landscape

For more than three decades, the commercial aircraft market above 100 seats has been defined by a duopoly. Airbus and Boeing together supply the world's widebody aircraft and have historically provided more than 93% of single-aisle aircraft globally. In 2024, Airbus delivered 766 aircraft to 86 customers worldwide — the best year since its all-time record of 863. Against this backdrop, COMAC's 13 deliveries in 2024 represent a market share measured in fractions of a percent.

## 3. THE STRATEGIC SITUATION

COMAC faces a multi-dimensional strategic challenge that can be summarised in three interlocking tensions:

**Tension 1 — Technological Dependency vs. Strategic Autonomy**: In June and July 2025, escalating US–China trade tensions led to a temporary US export halt on these engines, stalling production. Although exports resumed in August, the disruption created a lasting backlog.

**Tension 2 — Domestic Capture vs. Global Ambition**: COMAC aims to take a fifth of the global narrowbody market and a third of the Chinese market by 2035. Yet the US has blocked FAA certification for the C919, a decision tied to broader tensions over China's industrial policies.

**Tension 3 — Scale Deficit vs. Competitive Necessity**: The company delivered just five C919 aircraft in 2025, far below its original targets, and has since reduced its near-term production goals. By contrast, Airbus and Boeing each deliver more than 500 single-aisle jets annually.

## 4. EXTERNAL ENVIRONMENT ANALYSIS — PESTEL

### Political
China's government is simultaneously COMAC's owner, its primary customer (through state-owned airlines), its financier, and its regulator. In October 2018, the US Department of Justice indicted ten Chinese nationals, including intelligence officials, for allegedly stealing trade secrets from thirteen foreign aerospace companies involved in the C919 programme. Under President Donald Trump, the United States put COMAC on a military blacklist, severely limiting how companies could do business with it. On the other hand, China's Belt and Road Initiative is entering a new phase amid the intensifying US–China trade war and global supply chain restructuring, with Xi Jinping's state visits opening doors for COMAC to enter the ASEAN market.

### Economic
North America will remain the largest market, but emerging regions like China, India, and the Middle East are expected to drive future growth, with narrowbody aircraft increasing their share of the global fleet from 62% to 68% by 2035. The C919's estimated list price is $90–100 million, which is below the Airbus A320neo ($111 million) and Boeing 737 MAX 8 ($121 million).

### Social/Reputational
The Financial Times commented that China considers the success of the plane a source of national pride. However, there are signs that the C919 was achieving only around 50% of the average daily hours of the 737 and A320 as recently as February 2024 — a utilisation gap that raises questions about operational reliability.

### Technological
Materials: Western engines use advanced technologies like ceramic matrix composites and single-crystal blades, while China is still developing indigenous capabilities. To reduce its dependence on the West, China is developing its own CJ-1000A jet engine, which is close to completing certification, but analysts predict mass production won't occur until around 2030. The C919 also carries a performance deficit: the C919's range of 2,500–3,000 NM compares unfavourably to the A320neo's 3,500 NM.

### Environmental
Narrowbody aircraft will continue to dominate the future fleet, with their share increasing to 68% by 2035. The LEAP-1C is a modern, fuel-efficient powerplant, meaning the C919 is not at an immediate emissions disadvantage — but the transition to the CJ-1000A engine will require demonstrating equivalent fuel efficiency.

### Legal/Regulatory
Industry experts emphasise that obtaining type certifications from the US Federal Aviation Administration and EASA will significantly boost the C919's international expansion and user base. Despite steady progress, the EASA certification timeline remains uncertain due to regulatory complexity and geopolitical considerations. Industry sources suggest that political factors may also influence the pace of approval, even as technical requirements are addressed.

## 5. INDUSTRY ANALYSIS — PORTER'S FIVE FORCES

### Force 1 — Threat of New Entrants: LOW
The commercial aircraft industry is characterised by extraordinarily high barriers to entry: capital intensity, decades-long development cycles, complex regulatory certification requirements, and deep customer relationships built on safety records. The C919 programme's total development period of roughly 15 years illustrates the magnitude of these barriers. This force is low for incumbents but paradoxically high for COMAC itself as it attempts to break in.

### Force 2 — Bargaining Power of Suppliers: HIGH (for COMAC specifically)
For Airbus and Boeing, supplier power is moderate — they source from thousands of global suppliers. For COMAC, however, supplier power is critically HIGH. The core issue lies in COMAC's reliance on foreign suppliers for 50–60% of the C919's components. The aircraft depends on Western technology, including CFM International's LEAP-1C turbofan engines. In June and July 2025, escalating US–China trade tensions led to a temporary US export halt on these engines, stalling production. This is COMAC's most dangerous competitive exposure.

### Force 3 — Bargaining Power of Buyers: MODERATE-HIGH
Chinese state-owned airlines are effectively captive buyers under political direction, limiting their bargaining power in practice. However, China's COMAC is touting its C919 aircraft as a cost-effective alternative to the Airbus–Boeing duopoly, but success depends on overcoming significant challenges such as certification and establishing reliable maintenance networks — prerequisites that international buyers will demand before committing.

### Force 4 — Threat of Substitutes: LOW
There is no credible substitute for commercial narrowbody aircraft on routes of 500–3,500 km. This is a structurally attractive feature of the industry for all participants.

### Force 5 — Competitive Rivalry: INTENSE
Airbus holds a market share of 62% in the narrow-body segment. Both incumbents have enormous backlogs, established global MRO networks, deep customer relationships, and decades of safety data. COMAC's entry represents an attempt to break a duopoly — a task of extraordinary strategic difficulty.

**Summary**: The industry structure is attractive in aggregate (low substitutes, low new entrants), but COMAC's specific position is highly vulnerable: HIGH supplier power, HIGH international buyer power (certification gap), and INTENSE rivalry from incumbents with overwhelming scale advantages.

## 6. INTERNAL ANALYSIS — RESOURCE-BASED VIEW (RBV) & VRIN FRAMEWORK

The Resource-Based View (Barney, 1991) holds that sustained competitive advantage derives from resources that are **V**aluable, **R**are, **I**nimitable, and **N**on-substitutable (VRIN).

### Resource 1 — State Backing and Unlimited Capital ✅ VRIN
- **Valuable**: Provides insulation from market discipline and guarantees domestic market access.
- **Rare**: COMAC received nearly $7 billion in seed capital from a combination of central and local governments, state-owned banks, and other state-owned enterprises; up to end-2020, estimated access to nearly $72 billion in state subsidies. No private aerospace firm has access to comparable unconditional state financing.
- **Inimitable & Non-substitutable**: The combination of sovereign capital, industrial policy mandate, and captive domestic market is unique to China's political economy.
- **Verdict**: VRIN — Source of Sustainable Competitive Advantage (in the domestic context)

### Resource 2 — Domestic Market Scale ✅ VRIN
- **Valuable & Rare**: Emerging regions like China, India, and the Middle East are expected to drive future fleet growth. China's domestic market is effectively ring-fenced for COMAC through regulatory preference and state airline procurement mandates.
- **Verdict**: VRIN — Source of Competitive Advantage (but only domestically)

### Resource 3 — Engineering & Manufacturing Capability ⚠️ PARTIAL VRIN
- COMAC is one of only three entities in the world manufacturing narrowbody commercial jets, making this capability rare. However, the technology gap versus incumbents — Western engines use advanced technologies like ceramic matrix composites and single-crystal blades, while China is still developing indigenous capabilities — means this resource provides competitive parity, not advantage.
- **Verdict**: Partially VRIN — Competitive Parity, Not Advantage

### Resource 4 — Western Technology Dependency ❌ NEGATIVE RESOURCE (Liability)
COMAC's reliance on CFM LEAP-1C engines and Western avionics is not a resource — it is a strategic liability. It is a resource controlled by a potentially hostile external party. Reliance on the CFM International LEAP-1C engine, a Western-supplied powerplant, introduces geopolitical supply risk that both the manufacturer and operators must manage carefully.

### Resource 5 — Indigenous Engine Programme (CJ-1000A) 🔮 FUTURE VRIN
- The CJ-1000A is pivotal to reducing import vulnerabilities and fostering indigenous innovation in strategic sectors. If developed successfully, an indigenous engine would eliminate the single greatest supply chain vulnerability. However, mass production won't occur until around 2030.
- **Verdict**: Potentially VRIN — but not yet realised

**RBV Summary**: COMAC's most durable competitive advantages are its state-backed financing and its protected domestic market — both products of its political economy rather than commercial capabilities. The critical strategic task is to convert these VRIN state-backed resources into genuine commercial capabilities before geopolitical constraints sever access to Western technology.

*Rationale*: Scale is the prerequisite for everything else — MRO networks, cost reduction, supplier leverage, and international credibility. ## 11. CONCLUSION

COMAC's strategic situation is one of the most complex in contemporary business strategy. It is a state-owned enterprise attempting to break a deeply entrenched global duopoly, armed with unlimited state capital and a protected domestic market, but hamstrung by technological dependency on the very geopolitical adversaries it is trying to challenge.

The analytical chain is clear and coherent. **PESTEL** identifies geopolitics as the dominant external force. **Porter's Five Forces** reveals that supplier power — specifically Western technology dependency — is COMAC's most acute competitive vulnerability. The **RBV** demonstrates that COMAC's only truly VRIN resources are state-derived, not commercially earned. The **Ansoff Matrix** operationalises the response: domestic penetration for scale, BRI market development for near-term international presence, EASA certification for medium-term credibility, and indigenous engine development for long-term sovereignty.

The recommendations are sequenced to address the most acute vulnerabilities first (supply chain resilience), build credibility through demonstrated performance second (utilisation, safety record, EASA certification), and achieve technological sovereignty third (CJ-1000A, 80% indigenous content). Each recommendation is traceable to a specific analytical finding, and together they form a coherent strategic path.

The ultimate test of COMAC's strategy will not be whether it can survive under state protection — it can, indefinitely. The test is whether it can build genuine commercial competitiveness: an aircraft that airlines choose not because they are told to, but because it is the best commercial decision. That transition — from state-mandated champion to genuine global competitor — is the defining strategic challenge of the next decade.
