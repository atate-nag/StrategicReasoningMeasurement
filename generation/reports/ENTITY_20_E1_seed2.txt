# JAPEX Strategic Case Study: From Green Pivot to Hydrocarbon Renewal — Navigating the Energy Transition Paradox

---

## 1. Introduction and Case Overview

This case study examines the strategic situation facing **Japan Petroleum Exploration Co., Ltd. (JAPEX)**, a publicly listed Japanese upstream energy company that has undergone one of the most consequential strategic reversals in the Japanese energy sector in recent years. Between 2022 and 2025, JAPEX first committed to a bold energy transition strategy — pledging to balance profits equally between oil and gas exploration & production (E&P) and renewable/low-carbon businesses by 2030 — and then, in February 2025, abandoned that ambition in favour of a full-throated return to hydrocarbon investment, culminating in a $1.3 billion acquisition of U.S. tight oil assets in late 2025 and a new 10-year management plan targeting a quadrupling of production.

The central strategic question is: **Was JAPEX's reversal a rational, well-grounded strategic recalibration — or a short-sighted capitulation to commodity price signals that leaves the company exposed to long-run transition risks?** Using a suite of analytical frameworks — PESTEL, Porter's Five Forces, the Resource-Based View (RBV), Ansoff's Matrix, and scenario planning — this case analyses the internal and external forces shaping JAPEX's choices and concludes with a set of actionable recommendations.

## 2. Company Background

Japan Petroleum Exploration Company Limited (JAPEX) is a hydrocarbon exploration, production, and transportation company that explores and produces crude oil, natural gas, and liquefied natural gas reserves worldwide. JAPEX was founded in 1955 as a government-owned company in order to seek petroleum self-sufficiency for Japan. Today it is publicly traded on the Tokyo Stock Exchange (TSE: 1662). The Government of Japan owns a 34% stake in JAPEX.

Its main operating areas are Hokkaido, Akita, Yamagata and Niigata in Japan. Overseas, JAPEX is active in Canada, Indonesia, Iraq and other regions where it has offshore interests. The company's domestic and international portfolio spans conventional oil and gas fields, offshore gas production, LNG infrastructure, and nascent CCS projects.

## 3. The Strategic Situation: A Tale of Two Plans

### 3.1 The 2022 Transition Pivot

In 2022, Japan Petroleum Exploration Co. (JAPEX) had decided to support the energy transition by targeting equal distribution of profits between E&P and other businesses by 2030. Based on the "JAPEX Management Plan 2022-2030" formulated in March 2022, JAPEX has been working to strengthen profitability and to build a business foundation with a view to growth 2030 and beyond. This plan was explicitly designed to rebalance the company away from its hydrocarbon core, expanding into solar, wind, geothermal, and battery storage.

### 3.2 The 2025 Reversal

Japan Petroleum Exploration Co. (JAPEX) will focus on investment in oil and gas exploration and production (E&P) through 2030 as fair returns from renewable energy sources is challenging due to rising costs, President Michiro Yamashita told Reuters in an interview. Other global peers have also scaled back renewables investments due to lower returns. At the same time, profits from oil and gas have soared since Russia's invasion of Ukraine disrupted supply and propelled energy prices higher.

Yamashita said that the current ratio of E&P contributing 70%-80% of earnings will likely remain through 2030.

### 3.3 The Verdad Acquisition and New Management Plan

Japan Petroleum Exploration Corporation (JAPEX) announced its $1.3 billion acquisition of tight oil and gas assets in the DJ Basin of Colorado with the purchase of Verdad Resources Intermediate Holdings by Peoria Resources, a subsidiary of JAPEX (U.S.) Corp. The purchase includes 101,000 net acres and its associated stacked pay development in Colorado, as well as Wyoming, producing 35,000 barrels of oil equivalent per day.

Japan Petroleum Exploration (Japex) has unveiled plans to invest 1.16 trillion yen ($7.3 billion) in exploration and production in a bid to quadruple oil and gas output over the next decade, mainly in the United States. Japex has set a goal to raise net profit to 100 billion yen and achieve a 12% Return on Equity (ROE) by 2035.

## 4. PESTEL Analysis

### 4.1 Political

JAPEX operates in a uniquely politicised environment. The Government of Japan owns a 34% stake in JAPEX, meaning strategic decisions are never purely commercial — they are intertwined with national energy security imperatives. On 18 February 2025, the Japanese government approved the 7th Strategic Energy Plan, setting an updated course for Japan's future energy policy with a primary focus on achieving carbon neutrality by 2050. Crucially, this plan does not mandate a rapid phase-out of fossil fuels; the 7th Strategic Energy Plan retains a long-term role for fossil fuels, including LNG, with gas-fired power capacity set to increase due to new plant construction. This political alignment between government policy and JAPEX's hydrocarbon-focused strategy provides a supportive backdrop for the company's pivot.

However, geopolitical risk is acute. JAPEX is participating in Garraf oil field in southern Iraq through its consolidated subsidiary, Japex Garraf Ltd. Currently, production and shipments at Garraf oil field have been suspended following a force majeure declaration by the Iraqi government, and there is no prospect of resumption. This illustrates the political exposure inherent in JAPEX's overseas portfolio.

### 4.2 Economic

The post-Ukraine energy price environment has been transformative for JAPEX's economics. Profits from oil and gas have soared since Russia's invasion of Ukraine disrupted supply and propelled energy prices higher. At the same time, JAPEX's market valuation has remained suppressed: the P/B ratio for FY2024 hovered around 0.5 to 0.8x, suggesting that markets are discounting the company's asset base — a signal that the strategic narrative has not yet convinced investors of long-run value creation. The Verdad target itself showed strong underlying economics: VRIH logged $825.03 million in revenue and $341.26 million in operating income for the one-year period.

### 4.3 Social

Japan's public attitude toward energy is shaped by the legacy of the 2011 Fukushima disaster, which drove a mass shutdown of nuclear capacity and a reversion to fossil fuels, and by growing awareness of climate risk. However, renewable energy projects have proven unpopular in many corners of the country due to the destruction of natural landscapes. Social licence for large-scale onshore renewables in Japan is genuinely constrained.

### 4.4 Technological

JAPEX's core technological competencies lie in subsurface E&P. The company's competitive advantages lie in its comprehensive technological capabilities in oil and natural gas E&P, including 3D seismic technology and extended reach drilling. Importantly, these capabilities are transferable to adjacent low-carbon technologies: geothermal power generation, which uses hot steam and water rising from underground, has much in common with the oil/gas E&P business in that both explore resources underground. JAPEX has also been developing CCS technology, with JAPEX applying and receiving a drilling permit in September 2025, going forward with studies and preparations, including exploration drilling, toward the start of operations in 2030 at the Tomakomai CCS site in Hokkaido.

### 4.5 Environmental

JAPEX aims to achieve a 40% reduction in CO2 emissions per unit of production by 2030 (compared to fiscal 2019) and achieve carbon neutrality in 2050 with Scope 1 and Scope 2 emissions. The tension between this stated commitment and the decision to massively expand oil and gas output is the central environmental paradox of JAPEX's current strategy. The company's bet is that CCS and CCUS technologies will bridge this gap — the goal is to start injecting CO2 by the end of FY2030 with a storage capacity of 1.50 to 2.00 million tonnes at Tomakomai — but this remains unproven at commercial scale.

### 4.6 Legal/Regulatory

From FY2027, all ground-mounted commercial solar — the segment driving the bulk of Japan's utility-scale solar expansion — will be excluded from both FIT and FIP support schemes, regardless of project scale. At the same time, new renewable projects are required to connect under non-firm access arrangements, allowing curtailment without compensation during periods of system congestion. This regulatory deterioration in the renewable support framework directly validates JAPEX's decision to exit aggressive renewables expansion: the policy environment is making renewable returns even harder to achieve.

## 5. Porter's Five Forces Analysis

### 5.1 Threat of New Entrants — **Low to Moderate**

Upstream oil and gas E&P is capital-intensive, technically demanding, and subject to extensive regulatory licensing. JAPEX has extensive experiences and solid technical capabilities cultivated in the oil and natural gas E&P business. These accumulated competencies represent a significant barrier to entry. However, in the U.S. tight oil space where JAPEX is now expanding, the barriers are lower — the DJ Basin is a mature, well-understood play with established operators and readily available acreage.

### 5.2 Bargaining Power of Suppliers — **Moderate**

In E&P, suppliers include drilling contractors, oilfield services companies (Halliburton, SLB), and technology providers. The global oilfield services market is concentrated among a few major players, giving suppliers moderate power, particularly during periods of high drilling activity when rig availability tightens.

### 5.3 Bargaining Power of Buyers — **Moderate to High**

Crude oil is a global commodity priced on international benchmarks (WTI, Brent), so individual buyers have little power over price. However, for JAPEX's natural gas and LNG business — which feeds Japanese utilities — buyers are concentrated (JERA, Tokyo Gas, Osaka Gas) and have long-term contractual leverage. Competition is intensifying not only from traditional Japex competitors like INPEX but also from new entrants in the renewable space. Maintaining its market share in LNG requires agile trading operations and strategic partnerships.

### 5.4 Threat of Substitutes — **High (Long-Run)**

This is the existential threat underpinning the entire case. Japan's 7th Strategic Energy Plan targets 40-50% renewable energy and 20% nuclear power by 2040, implying that thermal power (JAPEX's core market) will face structural substitution pressure. The 7th Strategic Energy Plan retains a long-term role for fossil fuels, including LNG, with gas-fired power capacity set to increase due to new plant construction, providing near-term relief — but the trajectory is clear.

### 5.5 Competitive Rivalry — **Moderate to High**

INPEX Corporation stands as JAPEX's primary domestic competitor, boasting a larger market capitalisation and leading Japan's international upstream projects. Its financial heft allows for more aggressive investment in large-scale capital projects. Globally, JAPEX competes against supermajors (ExxonMobil, Shell, BP) and national oil companies in every basin it operates in. In the DJ Basin specifically, it now competes against established operators with deep local knowledge.

## 6. Resource-Based View (RBV) Analysis

The RBV (Barney, 1991) asks whether a firm's resources and capabilities are **Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN)** — the conditions for sustainable competitive advantage.

| Resource / Capability | Valuable? | Rare? | Inimitable? | Non-substitutable? | Assessment |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Subsurface E&P technology (3D seismic, extended-reach drilling) | ✓ | Moderate | Moderate | ✓ | Core competitive asset |
| Government relationship / national mandate | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | **Sustained advantage** |
| Domestic gas infrastructure (Akita, Niigata) | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | Moderate | Durable but declining |
| U.S. tight oil operator capability (via Peoria/Verdad) | ✓ | No | No | No | **Competitive parity** — must be developed |
| CCS/geothermal technical knowledge | ✓ | ✓ | Moderate | Moderate | **Emerging advantage** |
| LNG supply chain (Freeport stake) | ✓ | No | No | No | Commercially valuable, not rare |

The RBV analysis reveals a critical strategic gap: JAPEX's **genuinely rare and inimitable resources** — its government mandate, domestic infrastructure, and subsurface expertise — are concentrated in legacy hydrocarbon assets. Its new U.S. tight oil position, while financially attractive, does **not** constitute a VRIN-based competitive advantage; it places JAPEX in direct competition with far larger, more experienced U.S. operators on their home turf. The most strategically distinctive path for JAPEX is to leverage its subsurface expertise into CCS and geothermal — areas where its technical capabilities are genuinely transferable and where competition is currently limited.

## 7. Ansoff Matrix Analysis

Ansoff's (1957) growth matrix maps strategic options along two dimensions: markets (existing vs. new) and products/services (existing vs. new).

```
                    EXISTING PRODUCTS          NEW PRODUCTS
                 ┌──────────────────────┬──────────────────────┐
EXISTING MARKETS │  MARKET PENETRATION  │   PRODUCT DEVELOPMENT│
                 │  (Domestic Japan E&P)│   (CCS, geothermal,  │
                 │  Deepen Akita/Niigata│   hydrogen in Japan) │
                 │  gas production      │                      │
                 ├──────────────────────┼──────────────────────┤
NEW MARKETS      │  MARKET DEVELOPMENT  │    DIVERSIFICATION   │
                 │  (U.S. DJ Basin,     │   (Renewables outside│
                 │  Iraq, Indonesia)    │   Japan — abandoned  │
                 │                      │   2025)              │
                 └──────────────────────┴──────────────────────┘
```

JAPEX's 2022 Management Plan represented a **Diversification** strategy — new products (renewables) in new markets (international green energy). Its 2025 reversal represents a retreat to **Market Development** (existing E&P products in new geographies — U.S. tight oil) and **Market Penetration** (deepening domestic gas). The abandoned diversification strategy carried the highest risk but also the highest potential for long-run transformation. The current market development strategy in the U.S. is more familiar but exposes JAPEX to commodity price risk and operator-capability risk simultaneously.

Critically, the **Product Development** quadrant — applying JAPEX's existing subsurface expertise to new low-carbon products (CCS, geothermal) in its existing Japanese market — is the most strategically coherent option and is currently underweighted in the company's capital allocation.

## 8. Scenario Analysis

Two plausible macro scenarios frame the strategic risk landscape for JAPEX over the 2025–2035 horizon:

### Scenario A: "Prolonged Hydrocarbon Demand" (Favourable to Current Strategy)
- Global energy demand continues to rise, driven by emerging market growth and AI-related electricity demand
- Japan's 7th Strategic Energy Plan is implemented broadly as written, keeping thermal power at 30–40% of the mix to 2040
- Oil prices remain at $70–80/bbl (consistent with JAPEX's internal IEA-based assumption)
- U.S. tight oil remains profitable; Verdad assets generate strong cash flows
- **JAPEX's current strategy succeeds:** the $7.3bn investment programme generates the targeted 180,000 boe/d and ¥100bn net profit by 2035

### Scenario B: "Accelerated Energy Transition" (Unfavourable to Current Strategy)
- Renewable costs continue to fall; battery storage solves intermittency
- Japan accelerates nuclear restarts and offshore wind deployment, reducing gas demand faster than expected
- Oil demand peaks before 2030 as EV adoption accelerates; prices fall to $50/bbl
- Carbon pricing is introduced in Japan, imposing stranded-asset risk on hydrocarbon portfolios
- U.S. tight oil assets face impairment; the $1.3bn Verdad acquisition generates poor returns
- **JAPEX's current strategy fails:** the company is over-invested in depreciating assets and under-invested in the capabilities needed for the low-carbon economy

The company's current strategy is essentially a concentrated bet on Scenario A. The absence of a robust hedge against Scenario B is the most significant strategic vulnerability identified in this analysis.

## 9. Critical Evaluation of JAPEX's Strategic Pivot

### 9.1 What the Pivot Gets Right

The pivot is **rationally grounded in short-to-medium-run market realities**. Japan's renewable energy environment is genuinely challenging: Japan has limited land for onshore solar energy deployment due to its mountainous terrain and competition with agriculture for land use, and its steep coastal shelf limits its offshore wind potential. The regulatory deterioration — removing guaranteed revenue support while exposing projects to uncompensated curtailment risk substantially weakens the investment case for new large-scale renewable projects — further undermines the economics that underpinned the 2022 plan. JAPEX's decision to retreat from aggressive renewables expansion was therefore not irrational; it was a response to genuine market failure in the Japanese renewables sector.

Furthermore, the Verdad acquisition is financially credible. The assets included in the deal produce a mix of 49% light oil, 24% natural gas liquids, and 27% natural gas from the Niobrara and Codell formations in the DJ Basin. More than 1,000 wells are in production, with about the same number planned. This is a proven, cash-generating asset base, not a speculative exploration bet.

The Freeport LNG stake is also strategically coherent: JAPEX has participated in the project since 2024 through the acquisition of a stake in Gulf Coast LNG Holdings LLC. This positions JAPEX as both an upstream producer and an LNG infrastructure holder, creating an integrated value chain that supports Japan's energy security mandate.

### 9.2 What the Pivot Gets Wrong

The pivot's central weakness is its **temporal asymmetry**: it optimises for the next five to seven years at the potential cost of the decade beyond. Three specific concerns emerge:

**First, operator capability risk in the U.S.** Japex has held nonoperated stakes in the Eagle Ford Shale in Texas since 2012 and Woodford Shale in Oklahoma since around 2022. The Verdad acquisition is JAPEX's first *operated* position in the U.S. — a fundamentally different challenge requiring reservoir management, production optimisation, and workforce capabilities that JAPEX has not previously developed at scale in a North American context. The gap between being a passive equity holder and an active operator is significant.

**Second, the CCS bet is under-capitalised relative to the E&P investment.** The company plans to invest ¥1.16 trillion ($7.3bn) in E&P over the decade, while CCS investment — the primary mechanism for maintaining carbon neutrality commitments — is a fraction of that. If the energy transition accelerates under Scenario B, JAPEX will have neither the renewables portfolio nor sufficiently scaled CCS operations to credibly claim a low-carbon identity.

**Third, the Iraq force majeure event reveals portfolio concentration risk.** Production and shipments at Garraf oil field have been suspended following a force majeure declaration by the Iraqi government, and there is no prospect of resumption. Consequently, we cannot anticipate revenue from the project. This sudden loss of a major producing asset — which had been generating approximately 127,000 barrels per day — underscores the geopolitical fragility of JAPEX's overseas E&P portfolio and the risks of concentrating further investment in politically exposed regions.

## 10. Recommendations and Action Plan

The following recommendations are organised around a **three-horizon framework** (McKinsey), balancing near-term financial performance (Horizon 1), medium-term capability building (Horizon 2), and long-term strategic positioning (Horizon 3).

---

### Recommendation 1 (Horizon 1): Operationalise the Verdad Assets Rigorously — But Cap Further U.S. Tight Oil M&A

**Rationale:** The Verdad acquisition is made; the priority now is execution. JAPEX must build genuine operator capabilities in the DJ Basin rather than simply applying a Japanese corporate management layer to a U.S. asset. This means retaining Verdad's existing operational management team, integrating Peoria Resources as a genuine operating subsidiary with delegated authority, and establishing clear production KPIs tied to the ¥100bn net profit target.

However, JAPEX should **not** double down further on U.S. tight oil M&A in the near term. The plan places heavy emphasis on U.S. expansion, but operator capability is a stock that must be built over time, not purchased. Acquiring additional operated assets before the Verdad integration is complete would stretch management attention and risk compounding operator-capability gaps.

**Action:** Establish a dedicated DJ Basin operations board with mixed Japanese/U.S. leadership. Set a 24-month moratorium on further operated U.S. upstream acquisitions pending operational integration review.

---

### Recommendation 2 (Horizon 1–2): Accelerate the Tomakomai CCS Programme and Position It as a Commercial Business

**Rationale:** CCS is JAPEX's most strategically distinctive low-carbon capability. Geothermal power generation, which uses hot steam and water rising from underground, has much in common with the oil/gas E&P business in that both explore resources underground. The same logic applies to CCS — subsurface characterisation, drilling, reservoir management, and monitoring are all competencies JAPEX already possesses. This is a genuine VRIN resource (see Section 6). Japan's government has designated the Tomakomai area as its first CCS "specified area" under the CCS Business Act, and JAPEX applied and received a drilling permit in September 2025, going forward with studies and preparations, including exploration drilling, toward the start of operations in 2030.

JAPEX should treat CCS not as a carbon offset mechanism but as a **revenue-generating business** — offering CO2 storage services to Japanese heavy industry (steel, cement, chemicals) that face regulatory pressure to decarbonise. This creates a new income stream that is structurally uncorrelated with oil prices and leverages existing capabilities.

**Action:** Ring-fence a dedicated CCS business unit with its own P&L. Initiate commercial negotiations with Japanese industrial emitters (JFE Steel, Nippon Steel, Mitsubishi Chemical) for long-term CO2 storage contracts by FY2027. Target scaling Tomakomai to 3 MtCO2/year by 2035 (versus the current plan of 1.5–2 MtCO2).

---

### Recommendation 3 (Horizon 2): Hedge the Transition Risk Through Selective Geothermal Investment

**Rationale:** JAPEX's abandonment of renewables was rational given the poor economics of solar and wind in Japan. But geothermal is structurally different. Japan is the third-largest geothermal resource holder in the world, and geothermal development requires exactly the subsurface skills JAPEX possesses. JAPEX has been studying geothermal power generation since the 1970s. In recent years, we have conducted feasibility studies primarily in Hokkaido and the Tohoku region. Unlike solar and wind, geothermal is baseload, not intermittent — it does not face the curtailment risk that is undermining solar project economics.

A targeted geothermal programme of ¥50–80bn over the plan period would provide a credible low-carbon hedge without the capital intensity of large-scale wind or the regulatory uncertainty of solar. It would also provide a narrative bridge between JAPEX's hydrocarbon identity and its carbon neutrality commitments that is more credible than CCS alone.

**Action:** Allocate ¥60bn to geothermal development over 2026–2035, targeting 200 MW of installed capacity. Prioritise sites in Hokkaido (adjacent to existing domestic operations) to leverage existing infrastructure and regulatory relationships.

---

### Recommendation 4 (Horizon 2): Diversify the Overseas Portfolio Away from Geopolitical Concentration

**Rationale:** The Iraq force majeure event is a case study in geopolitical concentration risk. Production and shipments at Garraf oil field have been suspended following a force majeure declaration by the Iraqi government, and there is no prospect of resumption. Consequently, we cannot anticipate revenue from the project. JAPEX's overseas portfolio — Iraq, Indonesia, and now the U.S. — is geographically diverse but not geopolitically diverse; Iraq and Indonesia both carry elevated sovereign risk. The U.S. DJ Basin assets, while subject to regulatory and price risk, represent a markedly more stable operating environment.

JAPEX should use the Management Plan 2026–2035 period to **rebalance its overseas portfolio toward OECD jurisdictions** (U.S., Canada, Norway — where Longboat Japex Norge AS already provides a foothold) and away from high-sovereign-risk assets as existing contracts expire.

**Action:** Conduct a formal portfolio review of all non-OECD overseas assets by FY2027. Establish a maximum 20% threshold for E&P revenue from countries rated below investment grade on sovereign risk metrics. Reinvest proceeds from any divestments into the DJ Basin development programme or North Sea CCS opportunities.

---

### Recommendation 5 (Horizon 3): Address the Valuation Discount Through a Clearer Strategic Narrative

**Rationale:** The P/B ratio for FY2024 hovered around 0.5 to 0.8x, a persistent discount that suggests investors are not convinced that JAPEX's asset base is being managed to maximise long-run value. This is partly a communication problem — the rapid strategic reversal from the 2022 plan to the 2025 pivot has damaged strategic credibility — and partly a structural problem (the government's 34% stake limits free float and can create a perception of political rather than commercial decision-making).

JAPEX should develop a more coherent **"Integrated Energy Transition" narrative** that presents the current hydrocarbon investment not as a retreat from decarbonisation but as the cash-generation engine that funds CCS, geothermal, and low-carbon infrastructure. This is, in fact, the strategy being pursued — but it is not being communicated as such. Japex has set a goal to raise net profit to 100 billion yen and achieve a 12% Return on Equity (ROE) by 2035 — if achieved, this would justify a significant re-rating from the current P/B discount.

**Action:** Publish an integrated capital allocation framework by FY2026 that explicitly maps hydrocarbon cash flows to low-carbon investments, with quantified carbon abatement targets per dollar invested. Engage with ESG-focused institutional investors through a dedicated investor relations programme targeting the "transition investor" segment.

## 11. Conclusion

JAPEX's strategic situation is one of genuine complexity: a company with a government mandate for energy security, a legacy hydrocarbon skill base, an ambiguous commitment to carbon neutrality, and a new 10-year plan that bets heavily on U.S. tight oil while retaining CCS as a long-run hedge. The February 2025 pivot was rationally grounded — removing guaranteed revenue support while exposing renewable projects to uncompensated curtailment risk substantially weakens the investment case for new large-scale solar and wind in Japan — but it has left JAPEX strategically exposed to an accelerated energy transition scenario.

The coherent chain of analysis presented here — PESTEL identifies the regulatory and political tailwinds supporting hydrocarbon investment but also the long-run substitution threat; Porter's Five Forces reveals the competitive pressure from INPEX and global operators; the RBV shows that JAPEX's genuinely distinctive capabilities lie in subsurface expertise applicable to CCS and geothermal, not in U.S. tight oil operations; Ansoff's Matrix highlights the underweighted Product Development quadrant; and scenario analysis quantifies the asymmetric risk of the current strategy — all converge on the same conclusion: **JAPEX must use the cash flows from its hydrocarbon pivot to build the low-carbon capabilities that will determine its survival beyond 2035.**

The recommendations above — disciplined Verdad integration, CCS commercialisation, targeted geothermal investment, portfolio geopolitical rebalancing, and a clearer investor narrative — constitute a strategy that honours the short-run financial logic of the hydrocarbon pivot while systematically hedging the long-run transition risk. This is not a choice between being an oil company and being a green company. It is a choice about whether JAPEX uses the next decade to build the next decade's competitive advantage, or merely to harvest the last one.
