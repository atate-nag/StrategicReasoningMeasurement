# STRATEGIC CASE STUDY: SALESFORCE, INC.

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Salesforce, Inc. — the world's largest Customer Relationship Management (CRM) software company — faces one of the most consequential strategic inflection points in its 25-year history. Having built an unassailable position in cloud-based CRM, the company confronts a paradox: the very AI revolution it champions through its Agentforce platform simultaneously threatens to cannibalise the per-seat subscription model on which its $37.9 billion revenue base rests. Revenue growth has decelerated to approximately 8–9% year-on-year, while competitors — most notably Microsoft — intensify pressure through ecosystem bundling. Agentforce has demonstrated remarkable deal velocity but only a "modest" near-term revenue contribution, creating a dangerous gap between strategic narrative and financial reality. This case study analyses Salesforce's strategic situation through Porter's Five Forces, SWOT, the Resource-Based View (VRIN), Dynamic Capabilities, and the Ansoff Matrix, culminating in a coherent, evidence-based action plan.

## 2. COMPANY BACKGROUND & CONTEXT

Founded in 1999 by Marc Benioff, Salesforce pioneered the Software-as-a-Service (SaaS) model for enterprise software, displacing on-premise CRM incumbents. Its Customer 360 platform spans Sales Cloud, Service Cloud, Marketing Cloud, Commerce Cloud, and Slack. Salesforce reported FY25 revenue of $37.9 billion, with subscription and support revenue of $35.7 billion, up 10% year-over-year, setting a record for the company's recurring revenue mix. According to IDC, in 2024, Salesforce led all CRM vendors with a 20.7% share and generated the highest revenue among all CRM vendors. Salesforce has been named the #1 CRM provider by IDC for the 12th consecutive year.

The strategic situation crystallised in late 2024 when Salesforce unveiled Agentforce — a suite of autonomous AI agents — at Dreamforce 2024, positioning it as the company's most significant product launch in a generation. This launch set in motion a strategic bet that now defines every major decision the company makes.

## 3. THE STRATEGIC SITUATION

### 3.1 The Core Tension: Growth Deceleration vs. AI Promise

Salesforce's revenue growth has visibly decelerated. After posting double-digit growth rates in early FY2025, quarterly growth settled at 8% year-on-year through Q2 and Q3 FY25. This reflects structural saturation in the large-enterprise segment where Salesforce has historically dominated.

In September 2024, Salesforce unveiled Agentforce, a groundbreaking suite of autonomous AI agents that augment employees and handle tasks in service, sales, marketing, and commerce, driving unprecedented efficiency and customer satisfaction. FY25 Agentforce and Data Cloud highlights included $900 million in Data Cloud and AI annual recurring revenue, up 120% year-over-year, and since October 2024, Salesforce closed 5,000 Agentforce deals, including more than 3,000 paid.

Yet the financial contribution has not matched the strategic ambition. Speaking to investors, Amy Weaver, President and CFO, said: "On Agentforce, we are incredibly excited about the customer momentum we are seeing. However, the adoption cycle is still early as we focus on deployment with our customers. As a result, we are assuming a modest contribution to revenue in fiscal '26. We expect the momentum to build throughout the year, driving a more meaningful contribution in fiscal '27." While Marc and Salesforce are obviously keen to point out the company's wins, its forecast for 2026 revenue fell below Wall Street expectations, sending shares down around 5% in extended trading.

### 3.2 Competitive Intensification

Per IDC, Salesforce remains the #1 CRM provider for the 12th consecutive year, with 20.7% market share. Microsoft Dynamics comes in second with approximately 18% market share. Oracle and Adobe are close behind. Microsoft poses a significant threat, particularly as it bundles Copilot across its entire ecosystem, and it has the advantage of deep Office 365 integration. Oracle and Adobe are also making heavy investments in AI-powered CRM.

Companies can now access native AI agents, design their own AI agents with Copilot Studio, and leverage a native Copilot to draft customer emails, automate post-contact summaries, and more. Plus, the new Dynamics 365 Contact Center aligns closely with the CRM, enabling Microsoft to own more of the service stack than its competitors, including Salesforce.

Grand View Research estimates that the CRM market is projected to reach $163.16 billion by 2030, at a CAGR of 14.6%. The prize is large — but so is the competitive intensity.

### 3.3 The Pricing Instability Problem

In October 2024, Salesforce launched Agentforce at $2 per conversation. The pitch was attractive — you don't commit to a massive license uplift, you test, and you only pay when the agent is working. But it fell apart in production. What even counts as a "conversation" when a single customer query triggers 8 backend processes? Buyers couldn't model their costs. The original $2/conversation model immediately priced out nonprofits, SMBs, and any organisation without a large AI budget. Even enterprise customers called it unpredictable and disconnected from the real cost of serving AI actions.

Salesforce subsequently introduced Flex Credits ($0.10 per action) in May 2025, and a per-user licence add-on at $125/user/month — three pricing models in under 18 months. Salesforce has now shipped three different pricing models for Agentforce in roughly 18 months. This pricing volatility signals unresolved strategic uncertainty about how AI-delivered value should be monetised.

### 3.4 The Self-Disruption Dilemma

Perhaps the most acute strategic tension is existential. Investors are increasingly asking a pointed question: if autonomous AI agents can do much of what Salesforce's traditional software does, why would enterprises keep paying premium subscription fees for legacy platforms? This is a classic innovator's dilemma — Salesforce must disrupt itself before a competitor does it for them.

## 4. FRAMEWORK ANALYSIS

### 4.1 Porter's Five Forces (Porter, 1980)

Porter's Five Forces framework analyses the structural attractiveness of an industry by examining five competitive pressures.

**Force 1: Rivalry Among Existing Competitors — HIGH**

As of 2024, Microsoft Dynamics 365 holds 5.2% market share, while Oracle CRM captures 3.8% of the global CRM market. SAP CRM maintains 2.5% market penetration. However, these share figures understate Microsoft's strategic threat. All three of these companies have deep enough pockets to undercut prices to achieve more market share. The intensity of rivalry is further amplified by the simultaneous AI investment by all major players, compressing differentiation windows. **Rivalry: HIGH and increasing.**

**Force 2: Threat of New Entrants — MODERATE**

R&D spending in 2024 reached $7.2 billion, representing 20.3% of total revenue. The average time to replicate Salesforce's core CRM technology is estimated at 4–6 years. These factors create significant barriers. However, AI-native startups building vertical-specific agentic applications from scratch represent a new category of entrant that does not need to replicate the full platform. **New Entrants: MODERATE, rising.**

**Force 3: Threat of Substitutes — MODERATE-HIGH**

Salesforce stock dropped nearly 8% after soft revenue guidance raised concerns about slow AI monetisation and growing competition from AI-native platforms. If an AI agent can autonomously manage the full sales cycle — qualifying leads, drafting proposals, updating records, and closing deals — the need for a human-operated CRM interface diminishes. This is the central substitution risk. **Substitutes: MODERATE-HIGH.**

**Force 4: Bargaining Power of Buyers — MODERATE**

High switching costs curb customer bargaining power: migrating terabytes of CRM data, integrations, and retraining staff creates months-long projects and costs often exceeding $500,000 for mid-market firms, so clients rarely threaten churn. However, as Microsoft bundles competitive AI capabilities into existing enterprise agreements, buyers gain leverage to renegotiate or switch. Salesforce's average deal size of over $1 million means that decisions require executive sign-off, board approval, and extended procurement cycles. **Buyer Power: MODERATE, with upward trajectory.**

**Force 5: Bargaining Power of Suppliers — MODERATE-HIGH**

Suppliers (cloud IaaS, GPUs, data/API providers, elite talent) hold moderate-to-high leverage over Salesforce — FY2025 IaaS commitments exceed $4 billion, operating margin approximately 24%, R&D opex up approximately 12% in FY2024, AI role pay up 18% in 2024–25, GPU spot prices up 40% in 2024. **Supplier Power: MODERATE-HIGH.**

**Five Forces Summary:** The overall industry structure is moderately unattractive and deteriorating. The most critical forces are intensifying rivalry (Microsoft) and the threat of substitutes (AI-native disruption). These converge to create the strategic situation Salesforce must resolve.

- **Financial Scale:** FY25 revenue of $37.9 billion, and total remaining performance obligation of $63 billion, up 11% year-on-year, signals a powerful pipeline of future revenue.
- **AI Infrastructure:** At the technical core of Agentforce is the Atlas Reasoning Engine, which Salesforce said delivers results twice as relevant and 33% more accurate than competing solutions.

**WEAKNESSES**

- **Revenue Growth Deceleration:** Growth has slowed to 8–9% YoY, below the CRM market's projected 14.6% CAGR.
- **Pricing Instability:** Three Agentforce pricing models in 18 months signal unresolved strategic uncertainty and create friction in enterprise sales cycles.
- **AI Monetisation Gap:** Despite 5,000+ Agentforce deals, the product made only a "modest contribution to revenue in fiscal '26."
- **Integration Risk:** The acquisition of Informatica comes after failed negotiations in 2024 and adds integration complexity to an already ambitious transformation agenda.

**OPPORTUNITIES**

- **Agentic AI TAM Expansion:** The CRM market is projected to reach $163.16 billion by 2030, at a CAGR of 14.6%. Agentforce meaningfully expands Salesforce's TAM beyond CRM into the broader "digital labour" market.
- **International Expansion:** From 2005 to 2026, the Americas region made up an average of 70% of Salesforce's total revenue. Asia-Pacific and emerging markets remain significantly underpenetrated.
- **Consumption-Based Revenue:** Shifting from per-seat to consumption-based pricing aligns revenue with AI-delivered value and opens new growth vectors.
- **Vertical Specialisation:** Industry-specific clouds allow Salesforce to command premium pricing and reduce competitive exposure to horizontal platforms.

**THREATS**

- **Microsoft Bundling:** Microsoft poses a significant threat, particularly as it bundles Copilot across its entire ecosystem, with the advantage of deep Office 365 integration.
- **AI-Native Disruption:** AI-native startups building vertical-specific agentic applications could bypass Salesforce's platform entirely.
- **Self-Disruption:** If Agentforce succeeds, it reduces the number of human seats required — potentially cannibalising Salesforce's own per-seat subscription revenue base.
- **Regulatory Risk:** Evolving AI regulations (EU AI Act, data privacy laws) could constrain Agentforce's autonomous decision-making capabilities in key markets.

### 4.3 Resource-Based View (VRIN Analysis)

Barney's (1991) Resource-Based View holds that sustained competitive advantage derives from resources that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN). The RBV posits that firms achieve sustained competitive advantage through possessing and deploying resources that are valuable, rare, inimitable, and non-substitutable. This framework proves particularly relevant for analysing AI adoption, as its uneven implementation across firms can be explained by differences in access to AI-related VRIN resources and capabilities.

| Resource | V | R | I | N | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Customer Data Asset** (50T+ Data Cloud records) | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ | **Sustained Advantage** |
| **CRM Market Position** (20.7% share, 150K+ customers) | ✅ | ✅ | ⚠️ Eroding | ⚠️ | **Temporary Advantage** |
| **Ecosystem & AppExchange** (network effects) | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ | ⚠️ | **Sustained Advantage** |
| **Atlas Reasoning Engine** (AI architecture) | ✅ | ⚠️ | ⚠️ Replicable | ⚠️ | **Temporary Advantage** |
| **Switching Costs / Lock-in** (data + integrations) | ✅ | ⚠️ | ✅ | ✅ | **Sustained Advantage** |
| **Brand & Trust** (enterprise security/compliance) | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ | ⚠️ | **Sustained Advantage** |

**Critical Insight:** Salesforce's most durable VRIN resources are its **proprietary customer data asset** and **ecosystem lock-in** — not its AI technology per se. The Atlas Reasoning Engine can eventually be replicated; 50 trillion customer records and 25 years of enterprise relationship data cannot. This has a direct strategic implication: Salesforce should compete on *data and trust*, not on AI model performance alone. Competitors with larger R&D budgets (Microsoft, Google) will match or exceed Salesforce's AI capabilities over time. The irreplaceable moat lies in the data layer.

The Informatica acquisition is therefore strategically coherent through the VRIN lens. Nearly every Agentforce customer deployment involves Informatica's data infrastructure, according to Salesforce's President and Chief Engineering Officer Srini Tallapragada. The communicated rationale behind the acquisition is to build an agentic AI platform where Informatica solutions provide the metadata, data management functionality, and important pieces of the data layer. By acquiring these capabilities, Salesforce deepens the inimitability of its data layer — converting a temporary technical advantage into a more durable structural one.

The deepest integrations are Salesforce-to-Salesforce. The more customer data, segments, and AI models an organisation builds inside Data Cloud, the harder it becomes to migrate to a different platform. This "behavioural lock-in created by layered dependency over time" is precisely the kind of VRIN resource that sustains competitive advantage.

### 4.4 Dynamic Capabilities (Teece, Pisano & Shuen, 1997)

Teece, Pisano, and Shuen (1997) define dynamic capabilities as a firm's ability to integrate, build, and reconfigure internal and external competences to address rapidly changing environments. Teece (2007) further expanded this by identifying the micro-foundations of dynamic capabilities: sensing opportunities, seizing them, and transforming resources, necessary for sustainable performance.

**Sensing:** Salesforce demonstrated strong sensing capability by identifying the agentic AI paradigm shift before most enterprise software competitors. According to Salesforce SVP of Product Adam Evans, Agentforce builds on the company's four-year transformation of its core platform. At Dreamforce 2024, customers created over 10,000 autonomous agents in three days using the platform, confirming genuine market demand. The communicated rationale behind the Informatica acquisition is to build an agentic AI platform where Informatica solutions provide the metadata — a key ingredient — data management functionality, and important pieces of the data layer. This demonstrates Salesforce's ability to sense gaps in its own capability stack.

**Seizing:** Salesforce's seizing capability is evident in the rapid commercialisation of Agentforce. From 5,000 deals in Q4 FY25 to 18,500 in Q3 FY26 to 29,000 by Q4 FY26, Agentforce deal velocity has accelerated every quarter. Paid deals grew 50% quarter-over-quarter in Q3 FY26 alone, showing that pilots are converting to production-grade deployments at scale. However, seizing has been imperfect — the pricing instability reveals that the company has not yet found the optimal business model for AI monetisation.

**Transforming:** This is where Salesforce faces its greatest challenge. The transformation required is not merely technological — it is a fundamental reconfiguration of the business model, from per-seat subscription to consumption-based digital labour. The accelerated adoption of Agentforce and Data Cloud contributes to Salesforce revenue. The reason behind the shift in FY26 growth is the rapid adoption of Agentforce, enabling customers to work with autonomous AI agents and become a truly agentic enterprise. Yet the risk of organisational inertia is real: a $37.9 billion revenue base built on per-seat subscriptions creates powerful internal incentives to protect the existing model rather than cannibalise it.

**Dynamic Capabilities Assessment:** Salesforce scores strongly on *sensing*, adequately on *seizing*, but faces material execution risk on *transforming*. The company must accelerate the business model transformation before Microsoft or AI-native competitors consolidate the agentic enterprise positioning.

### 4.5 Ansoff Matrix (Ansoff, 1957)

Ansoff's Product/Market Growth Matrix evaluates Salesforce's growth options across four quadrants.

**Quadrant 1 — Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets):**
50% of Agentforce and Data Cloud Q3 FY26 bookings came from existing customer expansion, and nearly half of all Fortune 100 companies have adopted Agentforce and Data Cloud solutions. This is the lowest-risk growth vector and should remain the primary near-term revenue driver. However, with 20.7% market share in a maturing large-enterprise segment, incremental gains from pure penetration are diminishing. This quadrant alone cannot deliver the 10%+ organic CAGR required to reach Salesforce's $60 billion FY30 revenue target.

**Quadrant 2 — Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets):**
Salesforce has significant headroom in geographic expansion and in market segments currently underserved — particularly the mid-market. The original $2/conversation model immediately priced out nonprofits, SMBs, and any organisation without a large AI budget. The Flex Credits model at $0.10/action lowers the entry cost for smaller organisations. This quadrant represents a meaningful growth opportunity, particularly if Salesforce can simplify its product offering for the mid-market without sacrificing the platform depth that enterprise customers value.

**Quadrant 3 — Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets):**
This is Salesforce's primary strategic bet. Data Cloud and AI annual recurring revenue reached $900 million in FY25, up 120% year-over-year. From 5,000 deals in Q4 FY25 to 18,500 in Q3 FY26, Agentforce deal velocity has accelerated every quarter. The Informatica acquisition further extends the product portfolio into enterprise data management. This is the highest-priority quadrant for Salesforce's long-term growth, but it carries execution risk given the pricing instability and adoption cycle length.

**Quadrant 4 — Diversification (New Products, New Markets):**
Agentforce 360 spans Marketing, Service, Field Service, Revenue Management, Commerce, and IT Service — enabling Salesforce to move beyond CRM into a broader range of enterprise workflows. This is the highest-risk quadrant but also the largest TAM expansion. The risk is that diversification dilutes focus and stretches execution capacity at a moment when the core transformation is not yet complete.

**Ansoff Assessment:** The coherent strategic logic suggests Salesforce should prioritise **Quadrant 3 (Product Development)** as its primary growth engine, supported by **Quadrant 1 (Market Penetration)** as the revenue foundation and **Quadrant 2 (Market Development)** as a medium-term expansion lever. Quadrant 4 (Diversification) should be pursued selectively and only where the data and platform advantages are genuinely defensible.

## 5. CONNECTING THE CHAIN: THE COHERENT STRATEGIC LOGIC

The five frameworks, taken together, tell a single coherent story:

1. **The industry structure is deteriorating** (Porter's Five Forces): Rivalry is intensifying (Microsoft), substitutes are emerging (AI-native platforms), and the traditional per-seat SaaS model is under structural pressure.

2. **The durable competitive advantage lies in data, not AI models** (VRIN): By converging its proprietary data advantage, a robust trust architecture, a visionary agentic AI roadmap, and an aggressive acquisition strategy, Salesforce has built a formidable platform. The convergence of Salesforce's strategic pillars creates a powerful, self-reinforcing growth engine — a flywheel — that will be difficult for competitors to slow. But this flywheel depends on the data layer remaining inimitable — which requires continuous investment in Data Cloud and the Informatica integration.

3. **Salesforce has the sensing and seizing capabilities to navigate the transition, but transformation is the bottleneck** (Dynamic Capabilities): The company identified the agentic AI opportunity early and has moved quickly to commercialise it. The critical risk is organisational inertia — the difficulty of cannibalising a $35+ billion subscription revenue base.

4. **The growth strategy must pivot from market penetration to product development** (Ansoff): Salesforce announced a new long-term revenue target of $60 billion+ by fiscal year 2030, implying a 10%+ organic FY26 to FY30 compounded annual growth rate. The core CRM market cannot deliver this. Agentforce and the digital labour TAM must contribute meaningfully — which means accelerating the adoption cycle and resolving the monetisation model.

5. **The SWOT synthesis** confirms that Salesforce's strengths (data, ecosystem, brand) are sufficient to win the agentic era — but only if the weaknesses (pricing instability, AI monetisation gap, integration complexity) are addressed before competitors consolidate their positions.

The strategic situation is therefore not a crisis — it is a **managed transition with a clear but time-limited window of opportunity**. Salesforce has approximately 18–24 months to convert Agentforce from a deal-velocity story into a revenue-contribution story before Microsoft's bundling strategy and AI-native competitors materially erode its market position.

## 6. RECOMMENDATIONS & ACTION PLAN

The following six recommendations are derived directly from the analytical chain above. Each is mapped to its framework source, ensuring the analysis leads coherently to the action plan.

### Recommendation 1: Resolve the Monetisation Model — Adopt a Unified Outcome-Based Pricing Architecture
**Framework Source:** Dynamic Capabilities (Seizing) + SWOT (Weakness: Pricing Instability)

Salesforce has now shipped three different pricing models for Agentforce in roughly 18 months. Many people might look at this and see chaos — a company that can't make up its mind. But when the market hasn't converged on how to buy something, you don't force one model — you let customers self-select into the model that matches how they want to buy. While this logic has merit in the short term, prolonged pricing instability prevents enterprise budget owners from modelling total cost of ownership — the single most important factor in large procurement decisions.

**Proposed Action:** Consolidate around a **unified outcome-based pricing architecture** with two tiers: (a) Flex Credits ($0.10/action) for variable, transactional use cases; and (b) an **Enterprise Digital Labour Agreement (EDLA)** — a multi-year subscription bundling Agentforce capacity with Data Cloud and core CRM at a predictable annual fee, priced as a percentage of measurable AI-delivered value (e.g., cost savings from automated case resolution). This mirrors the successful Enterprise Licence Agreement models used by Microsoft and Oracle, which provide revenue predictability for the vendor while reducing procurement friction for buyers.

**KPI:** 30% of new Agentforce bookings under EDLA by end of FY27. Pricing model variants reduced from three to two by end of FY26.

### Recommendation 2: Accelerate the Data Moat — Make Data Cloud the Non-Negotiable Foundation
**Framework Source:** VRIN (Inimitable Resource) + Dynamic Capabilities (Transforming)

The VRIN analysis establishes that Salesforce's data asset is its most genuinely inimitable resource. The deepest integrations are Salesforce-to-Salesforce. The more customer data, segments, and AI models an organisation builds inside Data Cloud, the harder it becomes to migrate to a different platform. Organisations that may want to swap out their CRM, ESP, or commerce platform in the future should consider whether centralising their customer data inside a suite-embedded CDP creates an exit cost that outweighs the integration benefits.

**Proposed Action:** Make Data Cloud a mandatory component of every Agentforce enterprise deployment. Salesforce has already moved to make basic data ingestion free — the right strategic move to lower adoption barriers — while monetising value-added layers (AI model training, real-time segmentation, cross-cloud analytics). The Informatica integration must be accelerated with quarterly published milestones to maintain investor and customer confidence. Salesforce's acquisition of Informatica is a strategic move to enhance its data management and AI capabilities. By integrating Informatica's strengths with existing platforms like MuleSoft, Salesforce aims to provide a comprehensive, agent-ready data platform. However, careful execution will be crucial to realise the full benefits of this integration.

**KPI:** 100% of Agentforce enterprise deployments to include Data Cloud by end of FY27. Informatica integration milestones published quarterly.

### Recommendation 3: Defend the Installed Base Against Microsoft — Launch a Targeted Retention Programme
**Framework Source:** Porter's Five Forces (Rivalry) + SWOT (Threat: Microsoft Bundling)

Microsoft's tools benefit from deep integrations with Office 365, Teams, Outlook, and Azure. For any organisation already heavily in the Microsoft ecosystem, that's a huge plus. It's also appealing for IT leaders managing everything on Azure. Salesforce cannot win a price war against Microsoft — it must win a *value war*.

**Proposed Action:** Launch a **Customer Success Acceleration Programme** targeting the top 5,000 enterprise accounts, deploying dedicated Agentforce implementation teams to ensure customers achieve measurable ROI within 90 days of deployment. Early deployments demonstrate the potential: educational publisher Wiley reported a 40% increase in case resolution during peak back-to-school season using Agentforce. These case studies must be systematically replicated across 10 industry verticals. Simultaneously, Salesforce should leverage its claimed 71,000+ Microsoft customer relationships as a competitive intelligence asset, deploying targeted competitive response teams where Microsoft Copilot is being evaluated.

**KPI:** Enterprise churn below 3% annually. 100 verified ROI case studies across 10 industry verticals published by end of FY26.

### Recommendation 4: Penetrate the Mid-Market — Develop a Simplified Agentforce Starter SKU
**Framework Source:** Ansoff Matrix (Market Development) + SWOT (Opportunity: Mid-Market)

HubSpot held the largest market share in the marketing automation industry at 38.32% in 2024, demonstrating that the mid-market (companies with 50–500 employees) is a significant and growing opportunity that Salesforce currently underserves. The original $2/conversation pricing model "immediately priced out nonprofits, SMBs, and any organisation without a large AI budget."

**Proposed Action:** Develop a **Starter Agentic Suite** — a pre-configured Agentforce package for mid-market companies including Sales, Service, and Marketing agents with pre-built industry templates, limited customisation, and a flat monthly fee. This product should be sold through the partner channel (Salesforce ISV partners and system integrators) rather than the direct enterprise sales force, keeping cost-to-serve low. The Flex Credits model at $0.10/action provides the pricing foundation; the Starter Suite provides the product simplification.

**KPI:** 20,000 net-new mid-market customers by FY28. 10% of Agentforce deals from mid-market within 24 months.

### Recommendation 5: Manage the Self-Disruption Risk — Develop a Seat-to-Agent Migration Framework
**Framework Source:** Dynamic Capabilities (Transforming) + SWOT (Threat: Self-Disruption)

The most existential strategic risk Salesforce faces is that Agentforce's success reduces the number of human seats required — cannibalising per-seat subscription revenue. By the end of FY26, Salesforce had delivered 2.4 billion Agentforce Work Units (AWUs), growing 57% in a single quarter. It shifts the conversation from "how many licences did we sell?" to "how much work did AI actually do?" That's a meaningful distinction for any team trying to justify AI investment to a CFO.

**Proposed Action:** Develop a **Seat-to-Agent Migration Framework** — a structured programme helping customers transition from per-seat licensing to consumption-based Agentforce pricing as AI agents take on more work. This framework must be designed to ensure that total Salesforce spend per customer *increases* (not decreases) as agents replace seats, by expanding the scope of automation beyond what human users previously managed. AWU-based reporting must be included in all quarterly earnings releases to reframe the business model narrative before per-seat revenue decline becomes visible in reported results.

**KPI:** AWU-based reporting in all quarterly earnings by Q2 FY26. Average revenue per customer to increase 15% through agent-driven expansion by FY28.

---

### Recommendation 6: Invest in Trust Architecture as a Regulatory Differentiator
**Framework Source:** VRIN (Valuable, Rare) + Porter's Five Forces (Threat of Substitutes)

Enterprise AI adoption is constrained by concerns about data security, governance, and regulatory compliance — particularly in regulated industries. The Atlas Reasoning Engine integrates Data Cloud for context, Flow for automation, and the Einstein Trust Layer for governance. As the EU AI Act and similar regulations tighten requirements for explainability and human oversight of autonomous AI systems, Salesforce's trust architecture becomes increasingly valuable and increasingly difficult for AI-native competitors to replicate without the 25-year enterprise compliance infrastructure Salesforce has built.

**Proposed Action:** Obtain industry-specific AI compliance certifications (healthcare HIPAA, financial services SOC 2, government FedRAMP) for Agentforce, and publish a public **Agentforce Trust Index** — a transparent, auditable measure of agent accuracy, bias, and compliance — to differentiate from competitors on governance rather than just capability. This converts regulatory burden into competitive advantage.

**KPI:** Agentforce Trust Index published by Q3 FY26. Industry-specific compliance certifications for top 5 regulated verticals by FY27.

## 7. CONCLUSION

Salesforce occupies a genuinely paradoxical strategic position: it is simultaneously the incumbent most threatened by agentic AI and the company best positioned to lead the agentic enterprise transition. The Five Forces analysis reveals an industry structure under pressure from Microsoft's bundling strategy and the emergence of AI-native substitutes. The VRIN analysis, however, establishes that Salesforce's data asset, ecosystem lock-in, and trust architecture are genuinely inimitable — assets that no competitor can replicate on a 2–3 year horizon. The Dynamic Capabilities framework reveals that the company has sensed and seized the agentic AI opportunity effectively, but faces the harder challenge of transforming its business model before organisational inertia and competitor pressure close the window. The Ansoff Matrix confirms that product development (Agentforce as a new product for existing markets) is the primary growth engine, with market development (mid-market expansion) as the key secondary lever.

The six recommendations form a coherent chain: resolve the pricing confusion to accelerate monetisation (Rec. 1); deepen the data moat to make the platform inimitable (Rec. 2); defend the installed base against Microsoft (Rec. 3); expand into the mid-market (Rec. 4); manage the self-disruption risk proactively (Rec. 5); and invest in trust as a regulatory differentiator (Rec. 6). The outlook is strong, but dominance is not preordained. Success will depend on executing this complex strategy while navigating significant market headwinds and competitive pressures.

The strategic situation is not a crisis. It is a transition. The question is not whether Salesforce will survive the AI era — it is whether it will lead it.
