# STRATEGIC CASE STUDY: COMAC AND THE CHALLENGE OF BUILDING A GLOBALLY COMPETITIVE STATE-OWNED AIRCRAFT MANUFACTURER

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This case study examines the Commercial Aircraft Corporation of China (COMAC), a state-owned enterprise established in 2008 to challenge the Boeing–Airbus duopoly in commercial aviation. Despite significant state backing, COMAC faces a compounding strategic crisis: its flagship C919 narrowbody jet is technologically dependent on Western-supplied components it cannot yet replace; production targets have been missed by wide margins; international certification remains years away; and US export controls have demonstrated the fragility of its supply chain. Using **Porter's Five Forces**, the **Resource-Based View (VRIN/VRIO)**, **Dynamic Capabilities theory**, and **PESTEL analysis**, this study argues that COMAC's core strategic tension is a **capability–ambition gap**: state resources are abundant, but the organisational and technological capabilities to deploy them competitively are immature. The analysis leads to a sequenced action plan centred on supply-chain sovereignty, operational scaling, selective internationalisation, and institutional legitimacy.

---

## 1. BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### 1.1 The Organisation

COMAC (Commercial Aircraft Corporation of China) is China's ambitious state-owned aircraft manufacturer, steadily emerging as a serious contender to the long-standing Boeing–Airbus duopoly in commercial aviation, headquartered in Shanghai. The development programme for the C919 was launched in 2008 as a competitor to the Airbus A320 and Boeing 737. COMAC's state ownership structure is highly concentrated: COMAC received a CNY 44 billion ($6.2 billion) capital injection from eight state-owned shareholders, with the State-owned Assets Supervision and Administration Commission topping the list, boosting its stake to 53.1%.

On 29 September 2022, the C919 received its CAAC type certificate. The first production airframe was delivered to China Eastern Airlines on 9 December 2022 and was put into commercial passenger service on 28 May 2023. The C919 seats 158–192 passengers with a range of 4,075–5,555 km and features advanced cockpit design, low noise, and comfortable cabins.

### 1.2 The Strategic Situation

By 2025, COMAC found itself at a critical inflection point. COMAC reduced its 2025 production target from 75 to 25 C919 aircraft, according to confidential sources. China Eastern, Air China, and China Southern, in their financial reporting, expected 32 deliveries in 2025, but as of September only 5 had been delivered to customers, corroborating the production shortfall. The suspension of export licences means that COMAC faces a substantial challenge once existing engine inventories in China are depleted, directly impacting production rates and aircraft delivery schedules. The combined US restrictions on engines, aircraft systems, and avionics strike at China's ambitious strategic goal of creating a globally competitive range of commercial aircraft. European regulators in August 2025 confirmed that formal certification by the European Union Aviation Safety Agency (EASA) is unlikely before 2028 and could slip as far as 2031.

## 2. ANALYTICAL FRAMEWORK

This analysis applies four complementary frameworks in a deliberate sequence. **PESTEL** establishes the macro-environment. **Porter's Five Forces** diagnoses the competitive structure of the industry. The **Resource-Based View (VRIN/VRIO)** assesses whether COMAC's internal assets can generate sustainable competitive advantage. **Dynamic Capabilities** theory asks whether COMAC can reconfigure those assets fast enough to survive geopolitical and technological turbulence. Together: environment → industry structure → internal resources → capacity for change → recommendations.

## 3. PESTEL ANALYSIS

### 3.1 Political

COMAC is an instrument of Chinese industrial policy. The CJ-1000A was launched under the "Made in China 2025" framework in 2015, which set explicit targets for 70% domestic content in core aviation components by 2025. This political mandate is both an enabler—providing unlimited state funding—and a constraint, because it ties strategic decisions to geopolitical calculations. The geopolitical dimension further complicates matters: the US has blocked FAA certification for the C919, a decision tied to broader tensions over China's industrial policies. Conversely, the 125% duty imposed by China in April 2025 on American imports, in retaliation for US President Trump's tariff hikes, significantly raised the cost of Boeing jets being sold to Chinese airlines, with analysts believing Chinese airlines might begin to lean more strongly towards domestic alternatives like COMAC. **The political environment is simultaneously COMAC's greatest asset and its most unpredictable risk.**

### 3.2 Economic

All Chinese airlines are likely to induct a total of 6,000 new single-aisle aircraft by 2042, according to aviation analytics firm Cirium, and COMAC is likely to capture approximately 25% market share of these new additions. COMAC's registered capital has increased to CNY 94.1 billion from CNY 50.1 billion, following the late-2024 capital injection. Backed by state subsidies, COMAC doesn't face the same market pressures as Boeing or Airbus. It can afford to move slowly, absorb losses, and focus on long-term geopolitical goals. However, this financial insulation also reduces the incentive for operational efficiency—a classic principal–agent problem in SOEs.

### 3.3 Social

COMAC aims to take a fifth of the global narrowbody market and a third of the Chinese market by 2035. The Financial Times commented that China considers the success of the plane a source of national pride. This social legitimacy creates a protected domestic customer base. However, international airlines are governed by different norms: safety record, brand trust, and MRO network quality matter more than national origin. As of December 2024, all 16 operational C919s had carried more than one million revenue passengers—beginning to build an operational track record, but thin by global standards.

### 3.4 Technological

The C919 relies heavily on Western suppliers—CFM engines, Honeywell, Collins—despite Chinese branding. Without FAA/EASA certification, the C919 remains limited to China's domestic market. Over 55% of its components come from American firms. US sanctions imposed in May 2025 have suspended export licences for these parts, threatening COMAC's 50-aircraft annual production target. The C919's utilisation rate of 5.2 hours per day is below the narrowbody industry average of 7 hours per day, a proxy for operational immaturity. **The technological gap between COMAC's current capabilities and those needed for global competition is wide and will not close quickly.**

### 3.5 Environmental

The C919's CFM LEAP-1C engine delivers fuel burn comparable to the A320neo and 737 MAX, which is a genuine strength. However, as competitors develop next-generation propulsion, COMAC's technological dependence could leave it behind in the next technology cycle.

### 3.6 Legal/Regulatory

The C919 is still not certified by the FAA or EASA. Without these certifications, the C919 is limited to China's domestic market and a few other regions. In May 2024, there were indications that China hoped to secure EASA certification by 2025, but reports suggest this goal may be overly optimistic. IBA forecasts international expansion is constrained by the slow pace of EASA certification, now expected no earlier than 2028. Typically, the FAA requires between five and nine years to complete type certification. **Without EASA certification, COMAC cannot access European, North American, or most Middle Eastern markets.**

## 4. PORTER'S FIVE FORCES ANALYSIS

### 4.1 Rivalry Among Existing Competitors — VERY HIGH

Willie Walsh, the former CEO of British Airways and now head of IATA, told AGBI: "Comac is a 20-year issue." Last month, Airbus reported an order backlog of 8,720 jets, while Boeing held 6,319 unfulfilled orders, according to data from Forecast International. By 2030, IBA forecasts Comac will have secured around 65% of new narrow-body deliveries to Chinese operators, but globally the C919 and C909 will remain a niche player at approximately 2% of the global fleet in 2030. The competitive intensity is asymmetric: rivalry is not yet existential for incumbents, but is existential for COMAC's credibility.

### 4.2 Threat of New Entrants — LOW

Commercial aircraft manufacturing has some of the highest barriers to entry in any industry. The failure of Japan's Mitsubishi SpaceJet programme—abandoned after years of certification struggles—illustrates that new entrants can fail even with substantial industrial backing. **The lesson for COMAC is that entry barriers are bilateral: they protect incumbents but also punish under-resourced challengers.**

### 4.3 Bargaining Power of Suppliers — VERY HIGH

COMAC delivered only 13 C919 aircraft instead of the planned 75, primarily due to disrupted supplies of CFM International's LEAP-1C engines. A two-month US export ban in May–July 2025 created ripple effects throughout the year, despite COMAC's stockpiling efforts. Industry analysts estimate that direct financial losses for COMAC due to export restrictions could reach several billion US dollars over the next 3–5 years, stemming from slower production rates, increased costs of finding alternative sources, and delays in certification processes. **Supplier power is the most immediate strategic threat COMAC faces, and it is structurally embedded in the aircraft's design.**

### 4.4 Bargaining Power of Buyers — Domestic: LOW; International: HIGH

Domestic Chinese airlines have limited bargaining power in practice: they are state-owned and subject to government direction. All Chinese airlines are likely to induct a total of 6,000 new single-aisle aircraft by 2042, with COMAC likely to capture approximately 25% market share. However, international buyers have high bargaining power because they have genuine alternatives and require EASA/FAA certification as a precondition. The C919 is officially priced at $100 million, but sources close to COMAC suggest it can be purchased for half that amount, along with export credits of up to 85% of the purchase price—terms that reflect the buyer's leverage internationally.

### 4.5 Threat of Substitutes — MODERATE

Lessor orders for COMAC aircraft are largely concentrated among Chinese lessors and are expected to be primarily allocated to Chinese airlines in the future. Consequently, Boeing's proportional market share may continue to decline unless there are substantial improvements in its standing within the Chinese market. The substitute for the C919 in the Chinese market is the A320neo—available today, certified everywhere, and increasingly manufactured locally. Airbus is increasing production of comparable single-aisle jets within China, intensifying the competition for scarce parts.

### Porter's Five Forces — Summary Table

| Force | Intensity | Key Implication |
|---|---|---|
| Competitive Rivalry | Very High | COMAC is a marginal player in a market dominated by entrenched duopolists |
| Threat of New Entrants | Low | High barriers protect incumbents; COMAC benefits domestically |
| Supplier Power | Very High | Western component dependence creates existential vulnerability |
| Buyer Power | Domestic Low / International High | Captive home market essential; international expansion requires price concessions |
| Threat of Substitutes | Moderate | A320neo is a direct, certified, locally-produced substitute |

**Five Forces Conclusion:** The industry is structurally hostile for COMAC as a challenger. Supplier power is acute, international buyer power is high, and rivalry with entrenched incumbents is asymmetric. This means COMAC cannot compete on industry structure alone—it must build internal capabilities that offset structural disadvantage. This finding motivates the RBV analysis below.

## 5. RESOURCE-BASED VIEW (VRIN/VRIO) ANALYSIS

The Resource-Based View (Barney, 1991) holds that sustained competitive advantage flows from resources that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN). Where a firm also has the **Organisational** capacity to exploit such resources, the framework becomes VRIO.

### 5.1 State Financial Backing — Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, but Organisationally Constrained

COMAC's access to state capital is genuinely rare and inimitable. A USD 6.2 billion capital injection from eight state-owned shareholders in late 2024 nearly doubled COMAC's registered capital, signalling Beijing's continued willingness to fund the programme through industrial bottlenecks rather than market discipline. China's strategy for the COMAC C919 mirrors the playbook Airbus used in the 1970s and 1980s to challenge Boeing and McDonnell Douglas: it is leveraging massive state backing, subsidised financing, and a huge captive home market to establish credibility, accumulate operational data, and prove reliability before pushing abroad. However, the organisational capacity to deploy this capital efficiently is constrained by SOE governance: accountability for missed targets appears limited. **The resource is VRIN, but the 'O' (organisation) is weak—meaning the advantage is not yet sustainable.**

### 5.2 Captive Domestic Market — Valuable, Rare, Temporarily Inimitable

Chinese airlines are under strong political pressure to reduce reliance on Boeing. Boeing's Chinese customers are refusing delivery of new airplanes built for them because of US tariffs. This creates a protected market that Airbus and Boeing cannot access on equal terms. However, it is not permanently inimitable: Airbus is positioned to capture Boeing's lost market share in China, while also supplying critical components. **The domestic market is a strategic asset, but it is being eroded by Airbus's local production strategy.**

### 5.3 The C919 Aircraft Platform — Valuable, Moderately Rare, Partially Imitable

Over 55% of its components come from American firms, meaning the platform's value is contingent on continued access to foreign supply chains—a dependency that has already been weaponised. The C919 is a valuable but fragile resource whose competitive advantage is contingent rather than durable.

### 5.4 Human Capital and Institutional Knowledge — Valuable but Immature

The C919 started with minimal utilisation in its proving phase but has now reached 5.2 hours per day. This figure is expected to grow as the fleet expands and in-service maintenance, supply chains, and procedures mature. For comparison, the average utilisation rate for narrowbody aircraft is 7 hours per day. This gap is a proxy for institutional immaturity. **Human capital is a resource in formation, not yet a source of competitive advantage.**

### 5.5 CJ-1000A Engine Programme — Potentially VRIN, but Not Yet Deployable

As of May 2018, AECC projected certification by 2027 and entry into service by 2030, roughly eight years behind the original timeline. A domestic alternative, the CJ-1000A, is still in flight testing and years from readiness. If successfully developed, it would be genuinely rare (very few nations can produce commercial turbofans), inimitable, and non-substitutable. **The CJ-1000A is a future VRIN resource, but its development risk is high and its timeline is uncertain.**

### VRIO Summary Table

| Resource | V | R | I | N | O | Competitive Implication |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| State financial backing | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | Partial | Temporary advantage; SOE inefficiency limits exploitation |
| Captive domestic market | ✓ | ✓ | Partial | Partial | ✓ | Competitive parity (Airbus eroding it) |
| C919 platform | ✓ | Partial | ✗ | ✗ | Partial | Fragile competitive parity |
| Human capital/knowledge | ✓ | Partial | Partial | Partial | ✗ | Competitive disadvantage (immature) |
| CJ-1000A engine | ✓ (future) | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✗ (not ready) | Sustained advantage *if* achieved |

**RBV Conclusion:** COMAC currently holds no resource that meets all VRIN criteria in an *exploitable* form. The CJ-1000A represents the most strategically significant future resource—but it is a decade-long bet with high execution risk. This finding directly motivates the dynamic capabilities analysis.

## 6. DYNAMIC CAPABILITIES ANALYSIS

Dynamic capabilities (Teece, Pisano & Shuen, 1997) are the firm's ability to integrate, build, and reconfigure internal and external competences to address rapidly changing environments. They comprise three clusters: **sensing**, **seizing**, and **reconfiguring**.

### 6.1 Sensing Capabilities — Adequate

COMAC has correctly identified that Boeing's declining Chinese market share creates a window; that Southeast Asia is an accessible first international market; and that European suppliers can hedge against US export controls. COMAC's strategy, including roadshows in Malaysia and training workshops for Southeast Asian regulators in Beijing, aims to deploy the C919 on commercial routes to the region by 2026, leveraging free trade zones and harmonised legislation. COMAC used its presence at the 2025 Paris Air Show to demonstrate credibility on the global stage, showcasing its full product family. However, sensing has been weaker on downside risks: the 2025 production target of 75 aircraft was set without adequate contingency for supply-chain disruption. **Sensing is adequate for opportunity identification but has underestimated geopolitical risk.**

### 6.2 Seizing Capabilities — Critical Bottleneck

The company has the financial resources to act, but converting them into operational output has proven difficult. The acting EASA Director Luc Tytgat admitted that the agency is "starting anew" with some aspects of the C919's design, which could stretch the timeline further. COMAC's seizing capability is also constrained by SOE governance: decisions requiring rapid supplier switching are slowed by bureaucratic processes. **Seizing capability is the critical bottleneck—financial resources are available but operational agility is low.**

### 6.3 Reconfiguring Capabilities — Nascent but Slow

The lifting of US export licence suspensions in July 2025 for the CFM International LEAP-1C and GE CF34-10A engines provided temporary relief for the C919 and C909 supply chains, though dependence on US-made core modules remains a strategic vulnerability. COMAC has stockpiled some engines and key systems, yet the reality of modern aerospace manufacturing means these buffers cover only months of production rather than years. COMAC is pivoting to European suppliers. Safran stands to gain as COMAC seeks to reclassify engines as "European-made" to avoid tariffs. Similarly, Thales and Liebherr, which provide avionics and hydraulic systems, are now in demand as COMAC cuts ties with US suppliers. **Reconfiguring capability exists in intent but lags in execution speed.**

### Dynamic Capabilities Conclusion

COMAC's dynamic capability profile is asymmetric: reasonable sensing, weak seizing, slow reconfiguring. This is characteristic of large SOEs operating in institutionally complex environments. COMAC needs not just more resources, but different organisational routines—faster decision cycles, more commercial accountability, and genuine technology transfer mechanisms that build internal capability rather than dependence.

## 7. SYNTHESIS: THE STRATEGIC TENSION

The four frameworks converge on a single diagnosis: COMAC faces a **capability–ambition gap** with three interlocking dimensions.

**1. Technological dependency vs. sovereignty ambition.** While primarily powered by Western CFM LEAP-1C engines and relying on foreign components for key systems, the C919 has gained traction domestically. But this is not a temporary inconvenience—it is a structural vulnerability already exploited by US export controls. Until the CJ-1000A and domestic avionics are operational, COMAC's production is hostage to geopolitical decisions made in Washington.

**2. Domestic scale vs. international legitimacy.** COMAC claims more than 1,000 orders, with some sources citing 1,200. However, independent verification by Cirium places the number near 1,000. A significant portion consists of letters of intent and framework agreements from state-linked leasing companies. Firm commitments from identified airline customers sit closer to 300–400 aircraft. Notably, the sole international order comes from GallopAir of Brunei—a startup airline backed by Chinese investors that has not yet taken delivery of any aircraft. The captive home market sustains the programme financially, but not the global credibility needed to win international orders.

**3. State ownership as both shield and anchor.** State backing provides unlimited capital and political protection, but also creates principal–agent problems, reduces commercial agility, and generates the very geopolitical suspicion that makes international buyers hesitant. COMAC benefits from protectionist policies and state-directed procurement, ensuring a growing share of the Chinese aviation market, the world's second largest. But this protection is not transferable to international markets.

## 8. RECOMMENDATIONS AND ACTION PLAN

### Recommendation 1: Accelerate CJ-1000A Certification as the Highest Strategic Priority (Immediate–2030)

**Rationale:** The Five Forces analysis identifies supplier power as the most acute structural threat; the VRIO analysis identifies the CJ-1000A as the only resource with potential for sustained competitive advantage; and the Dynamic Capabilities analysis shows that reconfiguring away from Western dependence is the most critical capability gap. COMAC delivered only 13 C919 aircraft instead of the planned 75, primarily due to disrupted supplies of CFM International's LEAP-1C engines. A two-month US export ban in May–July 2025 created ripple effects throughout the year, despite COMAC's stockpiling efforts. A domestic engine removes this vulnerability.

**Action:** COMAC and AECC should treat the CJ-1000A as a national emergency programme. The engine should be designed from the outset to meet EASA airworthiness standards, not retrofitted later. Even after domestic CAAC certification, risks include production ramp-up delays and supply chain maturity, alongside the need for EASA or FAA recognition to enable international market access. Parallel-tracking CAAC certification (targeting 2027) and EASA pre-certification engagement is therefore essential.

**Why the evidence warrants this conclusion:** Dependence on US-made core modules remains a strategic vulnerability. COMAC has stockpiled some engines and key systems, yet the reality of modern aerospace manufacturing means these buffers cover only months of production rather than years. The US export ban provides empirical proof that the risk is not theoretical. The CJ-1000A is the only path to genuine supply-chain sovereignty.

---

### Recommendation 2: Stabilise and Scale Production Before Expanding Ambition (2025–2028)

**Rationale:** The Dynamic Capabilities analysis shows seizing capability is the critical bottleneck. Announcing targets of 75, then 100, then 200 aircraft annually while delivering 15 destroys credibility with customers, investors, and regulators. COMAC ended 2025 with 41 aircraft delivered (15 C919 narrowbodies and 26 C909 regional jets), well short of its original 75-unit C919 target.

**Action:** Adopt a "credibility-first" production strategy: set conservative targets (28–30 aircraft in 2026), meet them consistently, and use each successful delivery cycle to identify and resolve bottlenecks before scaling. This requires: (a) a dedicated supply-chain resilience function with authority to dual-source critical components; (b) an engine stockpiling strategy extending buffer stocks to at least 18 months; (c) transparent reporting to state shareholders on production metrics, with accountability mechanisms that reward operational delivery rather than target-setting.

**Why the evidence warrants this conclusion:** IBA forecasts annual deliveries could reach 90 by 2030, which represents significant growth, although still far below the monthly output of Airbus. This trajectory is achievable only if each year's target is met. The pattern of ambitious target-setting followed by revision undermines the airline customer confidence essential for the domestic market to remain captive.

### Recommendation 3: Pursue EASA Certification as a Strategic Investment, Not a Commercial Prerequisite (2025–2031)

**Rationale:** The PESTEL and Five Forces analyses show that EASA certification is the gateway to international markets. The certification process itself is strategically valuable: it forces COMAC to meet global airworthiness standards, improving the aircraft's safety profile and building institutional knowledge.

**Action:** COMAC should: (a) dedicate a senior technical team exclusively to EASA engagement with authority to make design modifications without waiting for corporate sign-off; (b) address the avionics issues identified as the specific sticking point by partnering with European avionics suppliers (Thales, Liebherr) who have existing EASA relationships; (c) use Southeast Asia as an operational proving ground to accumulate the safety data EASA requires; (d) commission independent safety audits and publish results.

**Why the evidence warrants this conclusion:** Acting EASA Director Luc Tytgat admitted that the agency is "starting anew" with some aspects of the C919's design, which could stretch the timeline further. This indicates the certification gap is partly a documentation and transparency problem, not only a technical one. By the time the C919 enters Western markets, it would be competing against the Airbus A320neo and Boeing 737 MAX families more than a decade after those aircraft entered service—making early engagement with EASA essential to minimise the competitive lag.

### Recommendation 4: Execute a Sequenced International Market Entry Strategy (2026–2035)

**Rationale:** The RBV analysis shows that COMAC's captive domestic market is being eroded by Airbus's local production. The company must begin building international credibility before the domestic market becomes contested. However, the Five Forces analysis shows international buyers have high bargaining power and require EASA/FAA certification.

**Action:** *Phase 1 (2026–2028):* Deploy C919 on Southeast Asian routes through Chinese state-owned carriers, building operational data on international routes and pursuing bilateral airworthiness agreements with ASEAN regulators. *Phase 2 (2028–2031):* Once EASA certification is achieved, target European low-cost carriers. *Phase 3 (2031+):* Use the CJ-1000A-powered C919 as the basis for a truly independent international offering.

**Why the evidence warrants this conclusion:** AirAsia CEO Tony Fernandes confirmed during the Belt and Road Summit in Hong Kong that the airline is in active talks with COMAC over a potential order. If finalized, AirAsia would become the first foreign airline to secure the Chinese-made single-aisle jet. COMAC's global aspirations are also attracting European interest: Ryanair CEO Michael O'Leary remarked: "If it were cheap enough, 10 or 20% cheaper than an Airbus aircraft, then we'd order it." These signals confirm genuine international demand—but it is price-sensitive and conditional on certification, confirming the sequenced approach.

### Recommendation 5: Restructure Governance to Separate Commercial and Political Objectives (Ongoing)

**Rationale:** The Dynamic Capabilities analysis identifies SOE governance as a systemic constraint on seizing and reconfiguring capabilities. The PESTEL analysis shows COMAC's state ownership generates geopolitical suspicion among international buyers and regulators. The VRIO analysis shows the 'O' (organisational capacity) is the weakest link in the chain.

**Action:** Establish a commercially-governed subsidiary responsible for international sales and certification, with a board that includes independent directors and international aviation industry expertise. This subsidiary should be insulated from direct SASAC direction on commercial decisions—similar to the relationship between Airbus and its national government shareholders. COMAC should also adopt transparent production reporting (monthly delivery data, certification progress milestones) to build the institutional credibility international buyers require.

**Why the evidence warrants this conclusion:** A USD 6.2 billion capital injection from eight state-owned shareholders in late 2024 nearly doubled COMAC's registered capital, signalling Beijing's continued willingness to fund the programme **through industrial bottlenecks** rather than market discipline. This framing—funding *through* bottlenecks rather than resolving them—is precisely the SOE principal–agent problem. China's strategy mirrors the playbook Airbus used in the 1970s and 1980s—but Airbus operated with commercial governance alongside state backing, and this was essential to its credibility in international markets.

## 10. CONCLUSION

COMAC's strategic situation is one of genuine but fragile progress. The C919 is a real aircraft carrying real passengers, backed by real state resources. But the Five Forces analysis reveals a structurally hostile competitive environment; the VRIO analysis shows no currently exploitable resource generating sustained advantage; and the Dynamic Capabilities analysis identifies seizing and reconfiguring as critical weaknesses. The PESTEL analysis confirms that the geopolitical environment is simultaneously COMAC's greatest enabler (state support, captive market, Boeing's decline) and its greatest threat (US export controls, EASA barriers, international buyer suspicion).

The coherent strategic conclusion is that COMAC must prioritise **supply-chain sovereignty** (CJ-1000A), **operational credibility** (consistent production), and **institutional legitimacy** (EASA certification, governance reform) before it can realistically claim to be a global competitor. As IATA CEO Willie Walsh observed, "Comac is a 20-year issue." The question is whether those twenty years are used to build genuine capability or to paper over structural weaknesses with capital injections. The recommendations above are designed to ensure the former.
