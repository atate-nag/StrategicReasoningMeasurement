# Strategic Case Study: Mercedes-Benz and the Electric Transition Dilemma (2021–2025)

## *A Strategic Analysis Using Porter's Five Forces, the Resource-Based View, Ansoff's Matrix, and Porter's Generic Strategies*

---

## 1. Introduction and Case Overview

Mercedes-Benz Group AG — one of the world's oldest and most prestigious automotive manufacturers — entered the 2020s with an ambitious strategic pivot. Under CEO Ola Källenius, the company turned back a decades-old strategy of chasing sales volume to focus on the industry's most profitable segments, launching a programme to cut costs by over 20% by 2025 to take the brand further upmarket. The logic appeared sound: if Mercedes could sell fewer but more expensive cars at higher margins, it would be insulated from cyclical volume pressures and commodity competition.

Yet by 2024–2025, this strategy had produced a compounding crisis. For 2024, the Mercedes-Benz Group reported revenue of EUR 145.6 billion (down 4.5% YoY), EBIT of EUR 13.6 billion (down 30.8% YoY), and net profit of EUR 10.4 billion (down 28.4% YoY). The flagship electric vehicles had failed commercially, the Chinese market — its largest — was collapsing, and arch-rival BMW had opened a significant lead. In 2024, BMW sold almost 250,000 more units than Mercedes. This case study asks: **Why did Mercedes-Benz's "top-end luxury" EV strategy fail to deliver, and what should the company do next?**

The analysis proceeds through four frameworks — Porter's Five Forces (industry structure), the Resource-Based View (internal capabilities), Ansoff's Growth Matrix (strategic direction), and Porter's Generic Strategies (competitive positioning) — before synthesising these into a coherent set of recommendations.

---

## 2. The Strategic Situation: Context and Evidence

### 2.1 The "Top-End" Bet and the EQ Brand

Mercedes-Benz's post-2020 strategy rested on two pillars: electrification and premiumisation. The share of top-end vehicle sales was projected to rise by around 60% by 2026, with a product offensive from Maybach, AMG and G-Class carrying dedicated EV characteristics and greater individualisation, alongside a "MYTHOS Series" of highly-exclusive collectible vehicles. Simultaneously, the EQ sub-brand was created to be the electric expression of this premium identity. An important lever was to increase net revenue per unit by raising the proportion of high-end electric vehicles such as Mercedes-Maybach and Mercedes-AMG models, while at the same time taking more direct control over pricing and sales.

### 2.2 The EQ Failure

The EQ strategy did not deliver. Sales of the EQS sedan and EQS SUV were down 52% in the U.S. in 2024 versus the previous year. The EQS's styling, defined by the ultra-aerodynamic "jelly bean" design, faced long-running criticism, which hindered the sales Mercedes had hoped for — and the automaker has since admitted it missed the mark with the EQS. In the first half of 2025, EQ deliveries in the U.S. slid nearly 50 percent compared with 2024. Critics cited an unorthodox design language and interiors that fell short of the tactile luxury buyers expect from the iconic E-Class and S-Class. This is a critical finding: the product failed not on powertrain grounds but on *brand promise* grounds — the very dimension Mercedes was supposed to own.

### 2.3 The China Collapse

The most significant factor behind the sales drop was the 7% reduction in Mercedes-Benz's sales in China, its largest market. High-end models such as the S-Class and Maybach saw a sharp decline in demand, as local consumers increasingly favoured local brands. Sales of the passenger car division in China fell by seven percent in 2024, and by a staggering 27 percent in the third quarter of 2025. At the same time, sales of luxury vehicles like the S-Class plummeted by 14 percent.

### 2.4 The Strategic Reversal

Faced with these results, CEO Ola Källenius now plans to manufacture premium vehicles across all price segments instead of concentrating solely on high-margin, top-tier models. Mercedes-Benz needs to sell more cars to make up for a tough 2025 and wants to return to 2019 sales levels. This reversal — from "fewer, more expensive" back toward volume — is itself a strategic signal of failure that demands explanation.

## 3. Framework 1: Porter's Five Forces — Industry Structure Analysis

Porter's Five Forces framework (1980) analyses the structural attractiveness of an industry by examining five competitive forces. Applied to Mercedes-Benz's position in the premium automotive sector, the framework reveals *why* the industry has become structurally less favourable, and why the EV transition amplified those pressures.

### 3.1 Rivalry Among Existing Competitors — **HIGH and Intensifying**

The luxury and premium automobile industry is very competitive. The major competitors of Mercedes-Benz include BMW, Audi, Volvo, Jaguar, and others. The level of competitive rivalry in the automobile industry is reasonably high. Crucially, rivalry has been amplified by the EV transition. China competition and discounting intensified in 2024–2025; Tesla's price agility and strong local EV players pressure margins and share. When Tesla cut prices aggressively, it forced other premium brands — including Mercedes — to follow suit, directly undermining the pricing power that was central to the top-end strategy. As EQ inventories accumulated, Mercedes slashed prices in response — a move antithetical to luxury brand management.

**Implication for the analysis:** Rivalry is the most immediately damaging force. The EV segment has lowered switching costs (buyers less tied to service networks) and introduced price-aggressive competitors from outside the traditional premium tier. This undermines the core assumption of the top-end strategy: that Mercedes could hold price premiums.

### 3.2 Threat of New Entrants — **MODERATE but Structurally Shifting**

Historically, the threat of new entrants into premium automotive was low due to capital intensity, brand equity requirements, and regulatory complexity. However, the EV transition has partially reset these barriers. Tesla entered the luxury segment without a century of heritage and beat Mercedes-Benz for the number three spot in new U.S. luxury-car registrations through September of a recent year — demonstrating that heritage alone no longer guarantees market position. Chinese OEMs represent a further structural shift: German manufacturers all invested heavily in China in recent decades but are now facing fierce competition from local rivals.

**Implication:** The entry barriers that historically protected Mercedes (brand, distribution, engineering reputation) are being eroded in the EV segment. New entrants do not need to replicate the full Mercedes value chain — they can compete on software, range, and price, bypassing the traditional moats.

### 3.3 Bargaining Power of Buyers — **MODERATE to HIGH**

The negotiation power of vehicle buyers has continued to strengthen as a result of many factors including greater price transparency, online comparison tools, and the proliferation of alternatives. In the EV segment specifically, buyer power is elevated because range, charging infrastructure, and software quality are now easily compared across brands. The failure of the EQS illustrates this: buyers could compare it directly to Tesla's Model S and found it wanting on design and value, exercising their power by not buying. Mercedes slashed prices for the EQE sedan from $76,050 to $66,100, and for the EQS sedan from $105,550 to $101,400 — a direct concession to buyer power.

### 3.4 Bargaining Power of Suppliers — **MODERATE**

Mercedes-Benz has established a very large network of approximately 4,000 direct suppliers in Asia, Europe, and North America. The large supplier network helps the company smoothly perform its various operations. However, the transition to EVs introduces new supplier dependencies — particularly for battery cells and semiconductor chips — where supplier concentration is high and Mercedes does not yet have the vertical integration of Tesla. This represents a structural vulnerability as the EV mix grows.

### 3.5 Threat of Substitutes — **MODERATE and Growing**

Beyond direct automotive competition, the broader threat of substitutes — ride-hailing, autonomous mobility-as-a-service, and public transport in dense urban markets — represents a long-term structural pressure. More immediately, within the luxury segment, Aston Martin, Bentley, and Rolls-Royce saw sales volumes shrink by 17%, 27%, and 13% respectively in a recent period, suggesting the ultra-luxury tier itself is facing demand headwinds from macroeconomic conditions.

**Five Forces Summary:** The overall industry structure has deteriorated for Mercedes-Benz. Rivalry has intensified, buyer power has grown, and new entrants have disrupted the premium EV segment. The forces analysis explains *why* the top-end strategy was structurally risky: it bet on premium pricing power precisely when the forces eroding that power were strongest.

## 4. Framework 2: The Resource-Based View — Internal Capabilities

The Resource-Based View (Barney, 1991) holds that sustained competitive advantage derives from resources and capabilities that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN). This framework shifts attention from the external environment to Mercedes-Benz's internal asset base, and helps explain both the original logic of the top-end strategy and why it broke down.

### 4.1 Strengths: VRIN Resources

**Brand Equity and Heritage:** Mercedes-Benz benefits from a century-long legacy, establishing instant global recognition. According to Interbrand, Mercedes-Benz currently stands as the most valuable luxury brand on a global scale. This brand equity is genuinely rare and historically inimitable — it took over 130 years to build. It is the foundation of the premium pricing strategy.

**Engineering and Innovation Legacy:** Since Carl Benz registered his patent for the motor car in 1886 and Gottlieb Daimler built his motorised carriage shortly afterwards, the history of Mercedes-Benz has for 140 years known only one direction: forward. This engineering culture represents an organisational capability embedded in human capital and institutional knowledge.

**Diverse Portfolio as Risk Mitigation:** A diverse product portfolio, spanning from vans to high-end models like AMG and Maybach, effectively mitigates market risks. The company's premium segment strategy is validated by strong sales growth in luxury vehicles during Q1 2024.

**Emerging Software Capability (MB.OS):** Mercedes-Benz's strategy of developing its own automotive OS aligns with the trend toward software-defined vehicles. By 2025, Mercedes-Benz intends to target €1 billion from software-enabled, features-on-demand. This is a capability under construction — not yet a VRIN resource, but potentially so.

### 4.2 Weaknesses: Resource Gaps

**EV Platform Limitations:** The EQ brand was built on the EVA2 platform, which proved inadequate. The MB.EA platform was meant to replace the EVA2 platform used in the EQE and EQS, but new reports suggest the two platforms could coexist for a few more years — indicating a transition period of competitive vulnerability.

**Software Capability Gap:** The Mercedes-Benz Group stock has come under pressure as the luxury automaker grapples with a sluggish transition to electric vehicles amid weakening global demand and fierce rivalry from Tesla's chip and robotaxi advances — specifically referencing software capability as a competitive gap.

**High Cost Structure:** Mercedes-Benz's focus on premium features drives up production expenses. This can make it harder to compete on price. In a market where Tesla uses software to create perceived value at lower cost, Mercedes's hardware-intensive cost structure becomes a liability.

**RBV Implication:** The RBV analysis reveals a paradox at the heart of the Mercedes strategy. Its most valuable VRIN resource — brand heritage — is precisely what the EQ line *failed to leverage*. The EQS did not feel like a Mercedes to buyers; it felt like a technology experiment. The brand resource was present but not deployed effectively in the product. This is not a resource problem per se but a *capability deployment* problem: Mercedes possessed the brand asset but lacked the product development capability to translate it into an EV that felt authentically premium.

## 5. Framework 3: Ansoff's Growth Matrix — Strategic Direction

Ansoff's Matrix (1957) maps growth strategies along two dimensions: products (existing vs. new) and markets (existing vs. new). It is useful here for evaluating whether Mercedes's strategic choices were coherent, and for identifying the most viable path forward.

### 5.1 Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets)

Mercedes's traditional strength — selling S-Class, GLE, and AMG models to established premium buyers in Europe and North America — represents its market penetration quadrant. This has been the most reliable source of revenue but has come under pressure. Reducing the number of compact models dramatically impacted sales volumes — which is exactly what happened. The decision to exit the volume segment of existing markets in pursuit of top-end only reduced the penetration of the core business without adequately replacing it.

### 5.2 Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets)

The EQ brand was fundamentally a product development strategy: new electric vehicles sold to existing Mercedes customers. This is where the strategy failed most visibly. The EVs were new products but they failed to meet the expectations of existing Mercedes buyers. The RBV analysis explains why: the brand promise of tactile luxury, heritage design, and engineering excellence was not embedded in the EQ product range. Interiors fell short of the tactile luxury buyers expect from the iconic E-Class and S-Class. A product development strategy only succeeds if the new product credibly extends the brand's existing value proposition.

The corrective pivot — the new CLA — represents a more credible product development move. Powered by an advanced 800-volt electric architecture, the new CLA maximises performance, efficiency and can significantly reduce charging times. With DC fast charging up to 320 kW, it is possible to add 100 miles of range in just five minutes. Crucially, the CLA is designed to look and feel like a Mercedes, addressing the brand-authenticity failure of the EQS.

### 5.3 Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets)

India delivered double-digit growth in 2024–2025, prompting expanded assembly in Chakan, Pune to add luxury EV models — representing a market development move into high-growth emerging markets. This is a lower-risk diversification of geographic exposure, reducing dependence on China.

### 5.4 Diversification (New Products, New Markets)

Mercedes-Benz intends to target €1 billion from software-enabled, features-on-demand by 2025 — representing a diversification into recurring digital revenue streams. OEMs are monetising software and ADAS through subscriptions and features-on-demand; Mercedes can leverage MB.OS and IONNA charging hubs to improve BEV convenience and recurring revenue streams. This is the highest-risk quadrant but also the highest-potential one, given that software margins far exceed hardware margins.

**Ansoff Implication:** Mercedes's error was attempting too many quadrants simultaneously — abandoning market penetration (volume), failing at product development (EQ), and investing heavily in diversification (software, luxury-only) before the foundations were secure. The Ansoff logic suggests a sequencing problem: product development must be credible before diversification can be funded.

## 6. Framework 4: Porter's Generic Strategies — Competitive Positioning

Porter (1980) argues that firms must choose a clear generic strategy — cost leadership, differentiation, or focus — to avoid being "stuck in the middle." Mercedes-Benz has historically pursued **focused differentiation**: targeting the premium segment with products differentiated by quality, heritage, and engineering. The question is whether the recent strategy remained coherent within this framework.

### 6.1 The Coherence of Focused Differentiation

Mercedes-Benz is not a mass production industry but is a premium brand with the slogan 'Best or Nothing'. The vehicles are characterised by excellence, safety, luxury, comfort, performance, and style. This is a textbook focused differentiation position. The "top-end" strategy of 2020–2024 attempted to *intensify* this position — moving from focused differentiation toward ultra-focused differentiation (Maybach, Mythos, AMG only).

### 6.2 Where the Strategy Became Incoherent

The intensification of the differentiation strategy created three problems:

**First, it narrowed the revenue base without strengthening differentiation.** One significant weakness is Mercedes-Benz's high pricing strategy: vehicles are positioned in the premium segment, which may limit their accessibility to a broader customer base. Reducing volume in the C-Class and compact segment removed the "entry point" through which aspirational buyers entered the Mercedes ecosystem. Without entry-level models, there is no pipeline to build future S-Class and Maybach buyers.

**Second, the EV products were not sufficiently differentiated.** A differentiation strategy requires that the basis of differentiation be *perceivable and valued* by target buyers. The EQS failed this test: buyers did not perceive it as sufficiently differentiated from a Tesla Model S to justify the price premium, and the design was widely criticised. Even Mercedes executives admitted that the ultra-streamlined shapes of the cars didn't have the gravitas that their buyers wanted. This is a fundamental differentiation failure.

**Third, the China collapse exposed geographic concentration risk.** The 3% decline to 1.98 million vehicles was led by a slump in deliveries in China, its largest market, where consumers are shunning models like the S-Class and Maybach in favour of local brands. A focused differentiation strategy in a single geography is inherently fragile.

### 6.3 The Current Strategic Pivot

CEO Ola Källenius now plans to manufacture premium vehicles across all price segments instead of concentrating solely on high-margin, top-tier models. In Porter's terms, this is a move back toward *broad differentiation* — maintaining the premium quality proposition but across a wider range of price points. This is strategically sound *if* Mercedes can maintain its differentiation basis (quality, design, engineering) at lower price points without diluting the brand.

## 7. Synthesis: The Coherent Chain of Analysis

The four frameworks, taken together, produce a coherent causal chain:

1. **Five Forces** shows that industry structure deteriorated: rivalry intensified (Tesla, BYD), buyer power grew (price transparency, alternatives), and new entrants disrupted the premium EV segment. This made the top-end strategy *structurally risky* from the outset.

2. **RBV** shows that Mercedes possessed the brand and engineering resources to pursue a premium EV strategy, but lacked the *product development capability* to translate those resources into EV products that felt authentically premium. The resource existed; the deployment capability did not.

3. **Ansoff** shows that Mercedes attempted simultaneous product development (EQ) and diversification (software, Mythos) while abandoning market penetration (volume), creating a sequencing error. New strategies require existing cash flows to fund them; Mercedes reduced its cash-generating base before the new strategies were proven.

4. **Generic Strategies** shows that the intensification of focused differentiation was incoherent because (a) the EV products were not sufficiently differentiated and (b) the narrowing of the product range removed the aspirational pipeline that feeds premium brand loyalty.

The result: a strategy that was logically consistent in theory but failed in execution because the product development capability was insufficient, the timing was wrong, and the geographic concentration in China created a single point of failure.

## 8. Recommendations and Action Plan

The following recommendations are derived directly from the analytical chain above. Each is grounded in evidence and linked to the specific framework diagnosis.

---

### Recommendation 1: Anchor the EV Product Line in Brand Heritage, Not Technology Novelty

**Diagnosis (from RBV + Generic Strategies):** The EQ failure was a brand-authenticity failure, not a technology failure. The most important VRIN resource — heritage and design identity — was not embedded in the EQ products.

**Action:** The new CLA platform (MMA/MB.EA) must be the template for all future EVs. The all-new electric CLA sets new standards with intuitive technologies, striking design, enhanced comfort and up to 374 miles of electric range — and crucially, it looks like a Mercedes. Every future EV launch must pass a "brand authenticity test": does this product feel like the natural evolution of the ICE model it replaces? The EQS failed this test; the new E-Class EV must not.

**Why this resolves the problem:** The RBV analysis shows that Mercedes's brand resource is its primary VRIN asset. Deploying it effectively in EVs converts a resource into a competitive advantage. The Generic Strategies analysis shows that differentiation must be *perceived* by buyers — which requires design and interior quality congruent with the Mercedes heritage.

---

### Recommendation 2: Restore the Volume Base Through Broad Differentiation

**Diagnosis (from Ansoff + Generic Strategies):** The abandonment of the compact and mid-range segment removed the aspirational pipeline and the cash-generating volume base. Mercedes wants to return to 2019 sales levels — but this requires re-entering segments it vacated.

**Action:** Relaunch competitive C-Class and A-Class equivalents in both ICE and EV variants. Adopt a *broad differentiation* strategy (Porter) rather than ultra-focused luxury. The new CLA, starting from $47,250, is the right price point to re-engage aspirational buyers. Expand this logic across the compact segment.

**Why this resolves the problem:** The Ansoff analysis shows that market penetration (selling to existing markets with credible products) is the lowest-risk growth strategy. Restoring volume stabilises the revenue base that funds the higher-risk diversification into software and ultra-luxury. Without this base, the Mythos and AMG strategies are financially unsustainable.

---

### Recommendation 3: Accelerate the MB.OS Software Platform as a Differentiation and Revenue Layer

**Diagnosis (from RBV + Ansoff Diversification):** Software is the emerging battleground. Tesla's competitive advantage is increasingly software-defined. Mercedes has a proprietary OS under development that could become a VRIN resource.

**Action:** Mercedes-Benz's strategy of developing its own automotive OS should be accelerated, targeting €1 billion from software-enabled, features-on-demand. MB.OS should be positioned not as a technology feature but as a *luxury experience layer* — personalisation, concierge AI, ambient intelligence — consistent with the brand promise. Partnerships with premium content providers (as already initiated with Tencent, Zoom, and gaming platforms) should be extended.

**Why this resolves the problem:** The Five Forces analysis shows that buyer power is elevated because EVs are easily compared on hardware specifications. Software differentiation is harder to compare and creates switching costs — directly addressing buyer power. The Ansoff analysis identifies this as the highest-potential diversification move, creating recurring revenue that is structurally superior to one-time vehicle sales.

---

### Recommendation 4: Reduce China Dependency Through Accelerated Market Development

**Diagnosis (from Five Forces + Ansoff Market Development):** China represents both the largest market and the largest single point of failure. German manufacturers all invested heavily in China in recent decades but are now facing fierce competition from local rivals.

**Action:** Pursue aggressive market development in India, Southeast Asia, and the Middle East — markets where the Mercedes brand carries strong aspirational value and local EV competition is less mature. India delivered double-digit growth in 2024–2025, prompting expanded assembly in Chakan, Pune. This should be accelerated. Simultaneously, in China, Mercedes should pivot from the flagging S-Class/Maybach positioning toward locally-resonant models with Chinese design input.

**Why this resolves the problem:** The Five Forces analysis shows that geographic concentration amplifies the impact of local competitive forces. Diversifying market exposure reduces the structural risk of any single market's deterioration. The Ansoff matrix confirms market development as a moderate-risk, high-potential growth vector.

---

### Recommendation 5: Implement a Disciplined Cost Reduction Programme Without Compromising Differentiation Integrity

**Diagnosis (from RBV + Generic Strategies):** Mercedes has announced cost-cutting targets, but these must be managed carefully to avoid undermining the differentiation basis.

**Action:** The company plans to cut production costs by 10% until 2027 and localise manufacturing in key markets to mitigate supply chain risks. Workforce adjustments — including voluntary buyouts, reducing hiring, and freezing executive salaries — are expected to deliver €2.5 billion in savings by 2025. However, cost reductions must be *structurally targeted*: cut complexity in platform architecture (fewer platforms, more shared components) rather than cutting quality in materials or craftsmanship. The latter would be fatal to the differentiation strategy.

**Why this resolves the problem:** The Generic Strategies analysis shows that a differentiation strategy requires cost discipline to be *sustainable*, but not cost leadership. The goal is to reduce costs to the minimum necessary to maintain the differentiation basis — not to compete on price. The "Next Level Performance" plan is directionally correct but must be implemented with this constraint explicitly stated.

---

### Recommendation 6: Maintain the Direct Sales (Agency) Model and Use It to Rebuild Pricing Power

**Diagnosis (from Five Forces — Buyer Power):** Buyer power is elevated partly because traditional dealer networks created price variability and discounting. The agency model addresses this directly.

**Action:** Under the agency model, the OEM becomes the retailer and the contractual partner, with dealers evolving from stationary sellers to agents who remain the physical touchpoint with the consumer. Mercedes is targeting 80 percent of European sales through the agency model by 2025 and wants to expand the scheme to an additional fifteen markets. This model should be maintained and expanded, as it gives Mercedes direct control over pricing — a critical lever for a differentiation strategy.

**Why this resolves the problem:** The Five Forces analysis identifies buyer power as a key structural threat. The agency model directly reduces buyer power by eliminating dealer discounting, maintaining price integrity, and giving Mercedes first-party customer data for personalisation — which feeds the MB.OS software strategy. The two recommendations are therefore mutually reinforcing.

## 9. Conclusion

The Mercedes-Benz case illustrates a fundamental strategic principle: even a well-resourced firm with a clear generic strategy can fail when the execution of that strategy misaligns with its own core competencies. The top-end EV strategy was coherent in theory — focused differentiation, premium pricing, brand elevation — but incoherent in practice because the EV products did not carry the brand's VRIN assets into the new technology domain, the volume base was abandoned before the premium strategy was proven, and geographic concentration in China created a single point of failure that the Five Forces analysis would have flagged as structurally dangerous.

The path forward is not a wholesale abandonment of the premium strategy — that would destroy the brand equity that remains Mercedes-Benz's most durable competitive advantage. Rather, it is a rebalancing: broad differentiation across price segments, anchored by authentic brand expression in every product, supported by a proprietary software platform that creates switching costs and recurring revenue, and diversified across geographies to reduce structural fragility.

The central lesson is one of strategic coherence: each element of the strategy must reinforce the others, and the sequencing of moves must respect the cash-flow logic that funds each step. Mercedes failed to sequence correctly. The recommendations above propose a sequence that begins with restoring the volume base (Recommendation 2), uses that cash flow to fund software investment (Recommendation 3), and builds toward the ultra-luxury and market development moves (Recommendations 1 and 4) once the foundations are secure.
