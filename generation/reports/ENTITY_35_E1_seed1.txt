# Shell plc: Navigating the Strategic Fault Line Between Value Creation and Energy Transition
## A Strategic Management Case Study

---

## Executive Summary

Shell plc faces one of the most consequential strategic dilemmas in contemporary business: how to sustain financial competitiveness and shareholder returns in the near term while credibly positioning for a long-term energy transition that may fundamentally erode its core business model. Under CEO Wael Sawan, Shell has pivoted sharply toward a "performance, discipline and simplification" doctrine — scaling back offshore wind investments, weakening near-term emissions targets, and doubling down on liquefied natural gas (LNG). This case study analyses that strategic situation through **PESTEL**, **Porter's Five Forces**, the **Resource-Based View (VRIO)**, and the **Ansoff Growth Matrix**, culminating in a set of actionable recommendations. The central argument is that Shell's current strategy is coherent in the short run but carries compounding long-term strategic risk unless anchored in a more explicit and credible transition pathway.

---

## 1. Background and Strategic Context

Shell plc is one of the world's largest integrated energy companies, operating across upstream oil and gas, integrated gas (including LNG), chemicals, refining, retail, and trading. In 2021, Shell launched its *Powering Progress* strategy, committing to net-zero emissions by 2050.

Shell published its first energy transition update since the launch of its *Powering Progress* strategy in 2021, and at the Capital Markets Day in June 2023, outlined how the strategy delivers "more value with fewer emissions." Yet the strategic trajectory shifted visibly from 2023 onwards.

The changes are part of a company-wide review launched in 2023 aimed at reducing costs as CEO Wael Sawan focuses on activities with the highest returns. In many cases, that has meant reducing spending on low-carbon and renewable businesses and increasing the focus on oil, gas and biofuels.

Shell is positioning itself to become "the investment case and partner of choice through the energy transition," committed to enhancing value for investors through disciplined investments, enhanced shareholder distributions, and maintaining a strong balance sheet. At its Capital Markets Day 2025, CEO Wael Sawan stated: "We want to become the world's leading integrated gas and LNG business and the most customer-focused energy marketer and trader, while sustaining a material level of liquids production."

The strategic situation is therefore a tension between two legitimate imperatives: the financial logic of focusing on high-return, proven capabilities versus the longer-term necessity of transitioning away from fossil fuels as climate regulation tightens and energy markets evolve.

## 2. External Environment Analysis: PESTEL

### 2.1 Political

The geopolitical landscape is deeply ambivalent for Shell. Energy security concerns following Russia's invasion of Ukraine elevated LNG as a strategic priority for European governments, creating near-term political tailwinds. Simultaneously, the EU's Green Deal and the US Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 structurally incentivise renewable energy investment. Government policies are increasingly steering energy markets towards decarbonisation, with initiatives like the EU's Green Deal and the US Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 encouraging renewable energy investments.

### 2.2 Economic

Shell's full-year 2024 earnings totalled $23.7 billion, a reported 16% decline from $28.3 billion in the prior year. Despite this, Shell generated free cash flow of $40 billion across the year, higher than 2023, in a lower price environment. The macro environment is characterised by persistently high capital costs, which disproportionately penalise long-duration renewable energy projects relative to short-cycle oil and gas developments — a key driver of Shell's portfolio rebalancing.

Global gas demand trends provide further economic rationale for Shell's LNG focus. The IEA's base case sees natural gas demand rising by nearly 1.5% annually between 2024 and 2030, translating to an increase of 380 bcm in absolute terms, with the Asia Pacific region accounting for half of that growth. Shell's own LNG Outlook echoes this: Shell's LNG Outlook 2025 forecasts that global demand for LNG is set to rise by around 60% by 2040, largely driven by economic growth in Asia.

### 2.3 Social

Public expectations around corporate climate responsibility have intensified. Shell's weakening of its 2030 emissions targets drew criticism from environmental groups and climate-focused investors. The oil major "updated" its target to cut the total "net carbon intensity" of all the energy products it sells to customers by between 15–20% between 2016 and 2030, against a previous target of 20%, while the 2035 target was "retired." Campaign groups characterised this as "essentially kicking the difficult decisions into the long grass."

At the same time, energy affordability and security have become dominant social concerns, providing some public legitimacy for Shell's argument that fossil fuels remain necessary during the transition.

### 2.4 Technological

LNG technology, floating LNG (FLNG), and carbon capture and storage (CCS) are areas where Shell holds genuine technological advantages. Shell's AI-enhanced trading platform uses real-time data from physical assets to inform algorithmic trading decisions, creating a feedback loop between physical and financial energy markets. However, the rapid cost decline in solar and wind power means that Shell's exit from direct renewable generation may progressively cede ground to pure-play renewable companies.

### 2.5 Environmental

The European Union's Emissions Trading System (EU ETS) set a carbon price at €80.66 per ton of CO₂ in January 2024, and the United Kingdom's Carbon Reduction Commitment requires Shell to purchase carbon allowances at £75.40 per ton. These regulatory carbon costs are material and rising. Shell's Scope 3 emissions — those arising from customers burning the fuels Shell sells — represent the vast majority of its climate footprint and remain the hardest to reduce without shrinking the fossil fuel business.

### 2.6 Legal

The legal environment is a defining feature of Shell's strategic context. On May 26, 2021, The Hague District Court issued a ruling ordering Shell to reduce the Shell Group's Scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions by 45% in 2030, relative to 2019 — the first time a national court had ordered a private company to reduce its emissions to meet the targets of the Paris Climate Agreement. On November 12, 2024, the Dutch Court of Appeals overturned that decision, stating there was "insufficient consensus" on a specific reduction percentage. While Shell "won" the appeal, the direction of climate litigation is clearly toward greater corporate accountability, and future rulings may be more precisely calibrated.

**PESTEL Summary:** The macro environment presents Shell with a bifurcated paradox. Near-term economic and political conditions favour continued fossil fuel investment, while medium-to-long-term environmental, legal, and social forces create escalating risk for a strategy overly dependent on hydrocarbons.

## 3. Industry Analysis: Porter's Five Forces

### 3.1 Rivalry Among Existing Competitors — **HIGH**

The global integrated energy sector is dominated by a small number of supermajors — ExxonMobil, BP, TotalEnergies, Chevron, and Shell — all pursuing broadly similar strategies. Shell faces intense competition from global energy giants like ExxonMobil and BP, who are also investing heavily in both traditional and low-carbon energy, evident in their substantial capital expenditures. National oil companies (NOCs) such as QatarEnergy and ADNOC possess structural cost advantages and are expanding aggressively into LNG, directly competing with Shell's stated growth ambition.

### 3.2 Threat of New Entrants — **LOW**

Barriers to entry in integrated oil and gas are extremely high. Capital expenditures for specialised oil and gas equipment include offshore drilling rigs at $650 million to $1.2 billion per unit, advanced seismic imaging equipment at $15–25 million, and subsea production systems at $200–500 million per project. These requirements, combined with technical expertise and regulatory licensing, make new entry into Shell's core markets highly unlikely.

### 3.3 Threat of Substitutes — **MODERATE AND RISING**

In the long run, renewable electricity, green hydrogen, and electrification of transport represent structural substitutes for fossil fuels. Shell's renewable energy portfolio represents 12.6% of total energy sales in 2024, with projected growth to 18.3% by 2026 — but the pace of substitution in the broader market is accelerating. LNG faces specific substitution risk from hydrogen and direct electrification of industrial processes, though these transitions are measured in decades rather than years.

### 3.4 Bargaining Power of Suppliers — **MODERATE**

Shell's reliance on highly specialised equipment means that a limited number of oilfield services companies hold meaningful bargaining power. However, Shell's scale and long-term contracting relationships partially offset this. In low-carbon supply chains (e.g., electrolyser manufacturers for hydrogen), supplier power is currently elevated due to limited production capacity.

### 3.5 Bargaining Power of Buyers — **MODERATE–HIGH**

Shell serves approximately 30 million business customers globally in 2024, with the top 10 industrial customers accounting for 42% of industrial volumes. Large industrial buyers and LNG off-takers have significant negotiating leverage, particularly as competing supply sources increase. Increasingly, large corporate buyers are demanding low-carbon products and supply chain transparency, giving them additional leverage.

**Five Forces Summary:** The industry structure is moderately attractive for incumbents in the near term but is being progressively disrupted. Shell's competitive position is strongest where barriers to entry are highest — integrated LNG, trading, deepwater upstream — and most vulnerable where substitution is fastest: retail fuels and chemicals.

## 4. Internal Analysis: Resource-Based View (VRIO)

The Resource-Based View (Barney, 1991) holds that sustainable competitive advantage derives from resources and capabilities that are **V**aluable, **R**are, **I**nimitable, and supported by **O**rganisation. The following VRIO table maps Shell's key strategic assets:

| Resource / Capability | Valuable | Rare | Inimitable | Organised | Competitive Implication |
|---|:---:|:---:|:---:|:---:|---|
| Integrated LNG value chain | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | **Sustained competitive advantage** |
| Global trading & optimisation platform | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | **Sustained competitive advantage** |
| Deep-water upstream technical expertise | ✓ | ✓ | Partial | ✓ | **Temporary competitive advantage** |
| Brand and retail network (>46,000 stations) | ✓ | Partial | Partial | ✓ | **Competitive parity** |
| Financial scale (FCF ~$40bn/yr) | ✓ | Partial | Partial | ✓ | **Competitive parity** |
| Low-carbon technology portfolio | ✓ | Partial | Low | Partial | **Capability gap / under-investment risk** |

**The LNG Integrated Value Chain** is Shell's most distinctive resource. Integration is not only a competitive advantage, it also makes Shell more resilient because it can capture value as it shifts to different parts of the LNG value chain. Through its joint ventures, Shell has the largest LNG liquefaction capacity among IOCs with plants in all major supply basins. This capability took decades and billions of dollars to construct and cannot be rapidly replicated. Shell's LNG Canada project — described as the largest private-sector investment in Canada's history — loaded its first LNG cargo in 2025, adding significant new supply to Shell's portfolio.

**Trading and Optimisation** is Shell's second critical capability. Shell's strategy requires "moving away from direct renewable generation toward intermediary roles in power trading and storage," leveraging an AI-enhanced trading platform that creates a feedback loop between physical assets and financial markets — a genuinely rare and difficult-to-imitate capability.

**The key strategic risk** revealed by this analysis is the relative weakness of Shell's low-carbon technology capabilities. Shell is investing $10–15 billion between 2023 and the end of 2025 in low-carbon energy solutions, including electric-vehicle charging, biofuels, renewable power, hydrogen and carbon capture and storage. However, this investment is spread across multiple technologies without achieving the scale or inimitability that Shell possesses in LNG and trading — a pattern the VRIO framework identifies as producing at best competitive parity, not sustained advantage.

## 5. Growth Strategy Analysis: Ansoff Matrix

The Ansoff Matrix (Ansoff, 1957) maps growth strategies along two dimensions: markets (existing vs. new) and products (existing vs. new). Shell's current strategy can be systematically mapped across all four quadrants:

### 5.1 Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets) — **Primary Current Strategy**

Shell's Capital Markets Day 2025 strategy aims to reinforce its leadership position in LNG by growing sales by 4–5% per year through to 2030, while growing top-line production across its combined Upstream and Integrated Gas business by 1% per year to 2030, sustaining 1.4 million barrels per day of liquids production. This is market penetration: extracting more value from existing products in existing markets through efficiency and scale. Shell has achieved structural cost reductions of $5.1 billion since 2022, driven by a focus on cost discipline, the simplification of processes, and by streamlining its portfolio.

### 5.2 Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets) — **Selective Pursuit**

Shell is pursuing LNG market development in Asia-Pacific, where demand is growing fastest. LNG Canada's Pacific-facing export terminal is specifically designed to serve Asian markets, representing a deliberate geographic expansion of an existing product into new geographic markets.

### 5.3 Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets) — **Underinvested**

Shell's investments in biofuels, hydrogen, and EV charging represent product development — offering new energy products to its existing customer base. Shell had installed approximately 54,000 public charge points for electric vehicles at Shell forecourts, on streets and at locations such as supermarkets by 2023, up from 27,000 in 2022. However, the retreat from offshore wind and the weakening of emissions targets suggest this quadrant is being under-resourced relative to the scale of transition required.

### 5.4 Diversification (New Products, New Markets) — **Largely Abandoned**

Shell made its biggest acquisition in the energy transition with the purchase of Denmark's Nature Energy for around $2 billion, making it Europe's largest producer of renewable natural gas, made from agricultural, industrial and household waste. However, the 2023–2024 strategic review effectively pulled Shell back from the diversification quadrant, concentrating resources on market penetration and selective market development.

**Ansoff Insight:** Shell's strategy is concentrating in the low-risk, high-certainty quadrants of the matrix. This is financially rational in the short term but creates a strategic dependency on a product category facing structural long-term demand uncertainty. A coherent long-term strategy requires a more deliberate and resourced move into product development — building inimitable low-carbon capabilities before the transition forces a reactive, value-destructive pivot.

## 6. The Core Strategic Dilemma: A Coherent Chain of Reasoning

The four frameworks converge on a single, coherent diagnosis:

**Step 1 (PESTEL → Diagnosis):** The external environment is bifurcated. Near-term conditions favour Shell's current strategy; long-term environmental, legal, and social forces create compounding risk for a fossil-fuel-dependent model. Shell itself acknowledges that "a growing share of its greenhouse gas emissions will be subject to regulation, resulting in increased compliance costs and operational restrictions," and that "climate activists and regulators may seek to limit certain oil and gas projects."

**Step 2 (Five Forces → Competitive Position):** Shell's strongest competitive positions are in markets with high barriers to entry. These positions are defensible for the medium term but not indefinitely, as NOC competition intensifies and substitution accelerates.

**Step 3 (VRIO → Capability Gap):** Shell's truly inimitable resources — integrated LNG and trading — justify the current strategic focus. However, the failure to build inimitable low-carbon capabilities creates a future resource gap. Dynamic capabilities theory (Teece, Pisano & Shuen, 1997) predicts that firms which fail to sense and seize emerging technological opportunities while their incumbent resources still generate cash flows will face a "capability trap" — the very success of the current model delays the investment needed to compete in the future model.

**Step 4 (Ansoff → Strategic Concentration Risk):** The concentration of strategy in market penetration is coherent for near-term value creation but insufficient for long-term resilience. Shell has streamlined from 70+ targets to 8 key metrics (4 financial targets, 4 lower emissions targets) — a simplification that improves focus but risks narrowing strategic vision at precisely the moment when the energy landscape is most uncertain.

**The Fault Line:** Shell aims "to take the lead in the energy transition where it can combine competitive strengths with strong customer demand and support from governments." This framing presents value creation and decarbonisation as complementary. The evidence from the analysis above suggests they are partially complementary in the near term but become increasingly contradictory beyond 2030 as the pace of energy transition accelerates — particularly if Shell's social licence to operate erodes and future regulatory intervention is more precisely targeted than the 2021 Dutch ruling.

## 7. Recommendations and Action Plan

The following recommendations are designed to resolve the strategic dilemma by preserving Shell's near-term financial strength while building the capabilities required for long-term resilience. They are organised into three time horizons.

---

### 7.1 Immediate (2025–2027): Consolidate and Credibilise

**Recommendation 1: Reinstate a credible, absolute Scope 3 emissions pathway.**

Shell's abandonment of the 2035 target and weakening of the 2030 target has damaged its credibility with institutional investors and regulators. Shell offered its first Energy Transition Strategy for submission to a shareholder advisory vote at the 2021 Annual General Meeting, with 89% of shareholders voting in support — but following engagements with institutional investors, expectations around issues such as the inclusion of an absolute Scope 3 target influenced the voting decisions of many investors in subsequent years. Rather than intensity-based targets (which can be gamed by selling less clean energy), Shell should adopt an absolute Scope 3 reduction trajectory — even a modest one — to demonstrate strategic seriousness.

*Reasoning:* PESTEL analysis identifies legal and regulatory risk as a rising threat. The VRIO analysis shows that Shell's social licence to operate is a valuable resource currently being eroded. Intensity targets, as critics note, are seen as a less ambitious measure as net emissions can simply be lowered by selling more clean energy alternatives — meaning Shell's targets are insufficient relative to what the world needs to reduce fossil fuel emissions well below 2018 levels.

---

**Recommendation 2: Ringfence a "Future Capabilities Fund" of $3–4 billion per year for focused low-carbon capability-building.**

Shell's current low-carbon investment is spread across multiple technologies without achieving inimitability in any of them. A focused, ringfenced fund — protected from short-term earnings pressure — should target two or three areas where Shell can build genuine competitive advantage: **CCS** (where its scale and existing infrastructure provide a head start), **advanced biofuels** (following the Nature Energy acquisition logic), and **LNG decarbonisation** (using renewable electricity and CCS to reduce the carbon intensity of its LNG plants). Shell aims, in the future, to power its LNG plants with renewable electricity and add carbon capture and storage to lower the carbon intensity of its LNG plants — LNG Canada, for example, will use natural gas and renewable electricity.

*Reasoning:* Dynamic capabilities theory predicts that firms must invest in future capabilities while current cash flows are strong. Of Shell's cost reductions delivered to date, $2.1 billion relates to portfolio changes and $3.0 billion relates to operational efficiencies — demonstrating that the discipline to generate savings exists; the challenge is to redirect a portion of those savings into future capability investment rather than exclusively into buybacks.

---

### 7.2 Medium Term (2027–2030): Selective Expansion in Transition Markets

**Recommendation 3: Re-enter offshore wind through a partnership or asset acquisition model rather than greenfield development.**

Shell stepped back from new offshore wind investments and split its power division following an extensive review, with CEO Wael Sawan stating: "While we will not lead new offshore wind developments..." The exit from greenfield development was financially rational given poor returns. However, the strategic logic of maintaining a presence in the power sector — as a trader, offtaker, and systems integrator — remains compelling. Shell should acquire minority stakes in operational offshore wind assets or enter long-term power purchase agreements (PPAs) that leverage its trading capability without requiring full project development risk. An analyst noted that acquiring stakes in existing projects provides "access to offshore wind projects at a much more reasonable price than actually building things from scratch."

*Reasoning:* Ansoff's product development quadrant requires investment. Shell's trading and optimisation VRIO capability can be extended into power markets if Shell retains a physical foothold in them. Exiting entirely forecloses this option.

---

**Recommendation 4: Accelerate the LNG decarbonisation pathway as a product differentiation strategy.**

As Asian buyers — particularly Japanese, Korean, and increasingly Chinese utilities — face their own net-zero commitments, demand for "low-carbon LNG" will grow. Shell should invest now in the infrastructure and certification frameworks needed to offer low-carbon LNG at a premium, converting its LNG scale advantage into a product differentiation advantage. Shell's LNG business recognises the critical role of LNG as a transition fuel and is focusing on 20–30% growth of its LNG business by 2030 — but growth alone is insufficient; differentiation on carbon intensity is needed to sustain premium pricing and retain buyers facing their own regulatory pressure.

*Reasoning:* This is a market development move (existing product, new market segment) that leverages Shell's core VRIO strength while responding to the environmental and legal dimensions of the PESTEL analysis. It converts a compliance cost (carbon pricing) into a potential revenue premium.

---

**Recommendation 5: Rationalise the downstream portfolio decisively and redeploy capital.**

Shell's refining and chemicals businesses are structurally challenged by both the energy transition and global overcapacity. Shell has completed the strategic review of its Energy and Chemicals Park Singapore with divestment as the preferred option, and is high-grading its European energy and chemicals parks — retiring certain units and continuing with some divestments already announced. This direction is correct and should be accelerated, with proceeds explicitly ring-fenced for the Future Capabilities Fund (Recommendation 2) and LNG decarbonisation (Recommendation 4).

*Reasoning:* Porter's Five Forces identifies chemicals and refining as areas of low competitive advantage and high substitution risk. The VRIO analysis shows these assets are valuable but not rare or inimitable. Capital freed from these businesses has a higher strategic return if deployed in Shell's two genuinely inimitable capability areas.

---

### 7.3 Long Term (2030–2040): Repositioning for the Post-Transition World

**Recommendation 6: Develop an explicit "post-LNG" scenario strategy for the 2035–2040 horizon.**

Shell's current strategy is explicit to 2030. Beyond that, the strategic landscape is highly uncertain. Shell should deploy its own long-established scenario planning methodology — it pioneered corporate scenario planning in the 1970s — to map the range of possible energy system states in 2035–2040 and identify the capabilities needed in each scenario. Specifically, Shell should identify which of its current assets and capabilities are "option-creating" (e.g., CCS infrastructure, hydrogen distribution networks, power trading platforms) and protect them from short-term financial pressure.

*Reasoning:* Dynamic capabilities theory emphasises the importance of "sensing" future opportunities before they become obvious. Shell's strategy is to "invest where we have competitive strengths" — but competitive strengths in 2035 will be different from those of 2025. Scenario planning is the mechanism by which Shell can make that determination before it becomes urgent.

---

**Recommendation 7: Engage proactively with climate policy architecture.**

Rather than treating climate regulation as an external threat to be managed reactively, Shell should position itself as a constructive architect of the regulatory frameworks governing carbon pricing, CCS incentives, and LNG transition pathways. Shell's CEO Wael Sawan stated following the Dutch appeals court ruling: "Our target to become a net-zero emissions energy business by 2050 remains at the heart of Shell's strategy and is transforming our business." This commitment should be operationalised through active engagement in the design of the policy instruments — carbon markets, CCS subsidy frameworks, hydrogen standards — that will determine the economics of Shell's future business.

*Reasoning:* The political dimension of the PESTEL analysis shows that regulatory frameworks are actively being shaped. Shell's scale, technical expertise, and global presence give it unique legitimacy in these conversations. Companies that help design the rules of the game are better positioned to compete under them.

## 8. Conclusion

Shell's strategic pivot under Wael Sawan — concentrating on LNG, trading, and financial discipline while scaling back offshore wind and weakening near-term emissions targets — is internally coherent and financially rational in the short run. The PESTEL analysis confirms that near-term macro conditions support this focus; the Five Forces analysis shows that Shell's strongest competitive positions are precisely where it is doubling down; and the VRIO analysis confirms that integrated LNG and trading are genuinely inimitable resources worth protecting. Shell's continued focus on simplification helped deliver over $3 billion in structural cost reductions since 2022, meeting its target ahead of schedule, whilst also making significant progress against all other financial targets.

However, the same analytical frameworks reveal a compounding long-term risk. The PESTEL environment is moving inexorably toward higher carbon costs and tighter regulation. The Five Forces show rising substitution threats. The VRIO analysis exposes a growing capability gap in low-carbon technologies. And the Ansoff Matrix reveals a strategy over-concentrated in market penetration, with insufficient investment in the product development and diversification needed for long-term resilience.

The resolution of this dilemma does not require Shell to abandon its financial discipline or sacrifice near-term returns. It requires Shell to use the cash flows generated by its current strengths to build the capabilities needed for its next competitive position — precisely as dynamic capabilities theory (Teece et al., 1997) prescribes. The seven recommendations above constitute not a retreat from financial discipline but its strategic completion: ensuring that Shell's performance today does not foreclose its competitiveness tomorrow. The strategic fault line is real, but it is bridgeable — if Shell chooses to bridge it now, while it still has the financial strength to do so on its own terms.
