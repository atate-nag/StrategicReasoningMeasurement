## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Bayer AG, one of Germany's oldest and most prestigious corporations, finds itself in one of the most consequential strategic crises in European corporate history. The 2018 acquisition of Monsanto — intended to create a global life-sciences powerhouse — instead triggered a cascade of legal, financial, and reputational consequences that have erased more than half of the company's market value. These challenges include U.S. litigation alleging harm from weed-killer glyphosate, a development setback for its most promising experimental medicine, weak agriculture markets, and investor pressure to spin off or sell businesses. This case study analyses Bayer's strategic situation using six established frameworks and proposes a structured, phased action plan to restore competitive viability.

## 2. COMPANY BACKGROUND

Founded in 1863 in Wuppertal, Germany, Bayer AG began as a chemical and dye manufacturer before evolving into a global life-sciences conglomerate. Bayer transformed from a chemical dye maker into a life-sciences leader with strengths in pharmaceuticals, consumer health, and crop science. Today, it operates across three core divisions:

| Division | 2024 Sales (approx.) | Key Products |
|---|---|---|
| **Crop Science** | ~€23 billion | Roundup, glyphosate, GM seeds, Preceon corn |
| **Pharmaceuticals** | ~€18 billion | Xarelto, Nubeqa, Eylea, Kerendia |
| **Consumer Health** | ~€5.5 billion | Aspirin, Claritin, Aleve, Bepanthen |

### The Monsanto Acquisition (2018)
Bayer completed the acquisition of Monsanto on June 7, 2018, for $63 billion including debt. The strategic rationale was compelling on paper: Bayer expressed a strong belief in the strategic rationale of the deal and expected cost synergies of up to $1.9 billion, which would be key to increasing free cash flow. Combining Bayer's crop protection chemicals with Monsanto's world-leading seed genetics and digital farming platform would create an integrated "field to fork" agricultural giant. What Bayer's leadership did not fully account for was the legal time bomb embedded in Monsanto's most iconic product: Roundup, the glyphosate-based weed killer.

## 3. THE STRATEGIC SITUATION: A MULTI-FRONT CRISIS

The CEO faces a deluge of problems, most of which stem from the 2018 takeover of Monsanto. CEO Bill Anderson, appointed in June 2023, publicly identified four "broken areas" within the business: litigation, debt levels, bureaucracy, and the research and development pipeline.

### 3.1 The Litigation Crisis
The most damaging consequence of the Monsanto acquisition has been the glyphosate litigation. As of October 2024, claimants in more than 100,000 Roundup lawsuits had reached settlements equalling approximately $11 billion, with more trials and settlements expected. Plaintiffs allege that glyphosate caused non-Hodgkin lymphoma. Bayer forecasts approximately €5 billion of lawsuit-related cash outflows in 2026 alone.

### 3.2 The Financial Crisis
Net financial debt amounted to €32.626 billion as of December 31, 2024, down 5.4 percent from year-end 2023. Net income came in at minus €2.552 billion (2023: minus €2.941 billion), while core earnings per share declined by 21.0 percent to €5.05. The company's market capitalisation collapsed from approximately €80 billion pre-acquisition to under €30 billion — a destruction of over €50 billion in shareholder value.

### 3.3 The Pipeline Crisis
In 2023, Bayer suffered a major clinical setback when asundexian, its most promising experimental blood-thinner, failed in a Phase III trial. This was a severe blow, particularly as the pharmaceutical pipeline was already under pressure from patent expiries on Xarelto in key markets.

### 3.4 The Operational Crisis
Anderson said that the 12 layers of management between himself and customers were "simply too much." Decision-making was slow and siloed, and the Crop Science division faced weaker market development, especially in Latin America, coupled with pricing pressures on crop protection products.

### 3.5 The Reputational Crisis
The situation brought to light a collapse in investor confidence, with investors now seeing the Monsanto acquisition as one of the worst strategic blunders in Bayer's history. The Monsanto brand became so toxic that Bayer discontinued it entirely after the acquisition, yet the reputational damage transferred to Bayer itself.

## 4. STRATEGIC ANALYSIS

### 4.1 PESTEL Analysis

**Political**
Regulatory scrutiny of glyphosate is intensifying in the EU, with the Farm to Fork strategy targeting a 50% reduction in pesticide use by 2030, directly threatening Crop Science revenues. In connection with the regulatory approval process for Monsanto, Bayer agreed to the divestiture of businesses which generated €2.2 billion in sales in 2017 for an aggregate base purchase price of €7.6 billion — weakening the combined entity from day one.

**Economic**
Net financial debt as of March 31, 2024, came in at €37.488 billion, up 8.7 percent from year-end 2023, mainly attributable to cash outflows from operating activities due to seasonal factors. Currency headwinds (particularly USD/EUR movements) caused currency headwinds of €525 million in Q1 2024 alone, illustrating the exposure of a euro-reporting company with large dollar-denominated revenues.

**Social**
Growing consumer scepticism toward GMOs and synthetic pesticides creates headwinds for Crop Science's core products. However, ageing populations in developed markets drive demand for pharmaceutical treatments in oncology, cardiovascular disease, and eye care — areas where Bayer's pipeline has genuine strength.

**Technological**
In 2024, Bayer's crop science sales reached around €23 billion, and they are investing in innovation and sustainable practices like regenerative agriculture. In pharmaceuticals, gene therapy and RNA-based medicines represent the next frontier, and Bayer plans to initiate a Phase II study in gene therapy for Parkinson's disease in 2024, with an additional candidate expected to enter Phase II later that year.

**Environmental**
Glyphosate's environmental profile is under sustained scientific and regulatory scrutiny. ESG investor pressure is mounting, with Bayer's litigation overhang and association with controversial agricultural chemicals leading to exclusion from several ESG-focused investment portfolios.

**Legal**
The Roundup lawsuit represents people who allege that the glyphosate in Bayer's Roundup led them to develop non-Hodgkin lymphoma. A potential U.S. Supreme Court ruling on federal preemption could be transformative: a favourable ruling would dramatically limit future liability, while an unfavourable one could extend the litigation horizon indefinitely. On June 30, 2025, the U.S. Supreme Court asked the Department of Justice to weigh in on whether it should accept Bayer AG's appeal concerning its Roundup herbicide.

### 4.2 Porter's Five Forces Analysis

**Force 1: Threat of New Entrants — LOW (Pharmaceuticals) / MEDIUM (Crop Science)**

In pharmaceuticals, barriers to entry are exceptionally high — drug development requires 10–15 years and $1–2 billion per approved molecule. Bayer possesses deeply entrenched distribution channels in both its healthcare and agriculture segments. Its extensive network of pharmacies and healthcare providers ensures broad product availability for its pharmaceuticals, while its agricultural distribution system reaches farmers worldwide. New companies would need to invest heavily to replicate this market access, competing against a company that reported over €50 billion in net sales in 2023.

**Force 2: Bargaining Power of Suppliers — LOW to MEDIUM**

Bayer sources APIs and agricultural chemical precursors from a global supply base. While some specialised inputs have limited supplier alternatives, Bayer's scale gives it significant purchasing leverage. Post-COVID supply chain disruptions temporarily elevated supplier power, but this has normalised.

**Force 3: Bargaining Power of Buyers — MEDIUM (Pharma) / HIGH (Crop Science)**

In pharmaceuticals, healthcare systems and pharmacy benefit managers have significant pricing power. In Crop Science, large farming cooperatives can negotiate aggressively, and the commoditisation of generic glyphosate has intensified price competition.

**Force 4: Threat of Substitutes — HIGH (Crop Science) / MEDIUM (Pharma)**

Regulatory setbacks, such as the limitations on glyphosate in certain markets, add to the challenges for Crop Science. Biological alternatives to chemical pesticides are growing rapidly, supported by EU regulatory tailwinds. In pharmaceuticals, generic substitution post-patent expiry is a well-understood threat, while biosimilars threaten biological products.

**Force 5: Competitive Rivalry — HIGH (Both Segments)**

The top competitors in the crop protection chemicals market are Bayer Crop Science, Syngenta International AG, BASF SE, Corteva AgriScience, and UPL Ltd. Bayer CropScience holds a 19.4% market share. In pharmaceuticals, rivalry with Pfizer, Novartis, Roche, AstraZeneca, and Johnson & Johnson is intense across all therapeutic areas.

**Overall Assessment:** The industry structure is broadly unfavourable, with high rivalry and buyer power in both core segments. Bayer's competitive position depends critically on sustaining innovation advantage through R&D — currently compromised by the financial and legal crisis.

## 5. STRATEGIC OPTIONS EVALUATION

Three broad strategic options present themselves:

### Option A: "Hold and Repair" — Maintain Three Divisions, Execute Turnaround
Anderson said "In short, on the question of structure, our answer is 'not now' — and this shouldn't be misunderstood as 'never.'" He explained that four "broken areas" in Bayer's business — litigation, debt levels, bureaucracy, and the research and development pipeline — need to be addressed first.

| Criteria | Assessment |
|---|---|
| Financial Feasibility | Moderate — requires sustained cash generation; litigation outflows create risk |
| Strategic Logic | Moderate — preserves optionality but delays value realisation |
| Execution Risk | High — simultaneous management of litigation, restructuring, and pipeline development |
| **Overall** | **Viable but insufficient as a standalone strategy** |

### Option B: "Staged Break-Up" — Spin Off Consumer Health, Then Evaluate Crop Science
Options under consideration include spinning off the crop science or consumer health divisions, doing a sequential three-way breakup, or keeping all three divisions as is. However, a simultaneous three-way breakup has been ruled out.

| Criteria | Assessment |
|---|---|
| Financial Feasibility | High — Consumer Health could command a premium valuation (comparable: Haleon, Kenvue) |
| Strategic Logic | High — removes cross-subsidisation; allows focused capital allocation; releases trapped value |
| Execution Risk | High — complex to execute; 2+ years to complete |
| **Overall** | **High potential but requires financial stabilisation first** |

### Option C: "Radical Pivot" — Exit Crop Science Entirely
Sell the entire Crop Science division to eliminate litigation exposure and raise capital.

| Criteria | Assessment |
|---|---|
| Financial Feasibility | Low — finding a buyer willing to absorb glyphosate litigation is extremely challenging |
| Strategic Logic | Low — destroys strategic value; crystallises losses |
| Execution Risk | Very High — regulatory approvals, litigation transfer complexities |
| **Overall** | **Not recommended** |

**Conclusion:** The optimal strategy is a **hybrid of Options A and B** — execute the operational turnaround to stabilise the business, then pursue a **staged separation of Consumer Health** to accelerate deleveraging and sharpen strategic focus. A full Crop Science separation should remain a medium-term option, contingent on litigation resolution.

## 6. RECOMMENDATIONS & ACTION PLAN

The following phased action plan is structured around three horizons:

---

### 🔴 PHASE 1: STABILISE (0–18 Months, 2025–Mid 2026)

**Priority 1: Resolve the Litigation Crisis**

The glyphosate litigation is the single greatest threat to Bayer's existence as an independent company. All other strategic initiatives are contingent on achieving litigation certainty.

- **Action 1a:** Pursue the U.S. Supreme Court federal preemption case aggressively. The U.S. Supreme Court has asked the Department of Justice to weigh in on whether it should accept Bayer AG's appeal concerning its Roundup herbicide — a favourable ruling would be transformative, potentially capping total liability at a fraction of current estimates.
- **Action 1b:** In parallel, negotiate a comprehensive global settlement framework for remaining Roundup claims, building on the $11 billion settlement template already established.
- **Action 1c:** Engage with the EPA and EFSA to reinforce the scientific consensus on glyphosate safety, building a regulatory firewall against future claims.
- **KPI:** Achieve litigation reserve certainty; reduce open claim count below 50,000 by end of Phase 1.

**Priority 2: Accelerate Debt Reduction**

- **Action 2a:** Implement the full €2 billion in DSO-related cost savings, with €500 million expected in 2025 and €2 billion total by 2026. Prioritise overhead reduction over R&D cuts to protect innovation capacity.
- **Action 2b:** In order to further reduce debt and gain greater flexibility, the company intends to pay out the legal minimum in dividends.
- **Action 2c:** Divest non-core assets and peripheral product lines to generate €1–2 billion in additional proceeds.
- **KPI:** Reduce net financial debt from €32.6 billion to below €28 billion by mid-2026.

**Priority 3: Stabilise the Pharmaceutical Pipeline**

- **Action 3a:** Accelerate regulatory submissions for finerenone (heart failure), elinzanetant (menopause), and sevabertinib (HER2-mutant lung cancer). Filing submissions are based on positive results from the Phase III FINEARTS-HF trial that showed finerenone achieved a statistically significant reduction of cardiovascular death and total heart failure events.
- **Action 3b:** Maximise Nubeqa commercial execution — Nubeqa is now the fastest growing androgen receptor inhibitor in the U.S., with nearly 45,000 patients — expand into a third prostate cancer indication.
- **KPI:** Achieve three new pharmaceutical approvals in Phase 1; maintain Nubeqa above €1.5 billion annual sales.

---

### 🟡 PHASE 2: RESTRUCTURE (18–36 Months, Mid-2026–2027)

**Priority 4: Spin Off Consumer Health Division**

Consumer Health is Bayer's most "separable" asset — it has distinct brand equity, a different customer base, and limited operational interdependencies with the other two divisions. Comparable spin-offs (Haleon from GSK/Pfizer in 2022; Kenvue from Johnson & Johnson in 2023) achieved premium valuations.

- **Action 4a:** Commission a formal strategic review and valuation of Consumer Health. Engage investment banks to assess IPO vs. trade sale vs. spin-off structures.
- **Action 4b:** Begin operational separation — standalone IT systems, management team, financial reporting — to reduce execution risk and maximise valuation.
- **Action 4c:** Execute a partial IPO (retaining 20–30% stake initially) to raise €8–12 billion, deploying proceeds directly to debt reduction.
- **Rationale:** This would reduce net debt to approximately €16–20 billion, dramatically improving the debt/EBITDA ratio and restoring investment-grade credit headroom.
- **KPI:** Complete Consumer Health separation; reduce net debt below €20 billion.

**Priority 5: Transform Crop Science from Chemicals to Biology & Data**

Rather than selling Crop Science (impractical given litigation), Bayer should transform it from a chemicals-led to a **biology-and-data-led** business model.

- **Action 5a:** Accelerate investment in biological crop protection (biopesticides, biostimulants) to reduce regulatory risk from glyphosate restrictions and capture the growing sustainable agriculture market.
- **Action 5b:** Monetise the FieldView digital farming platform — consider a SaaS-style subscription model that generates recurring revenues and embeds Bayer deeply into farmers' decision-making.
- **Action 5c:** Develop the Preceon short corn product as a premium, proprietary offering. Proprietary products insulated from generic competition are critical to margin defence.
- **KPI:** Biologicals to represent 20% of Crop Science revenues by 2028; FieldView to reach 200 million acres under management.

**Priority 6: Complete DSO Transformation**

- **Action 6a:** Scale DSO so that every layer of the organisation is working in this way by the end of 2024, with 2,000 autonomous teams operating on 90-day cycles.
- **Action 6b:** Implement rigorous performance management to ensure DSO teams are genuinely accountable for outcomes, not just activity.
- **Action 6c:** Invest in digital tools and data infrastructure to enable distributed decision-making without loss of coordination.
- **KPI:** Achieve €2 billion in annualised cost savings; reduce management layers from 12 to 5–6.

---

### 🟢 PHASE 3: REINVEST & GROW (36–60 Months, 2028–2030)

**Priority 7: Rebuild Pharmaceutical Leadership**

With debt reduced and litigation largely resolved, Bayer should shift from defence to offence in pharmaceuticals.

- **Action 7a:** Increase R&D investment in gene therapy — Bayer has gene therapy candidates for haemophilia and Parkinson's disease entering Phase II studies — representing the next generation of high-value, defensible pharmaceutical assets.
- **Action 7b:** Pursue targeted, disciplined M&A to fill pipeline gaps — focus on late-stage assets (Phase II/III) in oncology, cardio-renal, and rare diseases to reduce development risk.
- **Action 7c:** Build out cell and gene therapy manufacturing capability, establishing a competitive moat in a segment where manufacturing expertise is as important as scientific innovation.
- **KPI:** Achieve pharmaceutical pipeline of 5+ late-stage assets; pharmaceutical EBITDA margin above 30%.

**Priority 8: Evaluate Crop Science Strategic Options**

By 2028–2030, the strategic situation for Crop Science should be clearer — either glyphosate litigation has been resolved (opening the door to a premium sale or spin-off) or the division has been successfully transformed into a biologicals-led business.

- **Action 8a:** If litigation is resolved, conduct a full strategic review of Crop Science separation. A standalone entity or merger with a competitor like Corteva could unlock significant value.
- **Action 8b:** If litigation persists, continue the biologicals transformation strategy and manage Crop Science as a cash-generative, stable business.

## 7. CONCLUSION

Bayer AG's strategic crisis is, at its core, a case study in **acquisition hubris and due diligence failure**. The Monsanto deal was strategically logical — the combination of seeds and crop protection chemicals was genuinely synergistic — but the catastrophic underestimation of glyphosate litigation risk turned a bold strategic move into a potentially existential threat. Bayer's $63 billion Monsanto acquisition sparked tens of thousands of lawsuits and over $10 billion in settlement costs, serving as a cautionary tale in M&A due diligence.

However, Bayer is not without strategic assets. Bayer CropScience remains the most prominent participant in the crop protection market with a 19.4% market share, constantly engaging in product innovation. Bayer's pharmaceutical segment strategically targets areas with significant unmet medical needs, including oncology, cardiovascular diseases, neurology, and rare diseases.

The path to recovery is long — the CEO has described 2025 as a "pivotal" and "most difficult" year within the company's turnaround plan, with expectations for improved financial performance starting in 2026. But it is navigable. The strategic imperative is clear: **resolve litigation, reduce debt, separate Consumer Health, transform Crop Science, and rebuild the pharmaceutical pipeline**. Executed with discipline and urgency across the three phases outlined above, this programme can restore Bayer to its rightful place as a leading global life-sciences company.

The Bayer case is a powerful reminder that **strategic decisions carry long-tail consequences** that extend far beyond the boardroom horizon. Due diligence must encompass not only financial and operational risk but also legal, reputational, and societal dimensions. For Bayer, the lesson has been learned at enormous cost. The task now is to ensure that cost is not fatal.
