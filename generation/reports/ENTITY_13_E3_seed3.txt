# DIAGEO PLC: NAVIGATING STRUCTURAL HEADWINDS — A STRATEGIC CASE STUDY

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Diageo plc, the world's largest spirits company by retail sales value, entered a period of acute strategic stress between fiscal year 2024 and fiscal year 2026. A convergence of three forces — a demand-side collapse in Latin America and the Caribbean (LAC), a structural volume contraction in its largest single market (the United States), and a secular shift in consumer preferences toward moderation and non-alcoholic alternatives — simultaneously eroded the revenue growth model that had generated consistent outperformance since the company's formation in 1997. This case study analyses that strategic situation using **PESTEL analysis**, **Porter's Five Forces**, the **Resource-Based View (RBV)**, and the **Ansoff Growth Matrix**, before deriving a set of prioritised strategic recommendations. Each claim carries an attributable source; each claim connects to the analysis; each inferential step states its warrant.

## SECTION 1: THE STRATEGIC SITUATION

### 1.1 Company Background

Diageo is now selling over 200 brands in nearly 180 countries and is the number one company by retail sales value in international spirits, including tequila. Its portfolio spans Johnnie Walker (Scotch whisky), Guinness (beer), Smirnoff (vodka), Don Julio and Casamigos (tequila), Tanqueray (gin), and Crown Royal (Canadian whisky). The company boasts over 200 brands, with spirits contributing 75% to its sales, beer accounting for 16%, and other products making up the remaining 9%.

### 1.2 The Presenting Problem

Organic net sales declined $129 million or 0.6%; positive price/mix of 2.9pps was more than offset by a 3.5% volume decline, primarily driven by a 21.1% decline in the Latin America and Caribbean region (LAC). Organic operating profit declined by $304 million or 4.8%, of which $302 million was attributable to LAC; organic operating margin contracted 130bps. Excluding the impact of LAC, organic net sales grew $330 million or 1.8%, driven by price/mix of 3.9pps, partially offset by a 2.1% volume decline. A decrease in organic net sales in North America of 2.5% was more than offset by resilient growth in Africa, Asia Pacific, and Europe.

Johnnie Walker owner Diageo has reported net sales growth of 1.7% for the year ending 30 June 2025 despite a decline in the fourth quarter. However, organic profit declined by 0.7%, including the Cîroc transaction impact. The situation then worsened further: the London-headquartered firm reported a 4% net sales decline to US$10.5 billion for the six months ended 31 December 2025. Within North America (Diageo's biggest market with 36% of net sales), the group's US spirits sales saw an organic drop of 9.3%. Chinese spirits plunged 56% in Diageo H1.

The strategic question is therefore not merely operational: why did a company with unrivalled brand equity and global scale find its growth model simultaneously challenged across multiple geographies, and what must it do to restore sustainable, profitable growth?

## SECTION 2: EXTERNAL ENVIRONMENT — PESTEL ANALYSIS

PESTEL analysis identifies the macro-environmental forces acting on Diageo. Each factor is assessed for its direction of impact and its connection to Diageo's specific performance problems, not catalogued in isolation.

### 2.1 Political

The re-emergence of trade protectionism represents an acute political risk. Diageo was "taking a number of actions to mitigate the impact and disruption to our business that tariffs may cause," engaging directly with the US administration on the "broader impact that this will have on everyone supporting the US hospitality industry, including consumers, employees, distributors, restaurants, bars and other retail outlets." The relevance is direct: Scotch whisky and Irish whiskey are imported into the United States, meaning tariff escalation raises costs for Diageo's distributors and, ultimately, consumer prices — compounding existing demand-side weakness. Reports circulated that Diageo was assessing the sale of its baijiu operation in China, but the group refused to comment. Diageo owns the Shui Jing Fang baijiu brand, among others. Political risk therefore operates as a multiplier on already-challenging demand environments in Diageo's two largest profit pools.

### 2.2 Economic

The economic environment across Diageo's key markets has been characterised by uneven recovery from post-pandemic inflation. The decline in LAC was driven by a strong double-digit net sales growth comparator as well as lower consumption and consumer downtrading due to macroeconomic pressures in the region. In the United States, weakness in North America reflected pressure on disposable income impacting US Spirits. The IWSR corroborates this at the industry level: for the first time in nearly 30 years, IWSR recorded a decline (-2% in 2023) in the volume of spirits sold in the key US market.

The economic mechanism is clear: when real disposable incomes are squeezed, consumers either trade down within spirits or substitute toward lower-cost categories such as beer and wine, both of which undermine Diageo's premium-price strategy. Crucially, total beverage alcohol (TBA) consumption volume declined -1% during H1 2025 compared to the same period the year before, according to IWSR's mid-year data update. Despite the volume drop, TBA value in H1 2025 held steady. This distinction matters enormously for Diageo's long-term thesis: premiumisation remains structurally intact even if cyclically subdued.

### 2.3 Social

The most structurally significant social trend is the emergence of the "sober curious" movement. The mature US spirits market is contracting. The US spirits industry cannot rely on Generation Z because this generation is leaning toward moderation, zero alcohol alternatives, and being sober. "Sober curious" and moderation movements have gone mainstream, leading to a boom in low-alcohol and non-alcoholic beverages. More consumers, especially Millennials and Gen Z, are embracing alcohol-free options for health, wellness, or simply to try new flavors.

This creates a bifurcated challenge for Diageo: the consumers most likely to trade up to premium are also the consumers most likely to reduce overall alcohol intake. The social trend therefore does not simply reduce volume; it also complicates the premiumisation strategy by narrowing the addressable market for high-margin products.

### 2.4 Technological

Technology is a source of competitive capability rather than threat. Diageo's monetization is amplified by advanced revenue growth management (RGM) using machine learning for precision pricing and promotional allocation. These capabilities support Diageo's ability to extract price/mix improvement even in volume-declining environments: in FY2024, despite a 3.5% volume decline, positive price/mix of 2.9 percentage points partially offset the revenue impact. Technology also enables the route-to-market transformation underway in the United States, described by management as "the most significant transformation in at least a decade in our US Spirits" distribution. The analytical implication is that Diageo's technological capabilities are a genuine strategic asset, but they are insufficient alone to offset structural demand headwinds.

### 2.5 Environmental

Diageo invested an additional 30 million euros — roughly $32.5 million — into producing the nonalcoholic beverage Guinness 0.0 at its St. James Gate brewery in Dublin. Volume sales of Guinness 0.0 draught increased nearly 50% between February 2023 and February 2024. This investment is partly an environmental signal — aligning with moderation trends — as well as a commercial response to structurally shifting consumer occasions.

### 2.6 Legal

Regulatory risk is pervasive. Lawsuits filed in New York, Florida, and San Francisco suggest that Diageo's Don Julio and Casamigos brands contain a portion of alcohol derived from sources other than agave, and that consumers would not have paid a premium price had they known this. In the same period, Casamigos declined by 22%. That's a startling performance from a brand whose sale sparked a tequila gold rush and was the fastest growing of any spirits brand in the 2022 edition of Drinks International's Millionaires' Club. Legal exposure compounds an already-weakening brand trajectory in one of Diageo's highest-margin categories.

**PESTEL Inference:** The external environment presents a combination of cyclical headwinds (economic downtrading, tariffs) and structural shifts (sober curiosity, Gen Z moderation). Cyclical factors are addressable through operational agility; structural factors require portfolio repositioning. Both converge on the same strategic imperative: Diageo must reduce dependence on volume-driven growth in mature, regulated markets and accelerate exposure to structurally growing segments.

## SECTION 3: INDUSTRY STRUCTURE — PORTER'S FIVE FORCES

Porter's Five Forces is used here to assess the structural attractiveness of the international spirits industry and to explain where Diageo's competitive position is strong, where it is eroding, and why.

### 3.1 Threat of New Entrants — LOW TO MODERATE

The global spirits industry has high barriers to entry at scale: aged-spirit production requires years of inventory investment, distribution relationships are entrenched, and brand building requires sustained marketing expenditure. Diageo operates in a highly competitive global alcoholic beverage market. Its most prominent direct competitors in the spirits sector include Pernod Ricard, Bacardi, Beam Suntory, Brown-Forman, Constellation Brands, LVMH's Moët Hennessy Division, Campari Group, William Grant & Sons, and Rémy Cointreau. However, the threat is moderate rather than low because the digital era has dramatically reduced the cost of brand creation at the premium end: celebrity-backed spirits and artisanal craft producers can achieve rapid scale through social media, crowding the premium tequila segment where Don Julio and Casamigos compete.

### 3.2 Bargaining Power of Suppliers — LOW TO MODERATE

Diageo's scale gives it significant countervailing purchasing power over agricultural input suppliers. The more strategically relevant supply-side constraint is the long maturation cycle for aged spirits: the Scotch whisky casks Diageo sells today were filled years or decades ago. This constraint cuts both ways — it protects premium pricing power through scarcity, but limits rapid supply expansion in response to demand upswings.

### 3.3 Bargaining Power of Buyers — MODERATE TO HIGH

Walmart, Costco, and Tesco control 59.2% of global alcohol retail distribution channels. These retailers negotiate prices aggressively, demanding volume discounts. This buyer power compresses Diageo's margin at the retail level and limits its ability to pass through price increases without losing distribution. Diageo's strategic emphasis on the on-trade and premium channels is therefore partly a rational response to the margin-compressing power of large retailers, where brand differentiation matters more to the end consumer.

### 3.4 Threat of Substitutes — HIGH AND RISING

This is the most structurally significant force acting on Diageo. Substitutes operate at two levels. First, within alcohol, beer and wine represent lower-cost alternatives during economic stress. Second, and more structurally threatening, sober curious and moderation movements have gone mainstream, leading to a boom in low-alcohol and non-alcoholic beverages, with Millennials and Gen Z embracing alcohol-free options for health and wellness reasons. The RTD non-alcoholic cocktail segment, into which Diageo has launched a new line of ready-to-drink non-alcoholic cocktails in the US under its Ritual Zero Proof brand, marking the company's entry into the North American RTD non-alcoholic cocktail segment, represents both a substitute threat and a strategic response. The analytical implication is that Diageo's long-term volume trajectory in spirits is under structural substitution pressure, making premium pricing and margin expansion — rather than volume growth — the more defensible growth path.

### 3.5 Competitive Rivalry — HIGH

Pernod Ricard, a French company, is Diageo's most direct competitor and the second-largest spirits company globally. Pernod Ricard has intensified premium whisky competition in India, vying for leadership with Diageo. Brown-Forman pressures US whiskey share via brand extensions and global reach. LVMH Moët Hennessy sets luxury benchmarks in cognac and champagne, influencing Reserve pricing. Diageo gained or held total trade market share in markets that total 75% of net sales in fiscal 24, but this figure slipped to 65% of total net sales value in measured markets by H1 FY2025, indicating that competitive pressure is intensifying precisely as consumer demand softens.

**Five Forces Inference:** The industry remains structurally attractive due to premiumisation dynamics, but competitive rivalry and substitution threats are intensifying simultaneously. Diageo's scale advantage is real but insufficient alone — it must compete for the consumer's total beverage occasion, not just within spirits.

## SECTION 4: INTERNAL CAPABILITIES — RESOURCE-BASED VIEW (RBV)

The RBV framework (Barney, 1991) holds that sustained competitive advantage derives from resources and capabilities that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-Substitutable (VRIN). Applying this to Diageo identifies which internal assets can sustain competitive advantage through the current downturn and which are becoming vulnerable.

### 4.1 Brand Portfolio — VRIN: VALUABLE, RARE, IMPERFECTLY IMITABLE, PARTIALLY SUBSTITUTABLE

The luxury spirits market, valued at $12.7 billion, has been growing at a compound annual growth rate of 12% in volume and 18% in value since 2020, according to IWSR data. The Diageo Luxury Group aims to accelerate growth for brands priced at $100 and above, leveraging long-term consumer trends that favour premiumisation and experiential luxury. Diageo's heritage brands — Johnnie Walker, Guinness, Don Julio — possess the authenticity and provenance that command luxury pricing. However, brand equity is not fully inimitable: celebrity brands and craft producers can achieve premium positioning rapidly. **Warrant:** The 22% decline in Casamigos demonstrates that brand equity can erode rapidly when legal challenges or competitive pressure is applied — making brand maintenance a strategic priority, not a given.

### 4.2 Global Distribution Network — VRIN: VALUABLE, RARE, HIGHLY INIMITABLE, NON-SUBSTITUTABLE

In Africa, Diageo works with 15 partners in various markets across Africa to drive growth in the region. These markets are managed through third parties who manufacture, distribute, and commercialise beer, RTDs and mainstream spirits brands. This partner-market model has delivered sustained outperformance: Africa grew organically 9% in FY2024 and 10.9% in H1 FY2026. The distribution network is the most capital- and relationship-intensive resource to replicate. **Warrant:** Africa's sustained outperformance even as North America and Asia Pacific decline validates this asset as a source of durable advantage — it enables Diageo to deploy existing brands into structurally growing markets where competitors lack comparable infrastructure.

### 4.3 Revenue Growth Management (RGM) Capability — VRIN: VALUABLE, MODERATELY RARE, IMITABLE OVER TIME

Since fiscal 18, Diageo has generated annual savings of approximately $500 million through productivity savings, efficiencies and disciplined cost control and utilising its scale to fuel investments. In FY2024, the productivity target for fiscal 24 was exceeded: by the end of fiscal 24, Diageo delivered $698m in productivity savings across all cost categories including supply, marketing and indirect overheads, against a target of $505m. This capability is moderately rare but not permanently inimitable — competitors are investing in similar analytical tools. **Warrant:** RGM capability is best understood as a multiplier on brand and distribution strengths rather than a standalone advantage. It enables Diageo to sustain margins during downturns but cannot substitute for volume growth in the long run.

### 4.4 Innovation Pipeline — VRIN: VALUABLE, PARTIALLY RARE, IMITABLE

Diageo has announced a further €30 million investment into the production of Guinness 0.0 at its site in St. James's Gate, Dublin. The funding will boost Guinness 0.0 production capacity to 176 million pints per year, almost doubling capacity. This innovation is valuable because it allows Diageo to capture consumers who are reducing alcohol intake while retaining them within its brand ecosystem. However, non-alcoholic product development is not rare — every major beverage company is investing in this space. **Warrant:** Innovation in no/low-alcohol is a brand-equity preservation strategy rather than a growth engine in itself, but its absence would cede the next generation of consumers to competitors.

**RBV Inference:** Diageo's most durable competitive advantages are its brand portfolio and global distribution network. Its RGM and innovation capabilities are genuine assets but are more easily imitated. The strategic implication is to concentrate investment on the two VRIN-qualified resources — brands and distribution — in structurally growing markets, while using RGM and innovation to defend margins and capture emerging consumer occasions.

## SECTION 5: GROWTH OPTIONS — ANSOFF MATRIX

The Ansoff Matrix classifies Diageo's strategic growth options along two dimensions: markets (existing vs. new) and products (existing vs. new), and is used here to evaluate where growth energy should be directed given the external and internal analysis above.

### 5.1 Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets)

Diageo's primary near-term focus in North America is recovering share with existing brands through improved route-to-market execution and RGM-driven pricing. The Crown Royal Blackberry launch was an example of commercial execution excellence and resulted in consumer recruitment into whiskey and share gains for the trademark. Market penetration is the lowest-risk strategy but, given the structural volume decline in US spirits, also the lowest-upside option. **Warrant:** Market penetration in North America is necessary but not sufficient — it protects existing revenue but cannot generate the growth needed to offset structural volume decline, as confirmed by the share-in-measured-markets metric declining from 75% (FY2024) to 65% (H1 FY2025).

### 5.2 Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets)

Africa and India represent Diageo's most compelling market development opportunities. In Africa, Diageo's strategy is to grow its beer portfolio fast and its spirits brands faster. The IWSR confirms the structural tailwind: IWSR now forecasts India's TBA volume to surpass Japan in 2027, making it one of the most important emerging markets for international spirits. Deploying existing premium brands — Johnnie Walker, Tanqueray, Don Julio — into these structurally growing markets is a medium-risk, high-upside strategy that directly leverages Diageo's distribution VRIN resource. **Warrant:** Market development into Africa and India is the most strategically coherent growth option because it deploys Diageo's VRIN distribution asset in markets where structural tailwinds — rising middle-class incomes, demographic growth, spirits gaining share of TBA — are strongest, directly addressing the over-dependence on mature, volume-declining markets.

### 5.3 Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets)

Diageo's no/low-alcohol product development falls into this quadrant. Volume sales of Guinness 0.0 draught increased nearly 50% between February 2023 and February 2024, justifying the €30 million capacity investment. Diageo's launch of Ritual Zero Proof RTD non-alcoholic cocktails in the US expands the company's presence beyond its existing non-alcoholic spirits range, connecting to consumer demand for moderation and convenience. **Warrant:** Retaining a Gen Z consumer within the Guinness brand ecosystem in a non-alcoholic occasion is strategically superior to losing that consumer entirely to a competitor — the Five Forces substitution threat is partially neutralised by extending the brand into the substitute category itself.

### 5.4 Diversification (New Products, New Markets)

The luxury spirits market, valued at $12.7 billion, is growing at 12% CAGR in volume and 18% in value since 2020. The Diageo Luxury Group aims to accelerate growth for brands priced at $100 and above, leveraging long-term consumer trends that favour premiumisation and experiential luxury. The Johnnie Walker Vault holds, at any given time, a selection of 500 whiskies from rare, aged and ghost casks, personally curated by the Master Blender. The Vault will bring its creativity and craftsmanship to life through rare products, experiences and collaborations. This experiential luxury model competes with LVMH's Moët Hennessy on its own terms. **Warrant:** In a market where volume growth is structurally constrained, value growth through luxury trading-up is the most sustainable path to margin expansion, and Diageo's heritage brand equity is the most inimitable resource for competing in this segment.

**Ansoff Inference:** Diageo's optimal growth portfolio is a staged combination: (1) market penetration in North America as a defensive priority; (2) market development in Africa and India as the primary growth engine; (3) product development in no/low-alcohol as a brand-equity hedge; and (4) selective luxury diversification as a high-margin, long-term option. This portfolio approach reduces geographic and category concentration risk while deploying VRIN resources where they generate the highest returns.
