# 📡 STRATEGIC CASE STUDY: MTN GROUP — NAVIGATING THE MULTI-MARKET DILEMMA IN PAN-AFRICAN TELECOMS

**Module: Strategic Management | Level: Postgraduate**

---

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

MTN Group, Africa's largest telecommunications operator by subscriber base, faces a defining strategic inflection point. The group connected 290.9 million subscribers (up from 284.7 million in 2023), enabled internet access for 157.8 million active data users, and extended broadband coverage to 93% of the population. Yet this operational strength is being tested by two simultaneous and compounding disruptions: **(1)** severe macroeconomic volatility — most acutely the devaluation of the Nigerian naira — which wiped out reported revenues despite strong local-currency growth; and **(2)** the intensifying threat of over-the-top (OTT) digital players and fintech challengers eroding traditional revenue streams while competing directly in the mobile money space MTN is trying to dominate.

This case study analyses MTN's strategic situation using PESTEL, Porter's Five Forces, SWOT, VRIO, the Ansoff Matrix, and the McKinsey 7S model, culminating in a prioritised set of strategic recommendations.

---

## 2. CASE BACKGROUND & CONTEXT

### 2.1 Company Profile

MTN Group Limited, headquartered in Johannesburg and listed on the JSE, was founded in 1994. MTN's purpose is embodied in the belief that "everyone deserves the benefits of a modern connected life," with the strategy being "Ambition 2025: Leading digital solutions for Africa's progress." The group's core portfolio spans **Connectivity** (voice, data, 4G/5G), **Fintech** (MTN MoMo), **Digital Services** (Ayoba, enterprise cloud), and **Wholesale/API Services**.

### 2.2 The "Ambition 2025" Strategy

MTN Group is transforming into a technology company, focusing on digital services and financial inclusion with its "Ambition 2025" strategy. A key development was Mastercard's $200 million investment in February 2024, valuing the fintech segment at $5.2 billion. The Group also announced an evolution of strategy, unveiling Ambition 2030, which streamlines execution into three principal platforms: Connectivity, Fintech, and Digital Infrastructure.

### 2.3 The Competitive Landscape

MTN's primary pan-African rivals are:
- **Airtel Africa**: Strong East/West Africa presence; competes on value pricing and mobile money expansion.
- **Vodacom/Vodafone**: Rivals Vodacom on 4G/5G and fintech wallets across southern markets.
- **Safaricom/M-PESA**: The archetype platform company — this case study investigates how M-PESA transformed Safaricom from an incumbent telecommunications operator into a digital platform working across different industries and sectors through an extensive business ecosystem.

---

## 3. THE STRATEGIC SITUATION: THE DUAL DISRUPTION DILEMMA

### 3.1 Macroeconomic Headwind: The Nigeria Crisis

Nigeria contributes roughly 35% of MTN's group service revenue. The naira floatation of 2023 caused catastrophic reported losses: MTN Nigeria's record revenue of N3.36 trillion for 2024 was overshadowed by a loss after tax of N400.44 billion. At group level, MTN Group, which has 288 million customers across 18 markets in Africa, saw its group service revenue decrease 20.8% to 85.3 billion rand — though in constant currency, group service revenue rose 12.1%. In 2024, these telecom giants took decisive steps to slash their foreign debt, repaying a combined $1.2 billion to alleviate the pressures of foreign exchange fluctuations.

### 3.2 Competitive Disruption: OTT and Fintech Encroachment

Airtel's loan disbursement totalled approximately $980 million in 2024 — smaller than MTN's but growing at 37% compared to MTN's 24%. In the fintech space, Safaricom's new M-PESA Core embeds AI capabilities designed to improve fraud detection, enable self-healing, and power real-time monitoring, built on a cloud-native foundation allowing instant scalability. In 2024, the volume and value of mobile money transactions grew by 22% and 15% respectively, attracting global payment giants to Africa's mobile money market.

### 3.3 The Structural Opportunity

Despite these pressures, the long-term structural case is compelling: only 38% of Africa's population is using the internet, significantly below the global average. About 200 million people lack mobile broadband coverage, constituting nearly half of the global unconnected population. McKinsey's analysis suggests that at-scale deployment of gen AI with telcos in Africa can unlock $6.0 billion to $9.6 billion in economic value.

## 4. STRATEGIC ANALYSIS

### 4.1 PESTEL Analysis

**Political**: MTN operates across 18 sovereign states, each with distinct political risk profiles. Across Africa, telecom regulation is becoming increasingly central to the continent's digital future, with rapid expansion of mobile broadband, mobile money, and spectrum licensing creating both opportunity and regulatory exposure.

**Economic**: MTN's ability to adapt to regulatory changes while managing the economic pressures of currency devaluation and inflation is pivotal to its strategy for stabilising and eventually increasing its revenue streams.

**Social**: Africa's median age is approximately 19 years — the world's youngest and most mobile-first population. Adults in rural areas are 25% less likely to use mobile internet than their urban counterparts, with this gap widening to 48% in sub-Saharan Africa.

**Technological**: Different countries often adopt varying frequency allocations, regulatory frameworks, and licensing conditions, which creates inefficiencies, drives up equipment costs, and complicates cross-border operations. Meanwhile, Helios Towers sees Africa growth driven by 5G and AI, posting 14% growth in adjusted EBITDA to $421 million in 2024.

**Environmental**: "You get a 40% to 50% carbon reduction per tenant through sharing versus everyone having their own towers" — making infrastructure sharing a sustainability imperative as well as a cost strategy.

**Legal**: The slow and inconsistent allocation of spectrum across the continent makes things more difficult. South Africa, one of the most advanced telecom markets in Africa, saw spectrum auctions delayed for years, which made the big players stronger.

**PESTEL Summary**: The macro-environment is characterised by high political and currency risk but profound structural growth tailwinds from demographics, digitisation, and financial inclusion. MTN must build **resilience against downside shocks** while **investing aggressively in long-run growth**.

---

### 4.2 Porter's Five Forces

**Force 1: Competitive Rivalry — HIGH**

The competitive landscape for MTN Group is characterised by intense rivalry from established mobile network operators and emerging digital service providers. MTN's primary direct competitors include Vodacom Group, Airtel Africa, and Orange. In the fintech battleground, Airtel Money has shown faster user growth (~20% y/y) compared to MTN's MoMo (~10% y/y) recently, giving it a momentum edge in fintech.

**Force 2: Threat of New Entrants — MODERATE TO HIGH**

Traditional entry barriers (spectrum, capex) remain significant for MNOs. However, OTT players and fintechs have effectively entered without requiring spectrum. Airtel Africa's satellite connectivity partnerships with Starlink aim to boost network resilience and expand digital services across its 14-country footprint — illustrating how the definition of "entry" has expanded.

**Force 3: Threat of Substitutes — HIGH**

Voice and SMS revenues are structurally substituted by OTT messaging. In fintech, in October 2024, Kenya's regulator announced plans to roll out a new fast payment system (FPS), allowing users to send and receive money across all digital payment systems — a central-bank-led substitute that threatens MoMo's fee income.

**Force 4: Bargaining Power of Buyers — MODERATE AND RISING**

Firms have spent time and energy ensuring their customers are well protected and incentivised so as to stick to their respective mobile networks — reflecting rising consumer switching pressure. Mobile money interoperability mandates further reduce wallet stickiness.

**Force 5: Bargaining Power of Suppliers — MODERATE**

IHS Towers, Helios Towers, and American Tower collectively managed more than 80% of third-party tenancies in 2024, underscoring a moderately concentrated supply structure. MTN's 25% stake in IHS Towers partially mitigates this power.

**Five Forces Summary**: The traditional "pipe" model of telecoms is commoditising. The only viable strategic response is to move up the value chain into platform and ecosystem businesses.

### 4.4 VRIO Framework (Resource-Based View)

The VRIO framework (Barney, 1991) assesses whether resources generate **sustained competitive advantage** by being Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Organised.

| Resource / Capability | V | R | I | O | Competitive Implication |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Pan-African subscriber base (290M+) | ✅ | ✅ | ⚠️ | ✅ | **Temporary Advantage** |
| **MTN MoMo platform & wallet corridors** | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ | ⚠️ | **Sustained Advantage (if deepened)** |
| **MTN Brand equity** | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ | **Sustained Advantage** |
| Network infrastructure (towers, spectrum) | ✅ | ⚠️ | ❌ | ✅ | **Competitive Parity** |
| **Customer data & AI analytics** | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ | ❌ | **Unused Advantage (needs investment)** |
| Regulatory relationships (18 markets) | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ | **Sustained Advantage** |
| Digital content (Ayoba, MTN Play) | ⚠️ | ❌ | ❌ | ⚠️ | **Competitive Disadvantage** |

**VRIO Key Insight**: MTN's most defensible advantages lie in MoMo (network effects + regulatory moats), brand, and customer data. Its weakest position is in digital content. **Strategic implication: double down on fintech platform depth and data monetisation; exit or partner in content.**

## 6. RECOMMENDATIONS & ACTION PLAN

### RECOMMENDATION 1: EXECUTE THE "PLATFORM FORTRESS" FINTECH STRATEGY
**Priority: CRITICAL | Timeframe: 0–24 months**

MTN's most defensible competitive advantage is MTN MoMo. M-PESA's strategic flywheel works as follows: the more valuable and reliable the platform is for developers, the more innovative services will be built upon it, which in turn makes the entire ecosystem more valuable for end-users — this is how a platform defends market leadership against agile competitors, not just by being a better product, but by being the indispensable platform for an entire ecosystem. MTN must replicate this flywheel.

**Specific Actions**:
1. **Launch MTN MoMo Open API Programme**: Create a developer portal enabling third-party fintechs and enterprise customers to build on MoMo infrastructure. Target 500+ registered developers within 18 months.
2. **Expand Advanced Financial Services**: Accelerate AI-powered micro-lending, micro-insurance, and wealth management. There is a growing demand for enterprise-focused and financial services, with a key challenge being finding novel ways to provide these services to the more than 40 million micro-businesses that may only have mobile phones.
3. **Deepen Merchant Acceptance Network**: Target 2 million active merchants via QR code and POS rollout, creating two-sided network effects.
4. **Leverage Mastercard for Cross-Border Remittances**: In April 2024, MTN's Mobile Money (MoMo) service expanded to 10 new countries, establishing 25 new wallet corridors, facilitating international remittances and connecting over 200 million wallets across 24 African nations. Expand to 50 corridors by 2026.
5. **Pursue MTN Group Fintech IPO**: A separate listing (already valued at US$5.2 billion) would unlock capital and sharpen management focus. Target a listing by 2027.

**KPIs**: MoMo Monthly Active Users (target: 80M by 2026); fintech revenue as % of group service revenue (target: 30%+); active merchant acceptance points.

---

### RECOMMENDATION 2: IMPLEMENT THE "CURRENCY SHIELD" PROGRAMME
**Priority: CRITICAL | Timeframe: 0–12 months**

The naira devaluation demonstrated that operational excellence in local currency can be entirely negated at the group level by FX translation. MTN's headline earnings per share fell 72.3%, while adjusted HEPS fell 9.5% — driven primarily by devaluation of the naira against the US dollar, which was the strongest headwind to the company's business.

**Specific Actions**:
1. **Systematic FX Hedging**: Implement rolling 12–18 month hedging programmes for hard-currency obligations in the top 5 volatile markets (Nigeria, Ghana, Ethiopia, Sudan, Zambia).
2. **USD Revenue Acceleration**: Prioritise growth in enterprise and wholesale services (USD-denominated) in high-volatility markets.
3. **Portfolio Rebalancing**: Increase investment weighting toward more stable macroeconomic environments (Rwanda, Côte d'Ivoire) and reduce discretionary capex in the most volatile markets during acute FX stress.
4. **Local Currency Debt Refinancing**: Refinance USD-denominated debt with local-currency instruments. MTN's 25-billion-rand capital raise in 2024 is a model to replicate.

**KPIs**: % of group debt in local currency (target: 60%+ by 2026); FX hedging coverage ratio; constant-currency vs. reported revenue gap.

---

### RECOMMENDATION 3: PURSUE INFRASTRUCTURE MONETISATION VIA IHS TOWERS ACQUISITION
**Priority: HIGH | Timeframe: 12–36 months**

MTN entered advanced talks to acquire IHS Towers, in which it already holds a 25% stake. A full acquisition could value the remaining shares at about $2.06 billion. Africa's telecom tower market is projected to reach $4.03 billion in 2026.

**Specific Actions**:
1. **Complete IHS Towers Acquisition**: Structure the deal to minimise immediate cash outlay (share-for-share exchange, deferred payments).
2. **Optimise Tenancy Ratios**: Post-acquisition, market tower space to Airtel, Vodacom, and Orange — generating USD-denominated tenancy revenue. IHS Towers has pushed their tenancy ratio above the 1.5x mark; the 2025 strategy focuses on increasing co-locations and driving operational efficiencies. Target 2.0x+ within 3 years.
3. **Integrate Energy Management**: Tower sharing delivers a 40–50% carbon reduction per tenant, reducing diesel costs and supporting ESG commitments.

**KPIs**: Tower tenancy ratio (target: 2.0x by 2027); TowerCo revenue contribution to group; energy cost per site.

---

### RECOMMENDATION 4: EXECUTE A STRUCTURED "RURAL FRONTIER" PROGRAMME
**Priority: MEDIUM-HIGH | Timeframe: 24–60 months**

Between 2012 and 2022, the number of people in Africa with mobile broadband access more than tripled from 114 million to almost 400 million. Despite this, Africa still has the greatest connectivity gap of any region in the world.

**Specific Actions**:
1. **Adopt a "Coverage-as-a-Service" Model**: Partner with Helios Towers and American Tower to deploy shared rural sites, splitting capex and operating costs. Target 5,000 new rural sites over 3 years.
2. **Leverage DFI Financing**: Partner with the World Bank, IFC, and African Development Bank to co-finance rural connectivity in exchange for coverage commitments.
3. **Deploy LEO Satellite Backhaul**: Satellite connectivity partnerships with Starlink aim to boost network resilience and expand digital services — a model MTN should adopt for rural backhaul.
4. **Device Financing Programme**: Launch smartphone installment payments via MoMo to accelerate penetration, particularly targeting women (28% less likely to own a smartphone).

**KPIs**: New rural sites deployed; new rural subscribers; rural MoMo activation rate.

---

### RECOMMENDATION 5: RATIONALISE DIGITAL CONTENT — PARTNER, DON'T OWN
**Priority: MEDIUM | Timeframe: 0–18 months**

VRIO analysis clearly shows that MTN's digital content assets are not rare or inimitable. MTN cannot out-invest Netflix, YouTube, or TikTok. However, MTN can leverage its distribution (290M subscribers) as a bargaining chip.

**Specific Actions**:
1. **Negotiate Zero-Rating Partnerships**: Partner with Netflix, Spotify, YouTube, and TikTok to zero-rate their content on MTN networks in exchange for revenue sharing. This drives data consumption and reduces churn.
2. **Reposition Ayoba as an African Super-App**: Rather than competing with global messaging apps, reposition Ayoba around local services — mobile money, local news, government services, healthcare — that global platforms do not serve well.
3. **Divest MTN Play**: Redirect capital to fintech and connectivity, while retaining user engagement data for AI/analytics.

**KPIs**: Revenue from content partnerships; Ayoba MAUs as a super-app; capital freed from content divestment.

## 8. CONCLUSION

MTN Group's strategic situation exemplifies the central paradox of pan-African telecoms: extraordinary long-run growth potential coexisting with acute short-run vulnerability. While the fundamentals of Ambition 2025 remain relevant and appropriate, MTN is streamlining its focus into three principal platforms — Connectivity, Fintech, and Digital Infrastructure — as it looks beyond 2025.

The multi-framework analysis reveals a consistent strategic imperative: **MTN must urgently deepen its platform moat in fintech while simultaneously building financial resilience against macroeconomic volatility.** The company's genuine competitive advantages — the MoMo platform, the brand, the subscriber data, and the regulatory relationships — are sufficient to sustain leadership if organised and invested in effectively. The weaknesses — FX concentration, systems fragmentation, and talent gaps — are addressable through disciplined execution.

The "Platform Fortress" strategy, combined with the "Currency Shield" programme and structured infrastructure monetisation, offers MTN a path to becoming not merely Africa's largest telecom operator, but Africa's most valuable digital platform company. MTN Group reported its strongest annual performance to date in 2025, with service revenue rising nearly 25% to R218 billion and adjusted headline earnings per share up 67% — evidence that the strategy is beginning to deliver. The strategic window is open, but it will not remain open indefinitely. Airtel Money's 37% loan growth, Wave's zero-fee disruption, and M-PESA's Fintech 2.0 upgrade are all evidence that the competition is moving fast. MTN's response must be faster.
