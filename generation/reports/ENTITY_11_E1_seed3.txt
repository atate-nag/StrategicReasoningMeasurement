# Strategic Case Study: De Beers Group — Navigating Structural Disruption and the Anglo American Divestment

---

## 1. Executive Summary

This case study examines De Beers Group — the world's leading natural diamond company and an 85%-owned subsidiary of Anglo American plc — at a moment of compound strategic crisis. Three interlocking pressures converge simultaneously: (i) a structural demand collapse driven by the rapid rise of laboratory-grown diamonds (LGDs); (ii) a parent-company divestment process that has introduced deep ownership uncertainty; and (iii) a complex web of sovereign stakeholders whose political objectives complicate any clean transaction. The analysis applies **Porter's Five Forces**, the **VRIO framework**, the **Ansoff Matrix**, **Stakeholder Mapping**, and **BCG Portfolio Theory** to build a coherent chain of reasoning from environmental threat through internal capability to strategic recommendation.

## 2. Background and Context

### 2.1 Anglo American's Portfolio Transformation

In response to BHP's takeover proposals, Anglo American unveiled a restructuring strategy that includes plans to demerge its De Beers diamond business, exit steelmaking coal over time, and consider divestment or restructuring of some platinum and nickel operations. The strategic rationale was explicit: Anglo American is simplifying its portfolio at pace to focus on copper, premium iron ore and crop nutrients, offering a highly attractive and differentiated investment proposition with a structurally lower cost base.

By the end of 2024, the company agreed to sell its steelmaking coal business for up to $4.9 billion in gross cash proceeds, and also finalised the sale of its nickel business for up to $500 million. The full-year 2024 results recorded a loss attributable to equity shareholders of $3.1 billion, driven by net impairments of $3.8 billion, and net debt of $10.6 billion. Despite this, the 2024 financial performance delivered a Total Shareholder Return of 24%, outpacing broader market indices, and CEO Duncan Wanblad emphasised the company's operational excellence. The strategic endpoint became clearer in 2025: Anglo American and Teck Resources announced a merger of equals to create Anglo Teck, a top-five global copper producer with more than 70% exposure to copper, headquartered in Vancouver, Canada.

### 2.2 De Beers: A Crown Asset Under Pressure

In May 2024, Anglo announced plans to separate from De Beers, either through a sale or demerger. The decision followed the BHP takeover bid and formed part of a wider restructuring designed to streamline Anglo's portfolio, unlock value, and strengthen shareholder returns — with a sharpened focus on iron ore and copper.

De Beers' financial performance has deteriorated sharply. De Beers posted a US$189 million loss in the first half of 2025 in the midst of a prolonged downturn in global rough-diamond demand and competition from synthetic stones. De Beers suffered a $288 million net loss in 2024, primarily the result of a 44% plunge in diamond sales. By 2025, the world's largest diamond miner had stocked up about $2 billion in unsold natural stones and announced plans to cut more than 1,000 jobs across major operations. Anglo American's impairment charge explicitly referenced shifting consumer preferences, indicating corporate recognition that synthetic diamond adoption represents permanent rather than temporary market structure change.

## 3. The Strategic Situation: Three Interlocking Crises

### Crisis 1 — The Lab-Grown Diamond Disruption

In 2024, lab-grown diamonds accounted for 14% of the US jewelry market, with 45% of engagement rings sold in the US set with lab-grown diamonds, reflecting a significant shift in consumer purchasing patterns. By 2025, some 61% of consumers who took part in a recent survey picked a synthetic centre stone, representing a large rise from 2024 when the number of consumers purchasing a lab-grown centre stone passed 50% for the first time. Tenoris reported that lab-grown diamond jewellery unit sales grew 43% overall in 2024, resulting in a 31% increase in sales. The global LGD market was valued at $27.24 billion in 2024 and is expected to grow at a CAGR of around 11.77% during the forecast period to 2033.

De Beers' own strategic response — the Lightbox LGD brand launched in 2018 — ultimately failed. According to reports, the decision to close Lightbox reflects a dramatically changed market environment, with the sharp decline in lab-grown diamond prices making the Lightbox model increasingly uncompetitive. The closure of Lightbox supports De Beers' Origins Strategy, introduced in May 2024, which emphasises focusing on areas with stronger returns and streamlining operations.

### Crisis 2 — The Ownership Uncertainty

Anglo American, which holds an 85% stake in De Beers, values the business at about $5 billion. Analysts at UBS estimate the final sale price could range from $3 billion to $4 billion, given soft market conditions. Anglo CEO Duncan Wanblad said he expects the sale process to conclude within six months but did not rule out spinning off De Beers if negotiations fail. The sale has attracted interest from at least six consortia, including billionaire Anil Agarwal, Indian firms KGK Group and Kapu Gems, and Qatari funds.

### Crisis 3 — The Botswana Sovereignty Dimension

Anglo American's handling of the De Beers sale has angered Botswana, which owns 15% of the diamond producer, because the government believes that it hasn't been properly consulted. Botswana's president has declared that securing a majority share is "a matter of economic sovereignty," representing the logical next step after securing a crucial 2025 deal that increases Botswana's share of rough stones from Debswana to 50%. Angola has also entered the contest: Angola formally offered to acquire Anglo American's 85% stake in De Beers through state-owned gem producer Endiama EP, positioning itself against Botswana and global investor groups in one of the most high-stakes resource negotiations on the continent.

## 4. Porter's Five Forces — Industry Attractiveness

Porter's Five Forces (1980) assesses the structural attractiveness of an industry. Applied to the natural diamond jewellery sector, the analysis reveals a fundamentally deteriorating competitive environment.

**Force 1 — Threat of New Entrants: LOW to MODERATE (shifting)**
Historically, barriers to entry in natural diamond mining were near-absolute — enormous capital requirements, geological scarcity of kimberlite deposits, and De Beers' tightly controlled sightholder distribution network. However, this force has been partially neutralised by LGD technology: the barriers to *producing diamonds* have collapsed. A CVD diamond reactor requires far less capital than a mine, and Chinese manufacturers have scaled rapidly.

**Force 2 — Bargaining Power of Suppliers: LOW**
De Beers' vertical integration from mine to retail gives it direct control over primary inputs. However, Botswana's 2025 deal increasing its share of rough stones from Debswana to 50% represents a form of sovereign supplier leverage not captured in classical analysis — addressed in the stakeholder section.

**Force 3 — Bargaining Power of Buyers: HIGH and RISING**
During the first quarter of 2026, the consolidated average realised price for De Beers diamonds fell 19% to $101 per carat, driven by a 17% decline in the average rough price index alongside a shift in sales mix toward a higher proportion of lower-value goods. This dual compression of price and mix is structurally damaging, and internet-enabled direct-to-consumer platforms have further reduced information asymmetry.

**Force 4 — Threat of Substitutes: VERY HIGH (dominant force)**
The $2.3 billion impairment charge to Anglo American's carrying value of De Beers reflects management's assessment that consumer preference shifts represent permanent rather than temporary market changes, specifically citing greater shifting of customer preference between natural diamonds and laboratory-grown diamonds as a primary driver. Unlike traditional substitutes (e.g., cubic zirconia), LGDs are chemically and physically identical to mined diamonds, removing the most powerful historical objection to substitution.

**Force 5 — Competitive Rivalry: HIGH**
Within natural diamonds, De Beers competes with Alrosa, Lucara, and Petra Diamonds. G7 sanctions on Russian diamonds have temporarily benefited De Beers, but within the broader jewellery market, luxury brands and online retailers have intensified rivalry.

**Porter's Conclusion:** The natural diamond industry has moved from a structurally attractive oligopoly to a structurally challenged market facing existential substitution pressure. De Beers cannot compete on price or volume; it must compete on differentiation.

## 5. VRIO Analysis — Internal Resource Audit

The VRIO framework (Barney, 1991) assesses whether resources are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Organised — the conditions for sustained competitive advantage.

### Resource 1 — Brand Equity and Marketing Heritage

*Valuable?* Yes. De Beers created the cultural norm of diamond engagement rings and has sustained it for 75 years. *Rare?* Yes. No competitor possesses comparable brand heritage. *Inimitable?* Partially — the brand cannot be replicated, but emotional associations are being contested by LGD marketers. *Organised?* Improving: De Beers announced a collaboration with Signet Jewelers in May 2024; Signet is the world's largest retailer of diamond jewellery, and its iconic retail brands include KAY Jewelers, Zales, Jared, Diamonds Direct, and Blue Nile. In October 2024, De Beers launched "Worth the Wait," a natural diamond campaign focused on the themes of modern love and evolving relationships.

**Verdict: Competitive advantage — but eroding. Requires urgent reinvestment.**

### Resource 2 — Mining Asset Portfolio

*Valuable?* Yes. De Beers has a highly competitive portfolio of mining assets that are among the best on the cost curve and have substantial production capability and reserves. With global natural diamond production forecast to decline over the coming decade, De Beers' assets represent a structural scarcity advantage. *Rare?* Yes — world-class kimberlite deposits are geologically scarce. *Inimitable?* Yes. *Organised?* Yes.

**Verdict: Sustained Competitive Advantage — the bedrock of De Beers' long-term value.**

### Resource 3 — Tracr Blockchain Provenance Platform

*Valuable?* Yes. De Beers has made a substantial $20 million investment in Tracr, the blockchain-based diamond traceability initiative. Tracr, which enables the provision of provenance information from source to sightholder to store on a secure blockchain, will underpin confidence in natural diamonds. *Rare?* Yes. Since its inception in 2018, Tracr has made significant strides; today, De Beers registers over two-thirds of its global production by value on the platform. *Inimitable?* Yes in the short-to-medium term — first-mover scale creates a network effect. *Organised?* Partially: ORIGIN – De Beers Group is a new branded polished diamond offering designed to enable retailers to tell the individual stories of natural diamonds sourced by De Beers Group, strengthening the connection to their provenance.

**Verdict: Potential Sustained Competitive Advantage — under-commercialised. De Beers' most strategically important emerging asset.**

### Resource 4 — Sightholder Distribution Network

Once a near-absolute competitive moat, this resource has eroded as De Beers' market share has fallen from ~80% in the 1980s to below 30% today. **Verdict: Parity resource at best.**

### VRIO Conclusion

De Beers' sustainable competitive advantages rest on two pillars: irreplaceable mining assets (supply scarcity) and the Tracr provenance platform (a "proof of nature" differentiator that LGDs structurally cannot match). These define the only viable standalone strategy.

## 6. Ansoff Matrix — Strategic Options

The Ansoff Matrix (1957) maps strategic growth options across products and markets.

**Quadrant 1 — Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets):** Intensified natural diamond marketing in the US, Europe, and Japan. De Beers' Origins strategy is a comprehensive five-year plan aimed at increasing value across its operations, with De Beers planning to rejuvenate demand for natural diamonds with new marketing strategies aimed at modern consumers, with collaborations with retailers central to the approach. This is necessary but insufficient alone — the addressable natural diamond market is structurally shrinking.

**Quadrant 2 — Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets):** Expansion into India and China. Both are large and growing, but Chinese consumers have shown strong affinity for LGDs, and Indian midstream players are already integrated into the LGD supply chain. Moderate upside; does not resolve the substitution problem.

**Quadrant 3 — Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets):** Pivot Element Six's synthetic diamond R&D toward industrial applications — semiconductors, quantum computing, and thermal management. Investors should monitor Element Six's industrial revenues, as breakthroughs in tech partnerships could transform synthetic diamonds from a competitive threat into a revenue stream. This is the most strategically underexplored option.

**Quadrant 4 — Diversification (New Products, New Markets):** Leverage Tracr as a standalone provenance-as-a-service platform for other luxury goods (coloured gemstones, gold, art). The GIA's acquisition of a 30% stake in Tracr reinforces its position as a platform created by the industry, and GIA's collaboration with Tracr enables the inclusion of diamond provenance information on eligible grading reports.

**Ansoff Conclusion:** The recommended strategy is a portfolio of moves — market penetration to stabilise revenue, market development to extend the addressable market, and product/market development to build a long-term business structurally insulated from LGD competition.

## 7. Stakeholder Analysis (Mendelow's Power/Interest Matrix)

**High Power / High Interest — Critical Zone:**

*Anglo American:* As 85% owner, Anglo has ultimate authority over the sale. Its interest is to maximise proceeds and complete the divestment. CEO Duncan Wanblad stated: "We are fast transforming Anglo American into a far higher margin and more valuable mining company focused on exceptional copper, premium iron ore and crop nutrients assets." The tension is that Anglo's urgency conflicts with De Beers' need for a stable, long-term owner committed to the Origins strategy.

*Government of Botswana:* Anglo American aims to sell off its 85% stake in De Beers by the end of 2025 as it restructures. De Beers suffered a $288 million net loss in 2024, primarily the result of a 44% plunge in diamond sales. Botswana's economic stakes are existential — President Boko has declared that securing a majority share is "a matter of economic sovereignty."

**High Power / Lower Interest:** G7 governments, whose sanctions on Russian diamonds have created a regulatory demand for provenance certification that plays directly to De Beers' Tracr advantage.

**Moderate Power / High Interest:** Sightholders (midstream), De Beers' 20,000+ employees, and Signet Jewelers — all of whom face strategic uncertainty during the prolonged sale process.

**Stakeholder Conclusion:** The De Beers sale is not a conventional M&A transaction. It is a geopolitically embedded divestment in which sovereign interests have veto-equivalent power. Any viable ownership solution must be designed *around* Botswana's interests, not despite them.

## 8. BCG Portfolio Matrix — Anglo American's Divestment Logic

The BCG Growth-Share Matrix provides a framework for understanding Anglo American's divestment rationale.

Anglo American's Quellaveco copper mine in Peru delivered more than 300,000 tonnes of copper in both 2023 and 2024, with an expected 36-year mine life and first-quartile cost curve positioning. Copper is structurally driven by electrification and EV adoption — a classic **Star**. Anglo American and Teck believe the merger will enhance portfolio quality, resilience and strategic positioning, bringing together proven capabilities in technical and operational excellence.

De Beers, by contrast, is a **Dog** within Anglo's portfolio — Anglo's actions came as De Beers reported a $511 million loss for 2025, significantly worse than the $25 million loss it posted in 2024. The BCG analysis makes the divestment logic explicit: De Beers consumes management attention and capital in a structurally unattractive market while Anglo's Stars are capital-hungry and strategically aligned with global megatrends.

However, as a **standalone entity**, De Beers is not a Dog. Its world-class mining assets, globally recognised brand, and Tracr platform define a viable luxury differentiation strategy — one that has been suppressed by its management as a mining subsidiary rather than as a luxury goods company.

## 9. Synthesis: The Coherent Chain of Causation

The five frameworks connect into a single causal chain:

1. **Environmental shift (Porter):** The natural diamond industry's structural attractiveness has collapsed — the LGD substitution threat is permanent, confirmed by Anglo's $2.3 billion impairment.
2. **Portfolio logic (BCG):** De Beers is a "Dog" within Anglo American's portfolio; the divestment is strategically rational and irreversible.
3. **Internal capability (VRIO):** De Beers retains two genuine sources of sustained competitive advantage — irreplaceable mining assets and the Tracr provenance platform — which define the only viable strategic path.
4. **Strategic options (Ansoff):** De Beers cannot compete on price; it must compete on differentiation through a luxury provenance model.
5. **Stakeholder constraints (Freeman/Mendelow):** The ownership transition must accommodate Botswana's sovereignty interests — a structure that excludes Botswana will fail regardless of commercial merit.

The chain: *structural disruption forces divestment → divestment creates ownership uncertainty → internal capabilities define the viable path for a standalone De Beers → stakeholder power shapes the ownership structure that can execute that strategy.*

## 10. Recommendations and Action Plan

### Recommendation 1: Botswana-Anchored Consortium Ownership (0–12 Months)

Anglo American should negotiate a structure in which the Government of Botswana acquires a controlling stake (51%+), with Anglo retaining a transitional minority (15–20%) and a commercially sophisticated co-investor (luxury conglomerate or Gulf sovereign wealth fund) taking 25–30%. Wanblad said Anglo was under no obligation to accept any offer but welcomed growing regional interest from Angola and Botswana, and confirmed Botswana's government will join negotiations with shortlisted bidders. The valuation gap between Anglo's $5 billion estimate and UBS's $3–4 billion can be bridged by deferred payments tied to diamond price recovery, aligning all parties' incentives.

**Rationale:** Botswana's political legitimacy in its primary mining jurisdiction is non-negotiable. A Botswana-controlled De Beers secures the long-term licence to operate, while the commercial co-investor provides operational expertise that a government-led structure alone cannot supply.

### Recommendation 2: Accelerate the Origins Provenance Premium Strategy (0–24 Months)

De Beers should scale Tracr registration to 100% of production by value, make ORIGIN-branded polished diamonds the primary consumer product offering (with QR-code provenance certificates at point of sale), and increase category marketing spend to pre-2010 levels in partnership with Signet. Tracr is not only valuable for registering the information required under new diamond import rules within G7 countries, but also offers an enhanced consumer proposition through connecting diamonds to their source. With global natural diamond production forecast to decline over the coming decade, De Beers' assets represent a structural scarcity advantage — but only if consumer demand for natural diamonds is maintained through effective marketing.

**Rationale:** The VRIO analysis identifies Tracr as De Beers' most strategically important emerging asset. A natural diamond's geological provenance is the one attribute a CVD reactor cannot replicate.

### Recommendation 3: Pivot Element Six to Industrial Applications (12–36 Months)

De Beers should redirect its synthetic diamond R&D capability through Element Six toward high-value industrial applications — semiconductor substrates, quantum computing, and thermal management. Breakthroughs in tech partnerships could transform synthetic diamonds from a competitive threat into a revenue stream. Partner with semiconductor firms on joint development agreements to accelerate commercialisation and share capital risk.

**Rationale:** The Ansoff analysis identifies product development in new markets as De Beers' highest-growth option. This diversification reduces existential dependence on jewellery demand.

### Recommendation 4: Midstream Supply Curtailment (0–18 Months)

De Beers should implement a structured rough diamond production curtailment of 10–15% over 18 months to reduce the $2 billion inventory overhang and support price stabilisation. The dual compression of falling unit prices and a deteriorating product mix creates a compounding drag on total revenue that is particularly damaging to buyer valuation models. Supply discipline — a classic De Beers tool — must be reinstated while the market adjusts to LGD substitution.

### Recommendation 5: G7 Regulatory Moat via Tracr Standardisation (Ongoing)

De Beers should actively position Tracr as the industry standard for G7 diamond import compliance. Tracr enables the provision of provenance information from source to sightholder to store on a secure blockchain, underpinning confidence in natural diamonds. If Tracr becomes the de facto compliance standard, De Beers gains a structural regulatory moat: every natural diamond sold in G7 markets must pass through a system De Beers controls.

## 11. Conclusion

De Beers Group faces a strategic situation of genuine complexity — a structural market disruption, a politically embedded ownership transition, and a compressed timeline in which decisions made in 2025–2026 will determine whether the company emerges as a viable luxury provenance business or a declining commodity producer.

The coherent chain of the argument runs as follows: Porter's Five Forces confirm that De Beers cannot survive as a volume natural diamond miner competing against LGDs on price. The BCG matrix confirms that Anglo American's divestment is strategically rational and irreversible. The VRIO analysis reveals that De Beers retains two genuine sources of sustainable competitive advantage — its irreplaceable mining assets and the Tracr provenance platform — which define the only viable strategic path: a luxury differentiation model anchored in "proof of nature." The Ansoff matrix maps the specific moves required to execute this model. And the stakeholder analysis establishes the non-negotiable constraint: any ownership structure must be built around Botswana's sovereign interests.

The recommendations — a Botswana-anchored ownership consortium, full commercialisation of the Tracr provenance premium, an Element Six pivot to industrial applications, supply discipline in the midstream, and a G7 regulatory moat — are mutually reinforcing. Together, they constitute not a retreat from the diamond business but a strategic repositioning of De Beers: from a mining company that sells diamonds to a luxury technology company that certifies and markets the irreplaceable.

With global natural diamond production forecast to decline over the coming decade, the supply scarcity thesis will eventually tighten the natural diamond market — but only if De Beers can stabilise consumer demand among the provenance-conscious segment before the opportunity closes. The strategic imperative is to be positioned to capture that recovery, under stable ownership, with the Tracr infrastructure fully deployed.
