# JAPEX Strategic Case Study: Navigating the Dual Imperative of Growth and Transition

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Japan Petroleum Exploration Co., Ltd. (JAPEX) stands at a pivotal strategic crossroads. Having unveiled an ambitious decade-long Management Plan 2026–2035 that commits ¥1.5 trillion (~$9.5 billion) to quadrupling oil and gas production, JAPEX is simultaneously pursuing Carbon Capture, Utilisation and Storage (CCUS) commercialisation and renewable energy development. This case study examines the strategic coherence of this dual-track approach, identifies the tensions it generates, and develops recommendations grounded in Porter's Five Forces, VRIO analysis, Ansoff's Growth Matrix, and stakeholder theory. The central argument is that JAPEX's domestic infrastructure and CCUS expertise — not overseas E&P scale — are its most durable competitive advantages, and that a sequenced dual-track strategy with explicit governance gates is the most coherent path forward.

---

## 1. COMPANY BACKGROUND AND STRATEGIC SITUATION

JAPEX was founded in 1955 as a government-owned company in order to seek petroleum self-sufficiency for Japan. It is now publicly traded on the Tokyo Stock Exchange (TSE: 1662), and the Government of Japan owns a 34% stake in JAPEX. This quasi-public character shapes both the firm's strategic mandate and its governance constraints.

JAPEX's core activities span oil and natural gas E&P in Japan and overseas; domestic gas pipeline infrastructure; LNG procurement and supply; renewable energy development; and emerging CCUS business. JAPEX conducts the business regarding oil and natural gas E&P business in Japan and abroad, infrastructure and utility business including domestic natural gas supply and renewable energy, and new business development centred on the creation of environmentally friendly businesses that contribute to realising a carbon neutral society.

The strategic situation is defined by three simultaneous pressures. First, **scale inadequacy**: INPEX Corporation stands as JAPEX's primary domestic competitor, boasting a larger market capitalisation and leading Japan's international upstream projects; its financial heft allows for more aggressive investment in large-scale capital projects. Second, **transition ambiguity**: the global energy landscape presents a need for a realistic transition path toward a carbon-neutral society while ensuring a stable energy supply, with renewed awareness of the importance of energy security and oil/natural gas due to rising geopolitical risks, and the goal of net-zero emissions maintained but being fine-tuned. Third, **capital market pressure**: Japan's stock market has been trading at low valuations relative to profit growth for many years, due to its opaque decision-making and corporate actions that sometimes undermine shareholder rights; since 2023, a gradual stock market revaluation has been underway.

The P/B ratio for FY2024 hovered around 0.5 to 0.8x. JAPEX itself identified this as a primary governance and value-creation challenge it needs to address to build confidence in its sustainable growth.

The strategic question this case study addresses is: **Can JAPEX's dual-track strategy — aggressive upstream scale-up combined with CCUS and renewables diversification — be executed coherently, and what must management prioritise to succeed?**

## 2. EXTERNAL ENVIRONMENT ANALYSIS

### 2.1 PESTLE Overview

**Political/Regulatory**: Japan depends on the Middle East for more than 90% of its crude oil imports. This makes energy security a persistent national priority. According to the Japanese government's 7th Strategic Energy Plan, CCUS can support decarbonisation in sectors where electrification is hard to achieve only with renewables, by abating emissions from fossil fuel sources. Crucially, in Japan, the "Long-term Roadmap for CCS" was compiled in 2023, and in 2024, the "Act on Carbon Dioxide Storage Business" (CCS Business Act) was enacted, steadily advancing efforts toward the practical application of CCS. These policy signals are strongly favourable to JAPEX's twin strategy.

**Economic**: Rising interest rates and the cost of capital are identified by JAPEX itself as a key external risk in its Management Plan. The all-equity structure of the Peoria–Verdad deal suggests strong confidence from JAPEX and Peoria in the long-term value of the assets and reflects a financially robust position that avoids taking on new debt in a fluctuating interest rate environment. On demand, oil demand is projected to remain broadly stable at ~100 million bbl/d through 2035, based on the IEA's World Energy Outlook 2025 Stated Policies Scenario (STEPS).

**Social/Environmental**: In 2023, global investment in renewable energy reached an all-time high of $589 billion, a 17% increase from 2022, according to the International Energy Agency, underscoring the strong societal push for decarbonisation. JAPEX's exposure to the Sakhalin 1 project is a reputational risk: the Japanese government, Itochu, JAPEX, Marubeni and INPEX are part of a Japanese consortium that holds a 30% stake in the Sakhalin 1 oil and gas project.

**Technological**: CO₂ to be captured through CCUS is projected to grow from 43 million tons per year in 2024 to 158 million tons per year by 2035 under the IEA STEPS scenario — a 3.7-fold increase that represents a major emerging market. JAPEX's subsurface expertise is directly transferable: JAPEX aims to achieve the early implementation and commercialisation of CCS/CCUS as a pioneer in Japan, making the most of JAPEX strengths accumulated through oil and natural gas E&P in exploring and selecting candidate deep saline aquifers, drilling injection wells, and monitoring stored CO₂.

**Legal**: Operators in the DJ Basin must navigate rules that include a 2,000-foot setback from homes and schools, comprehensive assessments of cumulative environmental and community impacts, and aggressive measures to cut methane emissions. This creates a distinct compliance burden for JAPEX's newly acquired US operations.

### 2.2 Porter's Five Forces Analysis

**Threat of New Entrants — LOW to MODERATE**

Upstream E&P has extraordinarily high capital requirements, long lead times, and significant technical barriers. JAPEX's domestic position is protected by 70 years of subsurface knowledge and established pipeline infrastructure. However, following a surge in deal-making in 2023 and 2024, companies are increasingly looking beyond the hyper-competitive Permian Basin for value, and basins like the DJ, Eagle Ford, and Williston are attracting renewed interest from investors seeking quality assets at more reasonable valuations — suggesting moderate entry pressure in JAPEX's new operating geography.

**Bargaining Power of Buyers — MODERATE to HIGH**

The company's primary customers are local power utilities and city gas operators throughout Japan, positioning JAPEX as a fundamental pillar supporting the country's infrastructure and energy needs. These are large, sophisticated purchasers with alternative import options. Japan's energy market liberalisation has increased buyer leverage, and internationally, oil is a commodity priced by global markets.

**Threat of Substitutes — MODERATE and RISING**

The energy transition creates a structural substitution threat over the medium to long term. However, the IEA's own STEPS scenario shows oil demand remaining near current levels through 2035 and natural gas demand rising — providing a more benign near-term substitution environment than an aggressive transition scenario would imply.

**Competitive Rivalry — HIGH**

JAPEX's scale is modest: its scale is modest compared to global supermajors, but its role is indispensable within Japan, holding an estimated 15% share of domestic gas production. Against INPEX and global majors, JAPEX cannot compete on scale alone and must differentiate through specific geographic or technological niches.

**Five Forces Summary**: The forces analysis reveals that JAPEX's domestic position (low new entrant threat, captive infrastructure relationships) is more defensible than its international position (high rivalry, commodity pricing). This creates an internal logic for the dual strategy: use international scale-up to improve financial returns while using domestic CCUS and infrastructure to build a differentiated, defensible position.

## 3. INTERNAL CAPABILITY ANALYSIS: VRIO FRAMEWORK

The VRIO framework (Barney, 1991) evaluates whether a resource or capability is Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and supported by Organisation — determining whether it constitutes a sustainable competitive advantage.

| Resource / Capability | V | R | I | O | Competitive Implication |
|---|:---:|:---:|:---:|:---:|---|
| 70-year domestic subsurface database | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | **Sustained Advantage** |
| 800+ km domestic gas pipeline network | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | **Sustained Advantage** |
| CCUS technical expertise (subsurface) | ✓ | ✓ | Partially | ✓ | **Temporary Advantage** |
| Government 34% ownership / METI alignment | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | Partially | **Sustained Advantage** |
| US tight oil operator status (DJ Basin) | ✓ | No | No | Building | **Competitive Parity** |
| International E&P experience (Norway, Iraq) | ✓ | No | No | ✓ | **Competitive Parity** |

**Key Insight 1 — Domestic Infrastructure as Core Moat**: JAPEX has built a network of high-pressure gas pipelines, extending a total of 800 kilometres or longer and central to its domestic gas supply network, connecting domestic oil and gas fields and LNG terminals to supply natural gas to clients along the pipelines. This network is genuinely inimitable — it cannot be replicated without decades of investment and regulatory approval. It is JAPEX's most durable competitive advantage and the strategic asset most at risk of being undervalued in a growth narrative focused on overseas E&P.

**Key Insight 2 — CCUS as a Transferable Advantage**: Geothermal power related businesses, which use hot steam and water rising from underground, have much in common with the oil/gas E&P business in that both explore resources underground. The same logic applies to CCUS: JAPEX's subsurface competence transfers directly to CO₂ storage site selection and monitoring. This is partially inimitable because it is embedded in organisational knowledge accumulated over 70 years — but only "partially" because INPEX and other E&P firms share similar geological competences.

**Key Insight 3 — US Operator Status is a Capability Gap**: The acquisition would give JAPEX its first operated stakes in the United States, where it focuses on shale oil. Operating tight oil assets in Colorado is fundamentally different from prior non-operator positions. Cultural integration challenges between Japanese corporate culture and North American operational practices require systematic management to maintain operational effectiveness; JAPEX's prior international experience provides a relevant framework for managing cross-cultural integration, but the operator role requires more intensive local management than passive investment positions.

## 4. STRATEGIC POSITION: ANSOFF MATRIX

Ansoff's Growth Matrix maps JAPEX's strategic moves across existing vs. new markets, and existing vs. new products/services.

**Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets)**

JAPEX continues to develop additional reserves in its mature domestic Japanese fields. This is low-risk but offers limited growth given the maturity of Japan's domestic hydrocarbon basins.

**Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets)**

The DJ Basin acquisition is the clearest example of market development. JAPEX aims to boost its net production from approximately 45,000 barrels of oil equivalent per day (boe/d) in FY2025 to 180,000 boe/d by FY2035, with more than half of the overseas E&P budget — roughly 1.1 trillion yen — flowing to the United States, including about 200 billion yen to develop and expand the newly acquired Verdad assets plus additional acquisitions. This leverages existing technical capabilities but carries operator execution risk in a new geography.

**Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets)**

JAPEX's CCUS business represents product development: new services (CO₂ storage) sold to existing and adjacent Japanese industrial customers. A portion of the offshore area of Tomakomai City, Hokkaido, was designated by the Minister of Economy, Trade and Industry in February 2025 as Japan's first "specified area" under the CCS Business Act. JAPEX applied and received a drilling permit in September 2025, and will proceed with studies and preparations, including exploration drilling, toward the start of operations in 2030. This is the highest-differentiation move in JAPEX's portfolio.

**Diversification (New Products, New Markets)**

Geothermal, offshore wind, biomass, and hydrogen represent diversification. JAPEX's strategy involves diversification into geothermal, offshore wind, and hydrogen driven by government backing, with key legislation including the Hydrogen Society Promotion Act and Offshore Renewable Energy Act amendments of 2024 signalling strong policy support. However, these businesses are nascent and JAPEX's competitive position in renewables is not yet established.

**Ansoff Diagnosis**: JAPEX is simultaneously pursuing all four quadrants of the Ansoff matrix, which risks capital and management dilution. The strategic coherence question is whether the firm has the organisational capacity to execute market development (US E&P scale-up), product development (CCUS commercialisation), and diversification (renewables) concurrently — all within a single decade.

## 5. THE CENTRAL STRATEGIC TENSION: GROWTH VS. TRANSITION

The core tension in JAPEX's strategy can be stated precisely: the Management Plan 2026–2035 commits the majority of its ¥1.5 trillion investment to fossil fuel E&P (¥1.16 trillion for E&P alone), while simultaneously committing to net-zero CO₂ emissions from group operations by 2050. This generates three specific tensions:

**Tension 1 — Capital Allocation**: The company aims to increase oil and gas output to 180,000 boe/d by 2035, up from a current 45,000 boe/d, and to store 1.5–2 MtCO₂/year by 2031, targeting a cumulative total of 8 MtCO₂/year or more by 2035. These CCUS targets are meaningful, but the capital allocated to achieve them must compete with the dominant E&P investment programme. When 77% of growth capital is earmarked for E&P, CCUS is structurally a secondary priority.

**Tension 2 — Stranded Asset Risk**: Global stranded assets as the present value of future lost profits in the upstream oil and gas sector exceed US$1 trillion under plausible changes in expectations about the effects of climate policy. A quadrupling of JAPEX's production base by 2035 substantially increases its exposure to this risk if the energy transition accelerates beyond the IEA STEPS scenario.

**Tension 3 — Legitimacy**: JAPEX's 34% government ownership means its strategy must be defensible to the Japanese public and METI. Japan used diplomatic muscle to push carbon capture in Asia, with the Japan CCUS Summit attended by major domestic and international oil and gas firms as well as government officials from multiple countries — positioning CCUS as a bridge technology rather than a permanent fossil fuel justification. An aggressive E&P expansion narrative risks conflicting with this positioning.

## 7. STRATEGIC OPTIONS EVALUATION

Three broad strategic options are available to JAPEX:

**Option A — Full Commitment to Management Plan 2026–2035 as Stated**

Proceed with the full ¥1.5 trillion investment, quadrupling production to 180,000 boe/d by 2035, while pursuing CCUS and renewables as secondary activities.

*Merits*: Maximises near-term financial returns under the IEA STEPS scenario; improves P/B ratio through earnings growth; aligns with government energy security mandate.

*Risks*: Stranded asset exposure if transition accelerates; operator execution risk in DJ Basin; capital and management bandwidth overstretched across all four Ansoff quadrants simultaneously.

**Option B — Pivot to Transition Leadership**

Redirect the majority of capital to CCUS, renewables, and hydrogen, scaling back E&P ambitions.

*Merits*: Positions JAPEX ahead of transition; reduces stranded asset risk; improves ESG profile.

*Risks*: Abandons JAPEX's core competence; sacrifices near-term earnings needed to fund transition investments; CCUS and renewables are not yet commercially proven at scale for JAPEX.

**Option C — Sequenced Dual-Track with Explicit Milestones (Recommended)**

Execute the E&P scale-up in Phase 1 (FY2026–2030) as planned, using the cash flows generated to fund a materially larger CCUS and renewables programme in Phase 2 (FY2031–2035), with explicit decision gates linked to external energy transition indicators.

*Merits*: Uses E&P cash flows to self-fund the transition; maintains near-term legitimacy with government and capital markets; builds CCUS capability progressively. This is consistent with the Management Plan's own phasing: a period from FY2026 to FY2030 is considered as a phase for building "core assets" by focusing on overseas E&P and CCUS, and a period from FY2031 to FY2035 is regarded as a phase when revenue contributions from the "core assets" will materialise.

*Risks*: Requires disciplined governance to ensure the Phase 2 pivot actually occurs; Phase 1 investments could become stranded if transition accelerates.

**Option C is recommended** because it is most consistent with JAPEX's existing capabilities (VRIO analysis), its stakeholder environment (government energy security mandate + capital market pressure), and the external environment (IEA STEPS demand scenario), while building in the transition optionality that Options A and B sacrifice.

## 8. RECOMMENDATIONS AND ACTION PLAN

### Recommendation 1: Prioritise DJ Basin Operational Excellence Before Further US Acquisitions (FY2026–2028)

**Rationale**: The Verdad acquisition gives JAPEX its first operated US stakes — a capability gap identified in the VRIO analysis. Technology transfer execution risks involve adapting JAPEX's global technical capabilities to DJ Basin operational requirements and regulatory standards; while technology transfer offers potential competitive advantages, implementation challenges could delay expected performance improvements or increase development costs. Committing to further US acquisitions before the Verdad integration is proven would compound this execution risk.

**Action**: Establish a dedicated DJ Basin integration team with North American operational leadership — not seconded Japanese managers — with a formal 24-month integration review gate before committing to additional US acquisitions. Commission an independent operational audit of Verdad assets by FY2027.

**Inferential Step**: The VRIO analysis classifies US operator status as "competitive parity" rather than an advantage. Scaling a capability that is not yet proven risks amplifying losses rather than gains. The integration review gate converts a binary commitment into an option: JAPEX proceeds with further acquisitions only if operational benchmarks confirm that the capability gap is closing. This is consistent with Option C's sequencing logic.

---

### Recommendation 2: Ringfence CCUS Capital Allocation with Binding Commitments (Immediate)

**Rationale**: The tension identified in Section 5 — that E&P receives 77%+ of growth capital while CCUS is secondary — risks CCUS being perpetually deferred. Yet CCUS is JAPEX's most differentiated future capability. In 2024, two domestic projects in which JAPEX is involved — Tomakomai in Hokkaido and Higashi-Niigata in Niigata Prefecture — and one overseas project involving storage in Sarawak, Malaysia, were selected as Advanced CCS Projects, which provide integrated support for the CO₂ value chain. This government selection validates JAPEX's CCUS position but does not guarantee capital will flow to it.

**Action**: Establish a dedicated CCUS business unit with its own P&L, a ring-fenced capital budget of at least ¥150 billion over FY2026–2030, and board-level sponsorship independent of the E&P segment. Set binding interim milestones: commence Tomakomai CCS operations by 2030 as planned; achieve 1 MtCO₂/year storage by 2031. Link executive remuneration to CCUS milestone achievement alongside E&P production targets.

**Inferential Step**: Without institutional separation, CCUS competes for capital against E&P projects that have shorter payback periods and more familiar risk profiles — and will systematically lose. Ring-fencing converts CCUS from an aspiration into an obligation, making the dual-track strategy genuinely dual rather than E&P-dominant. The VRIO analysis warrants this: CCUS expertise is rare and partially inimitable, making it the resource most worth protecting from capital crowding.

---

### Recommendation 3: Monetise and Reinforce Domestic Infrastructure as the Strategic Anchor (FY2026–2030)

**Rationale**: JAPEX's domestic gas pipeline network is identified in the VRIO analysis as a sustained competitive advantage — valuable, rare, inimitable, and well-organised. Yet the Management Plan's growth narrative focuses almost entirely on overseas E&P, risking underinvestment in this moat. Companies that embrace operational rigour, sharpen capital discipline, and reform governance structures will be better positioned to attract long-term capital, boost valuation multiples, and create sustainable shareholder returns. The domestic infrastructure business, with its stable, contracted cash flows, is precisely the kind of asset that supports a premium valuation multiple.

**Action**: Conduct a strategic review of the domestic gas infrastructure business with a view to either (a) creating a separately reported infrastructure segment with transparent returns to demonstrate value to capital markets, or (b) exploring infrastructure monetisation (e.g., a partial sale or joint venture) to release capital for CCUS and overseas E&P. Simultaneously, invest in adapting the pipeline network for future hydrogen or CO₂ transport, since utilisation of existing infrastructure for the transportation and storage of new energy carriers like hydrogen is explicitly identified as a strategic opportunity.

**Inferential Step**: The P/B ratio of 0.5–0.8x implies that capital markets either do not understand or do not trust JAPEX's asset base. Making the domestic infrastructure's value legible — through segment disclosure or partial monetisation — directly addresses this, because infrastructure assets typically command higher valuation multiples than E&P assets due to their stable, regulated cash flows. This recommendation therefore simultaneously addresses the valuation gap (Recommendation 5) and future-proofs the infrastructure for transition uses (consistent with Option C's Phase 2 pivot).

---

### Recommendation 4: Establish Explicit Energy Transition Decision Gates for Phase 2 (FY2029–2030)

**Rationale**: Option C depends on a genuine Phase 2 pivot from E&P scale-up to transition leadership. Without explicit governance mechanisms, the path of least resistance is to continue E&P investment indefinitely, accumulating stranded asset risk. Global stranded assets as the present value of future lost profits in the upstream oil and gas sector exceed US$1 trillion under plausible changes in expectations about the effects of climate policy. A company planning to triple its proved reserves by 2035 must take this risk seriously. The IEA STEPS scenario, on which JAPEX's plan is based, is explicitly not a net-zero scenario — it is a "stated policies" baseline.

**Action**: By FY2029, the Board should commission a formal scenario analysis comparing JAPEX's asset portfolio value under (a) IEA STEPS, (b) IEA Announced Pledges Scenario (APS), and (c) IEA Net Zero Emissions (NZE) scenario. This analysis should inform a binding FY2030 decision gate: if agreed indicators (EV penetration rates, carbon price trajectories, policy announcements) suggest the APS or NZE scenario is materialising, Phase 2 capital allocation should shift materially toward CCUS, hydrogen, and renewables, with E&P investment limited to short-cycle, high-return assets such as DJ Basin infill drilling rather than new long-cycle exploration.

**Inferential Step**: The decision gate operationalises Option C by creating a pre-committed response to external evidence, rather than leaving the Phase 2 pivot to discretionary management judgment. This addresses the legitimacy tension in Section 5: capital markets and environmental NGOs both require evidence that JAPEX's net-zero 2050 commitment is credible. A publicly disclosed decision gate, with observable trigger conditions, is more credible than a qualitative aspiration — and thus directly supports the valuation improvement targeted in Recommendation 5.

---

### Recommendation 5: Address the P/B Valuation Gap Through Improved Capital Discipline and Disclosure (Immediate and Ongoing)

**Rationale**: JAPEX's P/B ratio of 0.5–0.8x is a symptom of capital market scepticism about the company's ability to generate returns above its cost of capital. A P/B value below one indicates a firm is valued at less than the sum of its assets, either because it is undervalued, or because the market has doubts about its future profitability. Incrementalism won't unlock value in Japan; instead, companies must embrace operational rigour, sharpen capital discipline, and reform governance structures. JAPEX conducted a 5-for-1 stock split in October 2024 — a positive liquidity signal — but stock splits do not address underlying return-on-equity concerns.

**Action**: (a) Publish explicit ROE and ROACE targets for each business segment (E&P, Infrastructure, CCUS, Renewables) with annual disclosure of performance against targets. (b) Introduce a formal capital allocation framework with a minimum hurdle rate, and disclose the hurdle rate publicly. (c) Increase the proportion of independent directors on the Board to strengthen governance credibility with international investors. (d) Enhance English-language disclosure of the CCUS and renewables business to attract ESG-focused international capital.

**Inferential Step**: A higher P/B ratio is not merely a financial objective — it is a strategic enabler. A lower cost of equity capital reduces the return threshold that JAPEX's ¥1.5 trillion investment programme must clear, making all other recommendations more financially viable. Moreover, as investors start to acknowledge the essential changes that corporate Japan is prioritising to create shareholder value, transformational change often leads to market inefficiencies — meaning JAPEX has a narrow window in which improved disclosure can generate disproportionate valuation benefits.

## 9. INTEGRATED STRATEGIC LOGIC

The five recommendations form a coherent chain that can be stated explicitly:

1. **Operational excellence in DJ Basin** (Rec. 1) ensures that the E&P scale-up — the primary cash engine of the strategy — actually delivers the projected returns, rather than being eroded by integration failures. *Without this, the financial foundation for all subsequent recommendations collapses.*

2. **Ringfenced CCUS capital** (Rec. 2) ensures that transition investment is not crowded out by E&P, building the differentiated capability that JAPEX's VRIO analysis identifies as its most defensible future position. *Without this, the "dual-track" strategy becomes single-track E&P in practice.*

3. **Domestic infrastructure monetisation** (Rec. 3) unlocks latent value from JAPEX's most durable competitive advantage, providing capital for both E&P and CCUS while improving the P/B ratio. *This recommendation makes the other recommendations more affordable.*

4. **Phase 2 decision gates** (Rec. 4) provide the governance mechanism that makes the dual-track strategy credible rather than contradictory — ensuring that E&P growth does not become an end in itself at the expense of transition positioning. *Without this, Option C degenerates into Option A.*

5. **Valuation and disclosure improvement** (Rec. 5) reduces the cost of capital for the entire programme, making all other recommendations more financially viable. *This is the enabling condition that runs through all other recommendations.*

## 10. CONCLUSION

JAPEX's Management Plan 2026–2035 is strategically ambitious but internally tensioned. The Five Forces analysis reveals that its domestic position is more defensible than its international one, while the VRIO analysis identifies CCUS expertise and domestic infrastructure — not overseas E&P scale — as JAPEX's most durable advantages. The Ansoff analysis exposes the risk of simultaneous pursuit of all four growth quadrants without sufficient sequencing. Stakeholder analysis confirms that the government's energy security mandate provides legitimacy for E&P expansion, but capital markets and environmental stakeholders require credible transition commitments.

The recommended path — sequenced dual-track with explicit governance gates — is not a compromise between growth and transition but a recognition that, for JAPEX specifically, E&P cash flows are the most realistic funding source for the transition investments that will define the company's long-term competitive position. JAPEX has achieved certain results, such as increased profits from continuous investment in U.S. tight oil development and an achievement of profit targets for non-E&P fields with the progress of building assets such as renewable energy and overseas LNG supply infrastructure; however, from the perspective of building business assets that contribute to sustainable growth, the goal has not been fully achieved. This self-diagnosis — published in JAPEX's own Management Plan — is the most honest starting point for the strategic work ahead.

The company that emerges from this decade with proven CCUS operations, a future-proofed domestic infrastructure network, and a demonstrated track record as a US tight oil operator will be far more valuable — and far more resilient — than one that pursued any single dimension of this strategy alone.
