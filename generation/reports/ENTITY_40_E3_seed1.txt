# Vattenfall and Sweden's Energy Trilemma: Strategic Paralysis or Calculated Pivot?

## A Case Study in State-Owned Enterprise Strategy

---

## 1. Introduction and Case Context

This case study examines the strategic situation facing **Vattenfall AB**, Sweden's state-owned energy company, in the period 2023–2025. The central strategic problem is this: Vattenfall is simultaneously being pulled in three directions — by its commercial mandate to generate returns for the Swedish state, by its public-interest mandate to accelerate Sweden's energy transition, and by an increasingly contradictory policy environment that is blocking the investments needed to fulfil both mandates at once. The case is analytically rich because it involves a textbook dual-mandate tension inherent in state-owned enterprises (SOEs), a portfolio allocation dilemma between wind and nuclear, and a structural market failure in grid infrastructure — all of which converge in a single organisation.

The analysis proceeds through five steps: (1) context and stakeholder mapping; (2) external environment analysis using PESTEL; (3) internal capability analysis using the Resource-Based View (RBV) and BCG portfolio matrix; (4) strategic options analysis using Ansoff's Growth Matrix; and (5) a set of sourced, connected recommendations.

---

## 2. Organisational Context

Vattenfall AB is wholly owned by the Swedish state, with its headquarters in Solna, Sweden. It operates as an integrated utility across the full energy value chain — from generation through to retail — in Sweden, Germany, the Netherlands, the United Kingdom, Denmark, and other markets.

Governments establish and maintain SOEs for a multitude of strategic, economic, and social reasons; these entities are typically tasked with a dual mandate — achieving commercial viability while advancing national interests. Vattenfall exemplifies this tension precisely. The company has been transitioning from a base of fossil fuel generating assets for moral, mandate, and monetary reasons. Its stated corporate purpose — "fossil freedom that drives society forward" — is simultaneously a sustainability strategy and a business strategy, meaning the two mandates are, in principle, aligned. In 2024, Vattenfall delivered a stable financial result, advancing its key projects and sustainability goals despite global challenges; the Annual and Sustainability Report for 2024 introduces updated strategic targets for 2030 alongside updated financial targets.

However, this surface alignment conceals a deep structural tension: the commercial logic of where to invest capital is increasingly at odds with the political environment in which Vattenfall must operate. This tension is the strategic situation the case seeks to analyse.

**Financial baseline:** Vattenfall posted revenue of SEK 246bn (€22bn) in 2024 — down 15% compared to 2023 — and profit of SEK 33bn (€3bn, a 3.3× increase). Power generation declined by 1% to 100 TWh, mainly due to lower thermal generation related to asset disposals in Germany and the Netherlands.

**Ownership obligations:** Vattenfall's dividend policy, resolved at an extraordinary general meeting in 2017 and still in force, requires a return on capital employed of 8% and a dividend of 40–70% of profit after tax. This means the Swedish state extracts a significant financial return from Vattenfall, which directly constrains how much capital the company can retain for long-term investments in expensive infrastructure such as offshore wind or new nuclear.

## 3. External Analysis: PESTEL

### 3.1 Political

The Swedish political environment is the single most important external factor shaping Vattenfall's strategic options, and it is deeply ambiguous. On one hand, the Swedish Parliament has set an ambitious target: Sweden's 300 TWh planning target for electricity demand by 2045 requires roughly a doubling of current consumption, driven by the electrification of industry, transport, and heating. On the other hand, the government's 300 TWh planning target for electricity demand by 2045 is not underpinned by concrete planning for the sources of generation, locational needs, and grid infrastructure to support it. This gap between ambition and planning is the root cause of several of Vattenfall's most acute strategic problems.

The political environment is further complicated by the government's approach to offshore wind. In November 2024, the Swedish government rejected 13 applications to build offshore wind farms in the Baltic Sea due to defence concerns, representing a potential capacity of almost 32 GW. Sweden's Defence Minister stated: "In the serious security policy situation Sweden now finds itself in, with war in our immediate area, the defence interest must weigh heavily." This mass rejection signals that offshore wind in the Baltic is effectively off the table for the foreseeable future — a political constraint with direct commercial consequences for Vattenfall's portfolio.

Meanwhile, in the first half of 2024, almost all wind power projects in Sweden were cancelled due to local opposition, with parties in the governing coalition ramping up their rejection of new onshore wind projects. The government's preference is clearly tilting toward nuclear. Vattenfall itself acknowledged: "All fossil-free sources of energy will be needed for Sweden to reach the 300 TWh by 2045. In the short term, wind power is needed to meet the increased needs of industry, but it is an insurance policy for a state to invest in nuclear power." This statement reveals that Vattenfall is navigating a political reality rather than simply an economic one.

### 3.2 Economic

A year characterised by falling electricity prices saw Vattenfall's revenue decline 15%, though profit rose sharply due to capital gains from asset disposals and favourable hedging positions. This distinction between revenue and profit is strategically important: Vattenfall's profitability is increasingly dependent on financial engineering and asset recycling rather than operational generation — a trend that is unsustainable as a long-term business model.

Vattenfall's financial target of 8% return on capital employed creates a hard constraint: in an environment of falling electricity prices, new capital-intensive investments must clear a high hurdle rate. This is precisely why offshore wind projects have become commercially marginal. Vattenfall chose to put the 640 MW Kriegers Flak offshore wind farm on hold until further notice because the investment conditions in Sweden are not feasible at this time.

### 3.3 Social

Under normal circumstances, households and industries in northern Sweden benefit from consistently lower electricity prices thanks to abundant hydropower and limited local demand, while the south, with its higher population and industrial activity, faces steeper bills due to less local production and greater reliance on imports. This disparity is magnified during peak demand or market stress, when southern prices can surge to dramatic levels, at times exceeding northern prices by over a hundredfold. This regional inequality creates a social legitimacy problem for Vattenfall: as the state-owned energy company, it is implicitly expected to resolve a price disparity it did not create but is politically associated with.

### 3.4 Technological

Vattenfall has decided to proceed with American company GE Vernova and British company Rolls-Royce SMR for new nuclear power; both suppliers produce modular reactors considered to have the potential to deliver within a reasonable timeframe and budget, taking into account the conditions of the Värö Peninsula. However, none of the four evaluated SMR reactor types have been built in Europe yet, and would need to be adapted to Swedish conditions to varying degrees; the first reactor built in Sweden will therefore involve learning costs. This technological immaturity introduces execution risk that is not present in wind, where Vattenfall has a proven track record.

### 3.5 Environmental

Sweden's energy mix is 93% carbon-free, with renewables at 70% in 2024. Sweden's 2024 generation mix comprised: hydro 38%, nuclear 29%, wind 23%, biofuels and waste 7%, and solar 2%. This means Sweden is already among the world's cleanest electricity systems, which has an important strategic implication: the marginal environmental benefit of further decarbonisation in Sweden is lower than in most other European markets. Vattenfall's environmental case for domestic investment is therefore partly a political and social one rather than a purely environmental one.

### 3.6 Legal/Regulatory

Sweden lacks long-term clarity around grid build-out and grid connection costs, which Copenhagen Economics (commissioned by the Swedish Agency for Growth Policy Analysis) identified as a key barrier to offshore wind development. The legal dimension of this problem is particularly acute: Sweden's TSO Svenska Kraftnät dismissed a proposal to use congestion income to finance grid access for offshore wind farms, saying it would constitute illegal state aid under EU electricity market regulations. This legal constraint means the most obvious funding mechanism for resolving the grid problem is unavailable, leaving the problem structurally unresolved.

However, there is a recent positive regulatory development: Svenska Kraftnät has been tasked to build up to six grid connection points at sea with a combined capacity of up to 10 GW, and offshore wind developers will not have to pay to link their projects to those grid connection points — a change from the previous model where developers had to build their own transmission lines. This policy shift, if implemented, directly addresses the investment prerequisite that caused Vattenfall to pause Kriegers Flak.

## 4. Internal Analysis: Resource-Based View (RBV) and Portfolio Assessment

### 4.1 Resource-Based View

The RBV framework (Barney, 1991) holds that competitive advantage derives from resources that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN). Vattenfall's VRIN analysis yields the following picture:

**Hydro and Nuclear Assets (VRIN = High).** Vattenfall's Business Area Generation produced 72.6 TWh in 2024 from hydro power plants across the Nordics and Germany, and nuclear power plants in Sweden; nuclear generation totalled 37.9 TWh from a combined installed capacity of 5,500 MW. These assets are highly capital-intensive, geographically fixed, and impossible to replicate quickly — they are therefore genuinely rare and inimitable. They generate stable, dispatchable power that commands premium value in a grid increasingly stressed by intermittent renewables.

**Offshore Wind Development Capability (VRIN = Medium).** Vattenfall generates and sells 4.4 GW of wind energy from 1,200 wind turbines across its key markets. This represents a substantial operational and project-development capability. However, this capability is not inimitable — Ørsted, RWE, and others possess comparable or superior offshore wind expertise. The capability is valuable but not uniquely so.

**State Ownership as a Strategic Resource.** State ownership confers access to patient capital, political relationships, and implicit sovereign backing — resources unavailable to private competitors. Through 2024–2025, the Swedish government reaffirmed full ownership, valuing Vattenfall's equity at roughly SEK 160 billion, using the company as a strategic vehicle for energy security and climate targets while mandating commercially minded operations. This dual use — as both a commercial enterprise and a policy instrument — is itself a VRIN resource, but it is also a source of strategic tension, as analysed below.

### 4.2 BCG Portfolio Matrix

The BCG matrix classifies business units by market growth rate and relative market share, guiding capital allocation decisions. Applied to Vattenfall's portfolio:

| Business Unit | Market Growth | Relative Market Share | BCG Classification |
|---|---|---|---|
| Nordic Hydro & Nuclear | Low–Medium | High | **Cash Cow** |
| Offshore Wind (NW Europe) | High | Medium | **Star / Question Mark** |
| Offshore Wind (Sweden) | High (potential) | Low (blocked) | **Question Mark** |
| New Nuclear (SMR, Sweden) | High (long-term) | Low (pre-revenue) | **Question Mark** |
| Heat Networks (Germany) | Declining | Declining (sold) | **Dog** |

This classification reveals a portfolio imbalance: Vattenfall's primary cash generator (Nordic hydro and nuclear) is a mature, low-growth business. Its growth investments — offshore wind and new nuclear — are both in Question Mark territory, requiring substantial capital without guaranteed returns. The strategic risk is that the Cash Cow's earnings are being extracted via the dividend requirement rather than reinvested into Stars, which would be the BCG prescription for sustaining long-run competitiveness.

Vattenfall's growth investments total SEK 92 billion, with the largest share of SEK 59 billion planned for new wind power; other growth investments relate to EV charging infrastructure, hydro power expansion, and new nuclear development in Sweden. This allocation reflects a deliberate bet on wind as the primary growth engine — yet this bet is being undermined by the political and regulatory environment described in the PESTEL analysis.

## 5. The Core Strategic Problem: The Kriegers Flak Decision as a Microcosm

The September 2024 decision to pause Swedish Kriegers Flak crystallises the entire strategic situation in a single event and therefore merits close analysis.

Vattenfall decided to pause the development of Swedish Kriegers Flak — located about 30 km south of Trelleborg and estimated to generate 2.7 TWh of fossil-free electricity per year — due to unviable investment prerequisites in Sweden. The specific prerequisite cited was a lack of clarity on how the project would connect to the national grid.

**Why does this matter strategically?** First, Kriegers Flak is in SE4, Sweden's southernmost price zone — precisely the zone where spot prices reached 5 kronor per kWh in December 2024, while northern Sweden coasted at 0.02–0.03 kronor, a disparity of over 160×. A 2.7 TWh offshore wind farm in SE4 would directly address the region's structural supply deficit. Pausing it therefore has both commercial and public-interest costs. This demonstrates the first inferential step of the analysis: **the grid connection problem is not merely a technical issue — it is the mechanism by which the political failure to plan grid infrastructure translates into a commercial failure to invest.**

Second, Vattenfall has plans to develop projects that could provide Sweden with 18 TWh of electricity by 2035, but the issues related to the cost and installation of grid interconnects remain a hurdle to the industry. The Kriegers Flak pause is therefore not an isolated event but a signal about the viability of the entire Swedish offshore wind pipeline. This is the second inferential step: **if the grid problem is not resolved, Vattenfall's entire Swedish wind growth strategy — the largest component of its SEK 92bn investment plan — is at risk.**

Third, the pause creates a reputational contradiction: a state-owned company owned by the Swedish government is pausing its most mature offshore wind project because the Swedish government has failed to resolve the grid connection framework. SOEs are tasked with a dual mandate — achieving commercial viability while advancing national interests. When the policy environment makes the commercial and national-interest mandates contradictory rather than complementary, the SOE faces a principal-agent problem: it cannot simultaneously serve both principals (the state as shareholder seeking returns, and the state as policymaker seeking energy security) when those principals are sending conflicting signals. This is the third and most important inferential step: **Vattenfall's strategic problem is not primarily a firm-level problem — it is a governance failure at the level of the Swedish state's relationship with its own energy company.**

## 6. Strategic Options Analysis: Ansoff Matrix

Ansoff's Growth Matrix (1957) categorises growth strategies by whether they involve existing or new products/markets. Applied to Vattenfall's situation:

**Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets):** Vattenfall could focus on extracting more value from its existing hydro and nuclear assets in Sweden and the Nordics. This is the lowest-risk option. The BCG analysis suggests these are Cash Cows — reliable but not growth engines. This strategy would satisfy the dividend mandate but would not address the public-interest mandate to expand fossil-free generation to meet the 300 TWh target. **Verdict: Necessary but insufficient.**

**Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets):** Vattenfall could redirect its offshore wind development capability to markets with better investment conditions — as it has already done with its Nordlicht I and II offshore wind farms in Germany. Vattenfall took final investment decisions for the Nordlicht I and II offshore wind farms in the North Sea, Germany, in 2025. This strategy is commercially rational but politically problematic: it means the state-owned Swedish energy company is investing Swedish capital in German energy infrastructure rather than Swedish. **Verdict: Commercially viable but politically unsustainable as the primary strategy.**

**Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets):** The SMR nuclear programme represents a product development strategy — a new technology (SMRs) deployed in Vattenfall's existing Swedish market. Vattenfall and Industrikraft signed a €36 million investment and collaboration agreement to deploy SMRs at Vattenfall's existing Ringhals nuclear site, involving 17 leading Swedish industrial companies including ABB, SSAB, Saab, and the Volvo Group. This strategy aligns with government preferences and the 300 TWh target. However, none of the four evaluated SMR reactor types have been built in Europe yet, meaning the first Swedish reactor will involve learning costs. **Verdict: Strategically aligned but high-risk and long-dated.**

**Diversification (New Products, New Markets):** Vattenfall has already partially pursued this via its EV charging infrastructure investments. Other growth investments relate to the expansion of charging infrastructure for electric vehicles. This is a logical adjacency given Vattenfall's retail electricity business. **Verdict: Sensible adjacency but not a solution to the core strategic problem.**

The Ansoff analysis reveals that no single quadrant resolves Vattenfall's strategic problem. The optimal strategy is a portfolio approach — but one that requires the Swedish state to resolve the policy contradictions that are currently blocking the most commercially attractive option (Swedish offshore wind).

## 7. The Nuclear–Wind Tension: A Strategic Dilemma

The deepest strategic tension Vattenfall faces is between wind and nuclear investment in Sweden. This is not simply a technology choice — it is a question of what kind of organisation Vattenfall is and who it primarily serves.

The case for prioritising wind is commercial speed and lower capital intensity. The IEA noted that realistic timelines for new nuclear make it a more suitable option for meeting longer-term load needs, while wind is available sooner. Wind projects can be built in 3–5 years; new nuclear takes 10–15+ years. Given that Sweden faces an electricity gap now — evidenced by the SE4 price spikes — wind would deliver faster relief.

The case for nuclear is baseload reliability and political alignment. The current Swedish government actively favours nuclear over wind, and Videberg Kraft has applied for state aid for investment in new nuclear power, with the project company planning approximately 1,500 MW of SMR capacity. State aid would de-risk the investment for Vattenfall, making nuclear commercially viable in a way that offshore wind currently is not.

However, the two technologies are not substitutes — they are complements. Vattenfall itself acknowledged that all fossil-free sources of energy will be needed for Sweden to reach the 300 TWh target by 2045, and that wind is needed in the short term while nuclear is a longer-term insurance policy. The strategic error would be to treat this as a binary choice. The BCG analysis supports this: Vattenfall needs both a near-term Star (offshore wind, once grid issues are resolved) and a long-term Question Mark being developed into a Star (SMR nuclear). Abandoning either would leave a gap in the portfolio.

## 9. Recommendations and Action Plan

The following recommendations are directed at Vattenfall's Board and, where relevant, the Swedish state as owner. They are sequenced by time horizon and grounded in the analysis above.

---

### Recommendation 1: Condition Further Swedish Offshore Wind Investment on Grid Policy Resolution (Immediate, 0–12 months)

**Rationale:** The PESTEL analysis established that the grid connection cost problem is the primary barrier to offshore wind investment in Sweden. The RBV analysis showed that Vattenfall's offshore wind development capability is a valuable but non-unique resource that will atrophy if not deployed. The Ansoff analysis showed that Swedish offshore wind is the most commercially attractive growth option — but only if the investment prerequisites are met.

**Action:** Vattenfall should formally communicate to the Swedish government — as its sole shareholder — that resuming Kriegers Flak and progressing the 18 TWh Swedish offshore pipeline is contingent on three specific policy commitments: (a) Svenska Kraftnät completing its mandated grid connection points at sea within a defined timeline; (b) the government clarifying the cost-allocation model for grid connections in a manner consistent with EU state aid rules; and (c) the government providing conditional project exclusivity to mature projects (such as Kriegers Flak) to protect the development investment already made.

**Warrant:** Svenska Kraftnät has already been tasked to build up to six grid connection points at sea with a combined capacity of up to 10 GW, and offshore wind developers will not have to pay to link their projects to those grid connection points — this policy shift already exists in principle. Vattenfall's action is to hold the government to its own commitment and make its investment conditional on delivery, using its position as the state's own energy company to create accountability. Reduced grid connection costs for offshore wind will not only benefit developers but will also lead to increased security of energy supply, particularly for regions affected by fluctuations in electricity prices due to a lack of production capacity. The public-interest and commercial rationales are therefore aligned.

---

### Recommendation 2: Accelerate the SMR Programme with a Clear State Aid Framework (Short–Medium Term, 1–3 years)

**Rationale:** The Ansoff analysis identified SMR nuclear as a Product Development strategy aligned with government preferences and the 300 TWh target. The BCG analysis classified it as a Question Mark that needs active investment to become a Star. The key risk — identified in the PESTEL technological analysis — is that SMR technology is unproven in Europe, generating learning costs for the first mover.

**Action:** Vattenfall should: (a) finalise the selection of a single SMR supplier by end-2026 as planned, prioritising the technology with the lowest first-of-a-kind risk; (b) secure the state aid application for Videberg Kraft before a final investment decision, ensuring that the financial risk of learning costs is shared with the state; and (c) structure the Industrikraft consortium's 20% stake as a demand-side offtake commitment, converting uncertain future revenue into contracted cash flows that improve project bankability.

**Warrant:** Vattenfall and Industrikraft signed a €36 million investment and collaboration agreement, with Industrikraft involving 17 leading Swedish industrial companies. The consortium structure already exists; the action is to formalise it as an offtake agreement rather than a loose collaboration. An SMR project can include several reactors built in series, allowing lessons to be learned from each reactor constructed, and the selected suppliers offer a relatively lower cost of electricity. The series-build model is the appropriate risk-mitigation mechanism for the learning cost problem.

---

### Recommendation 3: Restructure the Dividend Policy to Enable Long-Cycle Investment (Medium Term, 2–4 years)

**Rationale:** The BCG analysis showed that Vattenfall's Cash Cow earnings (hydro and nuclear) are being extracted via the dividend rather than reinvested into growth. The current policy requires a dividend of 40–70% of profit after tax and a return on capital employed of 8%. In a period when Vattenfall needs to simultaneously fund offshore wind development and new nuclear construction — both multi-billion, multi-decade investments — this extraction rate is strategically inconsistent with the government's own 300 TWh ambition.

**Action:** The Swedish government, in its capacity as sole shareholder, should revise the dividend policy to allow Vattenfall to retain a higher proportion of profits during the investment-intensive period 2025–2035. A tiered policy — for example, 20–30% dividend when capital expenditure exceeds a defined threshold — would preserve state income while enabling the investment programme. The foregone dividend revenue to the state would be partially offset by the avoided cost of energy insecurity in SE4 and the avoided cost of procuring energy transition investments through other vehicles.

**Warrant:** The government's 300 TWh planning target is not underpinned by concrete planning for sources of generation, locational needs, and grid infrastructure. Simultaneously extracting 40–70% of Vattenfall's profits while expecting it to fund the generation and grid investments needed to reach 300 TWh is internally inconsistent. The recommendation resolves this inconsistency by aligning the financial governance of the SOE with the policy objectives of the state.

---

### Recommendation 4: Pursue a Selective Geographic Rebalancing of the Wind Portfolio (Medium Term, 2–5 years)

**Rationale:** The Ansoff Market Development analysis showed that Vattenfall has already demonstrated the viability of redirecting wind investment to markets with better conditions (Germany, Denmark). The risk is that this becomes the default strategy, hollowing out Vattenfall's Swedish investment base.

**Action:** Vattenfall should maintain a deliberate ratio of domestic to international wind investment — for example, targeting 60% of new wind capacity in Sweden and the Nordics once the grid policy conditions are met (per Recommendation 1), and 40% in other European markets. International projects such as Nordlicht should be structured as develop-to-sell or partnership vehicles (as with the Nordlicht 49% stake sale) to recycle capital back into the Swedish pipeline. The Norfolk Boreas divestment to RWE provides the template: Vattenfall divested the 4.2 GW Norfolk Offshore Wind Zone to RWE, stating that the divestment increases its ability to invest in fossil-free projects that better fit its overall portfolio and risk appetite, while securing the future of the Norfolk projects.

**Warrant:** Asset recycling — developing projects to a mature stage and then selling down a stake or divesting entirely — converts illiquid development assets into liquid capital that can be redeployed in Sweden. This strategy satisfies the commercial mandate (generating capital gains) and the public-interest mandate (channelling capital back into the Swedish energy transition) simultaneously.

---

### Recommendation 5: Establish a Formal Mechanism for Resolving Principal-Agent Conflicts (Structural, Ongoing)

**Rationale:** The core analytical finding of this case is that Vattenfall's strategic problem is fundamentally a governance failure: the Swedish state is simultaneously the company's owner (demanding returns), its primary customer market (requiring energy security), and the regulator of the environment in which it invests (creating the grid and permitting barriers that prevent investment). These three roles create conflicting signals that Vattenfall cannot resolve at the firm level.

**Action:** The Swedish government should establish a formal Energy Investment Coordination Council — comprising the Ministry of Enterprise (as Vattenfall's owner), the Ministry of Climate and Enterprise, Svenska Kraftnät, the Swedish Energy Agency, and Vattenfall's Board — with a mandate to align investment prerequisites with investment ambitions on an annual basis. This body would be responsible for ensuring that permitting decisions, grid planning, and dividend policy are mutually consistent.

**Warrant:** Sweden's electricity system will be the cornerstone of its energy transition plans; however, sizeable uncertainties exist in the long-term forecasts, including the scale of industrial electricity demand in the north, offshore wind development in the south, and the outlook for existing and new nuclear power plants. The IEA's 2024 review recommended that Sweden take a more co-ordinated approach to system planning, led by the electricity TSO Svenska Kraftnät. The proposed Council operationalises this IEA recommendation specifically for the Vattenfall-government relationship. Without such a mechanism, the contradictions identified in this case will recur with each new investment decision.

---

## 10. Conclusion

This case study has demonstrated that Vattenfall's strategic situation in 2023–2025 cannot be understood as a conventional corporate strategy problem. It is a problem of institutional design. The dual mandate of SOEs — commercial viability and national interest — is workable when the state's policy environment enables both simultaneously. In Sweden, it currently does not. The government's nuclear preference has led to the blocking of offshore wind permits; the failure to plan grid infrastructure has made Sweden's most mature offshore wind project commercially unviable; and the dividend policy extracts capital that the company needs to fund the very investments the government's own 300 TWh target requires.

The PESTEL, RBV, BCG, and Ansoff analyses converge on a single conclusion: Vattenfall has the capabilities and the pipeline to play a central role in Sweden's energy transition, but it cannot do so without coherent policy support from its owner. The five recommendations above are therefore directed as much at the Swedish state as at Vattenfall's management — because resolving the strategic situation requires reforming the governance relationship between the company and the government that owns it.
