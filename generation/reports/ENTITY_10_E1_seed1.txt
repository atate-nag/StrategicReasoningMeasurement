# COMAC AND THE C919: BREAKING THE DUOPOLY OR GROUNDED BY GEOPOLITICS?
## A Strategic Case Study of China's State-Owned Aircraft Manufacturer

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Commercial Aircraft Corporation of China (COMAC) is China's state-owned commercial aircraft manufacturer, created in 2008 with an explicit mandate to break the Boeing–Airbus duopoly in global aviation. Its flagship product, the C919 narrowbody jet, entered domestic commercial service in May 2023 and has accumulated over 1,000 orders. Yet by 2025, COMAC had delivered just 13–15 aircraft annually—a fraction of its targets—while facing export-control-induced supply chain crises, stalled international certification, and a geopolitical environment that simultaneously creates and constrains its strategic opportunities. This case study analyses COMAC's strategic situation through **Porter's Five Forces**, a **SWOT/TOWS analysis**, the **Resource-Based View (RBV/VRIN)**, and **Ansoff's Growth Matrix**, before presenting a coherent set of strategic recommendations.

---

## PART I — CASE BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### 1.1 Company Overview

COMAC (Commercial Aircraft Corporation of China, 中国商用飞机有限责任公司) was established in 2008 as a state-owned enterprise (SOE) headquartered in Shanghai. Its founding purpose was unambiguous: to develop China's own large commercial aircraft and reduce dependence on Western manufacturers. COMAC's product portfolio spans the C909 (formerly ARJ21) regional jet, the C919 single-aisle narrowbody (150–192 seats), and the forthcoming C929 widebody designed to challenge the Boeing 787 and Airbus A330.

The emergence of the C919 reflects a convergence of economic policy and geopolitics: state-subsidized aviation supporting a broader Chinese strategy. Through loss-leading exports, import substitution, infrastructure partnerships, and strategic positioning, the C919 is as much a geopolitical instrument as a commercial product.

### 1.2 The Strategic Situation

As of March 2025, COMAC had 1,317 firm orders, 155 options, and 185 deliveries. While most deliveries are still C909s, the C919's steady rollout in domestic fleets, including at China Eastern, suggests growing confidence in homegrown aircraft. However, the company has delivered only approximately 13 C919s per year in both 2024 and 2025. China's COMAC faced a challenging year, slashing its 2025 C919 delivery target from 75 units to just 25. Supply chain bottlenecks and geopolitics—specifically regarding the CFM LEAP-1C engines—hampered the ramp-up.

This case study focuses on the central strategic problem: **How can COMAC transition from a state-protected domestic producer to a credible global competitor, while simultaneously resolving its supply chain vulnerability and navigating an increasingly hostile geopolitical environment?**

### 1.3 Industry Context: The Boeing–Airbus Duopoly

At the same time, Boeing and Airbus are constrained by production capabilities, issues, and full order books. Airbus is reportedly sold out of its A320neo slots for the rest of the decade, and Boeing is swamped with quality and delivery setbacks. This could create openings for COMAC, especially if they can offer competitive pricing and attract international buyers.

## PART II — ANALYTICAL FRAMEWORKS

### 2.1 Porter's Five Forces Analysis

Porter's Five Forces framework (Porter, 1980) analyses the structural attractiveness and competitive intensity of an industry. Applied from COMAC's perspective, it illuminates both the formidable barriers that protect incumbents and the emerging structural shifts COMAC can exploit.

---

**Force 1: Threat of New Entrants — LOW (but COMAC *is* the entrant)**

Commercial aircraft manufacturing is one of the world's highest-barrier industries. The capital requirements are measured in tens of billions of dollars. With somewhere between $49 billion and $72.1 billion in state-related support, COMAC has benefitted from far more largesse than the $22 billion the WTO determined Airbus had received over many years from the European Union, Germany, France, and Spain. This means COMAC's entry has only been possible because of extraordinary state backing—which simultaneously makes the threat of *further* new entrants negligible, but also means COMAC's competitive position rests on political will rather than pure market dynamics.

---

**Force 2: Bargaining Power of Suppliers — HIGH and Rising**

COMAC's most acute strategic vulnerability is supplier power. The core issue lies in COMAC's reliance on foreign suppliers for 50–60% of the C919's components. The aircraft depends on Western technology, including CFM International's LEAP-1C turbofan engines, produced by a GE Aerospace and Safran joint venture. In June and July 2025, escalating U.S.–China trade tensions led to a temporary U.S. export halt on these engines, stalling production.

COMAC (Shanghai Pudong) is restructuring its supply chains to mitigate geopolitical risks following the suspension of CFM International LEAP-1C engine exports mid-2025. The manufacturer delivered fifteen C919 jets in 2025—just over half of the revised target of 28, and significantly below the initial target of 75. Production continues to face internal workforce shortages, alongside delays from foreign suppliers.

Despite being manufactured in China, the C919 relies heavily on foreign suppliers for critical components, creating vulnerabilities in its supply chain. The most significant dependency lies in its powerplant: the C919 uses CFM International LEAP-1C engines, produced through a joint venture between American and French firms. As one analyst noted, "a reliance on foreign parts, especially from geopolitical rivals, create long-term vulnerabilities for any state-backed programme."

COMAC has stockpiled some engines and key systems, yet the reality of modern aerospace manufacturing means these buffers cover only months of production rather than years.

---

**Force 3: Bargaining Power of Buyers — MODERATE (domestic) / HIGH (international)**

In the domestic market, COMAC's buyers are predominantly Chinese state-owned airlines. Government policy directs them towards COMAC procurement, and Airbus and Boeing face full order books, constraining buyer power domestically.

In the international market, however, buyer power is substantially higher. Non-Chinese leasing companies and airlines have indicated they would require EASA or FAA validation before considering the aircraft for their fleets. Despite a domestic order backlog exceeding 1,000 aircraft, the C919 has yet to secure firm international orders beyond smaller deals with allies like Brunei and Cambodia. Certification hurdles also present threats. Airlines such as AirAsia, while in advanced discussions about the C919, are negotiating from a position of choice—holding existing Airbus A320 orders and able to redirect demand.

---

**Force 4: Threat of Substitutes — MODERATE**

Within the single-aisle market, the primary substitutes for the C919 are the Airbus A320neo family and the Boeing 737 MAX. The A320neo family, launched in 2016, quickly became the standard in fuel-efficient, single-aisle aircraft, and has outsold the 737 MAX in recent years. Airlines are highly locked into their chosen narrowbody platform due to training, maintenance, and spare parts ecosystems—which actually *reduces* substitution risk once an airline commits to the C919, but *increases* the barrier to initial adoption.

---

**Force 5: Competitive Rivalry — INTENSE**

The Boeing–Airbus duopoly is deeply entrenched. The Chinese manufacturer, backed by significant government support, has been working for over 15 years to establish itself as a credible player in the aerospace sector. The C919, which entered commercial service in May 2023 with China Eastern Airlines, has already secured over 1,000 orders, predominantly from Chinese carriers such as Air China, China Southern, and Hainan Airlines. While this figure pales in comparison to the massive order books of Airbus and Boeing, it signals a strong domestic foundation that COMAC could leverage to expand internationally.

The C919 is China's most ambitious attempt to break into the global aviation market, where demand for commercial planes is booming, particularly in Asia. With its first deliveries already underway, the C919 signals China's intent to reduce reliance on Western manufacturers and assert itself as a major player in the aerospace industry.

### 2.2 SWOT Analysis

The SWOT framework (Andrews, 1971) maps internal strengths and weaknesses against external opportunities and threats, providing the foundation for the TOWS matrix.

**STRENGTHS**

- **Massive state financial backing**: As noted, COMAC has received between $49–72 billion in state-related support, enabling sustained investment in R&D, production capacity, and pricing flexibility.
- **Guaranteed domestic demand**: In January 2025, the aircraft manufacturer stated that it hoped to deliver 30 planes in 2025 as it scales up its production, while in 2025 expanding general production capacity to a volume of up to 50 units per year. The domestic order backlog of over 1,000 aircraft funds learning-curve improvements without market risk.
- **Strategic alignment with national policy**: The development of the CJ-1000A reflects China's strategic imperative to attain self-sufficiency in aero-engine technology. Launched under the "Made in China 2025" framework in 2015, this initiative set explicit targets for 70% domestic content in core aviation components by 2025.
- **Expanding production infrastructure**: To achieve its production goals, COMAC is expanding its facilities, including a second-phase project in Shanghai's Pudong New Area, which will feature a new assembly plant and parts warehouse spanning 330,000 square metres. This infrastructure investment, valued at approximately $1.65 billion, is designed to support mass production and enhance the C919's competitiveness.
- **Growing operational track record**: As of December 2024, all 16 operational C919s had carried more than one million passengers.

**WEAKNESSES**

- **Production ramp-up failure**: In 2024, the company delivered approximately 10–12 C919s, falling well short of earlier targets. For 2025, COMAC announced plans to deliver 30 units with production capacity scaled to 50 aircraft—still a fraction of the 150 annual units the company once projected for its fifth year of production.
- **Absence of international certification**: The C919, which aims to rival Airbus and Boeing's bestselling single-aisle aircraft, began commercial service in China in 2023 after receiving domestic certification in 2022. COMAC had initially hoped to achieve EASA approval by 2025 to support international sales. However, EASA Director Guillermet confirmed that certification within that timeframe is not possible—EASA must still validate the aircraft's design, components, and carry out flight testing.
- **Lagging operational performance**: The C919's current performance averages 5.9 flight hours per day, compared to 8.1 for Boeing's 737 MAX and 8.4 for Airbus's A320neo in China—suggesting a conservative operational start.
- **Technology credibility gap**: Many oversight committees skeptical of COMAC's relatively ambitious goals have brought to light issues over the manufacturing process. According to a representative from the Royal Aeronautical Society, "There's nothing in the ARJ21 or the C919 from China that keeps those planes in the air." However, representatives of COMAC have stated that while this was true early on in development, the manufacturing process is slowly transitioning away from outsourced materials.

**OPPORTUNITIES**

- **Boeing and Airbus production constraints**: As established, both incumbents face full order books and production bottlenecks, creating a structural supply gap.
- **Southeast Asia and Global South expansion**: AirAsia has expressed strong interest in purchasing the COMAC C919 as part of its long-term growth strategy in Southeast Asia. The move signals a deeper focus on expanding regional connectivity between Malaysia and China. Analysts forecast that without swift Western deliveries, COMAC could capture up to 25 percent of the Southeast Asian market.
- **Belt and Road Initiative leverage**: Strategically, Beijing would gain long-term leverage by embedding its aircraft within ASEAN's airline fleets, ensuring dependence not only on aircraft supply but also on training, maintenance, and spare parts logistics tied to Chinese systems. This aviation dependence could carry geopolitical weight.
- **EASA certification progress**: EASA has now entered an advanced stage of the certification process for the C919, with a strong focus on in-flight testing and technical validation. These test flights are part of a structured four-stage approval process required before certification.
- **Domestic engine independence (CJ-1000A)**: To reduce its dependence on the West, China is developing its own CJ-1000A jet engine, which is close to completing certification, but analysts predict mass production won't occur until around 2030.
- **Middle East interest**: Saudi Arabia's aviation regulator signed a preliminary deal with COMAC to explore aircraft assembly in the kingdom, signalling that some emerging markets are open to alternatives to the duopoly.

**THREATS**

- **US export controls and geopolitical weaponisation of supply chains**: The 2025 suspension of LEAP-1C engine exports demonstrated that the US can unilaterally halt COMAC's production. The lifting of US export licence suspensions in July 2025 for the CFM International LEAP-1C and General Electric CF34-10A engines has provided temporary relief for the C919 and C909 supply chains, though dependence on US-made core modules remains a strategic vulnerability.
- **EASA/FAA certification delays**: EASA certification could take up to six years; in April 2025, COMAC is not currently pursuing FAA certification. The geopolitical dimension further complicates matters: the U.S. has blocked FAA certification for the C919, a decision tied to broader tensions over China's industrial policies.
- **Western political and regulatory resistance**: In January 2021, the United States government named COMAC as a company "owned or controlled" by the People's Liberation Army (PLA) and thereby prohibited any American company or individual from investing in it. In January 2025, COMAC was added to a United States Department of Defense list.
- **Risk of WTO disputes**: Distortion of competition between the manufacturers is likely to arise, leading to aggravation within the triangle. Thus, in time, we are likely to see State-to-State disputes over subsidies in aircraft manufacturing brought again.
- **Technological leapfrogging by incumbents**: If Boeing or Airbus accelerates next-generation aircraft development, COMAC—still ramping up C919 production—could find itself competing with an obsolescent product.

### 2.3 TOWS Matrix (Strategic Options from SWOT)

The TOWS matrix (Weihrich, 1982) converts the SWOT analysis into actionable strategic options by systematically combining internal and external factors:

| | **Opportunities (O)** | **Threats (T)** |
|---|---|---|
| **Strengths (S)** | **SO — Aggressive Growth**: Use state financial backing and BRI relationships to aggressively pursue Southeast Asian and Global South orders, leveraging Boeing/Airbus supply constraints. Offer subsidised pricing and financing packages to price-sensitive carriers. | **ST — Defensive Sovereignty**: Accelerate CJ-1000A certification and domestic content increases to reduce supply chain vulnerability. Use state resources to stockpile critical components and develop alternative European suppliers (Safran) to hedge against US controls. |
| **Weaknesses (W)** | **WO — Certification-Led Internationalisation**: Prioritise EASA certification as the gateway to international markets. Invest heavily in MRO network development in Southeast Asia. Use AirAsia negotiations as a flagship deal to demonstrate international viability. | **WT — Damage Limitation**: Set conservative, achievable delivery targets. Communicate transparently with international stakeholders about certification timelines. Avoid overcommitting to international markets before domestic production is stable. |

### 2.4 Resource-Based View (RBV) and VRIN Analysis

The Resource-Based View (Barney, 1991; Wernerfelt, 1984) holds that sustained competitive advantage derives from resources and capabilities that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-Substitutable (VRIN). This framework reveals the gap between the *resources* COMAC possesses and the *capabilities* it needs to develop.

**Valuable, Rare, and Inimitable Resources:**

- *State financial capital and patient capital*: No private competitor can sustain 15+ years of losses at COMAC's scale. This is inimitable in the commercial sense. Only massive government subsidies, forced technology transfer, and guarantees of domestic sales have enabled it.
- *Captive domestic market*: China's 1.4 billion population and rapidly growing middle class represent a uniquely valuable and rare demand base. The combination of COMAC, AECC, AVIC, and state-directed airline demand creates an integrated industrial system that is difficult for foreign actors to replicate.
- *CAAC certification and domestic regulatory alignment*: COMAC's ability to obtain rapid CAAC certification and operate within China's regulatory framework is non-substitutable by foreign competitors seeking to serve Chinese airlines.

**The Critical Capability Gap:**

The RBV analysis reveals that COMAC's current competitive advantage is *domestically bounded*. Its VRIN resources are largely tied to China's political economy and do not translate to international markets. The capabilities COMAC *lacks*—and must develop—are:

1. **International regulatory capability**: The ability to navigate EASA/FAA certification processes, which requires different technical documentation, testing protocols, and institutional relationships than CAAC.
2. **Global MRO and aftermarket capability**: Before exporting its products, COMAC will need to secure overseas airworthiness licences and build out a transnational network of service and repair centres. Finally, despite efforts to wean itself off western technology, COMAC remains highly dependent on imported components. This leaves it vulnerable to geopolitical risk.
3. **Operational performance capability**: The C919's 5.9 daily flight hours versus 8.1–8.4 for competitors suggests operational efficiency gaps that must be closed to satisfy airline economics.
4. **Independent propulsion capability**: The CJ-1000A is key to China's aviation self-sufficiency, especially amid rising export restrictions on LEAP-1C engines. Until the CJ-1000A is certified and in mass production, COMAC's core production capability is held hostage to foreign supplier decisions.

These capability gaps explain why COMAC's strategic position, while strong domestically, remains fragile internationally.

### 2.5 Ansoff's Growth Matrix

Ansoff's (1957) Growth Matrix maps strategic growth options across two dimensions: markets (existing vs. new) and products (existing vs. new). Applied to COMAC, it provides a clear framework for sequencing strategic priorities.

| | **Existing Products (C919/C909)** | **New Products (C919-600 / C929 / CJ-1000A)** |
|---|---|---|
| **Existing Markets (China)** | **Market Penetration**: Increase C919 deliveries to domestic Chinese carriers. Maximise utilisation of the existing order backlog. This is COMAC's immediate priority—demonstrating production reliability before international expansion. | **Product Development**: Develop C919-600 (shortened fuselage variant) and accelerate C929 widebody for the Chinese long-haul market. CJ-1000A engine integration is a critical product development milestone. The certification and use of a high-bypass turbofan engine, the CJ-1000, will also be accelerated, the plan noted. |
| **New Markets (International)** | **Market Development**: Enter Southeast Asian, Middle Eastern, and Global South markets with the existing C919. AirAsia discussions, BRI partnerships, and EASA certification are the key enablers. This is COMAC's medium-term strategic priority. | **Diversification**: With the C909 laying the groundwork, the C919 proving itself in daily operations, and the C929 targeting long-haul markets later this decade, COMAC's climb onto the global stage looks less like a dream and more like an unfolding reality. The C929 is the highest-risk, highest-reward quadrant. |

The Ansoff analysis reveals a clear strategic sequencing logic: **COMAC must first consolidate market penetration in China (stabilise production), then pursue market development in Southeast Asia and the Global South, before attempting diversification into widebody international markets.** Attempting all quadrants simultaneously—as COMAC's current strategy implicitly does—spreads resources too thin and risks credibility in all markets.

## PART III — SYNTHESIS: THE COHERENT STRATEGIC NARRATIVE

The four analytical frameworks converge on a single coherent narrative:

**COMAC is a state-created strategic asset that has successfully achieved its first objective—building a certifiable commercial aircraft—but faces a structural transition problem: it must evolve from a domestically-protected industrial project into a commercially credible international competitor, while simultaneously resolving a supply chain vulnerability that its own state patron has been unable to prevent.**

The Five Forces analysis shows that the industry's high barriers to entry protect COMAC's domestic position but constrain its international expansion (certification, MRO networks, buyer power). The SWOT/TOWS analysis shows that COMAC's greatest strengths (state capital, captive market) are domestically bounded, while its greatest threats (US export controls, certification delays) are externally driven and largely outside its control. The RBV analysis confirms that COMAC's VRIN resources do not yet translate into international competitive advantage—the critical capability gaps (propulsion, certification, MRO) must be closed. The Ansoff matrix prescribes a clear sequencing: stabilise domestic production → develop Southeast Asian markets → then diversify into widebody international markets.

The central strategic tension is **the speed mismatch between geopolitical urgency and commercial readiness**. China's government wants COMAC to internationalise rapidly to assert strategic autonomy and challenge Western aerospace hegemony. But commercial aviation buyers—even in price-sensitive emerging markets—require demonstrated reliability, certification, and aftermarket support before committing to a new aircraft type. "I think in time, COMAC will be a global competitor… but it's going to take them time," Willie Walsh, Director General of the International Air Transport Association, has stated. Rushing internationalisation before these foundations are in place risks reputational damage that could permanently impair COMAC's global ambitions.

## PART IV — RECOMMENDATIONS AND ACTION PLAN

The following five recommendations are derived directly from the analytical synthesis and are organised into three time horizons.

---

### Recommendation 1: Stabilise the Production Engine — "Reliability Before Ambition" *(Immediate: 0–2 years)*

**The Problem**: COMAC has repeatedly missed delivery targets. The setback threatens China's ambitions in the lucrative narrowbody market, which is currently dominated by Airbus and Boeing. This is not merely an operational failure; it is a strategic credibility crisis. International buyers will not commit to an aircraft type whose manufacturer cannot meet its own targets.

**The Recommendation**: COMAC should immediately adopt a conservative, credibility-rebuilding production strategy. Set delivery targets at 80% of demonstrated production capacity rather than aspirational goals. Prioritise quality and consistency over volume. Invest in resolving internal workforce shortages and supply chain coordination failures. As Brian Yang Bo, a veteran aviation consultant, has stated: "For the C919, the key words are output and supply chains. This is a candid admission of the challenges it has faced."

**The Rationale**: The Ansoff matrix's "market penetration" quadrant must be secured before international expansion is viable. The TOWS WT strategy (damage limitation) is the prerequisite for all other strategies.

**KPI**: Achieve 90%+ of stated annual delivery targets for two consecutive years before announcing international expansion milestones.

---

### Recommendation 2: Accelerate Supply Chain Sovereignty — "The CJ-1000A Imperative" *(Immediate to Medium-Term: 0–5 years)*

**The Problem**: The 2025 LEAP-1C export suspension demonstrated that the US government can unilaterally halt COMAC's production. While most of the C919 is made in China, the usage of CFM engines keeps COMAC dependent on the West. As such, China is moving to develop its own powerplant for the aircraft. Shanghai's Aero Engine Corporation of China (AECC) is working on developing the CJ-1000 high-bypass turbofan engine to power the C919.

**The Recommendation**: COMAC and AECC should treat CJ-1000A certification and production ramp-up as the single highest-priority programme in Chinese civil aviation. It set a production strategy: accelerate the CJ-1000A, reduce reliance on Western suppliers across engines, avionics, and flight systems, and expand assembly capacity. In parallel, COMAC should diversify its non-engine component supply base away from US-controlled technologies, leveraging European suppliers—particularly Safran—as a bridge strategy. With U.S. engines off the table, COMAC is pivoting to European suppliers. Safran, a CFM International partner, stands to gain as COMAC seeks to reclassify engines as "European-made" to avoid US-origin restrictions.

**The Rationale**: The RBV analysis identifies independent propulsion capability as the single most important missing VRIN resource. The Five Forces analysis identifies supplier power as COMAC's most acute competitive threat. The TOWS ST strategy (defensive sovereignty) makes this the logical counterpart to international expansion.

**KPI**: CJ-1000A CAAC certification by 2026; first C919 with CJ-1000A in commercial service by 2028; 50% of new C919 deliveries powered by CJ-1000A by 2030.

---

### Recommendation 3: Pursue a "Certification-First" Internationalisation Strategy Focused on EASA *(Medium-Term: 2–5 years)*

**The Problem**: COMAC is not currently pursuing FAA certification, and EASA certification could take up to six years. The C919's delays highlight the fragility of global aviation supply chains and the challenges of building a competitive aircraft in a geopolitically charged environment. While COMAC's long-term ambitions—scaling to 200 annual deliveries by 2029—are ambitious, its short-term viability hinges on geopolitical stability and the ability to navigate international regulatory frameworks.

**The Recommendation**: COMAC should adopt EASA certification as its primary international regulatory priority. EASA Director Guillermet confirmed that "COMAC is putting a lot of resources, commitment and technical means into this process." This commitment must be sustained and increased. COMAC should establish a dedicated EASA liaison team in Europe, engage proactively with EASA's four-stage approval process, and treat every certification milestone as a strategic communications event to rebuild international credibility.

**The Rationale**: The Five Forces analysis shows that buyer power in international markets is high precisely because of certification absence. The TOWS WO strategy (certification-led internationalisation) identifies EASA certification as the key enabler of market development. EASA certification, once achieved, would unlock access to most international markets simultaneously, providing asymmetric strategic leverage.

**KPI**: Complete EASA Stage 2 (design validation) by 2026; achieve EASA type certificate by 2029–2030.

---

### Recommendation 4: Execute a "Beachhead" Market Development Strategy in Southeast Asia and the Global South *(Medium-Term: 2–5 years)*

**The Problem**: COMAC's order book is almost entirely domestic. Yet Boeing and Airbus's production constraints create a genuine window of opportunity in price-sensitive emerging markets.

**The Recommendation**: COMAC should pursue a focused "beachhead" strategy in Southeast Asia and the Global South, targeting markets where: (a) Boeing/Airbus delivery delays create genuine demand gaps; (b) BRI relationships provide political access; and (c) regulatory environments are more flexible than Western markets. AirAsia CEO Tony Fernandes confirmed the carrier is "in active discussions to buy the C919," signalling a potential first overseas order for China's single-aisle jet and underlining COMAC's wider push in Southeast Asia. This deal should be treated as the flagship transaction. COMAC should offer competitive financing packages leveraging state-backed export credit, establish a regional MRO hub in Singapore or Kuala Lumpur, and provide comprehensive training and technical support to reduce airline adoption risk.

**The Rationale**: The Ansoff "market development" quadrant is COMAC's most achievable near-term international growth path. The TOWS SO strategy (aggressive growth using state backing and BRI leverage) identifies Southeast Asia as the primary target. COMAC may create price competition by offering aircraft at lower or subsidised prices, especially in emerging markets or within the Global South. It would force Boeing and Airbus to innovate in how aircraft are marketed.

**KPI**: Secure 50+ firm international orders outside mainland China by 2027; establish first international MRO hub by 2026; achieve first international commercial C919 route by 2026–2027.

---

### Recommendation 5: Manage Geopolitical Risk Through Supply Chain Diversification and Multilateral Engagement *(Long-Term: Ongoing)*

**The Problem**: COMAC's designation as a PLA-linked entity and its addition to the US Department of Defense list in January 2025 creates structural barriers to Western market access and technology partnerships. The risk of further US export controls is real and recurring.

**The Recommendation**: COMAC should pursue a deliberate strategy of **geopolitical risk diversification**: (a) reduce the proportion of US-origin components in the C919 to below 10% within five years; (b) deepen partnerships with European suppliers who have independent interests in maintaining commercial relationships with China; (c) engage actively with EASA and other non-US regulators to build certification relationships that do not depend on US political goodwill; and (d) position the C919 in emerging markets as a symbol of aviation sovereignty for the Global South. The current jet engine trade resumption from the US to China suggests a strategic move forward for both states, where economic benefits seem to transcend geopolitical tensions. It also gives COMAC a further chance at challenging the current duopoly in aircraft manufacturing, as it continues to penetrate the Asian market deeper and works towards certification further afield.

**The Rationale**: The SWOT threat analysis identifies US export controls and geopolitical weaponisation of supply chains as the most dangerous external threats. The TOWS ST strategy (defensive sovereignty) and the RBV capability gap analysis both point to supply chain independence as the prerequisite for strategic autonomy.

**KPI**: US-origin component share below 10% by 2030; at least three non-Chinese international certification relationships (EASA plus two others) by 2028.

## PART V — CONCLUSION

COMAC's strategic situation is one of the most complex and consequential in contemporary global business. It is simultaneously an industrial policy success story (a certifiable commercial aircraft built from scratch in 15 years), a commercial work-in-progress (chronic production underperformance, no international certification), and a geopolitical flashpoint (the embodiment of China's challenge to Western technological supremacy).

The analytical frameworks applied in this case study converge on a clear conclusion: **COMAC's path to global competitiveness is real but requires disciplined sequencing.** The temptation—driven by national prestige and geopolitical urgency—is to attempt everything simultaneously: ramp production, certify internationally, expand globally, and develop indigenous technology all at once. The strategic logic of Ansoff, Porter, and the RBV all counsel against this. The foundations must be built in order: production reliability, then supply chain sovereignty, then certification, then international market development.

The disruption of the Western aviation duopoly is possible. As COMAC strengthens its position with government support and competitive pricing, a potential shift in the industry landscape is expected. The emergence of this new competitor poses challenges to Boeing and Airbus. Whether COMAC seizes this opportunity or squanders it through overreach and geopolitical miscalculation will be one of the defining industrial stories of the 2030s.
