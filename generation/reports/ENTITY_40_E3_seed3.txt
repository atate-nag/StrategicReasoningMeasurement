# **STRATEGIC CASE STUDY: VATTENFALL AB — NAVIGATING THE ENERGY TRILEMMA AS A STATE-OWNED ENTERPRISE**

---

## 1. INTRODUCTION AND CASE OVERVIEW

This case study examines the strategic situation of **Vattenfall AB**, Sweden's wholly state-owned energy company, at the intersection of three simultaneous pressures: a deteriorating economics of offshore wind, a rising imperative to invest in new nuclear capacity, and a structural transformation in Swedish electricity demand. The case is analytically interesting because Vattenfall is not a purely commercial actor—it carries a dual mandate that creates a distinctive version of the principal–agent problem. The analysis applies five frameworks in sequence—**Principal–Agent Theory**, **Porter's Five Forces**, a **Resource-Based View (RBV)**, a **SWOT Analysis**, and the **Ansoff Growth Matrix**—each building on the findings of the last to produce a coherent chain of reasoning that culminates in a strategic action plan.

---

## 2. COMPANY AND CONTEXT

Vattenfall was founded in 1909 as a state agency and has remained 100% in Swedish government ownership from its inception, funded through the national budget with a mandate to develop national infrastructure. Today it is a major European utility. The Swedish energy market is dominated by state-owned Vattenfall for power, and Vattenfall generates 43% of Sweden's power production. Through 2024–2025 the Swedish government reaffirmed full ownership—valuing Vattenfall's equity at roughly 160 billion SEK—using the company as a strategic vehicle for energy security and climate targets while mandating commercially minded operations.

Financially, Vattenfall's net sales decreased by 15% to SEK 245,570 million in 2024, though profit for the period reached SEK 33,380 million, inflated by one-off divestment gains. Its broader investment roadmap includes a SEK 170 billion plan for 2025–2029, with 61 percent allocated to growth and sustainability-focused initiatives, alongside a new SEK 165 billion plan for 2026–2030.

The operating environment is shaped by Sweden's exceptionally clean electricity system. Sweden stands out with an outstanding achievement in electricity generation, deriving the vast majority of its power from low-carbon sources including nuclear and hydro. Carbon-free sources represent 99% of Sweden's power mix, 71% of which comes from renewables. Yet this clean baseline masks acute strategic tensions, as the following analysis demonstrates.

## 3. ANALYTICAL FRAMEWORK 1: PRINCIPAL–AGENT THEORY — THE GOVERNANCE TENSION

**Why this framework first:** Before examining the competitive environment or Vattenfall's resources, it is necessary to understand *who* is directing strategy and whether their interests are aligned. State-owned enterprises (SOEs) face a distinctive governance structure that shapes every subsequent strategic choice.

**The Theory:** Principal–agent problems occur when two parties that are driven by competing self-interest negotiate the terms of a relationship or contract and act together toward a mutually defined end, but an informational asymmetry provides one party (typically the agent) with certain advantages, thus creating tensions. For SOEs, this problem is compounded because there are multiple principals. The varying self-interests in the multi-principal model of SOEs makes it difficult for the agent to act in the interests of both principals when they have potentially competing goals and objectives.

**Application to Vattenfall:** Vattenfall faces at least two distinct principals with partially divergent objectives:

- **The Swedish State as financial owner:** Vattenfall's dividend policy and financial targets are resolved by the general meeting according to the Swedish state's ownership policy; the dividend should amount to 40–70 per cent of profit after tax, and a return on capital employed of 8 per cent is required. This demands commercial discipline.

- **The Swedish State as policy-maker:** The object for the company's activities is to generate a market rate of return by operating a commercial energy business that enables the company to be among the leaders in the energy transition—a social and political mandate that may require investments (e.g., new nuclear, grid expansion) that a purely commercial firm would not undertake given current market signals.

**The Tension:** This dual mandate creates a concrete strategic dilemma. When offshore wind economics deteriorated, a purely commercial firm would have divested and returned capital. Vattenfall did partially divest—in 2024, Vattenfall divested the Norfolk wind power project and 49% of the Nordlicht wind power project, which gave rise to capital gains of SEK 9.7 billion—but it simultaneously pressed ahead with new nuclear investment that requires state risk-sharing, which a commercial firm would not need. A future investment decision will require a reasonable risk-sharing model with the state, which is necessary to lower financing costs and thereby enable a reasonable cost for electricity production that customers are prepared to pay. This is not a market failure; it is the principal–agent tension made visible: the policy-maker principal is subsidising the commercial principal's risk.

**Warrant:** The principal–agent lens reveals that Vattenfall's strategic choices cannot be evaluated purely on commercial NPV grounds. Some decisions are made because the state *as policy-maker* requires them for energy security, even when the state *as owner* would prefer the capital returned as dividends. This insight is foundational: it explains why Vattenfall continues to pursue nuclear even as wind economics collapse, and it frames the governance recommendations in Section 8.

## 4. ANALYTICAL FRAMEWORK 2: PORTER'S FIVE FORCES — THE COMPETITIVE ENVIRONMENT

**Why this framework second:** Having established the governance context, we can now map the industry structure that constrains Vattenfall's commercial options.

### 4.1 Threat of New Entrants — **Moderate to Low**

The Swedish electricity market has high capital barriers. Nuclear reactors in Sweden are owned and operated by Vattenfall (government-owned) and other utilities including Uniper SE (formerly E.ON Sweden) and Fortum Sweden (majority-owned by the Finnish government). New entrants face enormous capital requirements for baseload generation. However, in wind and solar, barriers are lower, and distributed generation is growing. Cumulative solar PV capacity in Sweden reached 4.8 GW in 2024, up from 0.14 GW in 2016. This rapid solar build-out by non-incumbent actors signals that the threat of new entry is rising specifically in intermittent renewables, eroding Vattenfall's pricing power in those segments.

### 4.2 Bargaining Power of Buyers — **Increasing**

The proliferation of variable renewables is creating price pressure that structurally benefits large buyers. From January to May 2024, Sweden experienced 668 hours of negative electricity prices, compared to a total of 310 hours the previous year, mainly due to the strong expansion of solar power generation in Europe. The increase in negative electricity prices is good for consumers, but in the long run it will affect the investment willingness of power producers. This means large industrial buyers—steelmakers, data-centre operators—are gaining leverage to negotiate long-term supply contracts at low prices, squeezing Vattenfall's margins on spot-exposed generation.

### 4.3 Bargaining Power of Suppliers — **High in Offshore Wind**

Although demand for fossil-free electricity is greater than ever, the market for offshore wind power is challenging. Higher inflation and capital costs are affecting the entire energy sector, but the geopolitical situation has made offshore wind and its supply chain particularly vulnerable, with cost increases of up to 40%. This supply-chain concentration gives turbine manufacturers and installation vessels significant pricing power, directly undermining the economics of Vattenfall's offshore wind pipeline.

### 4.4 Threat of Substitutes — **Low for Baseload, Moderate for Heat**

Sweden's near-total decarbonisation of electricity means there are few substitutes for clean baseload power. However, in heat—a segment Vattenfall has now exited—district heating competes with heat pumps, biomass boilers, and geothermal. In December 2023, Vattenfall decided to sell its entire district heating business in Germany to the State of Berlin, and the transaction closed on 2 May 2024. The exit from Berlin heat was partly driven by the substitution threat from alternative heat technologies, reducing the attractiveness of coal-based district heating.

### 4.5 Competitive Rivalry — **Moderate but Intensifying**

Vattenfall's primary competitors include major European energy providers such as Ørsted, Statkraft, and RWE. In the Nordic market, Fortum competes effectively against Vattenfall and Statkraft through its nuclear expertise and hydro assets, providing cost advantages in dispatchable power. The rivalry is intensifying as all major utilities race to secure long-term power purchase agreements with industrial decarbonisers, making the market for clean baseload contracts increasingly competitive.

**Five Forces Synthesis:** The industry structure is most favourable for providers of dispatchable, low-carbon baseload (hydro and nuclear)—where Vattenfall has entrenched positions—and least favourable for offshore wind, where supplier power is high and buyer power is rising. This finding directly informs the resource analysis that follows: Vattenfall's competitive advantage lies in assets that the Five Forces analysis identifies as strategically sheltered.

## 5. ANALYTICAL FRAMEWORK 3: RESOURCE-BASED VIEW — VATTENFALL'S STRATEGIC ASSETS

**Why this framework third:** The Five Forces analysis identifies *where* competitive advantage is structurally possible; the RBV identifies *whether* Vattenfall actually possesses the resources to exploit those positions. Following Barney (1991), sustainable competitive advantage requires resources that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN).

### 5.1 Hydropower Portfolio — Core VRIN Asset

Vattenfall is one of Europe's largest hydro power operators, and new hydro power plants are unlikely to be built in Sweden, meaning the capacity of existing units can only be increased incrementally. This asset is **valuable** (dispatchable, zero-carbon baseload), **rare** (the best Swedish river sites are fully exploited), **inimitable** (regulatory and physical barriers prevent replication), and **non-substitutable** for its specific role in grid balancing. The Five Forces analysis confirms this: hydropower faces low buyer and supplier bargaining power and high entry barriers. The RBV therefore warrants the conclusion that hydro is Vattenfall's most durable competitive advantage and should anchor the portfolio.

### 5.2 Nuclear Operating Expertise

Nuclear power in Sweden includes the Forsmark, Ringhals, and Oskarshamn plants, and Vattenfall operates these government-owned assets. Decades of nuclear operations constitute a tacit, organisational knowledge base that is difficult to replicate quickly. The Värö Peninsula, where the Ringhals nuclear power plant is located, is the place in Sweden that offers the best opportunities for rapid establishment of new nuclear, due to access to grid capacity, existing nuclear expertise, and its designation by the Swedish Energy Agency as a national interest site. The existing expertise is therefore a VRIN resource that lowers the cost and risk of new nuclear development relative to any greenfield competitor.

### 5.3 Industrial Decarbonisation Partnerships — Dynamic Capability

HYBRIT is a joint venture between Swedish power utility Vattenfall, miner LKAB, and steelmaker SSAB. The HYBRIT collaboration said hydrogen production costs could be cut by 25–40% when the first commercial plants are commissioned, and the pilot storage facility demonstrated cost savings of 26–31% in real-time optimisation against spot and intraday power markets. This partnership represents a **dynamic capability**—the ability to reconfigure resources to serve emerging industrial demand—that is difficult for competitors to replicate because it is embedded in multi-party trust relationships and co-developed intellectual property.

### 5.4 Offshore Wind — Weakening Resource Position

Vattenfall's offshore wind portfolio, once a growth engine, has become a liability under current market conditions. Vattenfall's decision to halt its 1.4 GW Norfolk Boreas project in the UK after costs surged by 40% served as a critical market signal that the previous economic model was no longer viable. The resource is **valuable in principle** but currently **not inimitable** (other developers face the same cost structure) and **not competitively sheltered** (high supplier bargaining power erodes returns). The RBV therefore warrants the conclusion that offshore wind is a conditionally valuable resource requiring external conditions (lower supply-chain costs, better market design) to generate sustainable returns.

**RBV Synthesis:** Vattenfall's VRIN resources are concentrated in hydro and nuclear expertise. Its dynamic capability in industrial decarbonisation is an emerging strength. Offshore wind is a contingent asset. These findings directly inform the SWOT and Ansoff analyses below.

## 6. ANALYTICAL FRAMEWORK 4: SWOT ANALYSIS

**Why this framework fourth:** The SWOT integrates the external environment (from the Five Forces) with the internal resource base (from the RBV) into a single strategic snapshot that sets up the Ansoff growth choices.

### Strengths
- **Dominant market position:** Vattenfall generates 43% of Sweden's power production, giving it scale advantages in procurement, grid access, and customer relationships.
- **Fossil-free generation progress:** Vattenfall achieved over 90% fossil-free electricity generation and reduced emissions by 56% across its value chain since 2017.
- **Hydropower moat:** As established in the RBV, hydro is a VRIN asset that competitors cannot replicate.
- **Nuclear expertise at Ringhals:** Existing site, grid connection, and regulatory approvals create a first-mover advantage in new nuclear.

### Weaknesses
- **Offshore wind economics:** The investment prerequisites for offshore wind in Sweden are currently not viable, exposing a gap between the company's stated growth strategy and market reality.
- **Revenue sensitivity to electricity prices:** Net sales decreased by 15% in 2024, driven by falling electricity prices, demonstrating that Vattenfall's earnings are highly exposed to Nordic spot price volatility.
- **Dual mandate tension:** As established in the principal–agent analysis, the government's simultaneous demands for commercial returns and policy delivery create strategic incoherence that can slow decision-making.

### Opportunities
- **Surging electricity demand:** The government's 300 TWh planning target for electricity demand by 2045 implies a near-doubling of current consumption, driven by green steel, hydrogen, and data centres—all of which require clean baseload that Vattenfall is positioned to supply.
- **Data centre boom:** In Sweden, Norway, and Denmark, data centre electricity demand is expected to triple already by 2030. This creates long-term, credit-worthy offtake demand for Vattenfall's clean power.
- **New nuclear policy window:** In 2023, the government amended the legislation on nuclear power to allow new reactors to be built. This removes a decades-old policy barrier that previously foreclosed Vattenfall's nuclear growth option.
- **Industrial decarbonisation:** HYBRIT plans to remove about 8 million tonnes of annual CO2 emissions by 2030—10% of Sweden's total emissions—and Vattenfall is the electricity backbone of this transition, creating a large, captive industrial demand base.

### Threats
- **Negative electricity prices:** The number of hours with negative electricity prices doubled year-on-year in Sweden in 2024, and in the long run this will affect the investment willingness of power producers. Persistent negative pricing erodes the financial case for new generation investment.
- **Grid constraint:** Sweden's power surplus is projected to fall by 65% to 12 TWh by 2030 as demand grows, but the government's 300 TWh planning target is not underpinned by concrete planning for the sources of generation, locational needs, and grid infrastructure. Without grid investment, Vattenfall cannot monetise its northern generation assets in southern markets.
- **Nuclear financing risk:** None of the four evaluated SMR reactors have been built in Europe yet and would therefore need to be adapted to Swedish conditions, meaning the first reactor built in Sweden will involve significant learning costs. This technology risk could jeopardise the financial case for new nuclear.
- **Competitive pressure in renewables:** Ørsted, Statkraft, and private developers are competing for the same wind and solar sites, PPAs, and industrial customers.

**SWOT Synthesis:** The SWOT reveals a company whose greatest strengths (hydro, nuclear expertise, market scale) align with its greatest opportunities (demand growth, new nuclear policy, industrial decarbonisation). Its greatest weaknesses (offshore wind economics, price exposure) align with its greatest threats (negative prices, grid constraints). This alignment suggests a **concentration strategy**—doubling down on VRIN assets while managing the offshore wind portfolio opportunistically—which is precisely what the Ansoff matrix formalises.

## 7. ANALYTICAL FRAMEWORK 5: ANSOFF GROWTH MATRIX — STRATEGIC OPTIONS

**Why this framework fifth:** The Ansoff matrix structures growth choices along two dimensions—existing vs. new markets, existing vs. new products—and provides a risk-calibrated framework for allocating Vattenfall's SEK 170 billion investment plan.

The Ansoff Matrix evaluates the relative attractiveness of growth strategies that leverage both existing and new products and markets, with four quadrants: Market Penetration, Product Development, Market Development, and Diversification.

### Quadrant 1: Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets) — **Priority One**

**Action:** Expand clean baseload supply to existing Swedish and Nordic industrial customers through new nuclear and hydro optimisation.

**Evidence:** Vattenfall's SEK 170 billion investment plan for 2025–2029 includes strategic growth initiatives in hydro power and the development of new nuclear power capacity in Sweden. Videberg Kraft—a majority-owned Vattenfall subsidiary—has applied for state aid to deploy SMRs at Ringhals, backed by the Industrikraft consortium including ABB, SSAB, Saab, and the Volvo Group.

**Warrant:** Market penetration is the lowest-risk Ansoff quadrant because it leverages existing competencies in known markets. The Five Forces analysis showed that dispatchable baseload faces low competitive pressure; the RBV confirmed that nuclear expertise is a VRIN resource; the SWOT identified demand growth as the primary opportunity. Concentrating investment here is therefore strategically coherent. The state risk-sharing model—the financing and risk-sharing model decided by the Swedish Parliament has the potential to provide the necessary framework for a possible investment by Vattenfall—reduces the financing risk identified in the SWOT threat analysis, making this quadrant viable despite the technology uncertainty.

### Quadrant 2: Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets) — **Priority Two**

**Action:** Scale the HYBRIT industrial decarbonisation model into a commercial energy-as-a-service offering for heavy industry.

**Evidence:** The HYBRIT pilot demonstrated that it is technically possible to store fossil-free hydrogen gas for producing fossil-free iron and steel on an industrial scale, with real-time cost savings of 26–31%. SSAB plans to replace its blast furnaces in Sweden and Finland, removing about 8 million tonnes of annual CO2 emissions by 2030.

**Warrant:** Vattenfall already has the customer relationships (SSAB, LKAB, Volvo Group) and the clean electricity asset base. The new "product" is a bundled clean-power-plus-hydrogen-storage service. This leverages the dynamic capability identified in the RBV without requiring entry into unfamiliar markets. The risk is moderate: hydrogen technology is proven at pilot scale but not yet at commercial scale, and up to 100 TWh is expected to come from hydrogen by 2045 in Sweden's net-zero pathway, confirming the long-run market size.

### Quadrant 3: Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets) — **Selective**

**Action:** Selectively pursue offshore wind in markets with better support frameworks (e.g., UK Contracts for Difference, Dutch SDE++ scheme) while pausing in Sweden.

**Evidence:** The investment prerequisites for offshore wind in Sweden are currently not viable; however, should the investment prerequisites improve and the permits remain valid, the project can be resumed. Meanwhile, in the UK, Vattenfall has decided to invest in the Clashindarroch II wind farm, which will supply around 61,000 homes with fossil-free electricity.

**Warrant:** Vattenfall's offshore wind competency is a genuine organisational capability, even if the Swedish market currently cannot support it. Deploying that capability in markets with more favourable regulatory frameworks preserves the option value of the Swedish pipeline while generating returns. This is a market development strategy—same product (offshore wind), new regulatory market (UK, Netherlands). The risk is higher than Quadrant 1 because it requires navigating foreign regulatory environments, but the Five Forces analysis showed that supplier bargaining power is an industry-wide problem, not a Sweden-specific one, so geographic diversification does not fully resolve the cost challenge.

### Quadrant 4: Diversification (New Products, New Markets) — **Avoid for Now**

**Action:** Defer entry into unproven technologies (e.g., floating offshore wind, green hydrogen export) until core strategy is de-risked.

**Warrant:** The SWOT analysis identified multiple threats (negative prices, grid constraints, nuclear financing risk) that must be managed before capital is allocated to diversification. The principal–agent analysis also suggests that the government-as-owner would prefer commercial discipline and dividend returns over speculative diversification. Diversification is the highest-risk Ansoff quadrant and is not warranted given the investment demands of Quadrants 1 and 2.

## 8. INTEGRATED STRATEGIC ASSESSMENT

The five frameworks produce a coherent chain of reasoning:

1. **Principal–agent analysis** reveals that Vattenfall operates under a dual mandate that makes some commercially sub-optimal investments (new nuclear) politically necessary—but also means the state can provide risk-sharing that private firms cannot access.

2. **Porter's Five Forces** shows that the industry structure most rewards providers of dispatchable, low-carbon baseload, and penalises offshore wind developers through high supplier bargaining power.

3. **The RBV** confirms that Vattenfall's VRIN assets (hydro, nuclear expertise) are precisely those that the Five Forces identifies as competitively sheltered, validating concentration in those segments.

4. **The SWOT** integrates these findings and reveals a strategic alignment: Vattenfall's strengths match its opportunities (nuclear + demand growth), and its weaknesses match its threats (offshore wind + negative prices), pointing toward a concentration-and-selective-diversification strategy.

5. **The Ansoff matrix** operationalises that strategy into a prioritised investment hierarchy: nuclear and hydro market penetration first, industrial decarbonisation product development second, selective offshore wind market development third, and diversification deferred.

The central strategic situation is therefore this: **Vattenfall must manage a portfolio in transition—exiting stranded assets (German coal, Swedish Kriegers Flak), consolidating VRIN assets (hydro, nuclear), and building new capabilities (industrial decarbonisation)—while its dual mandate creates both a financing advantage (state risk-sharing for nuclear) and a governance risk (policy demands diverting capital from commercial priorities).**

## 9. RECOMMENDATIONS AND ACTION PLAN

### Recommendation 1: Formalise the Dual Mandate Through a Transparent Investment Charter *(Addresses Principal–Agent Problem)*

**Action:** The Swedish government and Vattenfall's board should publicly codify which investments are commercially motivated (expected to meet the 8% ROCE target) and which are policy-motivated (accepted below-market returns in exchange for state risk-sharing). This would reduce informational asymmetry between the state-as-owner and the state-as-policy-maker.

**Justification:** The principal–agent analysis showed that the dual mandate creates strategic incoherence. The varying self-interests in the multi-principal model of SOEs makes it difficult for the agent to act in the interests of both principals when they have potentially competing goals and objectives. A transparent charter would allow management to allocate capital clearly, allow the board to monitor performance against the correct benchmark for each investment, and allow the public to evaluate whether the policy subsidy (e.g., state risk-sharing for nuclear) delivers social value.

### Recommendation 2: Accelerate New Nuclear at Ringhals Under the State Risk-Sharing Framework *(Addresses Five Forces + RBV + SWOT Opportunity)*

**Action:** Proceed to final supplier selection between GE Vernova and Rolls-Royce SMR, and complete the state aid application for financing support, targeting first operation in the early 2030s.

**Justification:** The Five Forces analysis showed that dispatchable baseload faces low competitive pressure; the RBV confirmed that Ringhals is a VRIN site; the SWOT identified new nuclear policy as a key opportunity; and the Ansoff matrix ranked nuclear market penetration as Priority One. The government has indicated that state support—enabled by legislation effective from August 2025—includes low-cost loans and price guarantees to share costs and risks for up to approximately 5,000 MW of new capacity. This risk-sharing model directly addresses the financing risk identified in the SWOT threat analysis, making the investment viable. The Värö Peninsula offers the best opportunities for rapid establishment due to access to grid capacity, existing nuclear expertise, and its designation by the Swedish Energy Agency as a national interest site—confirming that Vattenfall's existing resource base materially lowers execution risk relative to a greenfield developer.

### Recommendation 3: Commercialise the HYBRIT Model as a Scalable Industrial Energy Partnership *(Addresses RBV Dynamic Capability + Ansoff Product Development)*

**Action:** Convert the HYBRIT pilot into a replicable commercial framework—clean power supply agreements bundled with hydrogen storage optimisation—offered to other heavy industrial customers beyond SSAB and LKAB.

**Justification:** The RBV identified HYBRIT as a dynamic capability embedded in trust relationships and co-developed IP. The HYBRIT pilot demonstrated hydrogen production cost reductions of 25–40% at commercial scale, and Sweden's data centre market is anticipated to soar from US$2 billion in 2024 to US$3.8 billion by 2030, creating additional industrial customers who will need guaranteed clean power. Extending the HYBRIT model captures the Ansoff Product Development opportunity without requiring entry into unfamiliar markets, and it generates long-term, price-stable revenue that reduces the spot-price exposure identified as a key weakness in the SWOT.

### Recommendation 4: Suspend Swedish Offshore Wind Until Market Design Improves; Redeploy Capital to Grid Infrastructure *(Addresses Five Forces Supplier Power + SWOT Threat)*

**Action:** Maintain the pause on Swedish Kriegers Flak; redirect freed capital toward Swedish grid investment (SEK 47 billion planned over five years) to unlock the value of northern hydropower for southern markets.

**Justification:** The Five Forces analysis showed that offshore wind faces high supplier bargaining power and rising buyer power. The investment prerequisites for offshore wind in Sweden are currently not viable, and Vattenfall has therefore decided to pause all further development of the project. Rather than forcing capital into a structurally challenged segment, grid investment addresses the SWOT threat of grid constraints: Sweden's power surplus is projected to fall by 65% to 12 TWh by 2030, and grid bottlenecks prevent northern clean generation from reaching southern demand centres. Resolving this constraint monetises existing VRIN hydro assets—the RBV's highest-priority resource—at lower risk than building new wind capacity.

### Recommendation 5: Establish Long-Term Power Purchase Agreements with Data Centres and Industrial Decarbonisers *(Addresses SWOT Opportunity + Buyer Bargaining Power)*

**Action:** Proactively negotiate 15–20 year PPAs with data-centre operators and green steel producers at price levels that cover the cost of new nuclear and hydro optimisation, reducing spot-price exposure.

**Justification:** The Five Forces analysis identified rising buyer bargaining power as a threat due to negative electricity prices. However, data centre electricity demand in Sweden is expected to triple by 2030, and these customers—unlike spot traders—have strong incentives to lock in long-term clean power supply for ESG compliance and operational certainty. Long-term PPAs convert buyer bargaining power from a threat into a revenue-stabilising mechanism. They also provide the revenue certainty that makes the nuclear and hydro investments in Recommendations 2 and 3 financeable at lower cost of capital, directly addressing the financing risk identified in the principal–agent and SWOT analyses.

## 11. CONCLUSION

Vattenfall stands at a strategic inflection point. Its legacy fossil assets are divested; its offshore wind ambitions are constrained by supply-chain economics and market design failures; and its most durable competitive advantages—hydropower and nuclear expertise—are precisely the assets that Sweden's forthcoming demand surge will most reward. The principal–agent tension inherent in state ownership is not merely a governance curiosity: it is the mechanism through which Vattenfall can access state risk-sharing for nuclear investment that no private competitor can replicate. Managed transparently, this is a competitive advantage. Managed opaquely, it is a source of strategic drift.

The recommended action plan—formalise the dual mandate, accelerate nuclear at Ringhals, commercialise HYBRIT, redirect wind capital to grid investment, and lock in long-term industrial PPAs—forms a coherent, sequenced programme that builds on Vattenfall's VRIN resources, addresses its principal–agent governance challenge, and positions it to be the backbone of Sweden's electrified, net-zero economy by 2045.
