# AbbVie Inc.: Strategic Case Study
## *Managing the Post-Humira Transition — From Cliff to Diversified Growth Platform*

---

## 1. Introduction and Strategic Situation

AbbVie Inc. is one of the world's largest biopharmaceutical companies, with operations spanning immunology, oncology, neuroscience, and aesthetics. Spun off from Abbott Laboratories in 2013, AbbVie built its early identity around a single molecule: Humira (adalimumab), a TNF-inhibitor that became the world's best-selling drug. In 2022, Humira accounted for around 37% of AbbVie's sales. That concentration created a strategic vulnerability of the first order: when U.S. patent exclusivity on Humira expired in January 2023, AbbVie faced the largest single-product patent cliff in pharmaceutical industry history.

The central strategic question this case examines is: **How has AbbVie managed the transition away from Humira dependence, and what further strategic actions are required to sustain long-run competitive advantage?** The analysis proceeds through four frameworks — Porter's Five Forces, the Resource-Based View (RBV), Ansoff's Growth Matrix, and a SWOT synthesis — before concluding with a structured set of recommendations.

---

## 2. Context: The Scale of the Humira Cliff

To understand the magnitude of the strategic challenge, the financial data must be placed in sequence. AbbVie delivered full-year net revenues of $54.318 billion in 2023, a decrease of 6.4 percent on a reported basis, due to Humira biosimilar competition; full-year global Humira net revenues were $14.404 billion. This represented a dramatic fall from Humira's U.S. peak: in January 2023, Amgen announced the launch of the first Humira biosimilar in the United States, called Amjevita; after Amgen, several other companies including Boehringer Ingelheim, Coherus BioSciences, and Novartis also launched their own Humira biosimilars, and at that point eight biosimilar versions of Humira had been launched in the United States.

The erosion accelerated sharply. In the fourth quarter of 2024, global Humira net revenues of $1.682 billion decreased 49.1 percent on a reported basis, with U.S. Humira net revenues decreasing 54.5 percent. By the full year 2024, AbbVie delivered full-year net revenues of $56.334 billion, an increase of 3.7 percent on a reported basis; global Humira net revenues were $8.993 billion, while global Skyrizi net revenues were $11.718 billion and global Rinvoq net revenues were $5.971 billion.

This data establishes a critical analytical anchor: by 2024, Skyrizi alone had surpassed Humira in revenue, confirming that AbbVie's successor strategy was beginning to succeed — but the analysis must examine *why*, *at what risk*, and *whether the strategy is sufficient*.

## 3. Porter's Five Forces Analysis

Porter's Five Forces framework (Porter, 1980) identifies the structural determinants of industry profitability. Applied to AbbVie's competitive environment, the framework reveals a sector with persistently high profitability but rising structural pressures on incumbents.

### 3.1 Threat of New Entrants — Moderate but Rising

Biopharmaceutical R&D barriers (capital intensity, regulatory timelines, clinical trial failure rates) historically suppressed new entrants. However, the biosimilar regulatory pathway created by the Biologics Price Competition and Innovation Act (BPCIA) has materially lowered barriers for *follow-on biologics*. AbbVie has used secondary and tertiary patents, filing a total of 247 patent applications on Humira in the U.S., 89% of which were filed after the drug was first approved and on the market. This "evergreening" strategy delayed biosimilar entry but could not prevent it indefinitely — and when entry arrived, it arrived in force. The implication for AbbVie is that any future blockbuster faces the same structural vulnerability unless protected by more durable barriers (clinical differentiation, indication breadth, patient switching costs).

### 3.2 Bargaining Power of Buyers — High and Increasing

In the U.S. pharmaceutical market, buyers include pharmacy benefit managers (PBMs), hospital systems, and, increasingly, the federal government. Negotiated price reductions under the Inflation Reduction Act's Medicare Drug Price Negotiation Program included reducing Imbruvica from $14,934 to $9,319 per 30-day supply; those first-round prices will go into effect on January 1, 2026. This is structurally significant: Imbruvica was one of AbbVie's top oncology earners, and the IRA's price negotiation mechanism directly compresses margins on high-volume Medicare drugs. The mechanism is expanding: CMS announced 15 drugs for the second round of negotiations on January 17, 2025, and announced on March 14, 2025 that all manufacturers had agreed to participate; together, the 15 drugs accounted for $40.7 billion in gross covered prescription drug costs under Part D. Buyer power, particularly governmental buyer power, is therefore a structural headwind that will intensify as more AbbVie products become eligible for negotiation.

### 3.3 Bargaining Power of Suppliers — Low

Active pharmaceutical ingredient (API) suppliers and contract manufacturers are numerous and competitive. AbbVie's manufacturing scale and long-term supplier relationships further limit supplier leverage. This force is not a material strategic concern.

### 3.4 Threat of Substitutes — High in Oncology; Moderate in Immunology

In oncology, AbbVie faces direct therapeutic substitution. Sales of AbbVie's top-selling cancer medicine Imbruvica fell by nearly a fifth in the second quarter as the leukemia drug continues to cede ground to a competing treatment from AstraZeneca; between April and June, Imbruvica earned just over $1.1 billion, a decline of 17% from the same period last year. AstraZeneca's Calquence (acalabrutinib), a next-generation BTK inhibitor with a superior safety profile, is the primary substitute. In immunology, Skyrizi faces competition from J&J's Stelara (ustekinumab), but head-to-head evidence favours AbbVie's product: in the Phase III SEQUENCE trial comparing Skyrizi against Stelara in Crohn's disease, on the second primary endpoint of endoscopic remission at week 48, Skyrizi demonstrated superiority to Stelara, with remission rates of 32% and 16% respectively; all secondary endpoints achieved statistical significance for superiority. Clinical superiority data reduces the substitution threat in immunology — but it must be continuously regenerated through ongoing investment.

### 3.5 Competitive Rivalry — Intense

The immunology and oncology markets are characterised by oligopolistic rivalry among a small number of well-capitalised firms (J&J, Pfizer, AstraZeneca, Eli Lilly, Roche). Rinvoq carries a black-box warning — the FDA's most serious — cautioning patients of an increased risk of serious infections, malignancy, and thrombosis. This class-wide JAK inhibitor warning constrains Rinvoq's prescribing universe and hands rivals with alternative mechanisms of action a competitive opening. Rivalry is thus not only about price but about differentiated clinical profiles and safety labelling.

**Five Forces Summary:** The overall structural assessment is that AbbVie operates in a moderately attractive industry with high entry barriers in novel therapeutics but rising governmental buyer power and intense rivalry. The key strategic implication is that AbbVie cannot rely on market structure alone to protect margins; it must continuously build product-level advantages.

## 4. Resource-Based View Analysis

The RBV (Barney, 1991) holds that sustained competitive advantage derives from resources and capabilities that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN). This section assesses AbbVie's key resource endowments.

### 4.1 Immunology R&D and Clinical Expertise (VRIN — Strong)

AbbVie's core competency is its deep expertise in immunology drug development, built over decades. The Skyrizi and Rinvoq programmes demonstrate this capability: AbbVie reaffirms its expectations for a high single-digit compound annual revenue growth rate through 2029; the company now expects combined Skyrizi and Rinvoq 2027 revenues of more than $31 billion, an increase of approximately $4 billion compared to previous guidance for combined revenues of more than $27 billion in 2027, with Skyrizi revenues of more than $20 billion and Rinvoq revenues of more than $11 billion in 2027. This upward revision — the third such increase in two years — reflects genuine competitive advantage: AbbVie has successfully expanded both drugs into new indications (IBD, Crohn's disease, ulcerative colitis) that are large markets previously dominated by Humira and Stelara. The ability to run multi-indication development programmes simultaneously is a rare organisational capability.

### 4.2 Commercial Infrastructure and Market Access (Valuable, Partially Imitable)

AbbVie's global commercial organisation — built to support Humira's multi-indication franchise — represents a significant asset. The same infrastructure that sold Humira to rheumatologists, dermatologists, and gastroenterologists now deploys Skyrizi and Rinvoq to the same prescriber base. This reduces commercial launch costs and accelerates market penetration. The evidence: in Q2 2025, global Skyrizi net revenues were $4.423 billion, an increase of 62.2 percent; global Rinvoq net revenues were $2.028 billion. Growth at this rate for drugs already generating multi-billion quarterly revenues is unusual and reflects the power of established commercial channels.

### 4.3 Patent Portfolio and Exclusivity Management (Valuable, Imitable Over Time)

AbbVie has actively managed exclusivity timelines. AbbVie has secured extended patent protection for Rinvoq until at least April 2037; Wells Fargo analyst Mohit Bansal described the settlement as a "big win" for AbbVie, noting that consensus estimates had previously modelled patent expiry in 2032. Similarly, the basic Skyrizi patent remains valid until 2033, which secures market extension for AbbVie. These extended exclusivity windows are strategically critical: they ensure that the Skyrizi/Rinvoq revenue peak can be fully realised before generic competition arrives, providing a long runway for the next generation of assets to mature.

### 4.4 Diversified Therapeutic Portfolio (Valuable, Increasingly Rare)

The 2020 Allergan acquisition and the 2024 acquisitions of ImmunoGen and Cerevel Therapeutics reflect a deliberate portfolio diversification strategy. The Allergan acquisition creates a biopharmaceutical company with leadership positions in key therapeutic areas: Immunology, Hematologic Oncology, Neuroscience, and Allergan Aesthetics, with a robust portfolio of on-market and pipeline assets well-positioned for sustainable long-term growth. The neuroscience portfolio now generates substantial revenue: full-year 2024 global net revenues from the neuroscience portfolio were $8.999 billion, an increase of 16.6 percent, with Botox Therapeutic revenues of $3.283 billion and Vraylar revenues of $3.267 billion. Portfolio breadth reduces dependence on any single franchise — the Humira lesson internalised.

### 4.5 Pipeline Risk: The Cerevel/Emraclidine Failure (Resource Weakness)

The RBV must also account for resource failures. AbbVie announced that emraclidine, which it acquired through its $9 billion acquisition of Cerevel Therapeutics, failed to significantly help patients in two Phase 2 trials; patients on different doses of the drug did not experience significant improvements on the Positive and Negative Syndrome Scale (PANSS) compared with the placebo group. This failure is strategically significant for three reasons: (1) it eliminates a key neuroscience pipeline asset that was intended to extend the franchise; (2) it raises questions about the due diligence rigour of AbbVie's M&A process; and (3) it creates a gap in the neuroscience pipeline that must be filled. The $9 billion acquisition price makes this one of the costliest clinical failures in recent pharmaceutical history.

## 5. Ansoff Matrix Analysis

Ansoff's Growth Matrix (1957) categorises growth strategies along two dimensions: existing vs. new products, and existing vs. new markets. AbbVie's recent strategic moves span all four quadrants.

### 5.1 Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets)

AbbVie's primary near-term growth engine is deepening penetration of Skyrizi and Rinvoq in their approved indications. The sequential revenue growth — in Q3 2025, global Skyrizi net revenues were $4.708 billion, an increase of 46.8 percent, and global Rinvoq net revenues were $2.184 billion, an increase of 35.3 percent — confirms that penetration in rheumatology, dermatology, and gastroenterology is still far from saturation. This strategy is low-risk (no new regulatory approvals required) and high-return (leverages existing commercial infrastructure).

### 5.2 Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets)

AbbVie has pursued geographic expansion, particularly for Rinvoq and Skyrizi in international markets where Humira biosimilar erosion has been ongoing for longer. The international/U.S. revenue split for Skyrizi (Q3 2025: $4.085 billion U.S., $623 million international) suggests significant headroom for international market development, particularly in emerging markets where biologic penetration remains low.

### 5.3 Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets)

This quadrant is the most strategically important for long-term sustainability. AbbVie completed the acquisition of ImmunoGen, adding its flagship antibody-drug conjugate (ADC) ELAHERE (mirvetuximab soravtansine-gynx) for folate receptor-alpha positive platinum-resistant ovarian cancer to AbbVie's portfolio; ImmunoGen's pipeline complements AbbVie's existing oncology pipeline, with potential to be transformative across multiple solid tumors and hematologic malignancies. The ADC platform represents a genuinely new product modality — one of the fastest-growing segments in oncology — which AbbVie did not previously possess. This is a strategically sound move: it extends AbbVie's oncology presence into a high-growth technology class while building on existing oncology commercial relationships.

### 5.4 Diversification (New Products, New Markets)

The Allergan acquisition in 2020 was AbbVie's most significant diversification move, entering the medical aesthetics market. However, full-year 2024 global net revenues from the aesthetics portfolio were $5.176 billion, a decrease of 2.2 percent on a reported basis; global Botox Cosmetic net revenues were $2.720 billion and global Juvederm net revenues were $1.177 billion. The aesthetics portfolio is underperforming, with Juvederm particularly weak due to Chinese market softness and rising competition. In Q2 2025, global net revenues from the aesthetics portfolio were $1.279 billion, a decrease of 8.1 percent. This suggests that the diversification into aesthetics, while strategically logical as a non-patent-cliff business, has not yet delivered the growth expected.

## 7. Strategic Coherence Assessment

Before moving to recommendations, it is important to assess whether AbbVie's current strategy is internally coherent. The strategy can be characterised as **"managed transition through platform diversification"**: using the cash flows generated by Humira's residual revenues and the rapidly growing Skyrizi/Rinvoq franchise to fund acquisitions that build new platforms (ADCs in oncology; neuroscience assets) while the immunology franchise matures.

The logic is sound: the RBV analysis shows that AbbVie's deepest competency is immunology, and Skyrizi/Rinvoq represent the fullest expression of that competency. The Ansoff analysis shows that market penetration and product development (new indications) in immunology are the highest-return, lowest-risk growth vectors. The diversification into aesthetics, neuroscience, and oncology reduces concentration risk but introduces execution risk.

The key strategic tension is between **depth** (doubling down on immunology, where AbbVie has VRIN capabilities) and **breadth** (diversifying into oncology and neuroscience, where AbbVie's capabilities are less established and acquisition-based). The emraclidine failure is a warning signal: acquiring late-stage assets in therapeutic areas where internal scientific expertise is limited increases the risk of paying for assets whose clinical value cannot be independently assessed.

## 8. Recommendations

The following recommendations are derived directly from the analytical chain above. Each addresses a specific strategic gap identified by the frameworks.

### Recommendation 1: Maximise Skyrizi's IBD Opportunity Aggressively

**Warrant:** The Five Forces analysis identifies Skyrizi's clinical superiority over Stelara in Crohn's disease as the most durable competitive advantage AbbVie currently possesses. The RBV analysis confirms that immunology is AbbVie's core VRIN capability. The Ansoff analysis classifies this as market penetration — the lowest-risk, highest-return growth strategy. Skyrizi demonstrated superiority to Stelara on the second primary endpoint of endoscopic remission at week 48, with remission rates of 32% versus 16%, and all secondary endpoints achieved statistical significance for superiority. This head-to-head data provides the clinical foundation for aggressive market share capture in the large IBD market, where Stelara's patent expiry (expected around 2025) will create switching opportunities. **Action:** Prioritise IBD commercial investment, including gastroenterologist education programmes and payer access negotiations that leverage the superiority data. Target Stelara patients switching at biosimilar entry.

### Recommendation 2: Rebuild the Neuroscience Pipeline Through Disciplined, Internally-Led Discovery

**Warrant:** The emraclidine failure demonstrates the risk of late-stage acquisition in areas where internal scientific expertise is limited. The RBV analysis shows that AbbVie's VRIN capabilities are in immunology, not neuroscience. Yet the neuroscience portfolio is strategically important: Vraylar's $3.267 billion in 2024 revenue and 18.5% growth rate confirm commercial momentum in psychiatry. The gap left by emraclidine must be filled, but the fill strategy should change. **Action:** Rather than pursuing another large late-stage neuroscience acquisition, AbbVie should (a) increase early-stage neuroscience research partnerships and option-to-acquire deals (as it has already begun doing with EvolveImmune Therapeutics in oncology), (b) leverage the remaining Cerevel pipeline assets (e.g., CVL-871 in dementia-related apathy), and (c) build internal neuroscience discovery capabilities through targeted hiring and academic partnerships. This reduces binary acquisition risk while maintaining pipeline optionality.

### Recommendation 3: Develop a Proactive IRA Response Strategy for At-Risk Assets

**Warrant:** The Five Forces analysis identifies rising governmental buyer power as a structural threat. Imbruvica's price reduction from $14,934 to $9,319 per 30-day supply under the IRA is already confirmed. As Skyrizi and Rinvoq grow to become among the highest-spending Medicare Part D drugs, they will become eligible for negotiation in future rounds. This is not speculative — it is mechanistic under the IRA's statutory criteria. **Action:** AbbVie should (a) model the IRA impact on Skyrizi and Rinvoq under different negotiation scenarios and incorporate these into long-range financial planning; (b) accelerate indication expansion for both drugs, since additional approved indications can influence the IRA's "therapeutic alternatives" analysis and potentially moderate negotiated price reductions; (c) engage proactively with CMS on the negotiation process rather than litigating. The objective is to manage, not eliminate, the IRA impact.

### Recommendation 4: Rationalise or Accelerate the Aesthetics Portfolio Decision

**Warrant:** The Ansoff analysis shows aesthetics as a diversification quadrant — the highest-risk, lowest-synergy growth strategy relative to AbbVie's core capabilities. In Q3 2025, global net revenues from the aesthetics portfolio were $1.193 billion, a decrease of 3.7 percent, with Juvederm net revenues of $253 million. Juvederm is in structural decline in China, and Botox Cosmetic faces intensifying competition from emerging toxins (e.g., Revance's DAXXIFY). The aesthetics business does not benefit from AbbVie's immunology R&D capabilities, does not face patent cliffs in the same way, and has different commercial channels. **Action:** AbbVie should conduct a strategic review of the aesthetics portfolio with two possible outcomes: (a) if the business can be repositioned for high single-digit growth (as management has guided), invest in Botox Cosmetic market share defence and Juvederm China recovery; or (b) if the growth trajectory cannot be credibly improved within two years, consider a partial divestiture or spin-off of the aesthetics business to unlock capital and management focus for the core pharmaceutical pipeline. The decision criterion should be whether aesthetics generates returns above AbbVie's weighted average cost of capital on a risk-adjusted basis.

### Recommendation 5: Establish a Second-Generation Immunology Pipeline to Prevent a Repeat of the Humira Cliff

**Warrant:** The single most important lesson from the Humira patent cliff is the danger of revenue concentration in a single asset. Skyrizi and Rinvoq currently dominate AbbVie's immunology portfolio and are projected to generate $31 billion combined by 2027. Their patents run to 2033 and 2037 respectively. This provides a window of approximately 8–12 years to develop the next generation of immunology assets. The RBV analysis confirms that immunology is where AbbVie's VRIN capabilities are strongest — making this the highest-probability area for successful internal discovery. **Action:** Increase R&D investment in next-generation immunology mechanisms (e.g., TYK2 inhibitors, IL-17 pathways, CAR-T for autoimmune disease). Establish a formal "Skyrizi successor" programme with defined milestones and a target of at least one Phase 2 asset in a major immunology indication by 2028. This is not merely defensive — it is the foundation of AbbVie's long-term competitive position.

## 9. Conclusion

AbbVie's strategic situation in 2025 is one of successful transition but incomplete transformation. The company has navigated the Humira patent cliff more effectively than most analysts expected: AbbVie's CEO stated that "we are entering 2025 with significant momentum and expect net revenues to exceed their previous peak in just the second full year following the U.S. Humira loss of exclusivity." This is a genuine strategic achievement, driven by the Skyrizi/Rinvoq franchise and the diversification provided by the Allergan acquisition.

However, the Five Forces analysis reveals rising structural pressures from governmental buyer power (IRA) and competitive rivalry (Calquence vs. Imbruvica; JAK inhibitor safety constraints on Rinvoq). The RBV analysis identifies the emraclidine failure as evidence that AbbVie's VRIN capabilities are concentrated in immunology and do not automatically transfer to neuroscience. The Ansoff analysis shows that the highest-return growth vectors remain in immunology (market penetration and product development), while aesthetics diversification is underperforming. The SWOT synthesis confirms that the greatest strategic risks are regulatory price pressure and pipeline concentration in two assets (Skyrizi and Rinvoq) that will themselves face patent cliffs in the 2030s.

The recommended action plan — aggressively monetising Skyrizi's IBD advantage, rebuilding neuroscience through disciplined early-stage investment, proactively managing IRA exposure, rationalising aesthetics, and beginning to develop the next immunology generation — forms a coherent strategic response. The connecting logic is consistent throughout: AbbVie's durable competitive advantage lies in its immunology capabilities, and the strategic priority must be to deepen and extend those capabilities while managing the risks that arise from assets and markets where those capabilities are weaker.
