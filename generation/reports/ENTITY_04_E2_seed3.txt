# Strategic Case Study: BAE Systems plc
## *Navigating Disruption at the Intersection of Geopolitical Opportunity and Technological Threat*

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

BAE Systems plc stands at a strategic inflection point. The world's third-largest defence contractor by revenue enjoys record financial performance, a £77.8 billion order backlog, and unprecedented tailwinds from rising global defence budgets. Yet beneath this headline strength lie three compounding strategic tensions: (1) the erosion of the traditional prime contractor model by software-first "neo-prime" entrants; (2) a structural talent crisis threatening programme delivery; and (3) the challenge of integrating a transformative $5.55 billion acquisition (Ball Aerospace) while simultaneously managing multi-decade platform programmes. This case study applies **Porter's Five Forces**, the **Resource-Based View (VRIO)**, and the **Ansoff Growth Matrix** to diagnose these tensions and derive an actionable strategic agenda.

## PART I: CASE BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### 1.1 Company Overview

BAE Systems plc (LON: BA.) is a British multinational defence, aerospace, and security company headquartered in London, formed in 1999 through the merger of British Aerospace and Marconi Electronic Systems. The company's workforce of approximately 107,000 skilled employees globally is a critical asset for delivering complex defence technologies. Its five business segments — Electronic Systems, Platforms & Services (US), Air, Maritime, and Cyber & Intelligence — give it broad multi-domain coverage across air, land, sea, space, and cyber.

### 1.2 The Strategic Situation: A Paradox of Strength and Vulnerability

BAE Systems announced strong financial performance for the year ending December 2024, marked by 14% growth in sales and underlying EBIT, and a 10% increase in underlying earnings per share, underpinned by robust programme execution and strategic acquisitions. Key financial highlights include a record order backlog of £77.8 billion and significant free cash flow generation of £2.5 billion.

These results, however, sit within a rapidly transforming competitive environment. Three structural forces create strategic tension:

**Force 1 — Geopolitical Demand Surge:** In response to rising threat perceptions, global defence spending rose to USD 2.46 trillion in 2024, with countries in Asia, the Middle East and North Africa, and Europe seeing major budget increases.

**Force 2 — Technological Disruption:** In late 2024, Palantir was reportedly building and leading a consortium with another dozen contractors, including SpaceX, OpenAI, and Saronic, to challenge the domination of the traditional "primes."

**Force 3 — Structural Talent Scarcity:** A study by Deloitte found that 67% of manufacturers, including those in Aerospace & Defence (A&D), see workforce shortages as a primary business problem.

The central strategic question is: **How should BAE Systems leverage its incumbent advantages to defend against technological disruption while scaling capacity to capture the geopolitical demand surge — all without being undermined by a structural talent crisis?**

## PART II: INDUSTRY ANALYSIS — PORTER'S FIVE FORCES

Porter's Five Forces framework (1979/2008) analyses the structural attractiveness of an industry by examining five competitive pressures. The defence contracting industry presents an unusual configuration: some forces are extraordinarily weak (creating structural profitability), while others are intensifying in ways that threaten incumbents' positions.

### Force 1: Threat of New Entrants — INCREASING (Medium-High)

Historically, the threat of new entrants in defence contracting has been negligible. A February 2022 study by the Department of Defense (DoD) found that after decades of consolidation, the number of defence prime contractors had shrunk from 51 to fewer than 10. Further, many segments of the defence market have become controlled by companies with monopoly or near-monopoly positions.

**However, the nature of the competitive threat is changing.** The traditional barriers protect hardware-intensive platform programmes but are far less effective against software-defined entrants. Anduril specialises in advanced autonomous systems, including drones and surveillance technology, and focuses on software-first development. Palantir Technologies provides data integration and analytics software to intelligence agencies and military clients and joined the S&P 500 index in September 2024. Looking at obligations per employee, SpaceX and Anduril perform better than several major traditional defence contractors, having surpassed Northrop Grumman, Boeing, and General Dynamics.

**Strategic implication for BAE:** The entry threat is not symmetric. BAE's naval shipbuilding and combat air programmes are effectively insulated by sovereign programme incumbency. But its Electronic Systems, Cyber & Intelligence, and mission software segments face genuine competition from technology-native entrants who can iterate faster and operate on fixed-price contracts.

### Force 2: Bargaining Power of Buyers — HIGH but Shifting Favourably

Governments are the dominant buyers of defence products, and their monopsony power is structurally high. Total NATO defence spending is estimated to increase 22 percent in real terms between 2022 and 2025, including an estimated 50 percent increase in spending by European members and Canada. This surge in buyer spending, combined with BAE's record backlog, substantially insulates it from short-term budget pressure. BAE's primary customers include national defence ministries, allied government agencies, and space and intelligence organisations, with major revenue concentrated in the UK MoD, US DoD, and expanding Australian defence procurement.

**The nuance:** buyer power shifts by programme type. For sovereign capability programmes (nuclear submarines, next-generation fighters), BAE holds unique leverage as the only qualified UK industrial partner. For information technology and data analytics contracts, buyer power is higher because credible new entrants exist.

### Force 3: Bargaining Power of Suppliers — MEDIUM and Rising

In recent years, vertical consolidations throughout the defence space have become more common. Most prime contractors for major programmes engage in little to no commercial business and have developed core competencies in dealing with the federal government and navigating the procurement process. The DoD's 2022 report warned that the defence industrial base had become "increasingly reliant on a small" number of specialised sub-tier suppliers, with vertical consolidation creating single points of failure. The talent supply chain — discussed in the VRIO section — is arguably the most binding constraint of all.

### Force 4: Threat of Substitutes — LOW for platforms, MEDIUM for services

For BAE's core hardware programmes — nuclear submarines, warships, armoured vehicles, fighter jets — there are no functional substitutes. These are sovereign capability requirements with no commercial analogue.

**However, in the growing domain of defence services and intelligence:** the defence industry long functioned as a critical engine of technological innovation and often "spun off" technologies it developed into the commercial realm. Today the reverse is increasingly the case. More and more, militaries depend on technologies developed initially for commercial markets and then spun into the defence sector. This "spin-in" dynamic means commercial AI, autonomous systems, and cybersecurity products can substitute for traditionally procured defence capabilities, eroding the value of BAE's Cyber & Intelligence segment if it fails to keep pace.

### Force 5: Competitive Rivalry — MODERATE but Intensifying

Among traditional primes, rivalry is managed through programme incumbency and long-term contracts. FY2024 was shaped by a mix of geopolitical urgency, technological evolution, and industrial adaptation. As the Pentagon sharpened its focus on strategic competition with China, sustained military aid to Ukraine, and the modernisation of US forces, the defence industry responded with innovation, scaled production, and record-breaking contracts. At the top of the list, the old guard — Lockheed Martin, RTX, and General Dynamics — retained their dominance, securing deals for fighter jets, missile defence, and warships.

**Rivalry is intensifying on a second dimension:** neo-primes prefer fixed-price contracts almost exclusively — Palantir and SpaceX use them exclusively, while Anduril uses them for 65–80% of their work. This setup encourages companies to invent efficiently since they get a set amount whatever the overruns. Traditional cost-plus contracts simply pay back all expenses plus profit. This creates a cost and agility advantage over the traditional cost-plus model that BAE must respond to.

**Five Forces Summary Verdict:** The industry remains structurally attractive for platform-focused incumbents (weak new entrant threat, low substitutes, moderate intra-traditional rivalry). However, structural attractiveness is eroding in the high-growth technology segments — precisely where future defence spending is increasingly directed. BAE's strategy must therefore protect the platform fortress while building credible capability in technology-intensive domains where the competitive dynamics are less favourable.

## PART III: INTERNAL CAPABILITY ANALYSIS — RESOURCE-BASED VIEW (VRIO)

The Resource-Based View (Barney, 1991) holds that sustained competitive advantage derives from resources and capabilities that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Organised (VRIO). From 30% to 45% of superior organisational performance can be explained by firm effects (resource-based view) and ~20% by industry effects (I/O view), indicating that the best approach is to look into both external and internal factors and combine both views to achieve and sustain competitive advantage. This validates applying VRIO alongside Porter's framework.

### Resource 1: Sovereign Programme Incumbency — **SUSTAINED COMPETITIVE ADVANTAGE**

**Valuable & Rare:** BAE is the sole UK-qualified designer and builder of nuclear submarines and the primary industrial partner on GCAP. The SSN-AUKUS programme is expected to create sustained shipbuilding revenue exceeding £30 billion over the programme life. GCAP spreads development costs across partners, reducing unit R&D burden and targeting operational capability by 2035.

**Inimitable:** Nuclear submarine construction requires decades of accumulated tacit knowledge, specialised facilities (Barrow-in-Furness), and security clearances that cannot be rapidly replicated. Key milestones include multi-decade roles on Dreadnought and Astute submarine programmes and deep US EW contracts; strategic moves cover AUKUS SSN-AUKUS collaboration and GCAP/Tempest partnerships, creating durable incumbency and a diversified global footprint.

**Organised:** BAE is actively scaling capacity. BAE Systems is scaling shipbuilding capacity for the SSN-AUKUS programme, increasing workforce and facilities in Barrow-in-Furness and South Australia to support multi-decade submarine production.

**VRIO Verdict:** This resource base is the bedrock of BAE's long-term financial stability and constitutes a genuinely sustained competitive advantage.

### Resource 2: Electronic Warfare and Sensor Expertise — **TEMPORARY TO SUSTAINED ADVANTAGE**

**Valuable:** BAE's Electronic Systems segment — covering radar, electronic warfare (EW), and signals intelligence — addresses one of the highest-growth areas in modern defence. R&D investment is a cornerstone of BAE Systems' strategy to stay ahead in technological innovation. The company allocated approximately £1.2 billion to R&D and capital expenditure in 2023, a figure expected to remain robust through 2024 and 2025.

**Organised but contested:** BAE has been working on technologies like directed energy weapons, hypersonics, and next-gen radar (e.g. its role in the UK's ECRS Mk2 radar for Typhoon). These are areas where future funding is headed. In 2025, internal R&D including customer-funded projects reached approximately £2.8 billion, with large shares directed to autonomy and EW programmes.

**VRIO Verdict:** Temporary to sustained competitive advantage — depends on continued R&D investment and talent retention.

### Resource 3: Space and Intelligence Capability (Post-Ball Aerospace) — **COMPETITIVE PARITY MOVING TO ADVANTAGE**

**Valuable:** The acquisition adds market-leading space, science and defence capabilities to the company's portfolio of products and services through a new business called Space & Mission Systems. This marks a significant milestone for the BAE Systems business, adding more than 5,200 US employees who share a culture of mission-focused innovation and operational excellence.

**Strategic pivot:** The 2024 acquisition of Ball Aerospace for $5.5 billion pivoted BAE Systems toward space and mission systems, integrating orbital capabilities with established defence platforms. This move accelerates its shift from hardware maker to data-driven intelligence provider.

**VRIO Verdict:** The acquisition moves BAE into a stronger position but does not yet confer sustained advantage in the space domain, where competition from SpaceX and Northrop Grumman is intense. Synergy realisation by 2026 is the key organising challenge.

### Resource 4: Skilled Workforce and Institutional Knowledge — **ADVANTAGE AT RISK**

**Valuable and Rare:** The company's workforce of approximately 107,000 skilled employees globally is a critical asset for delivering complex defence technologies. Tacit knowledge embedded in experienced engineers and programme managers is the true source of delivery capability.

**Organising failure:** The ADG industry is particularly exposed to Gen Z disruptions to the workforce, as companies rely on highly experienced and tenured employees to fill programme leadership roles and in many cases lag other industries in Gen Z hires. The aerospace and defence industry faces a potential one-million-person talent gap by 2030. The semiconductor engineer talent crisis is intensified by high retirement and workforce attrition rates across other critical roles, from technicians to mechanics.

**VRIO Verdict:** The talent resource is simultaneously BAE's most critical asset and its most fragile. Without deliberate investment in talent pipeline development, this resource will deteriorate from a source of competitive advantage into a source of competitive disadvantage — directly threatening delivery on the very backlog that defines BAE's current strength.

## PART IV: STRATEGIC GROWTH OPTIONS — ANSOFF MATRIX

The Ansoff Matrix (1957) provides a structured framework for evaluating growth strategies across four quadrants defined by product novelty and market novelty, with risk increasing from market penetration (lowest) to diversification (highest). Applied to BAE Systems, it maps current strategic moves and identifies gaps.

### Quadrant 1: Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets) — CURRENT STRENGTH

BAE's core strategy in its established UK and US markets is to deepen its position on existing programmes. BAE Systems CEO Charles Woodburn stated the company is "supporting our customers around the world, while shaping our portfolio towards higher growth and strategically important markets." The record backlog confirms the depth of existing customer relationships, but over-reliance on a small number of sovereign customers creates concentration risk.

### Quadrant 2: Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets) — ACTIVE PURSUIT

BAE is pursuing market development through two vectors:

**a) Geographic Expansion via AUKUS:** In August 2024, the US State Department determined that Australia and the UK have export-control systems comparable to the US, enabling new ITAR exemptions under the AUKUS framework. This change removed licensing requirements for over 70–80% of commercial defence trade between the three countries, streamlining defence-related exports significantly. This regulatory development directly reduces BAE's cost of serving the Australian market.

**b) European Rearmament:** Russia's invasion of Ukraine prompted a more immediate reaction from NATO members in terms of spending, as governments perceived a more tangible threat to their borders. Total defence spending is estimated to increase 22 percent in real terms between 2022 and 2025, including an estimated 50 percent increase in spending by European members and Canada. This creates substantial near-term demand for BAE's existing munitions, electronic warfare, and naval capabilities.

**Strategic Implication:** Market development is the lowest-risk growth path and is well-suited to BAE's current position. The company should prioritise this quadrant in the near term.

### Quadrant 3: Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets) — CRITICAL INVESTMENT

This quadrant is where BAE faces its most acute strategic challenge: it must develop new, technology-intensive products for existing sovereign customers who are increasingly demanding AI-enabled, autonomous, and cyber capabilities.

In 2024 alone, the EU committed €1.5 billion to defence-related R&D through initiatives like EDIRPA and the European Defence Fund. National programmes are expanding too: the UK pledged £400 million for defence innovation, while Germany doubled its military procurement budget. This capital is creating a market for new technology products that BAE must compete to supply.

Investment in dual-use tech skyrocketed 25%, from $954.5 billion in Q3 2024 to nearly $1.2 trillion in May 2025. Defence tech-specific investment jumped 27%, reaching $70.8 billion. This capital is flowing disproportionately to agile startups. BAE must either accelerate its own technology development or risk being relegated to a hardware integrator while software-defined competitors capture higher-margin mission systems work.

### Quadrant 4: Diversification (New Products, New Markets) — SELECTIVE AND CAUTIOUS

BAE's acquisition of Ball Aerospace represents a sound **related diversification** — a new product capability (space, ISR payloads) targeting a partially new market (US intelligence community). However, **unrelated diversification** into purely commercial markets is not recommended. Defence specialists — firms with little or no commercial business — account for 61 percent of the DoD's major programmes by value in 2024, up from just 6 percent when the Berlin Wall fell in 1989. The structural isolation of the defence industrial base from the commercial economy reflects the specialised nature of defence procurement; attempting to bridge this gap through unrelated diversification has historically destroyed value for defence primes.

## PART V: SYNTHESIS — THE COHERENT CHAIN OF REASONING

The three analytical frameworks converge on a coherent diagnosis:

**From Porter's Five Forces:** The industry's structural attractiveness is highest in platform-intensive segments (where BAE is strongest) and lowest in technology-intensive segments (where growth is fastest). BAE therefore faces a structural mismatch between where it is strong and where the market is moving.

**From VRIO:** BAE's most durable advantages (sovereign programme incumbency) are in exactly the segments where structural attractiveness is highest. But the resource most at risk (talent) threatens to undermine delivery across *all* segments — including the protected platform programmes.

**From Ansoff:** BAE's clearest growth opportunities lie in market development (AUKUS, European rearmament) and product development (AI, autonomy, space). The Ball Aerospace acquisition is a sound related diversification, but integration risk is real and must be managed actively.

**The coherent chain:** Rising geopolitical demand (external opportunity, Porter) creates revenue tailwinds that BAE's incumbent programme position (internal strength, VRIO) is well-placed to capture — *but only if* talent scarcity (internal weakness, VRIO) does not constrain delivery. Simultaneously, the shift of defence innovation toward commercial technology (external threat, Porter) requires BAE to invest in product development (Ansoff Quadrant 3) using the cash flows generated by its platform fortress. Failure to make this reinvestment would leave BAE as a powerful but ultimately declining hardware integrator in an increasingly software-defined defence world.

## PART VI: RECOMMENDATIONS AND ACTION PLAN

### Recommendation 1: Defend the Platform Fortress Through Capacity Investment

**Strategic Logic (linked to VRIO and Ansoff Q1/Q2):** BAE's sovereign programme incumbency is the foundation of its financial model and its only source of *sustained* competitive advantage per the VRIO analysis. The geopolitical demand surge creates both an opportunity and an obligation to scale. The "demand signal" from governments is encouraging BAE to boost capacity and efficiency — for instance, automating manufacturing processes will help with both current shell orders and future production of more complex systems.

**Actions:**
- Accelerate the Barrow-in-Furness expansion for SSN-AUKUS, targeting the £30 billion+ programme revenue over its life.
- Invest in digital manufacturing and model-based engineering to reduce unit costs and improve throughput.
- Establish long-term supply chain agreements with tier-2 and tier-3 suppliers to mitigate single-source dependencies identified in the DoD's 2022 industrial base report.

**Metric:** Maintain order backlog above £70 billion; achieve sales growth guidance of 7–9% per annum through 2027.

---

### Recommendation 2: Treat Talent as a Strategic Priority, Not an HR Function

**Strategic Logic (linked to VRIO):** The VRIO analysis identifies the skilled workforce as simultaneously the most critical and most fragile resource. The talent crisis is the single greatest threat to BAE's ability to execute on its existing backlog. According to the NAM Manufacturers' Outlook Survey for Q1 2024, over 65% of manufacturers identified the struggle to attract and retain employees as their top challenge. The consequences of failure are severe: Boeing experienced further production challenges when machinists in the IAM District 751 union began a strike in September 2024 over wage and benefit concerns, resulting in an estimated $5 billion in losses due to halted production and delays in deliveries. BAE cannot afford this risk given the scale of its programme commitments.

**Actions:**
- Launch structured early-pipeline programmes: STEM camps for secondary school students, apprenticeships with further education colleges, and university scholarship programmes targeting engineering, software, and cyber disciplines. The ADG sector will need to speak to what matters most to each subsequent generation to build a resilient, future-oriented workforce, potentially in the form of STEM learning camps for high schoolers, apprenticeships with community college students, or college scholarship programmes.
- Redesign the employee value proposition to compete with technology sector employers for Gen Z talent, emphasising mission purpose, career development, and flexibility.
- Establish talent exchange programmes with the UK MoD and US DoD, mirroring the Pentagon's own initiative of increasing talent exchange programmes with industry partners, to build a shared talent ecosystem.
- Invest in knowledge-capture systems to preserve institutional knowledge from retiring engineers before it is permanently lost.

**Metric:** Reduce time-to-hire for critical engineering roles by 30% within 24 months; achieve Gen Z representation at or above industry average within 36 months.

---

### Recommendation 3: Build Credible Technology Capability to Counter Neo-Prime Disruption

**Strategic Logic (linked to Porter Force 1 and Ansoff Q3):** The Porter analysis demonstrates that neo-prime entrants represent a genuine and growing competitive threat in technology-intensive segments. The product development quadrant of the Ansoff matrix is therefore critical. Commercially developed emerging technologies hold enormous potential for armed forces even when not originally developed for them. These technologies not only bolster the lethality of defence platforms but can also increase the efficiency of defence spending. When the private sector initially funds technology development for commercial markets, it covers a significant part of the initial development costs. BAE must engage with this dynamic rather than resist it.

**Actions:**
- Increase self-funded R&D as a percentage of revenue, building on the approximately £2.8 billion 2025 baseline, with explicit allocation to AI, autonomy, and cyber capabilities.
- Establish a corporate venture arm (building on FalconWorks' existing outside venture investments) to take minority stakes in dual-use technology startups, providing technology optionality without full acquisition integration risk.
- Adopt a hybrid contract model: selectively bid on fixed-price contracts in technology-intensive segments to demonstrate cost discipline and build credibility with procurement officials increasingly drawn to the neo-prime model.
- Partner with commercial technology firms to accelerate capability development in areas where commercial investment already leads military development, consistent with the NATO Parliamentary Assembly's 2024 finding.

**Metric:** Achieve 15% of revenue from technology-intensive segments (Cyber & Intelligence, Space & Mission Systems) within five years.

### Recommendation 4: Execute Ball Aerospace Integration as a Strategic Transformation

**Strategic Logic (linked to VRIO Resource 3 and Ansoff Q4):** The $5.55 billion Ball Aerospace acquisition is a sound related diversification, but integration risk is real. The 2024–2026 period involves Ball Aerospace synergy capture and integration, with short-term revenue uplift from civil space contracts and ISR payloads. Failure to realise synergies by the targeted 2026 timeline would destroy value and distract management attention from the broader strategic agenda.

**Actions:**
- Appoint a dedicated integration leadership team with explicit accountability for synergy realisation, targeting the £1.6–1.8 billion annualised revenue contribution identified in analyst projections.
- Leverage the August 2024 AUKUS ITAR exemptions to maximise cross-border technology transfer within the Space & Mission Systems sector across the US, UK, and Australia.
- Use Ball Aerospace as the foundation for a broader US DoD intelligence community growth strategy, targeting mid-to-high single-digit North American revenue growth driven by US DoD classified programme access and space capabilities.

**Metric:** Realise targeted Ball Aerospace synergies by end-2026; achieve Space & Mission Systems segment revenue growth of 10%+ per annum through 2028.

### Recommendation 5: Deepen AUKUS and European Market Development

**Strategic Logic (linked to Porter Force 2 and Ansoff Q2):** Market development is the lowest-risk growth path and directly leverages BAE's existing capabilities. International collaboration also means cost-sharing — GCAP funding is split three ways, AUKUS development will be shared with Australia and the US — reducing BAE's financial burden and ensuring a ready market for the end product. BAE's future growth areas are tightly aligned with the direction of allied defence spending: more high-tech systems, more digital integration, and stronger international industrial ties.

**Actions:**
- Accelerate Australian industrial development for SSN-AUKUS, building local workforce and facility capacity in South Australia as a long-term market development investment.
- Establish a dedicated European rearmament business development function to systematically capture the growing demand from NATO allies for munitions, air defence systems, and electronic warfare capabilities.
- Leverage GCAP's three-way cost-sharing structure (UK, Italy, Japan) as a template for future multilateral programme partnerships that reduce individual financial risk while expanding market access.

**Metric:** Achieve 25% of revenue from non-UK/US markets within five years, reducing concentration risk.

## PART VII: CONCLUSION

BAE Systems occupies a paradoxically powerful but increasingly contested strategic position. Its sovereign programme incumbency — the Dreadnought submarine, GCAP, the F-35 workshare — constitutes a VRIO-validated source of sustained competitive advantage that no competitor can rapidly replicate. BAE Systems delivered robust financial results in 2024, reflecting strong defence sector momentum. Annual sales reached £28.3 billion, a ~14% year-on-year increase in constant currency terms, and the order backlog swelled to a record £77.8 billion, indicating decades of future revenue.

Yet the analytical frameworks applied here converge on a clear warning: the forces that will determine BAE's competitive position in 2035 are not the same as those that explain its strength today. The biggest difference between traditional primes and neo-primes lies in their basic approach — traditional primes usually start with hardware development, while neo-primes take a software-first approach. This software-centred strategy helps them adapt faster to new threats. Simultaneously, with global defence spending reaching a record $2.7 trillion in 2024, governments and prime contractors expect more capability faster — making every unfilled role more visible and more costly than ever before.

The recommended action plan is structured as a **two-horizon strategy**: *defend and scale the platform fortress in the near term* (Recommendations 1, 4, 5) while *building the technology and talent capabilities* that will determine competitive position in the medium term (Recommendations 2, 3). The coherence of this plan rests on a single inferential chain: the cash flows generated by BAE's incumbent platform programmes must be deliberately reinvested in the capabilities — talent, technology, and new market access — that will sustain competitive advantage beyond the current geopolitical demand cycle. Failure to make this reinvestment would leave BAE as a powerful but ultimately declining hardware integrator in an increasingly software-defined defence world.
