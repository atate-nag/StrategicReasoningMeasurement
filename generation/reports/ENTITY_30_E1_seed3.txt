# Polestar: Strategic Case Study

## *Caught Between Prestige and Survival — Diagnosing and Resolving an Identity-Scale Paradox in the Premium EV Market*

---

## 1. Introduction and Case Overview

Polestar (Nasdaq: PSNY) presents one of the most instructive strategic puzzles in the contemporary automotive industry. Established as a premium electric car brand by Volvo Car Corporation and Geely Holding Group in 2017, Polestar benefits from the technological, engineering, and manufacturing capabilities of these established global car manufacturers. It entered public markets via a SPAC in 2022 with an enterprise value of approximately $20 billion, positioning itself as a pure-play premium EV brand with Scandinavian design credentials and a bold sustainability agenda.

Yet by 2024, the picture had darkened considerably. The EV maker has lost nearly all market value since its 2022 IPO and has accumulated $8 billion in cumulative losses. Soft demand, tariffs, and production delays continue to pressure Polestar's financial turnaround despite new funding. The company experienced a 15% decline in annual retail sales while simultaneously showing promising growth in new orders.

The **strategic situation** this case study examines is the following: Polestar occupies an ambiguous middle ground — too small to compete on scale with Tesla, BYD, or legacy OEMs, yet insufficiently differentiated in its premium positioning to command the pricing power of Porsche or Lucid. This "stuck-in-the-middle" dilemma (Porter, 1980) is compounded by a geopolitical supply chain crisis, a dependency on parent companies that simultaneously constrains and enables it, and a capital structure that is structurally loss-making. The case analyses this situation using Porter's Five Forces, the Resource-Based View (RBV), the Ansoff Growth Matrix, and a SWOT framework, before offering a coherent set of strategic recommendations.

## 2. Company Background

Polestar's relationship with Volvo Cars and Geely has provided it with a unique competitive advantage in its ability to rapidly scale and finance commercialisation activities while maintaining an asset-light balance sheet. This is achieved primarily through contract manufacturing and vehicle supply agreements with Volvo Cars and Geely. Polestar has also utilised Volvo Cars' and Geely's established research and development capabilities to accelerate technological advancements in automotive technology.

Unlike traditional automotive manufacturers that rely heavily on dealership networks, Polestar operates primarily through online sales channels. By bypassing dealerships, Polestar offers a seamless and hassle-free purchasing experience. This direct-to-consumer (D2C) model was a defining feature of the original brand vision.

CEO Michael Lohscheller has stated that Scandinavian minimalist design and the brand's sustainability framing remain the two differentiators that allow Polestar to coexist with Volvo Cars and Zeekr. Polestar has set an ambitious roadmap to reach its climate targets: halve greenhouse gas emissions by 2030 per-vehicle-sold and become climate-neutral across its value chain by 2040.

In terms of product portfolio, as of late 2025, Polestar's core consumer lineup centres on three vehicles: the Polestar 2 fastback, the Polestar 3 midsize SUV, and the Polestar 4 coupé-style SUV. The company has since announced an ambitious expansion, with four new models planned in the next three years, including the Polestar 5 — a four-door Grand Tourer — with deliveries expected from summer 2026.

## 3. The Strategic Situation: A Diagnosis

### 3.1 Financial Deterioration

The financial position is the most immediate symptom of the strategic problem. The Swedish automaker Polestar reported a second-quarter net loss of $1.03 billion, with ongoing cash burn, debt pressures, and delayed break-even projections underscoring the financial risks facing Polestar and other EV startups amid market uncertainty.

Polestar's net revenue for the six months ended June 30, 2024 was $905.8 million, a decrease of $331.8 million, or 27%, compared to $1,237.6 million for the six months ended June 30, 2023. The volume problem is equally stark: Polestar delivered approximately 44,851 vehicles in all of 2024 — a number that established players like BMW, Mercedes-Benz, and even Hyundai's Ioniq sub-brand surpass in a single quarter of EV sales alone.

The capital markets have also reflected this distress. The company first encountered a minimum bid price issue on July 5, 2024, when its American Depositary Shares (ADSs) traded below the $1.00 threshold for 30 consecutive trading days.

### 3.2 The Scale-Prestige Paradox

The core strategic tension is one of *competitive positioning*. Porter (1980) argues that sustainable competitive advantage derives from either cost leadership, differentiation, or focus — and that firms which fail to commit to one strategy risk being "stuck in the middle," achieving neither sufficient volume for cost efficiency nor sufficient uniqueness for premium pricing. Polestar's predicament fits this description precisely.

Despite Polestar's modern Swedish design and premium positioning — matched with a low-cost manufacturing base in China — the EV brand has been unable to scale to volume fast enough with its two pricey models. Tesla has cut prices repeatedly, compressing the premium segment from above. And legacy automakers — Volvo included — have launched their own compelling EVs, cannibalising whatever niche Polestar once occupied.

## 4. Industry Analysis: Porter's Five Forces

### 4.1 Threat of New Entrants — **Moderate to High**

The EV industry has relatively low barriers in software and design but very high barriers in manufacturing scale and capital. Polestar itself benefited from Geely/Volvo's infrastructure to enter at speed. However, new Chinese entrants — backed by state capital — continue to emerge. BYD's strengths include vertical integration, cost efficiency, and aggressive international expansion, which are accelerating its global rise. This constant entry pressure keeps the industry competitive, particularly at lower price points that threaten to commoditise the segment Polestar currently occupies.

### 4.2 Bargaining Power of Suppliers — **High**

Polestar's asset-light model, while capital-efficient, creates structural supplier dependency. Polestar uses batteries from multiple suppliers — including CATL (China), LG Energy Solution (South Korea), and Northvolt (Sweden) — depending on vehicle model, region, and battery chemistry. This diversification is prudent, but the company remains dependent on Volvo Cars and Geely for manufacturing capacity itself. Key risk factors include Polestar's ability to maintain agreements or partnerships with strategic partners such as Volvo Cars and Geely, and to maintain relationships with existing suppliers and source new suppliers for critical components. If Geely's own financial position deteriorates, this dependency becomes an existential threat.

### 4.3 Bargaining Power of Buyers — **High**

EV buyers in the premium segment are highly informed, have multiple alternatives, and benefit from a price war initiated by Tesla and BYD. Tesla's global EV market share in 2023 was 41.8%, with annual production of 1,845,985 vehicles. Polestar faces direct competition from manufacturers like BYD (1,615,000 vehicles in 2023) and Volkswagen Group (662,000 electric vehicles in 2023). With this range of alternatives, switching costs for buyers are low, and Polestar must work harder to justify its price premium.

### 4.4 Threat of Substitutes — **Moderate**

The primary substitute is the internal combustion engine (ICE) vehicle, but regulatory tailwinds across Europe and North America are progressively eliminating this option. Within the EV space, however, plug-in hybrids (PHEVs) remain a transitional substitute for buyers uncertain about range or charging infrastructure. For Polestar specifically, the greater threat comes from premium ICE brands (BMW, Audi, Mercedes-Benz) that have launched credible EV lines, offering brand heritage that Polestar lacks.

### 4.5 Competitive Rivalry — **Very High**

This is the most powerful of the five forces for Polestar. In a rapidly expanding global EV market, Polestar eked out just 6% growth in 2023 after 80% in the previous year. The competitive pressure is multi-directional: Tesla compresses from above on price; BYD compresses from below on value; legacy OEMs (BMW, Mercedes, Audi) compress laterally on brand heritage; and Geely's own Zeekr brand competes for the same technology-forward premium buyer. Competitors including Fisker, Lordstown, and Arrival have filed for bankruptcy after running out of funds, illustrating that the rivalry is not merely market-share-reducing but existentially threatening for undercapitalised players.

**Five Forces Summary:** The overall industry attractiveness for a player of Polestar's size and current financial profile is **low**. The company faces high rivalry, high buyer power, and high supplier power simultaneously, with limited ability to deploy the cost advantages that would neutralise these forces. This makes a clear differentiation strategy not merely desirable but structurally necessary for survival.

## 5. Internal Analysis: The Resource-Based View (RBV)

Barney (1991) argues that sustainable competitive advantage derives from resources that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN). Applying this framework to Polestar reveals both genuine strategic assets and significant resource gaps.

### 5.1 VRIN Resources

**Scandinavian Design Identity (Valuable, Rare, Partially Inimitable)**
Polestar's Scandinavian design language, characterised by minimalism, functionality, and elegance, sets it apart. This design philosophy appeals to consumers seeking a sophisticated aesthetic, differentiating Polestar from many competitors. Polestar uses recycled plastics, bio-based composites, and vegan interior options, tying into a broader brand narrative of sustainability-led luxury. This is rare in the sense that few EV brands can credibly claim Scandinavian heritage; however, it is potentially imitable over time by any brand willing to invest in design talent.

**Sustainability Transparency (Valuable, Rare, Inimitable in the Short Term)**
Polestar positions itself as the first automaker to publicly release life cycle assessments for every vehicle in its lineup. This expanded sustainability reporting could strengthen Polestar's ESG profile, potentially improving its appeal to environmentally focused investors and partners in the EV value chain. Polestar has implemented measures to reduce the carbon footprint of its vehicles, achieving a 25% reduction in emissions per EV sold according to its sustainability report. This radical transparency is a genuinely rare resource that builds institutional credibility with fleet buyers, regulators, and ESG-focused institutional investors — audiences that competitors have not systematically cultivated.

**Asset-Light Manufacturing Model (Valuable, but Not Rare or Inimitable)**
Polestar's relationship with Volvo Cars and Geely has provided it with a unique competitive advantage in its ability to rapidly scale and finance commercialisation activities while maintaining an asset-light balance sheet. However, this model is replicated by other EV startups and is vulnerable to the parent companies' own strategic priorities.

### 5.2 Resource Gaps

- **No proprietary manufacturing platform:** Unlike Tesla's Gigafactory model, Polestar owns no production facilities, limiting its ability to reduce costs through vertical integration.
- **Limited brand heritage:** Polestar was only formally established as an independent brand in 2017; it lacks the decades of brand equity that BMW or Porsche deploy to justify premium pricing.
- **Insufficient scale:** Polestar has never achieved the kind of scale necessary to make the economics of EV manufacturing work. Without scale, fixed-cost absorption remains punishing.

### Weaknesses
- The EV maker has accumulated $8 billion in cumulative losses and has no clear path to profitability in the near term
- Heavy dependence on Geely and Volvo for manufacturing, R&D, and financing — creating a principal-agent tension between independence and dependency
- Continued widening losses, underscoring the challenges the company faces as it competes in the crowded EV market
- Nascent brand heritage compared to legacy premium competitors (Porsche, BMW, Mercedes)

- Polestar continues to target compound annual retail sales volume growth of 30–35% for 2025 to 2027

## 7. Growth Strategy Analysis: The Ansoff Matrix

Ansoff's (1957) Product/Market Growth Matrix provides a structured way to evaluate Polestar's strategic options across four quadrants: Market Penetration, Market Development, Product Development, and Diversification.

### 7.1 Market Penetration (Existing Products / Existing Markets)

This is the most immediately actionable strategy and aligns with Lohscheller's early moves. Since assuming leadership, CEO Michael Lohscheller has shifted the company to a traditional dealership model and implemented cost-cutting measures. This pivot — away from the pure D2C model — is a market penetration move, deepening reach within existing geographies by expanding the retail network. In 2026, the operational focus will be on the continued expansion of the sales network, growing sales points by a planned 20%. The risk is that this erodes the brand's distinctive D2C identity, which was a genuine differentiator. The recommendation here is to pursue a *hybrid* penetration model — retain online configuration and purchase capability while adding physical touchpoints for test drives and aftersales.

### 7.2 Market Development (Existing Products / New Markets)

In addition to Europe, the brand is targeting Florida and California specifically with the Polestar 4, the two largest EV markets in the United States. In December 2025, Polestar opened a new retail location in Coral Gables, Florida. This represents a disciplined market development strategy focused on high-EV-penetration geographies within existing markets. However, the tariff environment constrains the US opportunity for China-manufactured models. A more promising market development avenue is the **corporate and fleet segment**: Polestar's radical LCA transparency is uniquely suited to organisations with Scope 3 emissions obligations under frameworks such as the GHG Protocol and TCFD, and this segment has been systematically under-exploited.

### 7.3 Product Development (New Products / Existing Markets)

This is Polestar's primary stated growth strategy. Polestar announced the largest model offensive in its history, with four new cars planned in the next three years. The Polestar 7 compact SUV is scheduled to launch in 2028, with production planned for Europe, expanding the company's manufacturing footprint beyond its current Asian base. The strategic logic is sound — diversifying the portfolio reduces single-model revenue risk and captures new buyer segments — but the execution risk is high given the capital constraints. Product development requires significant R&D and tooling investment that Polestar must fund through its parent relationships and external financing. An impairment loss of USD 739 million on the Polestar 3 CGU illustrates what can go wrong when product launches underperform expectations.

### 7.4 Diversification (New Products / New Markets)

This is the highest-risk quadrant and is not currently appropriate for Polestar given its capital constraints. However, a *related diversification* into software-defined vehicle (SDV) services — selling over-the-air performance upgrades, connectivity subscriptions, and data services — represents a lower-risk way to build recurring revenue streams. This is consistent with the industry trend: software is projected to represent nearly 40% of vehicle value by 2025. Polestar's existing Google Android Automotive OS integration provides a platform for this.

## 8. The Geopolitical Fault Line: Supply Chain Strategy

A distinctive and urgent dimension of Polestar's strategic situation is the geopolitical exposure created by its China-centred manufacturing model. While Tesla has diversified its supply chain and secured North American production hubs, Polestar's reliance on Asian manufacturing leaves it more exposed.

Polestar is responding with a geographic manufacturing diversification strategy. Polestar plans to mitigate tariff risks by producing future models like the Polestar 4 in South Korea and the Polestar 7 in Europe. This localised production strategy aims to reduce dependence on Chinese manufacturing and qualify for US EV incentives. Plans to build the Polestar 7 at Volvo's upcoming plant in Košice, Slovakia represent a concrete step in this direction.

This is strategically necessary but operationally complex. Moving production to Europe increases labour and overhead costs, potentially widening losses in the short term. The strategic logic, however, is compelling: it transforms a geopolitical vulnerability into a marketing asset (European-made premium EV) while qualifying for Inflation Reduction Act-style incentives in the US.

Michael was appointed Chief Executive Officer of Polestar in October 2024 and has two decades of senior level experience in the automotive industry. Previously, Michael served as the CEO of Nikola Motor Company, VinFast and Opel, and before that he was CFO of Opel, Volkswagen and Mitsubishi.

Lohscheller's background signals a deliberate shift from a *design-led* to an *operationally-led* strategic orientation. His predecessor Thomas Ingenlath was an industrial designer who built Polestar's brand identity; Lohscheller is a finance and operations specialist tasked with translating that identity into a viable business model. "Volume was up 51 per cent, revenue was up 56 per cent. For the first time in the history of Polestar we achieved a positive operating gross margin," Lohscheller stated, adding: "Costs are coming down and EBITDA losses are also reducing."

This early progress is encouraging but fragile. The risk of Lohscheller's approach is that operational cost-cutting and the shift to traditional dealerships may dilute the very brand attributes — minimalism, digital-first experience, radical transparency — that constitute Polestar's VRIN resources.

## 10. Strategic Recommendations

The analysis above generates a coherent set of recommendations, structured around a three-horizon framework (McKinsey, 1999): stabilise the core (Horizon 1), scale the differentiation (Horizon 2), and build future platforms (Horizon 3).

---

### Recommendation 1 — Horizon 1: Resolve the Manufacturing Geopolitical Risk Through Accelerated European Production

**Rationale:** The tariff environment is the single most immediate structural threat. EU and US import tariffs on Polestar's Chinese-made cars are becoming an issue, requiring "mitigating measures" to achieve cashflow breakeven. Polestar cannot control tariff policy, but it can reduce exposure.

**Action:** Accelerate the Košice (Slovakia) plant timeline for the Polestar 7 and explore whether the Polestar 3's European production can be expanded beyond the existing US dual-manufacturing arrangement. Negotiate with Volvo Cars for additional European manufacturing capacity in exchange for deeper commercial cooperation. European production would also allow Polestar to market its vehicles as "European-made" — a genuine premium signal in the EU market — and potentially qualify for EU battery passport requirements under the EU Battery Regulation, giving fleet and institutional buyers the supply chain transparency they increasingly require.

---

### Recommendation 2 — Horizon 1: Protect and Monetise the Sustainability Transparency Asset

**Rationale:** The RBV analysis identifies radical sustainability transparency as Polestar's most genuinely rare and inimitable resource. Polestar positions itself as the first automaker to publicly release life cycle assessments for every vehicle in its lineup, and this expanded sustainability reporting could strengthen Polestar's ESG profile, potentially improving its appeal to environmentally focused investors and partners in the EV value chain.

**Action:** Translate this asset into commercial advantage by actively targeting corporate fleet procurement officers at organisations with Scope 3 emissions obligations. Develop a dedicated B2B sales proposition — "The Only Car Brand That Can Prove Its Carbon Footprint" — supported by the LCA data. Partner with ESG rating agencies (MSCI, Sustainalytics) to ensure Polestar's transparency methodology is reflected in corporate sustainability scores. This creates a high-margin, lower-volume revenue channel that does not require competing on price with Tesla or BYD.

---

### Recommendation 3 — Horizon 2: Commit Fully to a Focused Differentiation Strategy — "Premium Performance Sustainability"

**Rationale:** Porter's framework warns against the "stuck-in-the-middle" trap. Polestar's current positioning — premium but not luxury, sustainable but not niche, tech-forward but not software-first — leaves it vulnerable from multiple directions. The company must choose a more sharply defined strategic position.

**Action:** Explicitly position Polestar in the segment between mass-premium (Tesla, Volkswagen ID) and ultra-luxury (Porsche Taycan, Lucid Air), defining it as the brand for *performance-oriented, sustainability-conscious professionals*. This requires: (a) ensuring the Polestar 5 Grand Tourer is priced and marketed as a genuine Porsche Taycan competitor; (b) using the Polestar 7 compact SUV to capture volume in the largest EV segment — which accounted for approximately one-third of total battery-electric vehicle volumes in Europe in 2025 — at accessible premium price points; and (c) resisting any temptation to compete on price with Tesla. The CEO's own words support this: Scandinavian minimalist design and the brand's sustainability framing remain the two differentiators that allow Polestar to coexist with Volvo Cars and Zeekr.

---

### Recommendation 4 — Horizon 2: Restructure the Go-to-Market Model as a Hybrid D2C/Franchise System

**Rationale:** The shift to traditional dealerships risks destroying the digital-first brand identity that differentiates Polestar from legacy OEMs. However, the pure D2C model has proven insufficient to drive the volume required for financial sustainability.

**Action:** Adopt a structured hybrid: retain full online configuration, financing, and purchase capability (preserving the D2C premium experience), but add a network of **Polestar-branded Experience Centres** — not traditional dealerships — in partnership with selected Volvo retailers for aftersales. Volvo has reduced its stake to 18% and Geely has stepped in to provide more direct financial and operational support, creating an opportunity to renegotiate the retail partnership terms to Polestar's advantage. This approach mirrors the strategy successfully deployed by Rivian and Genesis, which combine digital purchase with curated physical touchpoints.

---

### Recommendation 5 — Horizon 3: Build a Software-Defined Revenue Layer

**Rationale:** Polestar's existing Google Android Automotive OS integration provides a foundation for software-as-a-service (SaaS) revenue streams. As the automotive industry moves toward software-defined vehicles, recurring software revenue could transform Polestar's unit economics — reducing the dependence on vehicle margin for profitability.

**Action:** Develop a tiered software subscription model — "Polestar Performance Suite" — offering over-the-air powertrain upgrades, advanced driver assistance features, and connectivity services. This mirrors Tesla's Full Self-Driving subscription model but is anchored in Polestar's performance and sustainability brand values. Early revenue from this channel would be modest, but it would signal to investors a path to a higher-margin, capital-light business model, supporting equity valuation recovery.

---

### Recommendation 6 — Horizon 3: Resolve the Identity-Dependency Paradox with Geely

**Rationale:** Polestar's relationship with Geely is simultaneously its greatest asset and its greatest strategic risk. The asset-light model enables rapid scaling; the dependency creates brand ambiguity, geopolitical exposure, and governance risk. Polestar's 2025–2027 strategy hinges on three pillars: expanding its retail network, improving profit margins, and reducing costs. But whether it can transcend its reliance on Geely and Volvo to carve out a standalone identity remains the central question.

**Action:** Negotiate a formal **Strategic Independence Charter** with Geely and Volvo that: (a) guarantees manufacturing access and pricing for a defined period; (b) establishes clear boundaries between Polestar, Volvo, and Zeekr brand positioning to prevent internal cannibalisation; and (c) creates a pathway for Polestar to develop at least one proprietary manufacturing capability (e.g., battery assembly or software development) to reduce long-term dependency. This is not a request for full independence — that would be financially imprudent — but a structured framework for *managed autonomy*.

## 11. Conclusion: The Coherent Strategic Logic

The analysis reveals a company with genuine strategic assets — Scandinavian design identity, radical sustainability transparency, an asset-light model, and an improving financial trajectory — that are currently being undermined by four compounding forces: insufficient scale, geopolitical manufacturing exposure, an ambiguous competitive position, and a capital structure that constrains strategic investment.

The chain of reasoning is as follows: Porter's Five Forces establishes that the EV industry is structurally unattractive for undifferentiated mid-scale players, making a clear differentiation strategy mandatory. The RBV identifies Polestar's genuine VRIN resources — design and sustainability transparency — which must be protected and monetised rather than diluted. The SWOT analysis confirms that the primary threats (tariffs, rivalry, capital constraints) are external, while the primary weaknesses (scale, dependency) are structural and addressable. The Ansoff Matrix identifies product development and selective market development (corporate/fleet, European market deepening) as the most appropriate growth vectors, with diversification into software services as a longer-term platform.

Taken together, the recommendations constitute a **focused differentiation strategy** executed through geographic manufacturing diversification, a hybrid go-to-market model, and a new software revenue layer — all anchored in the brand's authentic core: *performance, design, and provable sustainability*. Full-year 2025 retail sales reached approximately 60,119 cars, a 34% increase versus 2024, suggesting that the foundations for this strategy are beginning to take hold. The question is whether Polestar can execute with sufficient speed and discipline before its capital position forces a more existential reckoning.
