# ✈️ STRATEGIC CASE STUDY: COMAC — China's State-Owned Aircraft Maker and the Battle for the Skies

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This case study examines the **Commercial Aircraft Corporation of China (COMAC)**, a state-owned enterprise (SOE) at the centre of one of the most ambitious industrial strategies of the 21st century. COMAC's flagship C919 narrowbody jet — designed to challenge the Boeing 737 MAX and Airbus A320neo — represents China's determination to break a Western duopoly that has dominated commercial aviation for decades. Yet, despite powerful state backing, COMAC faces a complex web of strategic challenges: geopolitical supply-chain vulnerabilities, production bottlenecks, regulatory barriers to international certification, and the credibility deficit of a new entrant in a safety-critical industry.

Using a suite of strategic frameworks — **PESTLE, Porter's Five Forces, VRIO, the Ansoff Matrix, the BCG Matrix, and McKinsey's 7-S** — this case study analyses COMAC's strategic situation and concludes with a prioritised set of strategic recommendations.

---

## PART 1: BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### 1.1 Company Overview

COMAC (Commercial Aircraft Corporation of China) is China's state-owned aircraft manufacturer that aims to rival aviation giants Airbus and Boeing in the global airliner market. COMAC was founded in May 2008 in Shanghai to design and build passenger jets that could compete with the Western duopoly. Its headquarters and main production lines are in Shanghai's Pudong district.

COMAC was founded when the Aviation Industry Corporation of China (AVIC) split off two of its Shanghai subsidiaries into a new standalone company and transferred with them the intellectual property for the ARJ21, a regional jet still in its early days of development. At the same time, COMAC took on five other investors: the State-owned Assets Supervision and Administration Commission (SASAC) under China's State Council, the Shanghai Guosheng Group, the Aluminum Corporation of China (CHALCO), China Baowu Steel Group, and Sinochem.

COMAC's product portfolio centres on two aircraft:
- **ARJ21 (C909):** A 90-seat regional jet — now in domestic commercial service
- **C919:** A 150–190 seat narrowbody — COMAC's primary challenger to the A320neo and 737 MAX

### 1.2 The Strategic Situation

The C919's order book is strong within China and affiliated markets, but the aircraft's true potential lies in whether it can win over regulators abroad. Without international certification, the C919 remains largely confined to domestic skies.

Initially targeting 75 deliveries in 2025, COMAC has slashed its goal to 25 units, with only five aircraft delivered by mid-2025. This underperformance underscores a critical challenge for China's aerospace ambitions: its reliance on foreign technology in an era of geopolitical friction.

The strategic situation is therefore one of **high ambition vs. structural constraint** — a state-backed challenger seeking to disrupt a global duopoly while grappling with technological dependency, geopolitical headwinds, and the steep credibility requirements of commercial aviation.

## PART 2: STRATEGIC ANALYSIS

### 2.1 PESTLE ANALYSIS

The PESTLE framework examines the macro-environmental forces shaping COMAC's strategic context.

---

#### 🔵 P — POLITICAL

COMAC's most powerful strategic asset is political. The Chinese state provides not just funding but industrial direction. China's approach to marketing the COMAC C919 hinges on aggressive government backing, state-bank financing, and accelerated delivery schedules designed to lure budget and regional carriers in emerging markets. By combining subsidised financing, rapid slot availability, and massive domestic demand, COMAC seeks to erode Western dominance on key routes.

However, politics is also COMAC's greatest vulnerability. The July 2025 LEAP-1C suspension demonstrated leverage: Washington could halt C919 production at will. Geopolitical rivalry between the US and China has turned commercial aviation into a theatre of industrial warfare. The 125% duty imposed by China in April 2025 on American imports, in retaliation for US President Trump's tariff hikes, significantly raised the cost of Boeing jets being sold to Chinese airlines. This creates a paradox: protectionism simultaneously shields COMAC domestically while threatening its access to Western-made components.

---

#### 🔴 E — ECONOMIC

China's aviation market is structurally enormous. IBA forecasts 90 C919 deliveries per year by 2030, engine-constrained. The domestic demand base alone provides a viable economic foundation. By combining subsidised financing, rapid slot availability, and massive domestic demand, COMAC seeks to erode Western dominance on key routes, build market confidence, and create a parallel commercial aviation ecosystem.

Yet production economics remain challenging. Looking ahead, COMAC aims to rebound with 100 deliveries in 2026, but persistent supply issues could drive costs 15–20% higher than planned. The cost structure of a new entrant — with heavy R&D amortisation, low production volumes, and supply-chain inefficiencies — means COMAC is unlikely to be commercially profitable in the near term.

---

#### 🟡 S — SOCIAL/SOCIOCULTURAL

Domestic consumer confidence in Chinese-made aircraft is growing. As of December 2024, all 16 operational C919s had carried more than one million revenue passengers. This operational track record is essential for building the social licence that aviation requires. Internationally, perceptions of Chinese manufacturing quality — particularly in safety-critical sectors — remain a barrier. COMAC must manage a narrative of safety and reliability to attract foreign airlines and passengers.

---

#### 🟠 T — TECHNOLOGICAL

COMAC's technological dependency is its most acute structural vulnerability. The aircraft still uses many Western-sourced components, from engines to avionics. Analysts estimate the jet uses components from around 48 US suppliers and 26 European suppliers.

The strategic response is the **CJ-1000A domestic engine programme**. Launched under the "Made in China 2025" framework in 2015, this initiative set explicit targets for 70% domestic content in core aviation components by 2025, positioning high-bypass turbofans like the CJ-1000A as pivotal to reducing import vulnerabilities and fostering indigenous innovation. However, a domestic alternative, the CJ-1000A, is still not ready for service, with entry into service not expected until the late 2020s at the earliest.

---

#### 🟢 L — LEGAL/REGULATORY

International airworthiness certification is the single greatest legal barrier to COMAC's global ambitions. European regulators in August 2025 confirmed that formal certification by the European Union Aviation Safety Agency (EASA) is unlikely before 2028 and could slip as far as 2031. Industry experts emphasise that obtaining type certifications from the US Federal Aviation Administration and EASA will significantly boost the C919's international expansion and user base.

COMAC has mobilised extensive resources, including enhanced technical exchanges, to support the EASA team's work, demonstrating the manufacturer's commitment to meeting international regulatory standards.

---

#### ⚫ E — ENVIRONMENTAL

The global aviation industry faces mounting pressure to decarbonise. Next-generation aircraft must demonstrate superior fuel efficiency. The C919 uses CFM LEAP-1C engines — the same engine family as the A320neo — giving it comparable fuel economics. However, COMAC's long-term environmental positioning will depend on whether its domestic CJ-1000A engine and future aircraft generations can match or exceed Western efficiency standards. Failure to do so could disadvantage COMAC in markets with strong environmental regulations, particularly Europe.

### 2.2 PORTER'S FIVE FORCES ANALYSIS

Porter's Five Forces examines the competitive intensity of the industry in which COMAC operates.

---

#### Force 1: 🔴 Threat of New Entrants — LOW (but COMAC IS the new entrant)

Commercial aircraft manufacturing has among the highest barriers to entry of any industry: decades of R&D investment, billions in capital expenditure, complex certification requirements, and deeply embedded customer relationships. COMAC's struggles underscore the steep climb China faces in breaking into the global aviation market. From the perspective of other potential new entrants (e.g., Russia's UAC/MC-21, Japan's Mitsubishi SpaceJet), barriers remain prohibitively high. COMAC's own entry has taken over 15 years and required sovereign state support.

---

#### Force 2: 🔴 Bargaining Power of Suppliers — HIGH (Critical Vulnerability)

This is COMAC's most dangerous competitive force. The July 2025 LEAP-1C suspension demonstrated that Washington could halt C919 production at will. With the CJ-1000A in mass production, that lever breaks. The lifting of US export licence suspensions in July 2025 for the CFM International LEAP-1C and General Electric CF34-10A engines has provided temporary relief, though dependence on US-made core modules remains a strategic vulnerability. Supplier power is existential — a single export control decision can ground COMAC's entire production line.

---

#### Force 3: 🟡 Bargaining Power of Buyers — MODERATE (Domestic: Low; International: High)

Domestically, Chinese state-owned airlines (Air China, China Eastern, China Southern) are essentially captive customers, directed by government policy to order and operate the C919. China Eastern, Air China, and China Southern are already flying the aircraft, while Tibet Airlines has ordered 40 units. This captive domestic market substantially reduces buyer power at home.

Internationally, buyer power is high. Foreign airlines have no obligation to purchase COMAC aircraft, have established relationships with Airbus and Boeing, and will demand proven safety records, robust after-sales support, and competitive total cost of ownership before switching.

---

#### Force 4: 🟡 Threat of Substitutes — MODERATE

The primary substitutes are the Airbus A320neo family and Boeing 737 MAX — both of which are more mature, internationally certified, and widely supported. For short-haul routes, high-speed rail (particularly in China) represents a partial substitute. However, the fundamental demand for air travel in Asia is structural and growing, limiting the overall threat from substitutes. COMAC's risk is not that airlines will stop buying narrowbody jets — it is that they will continue buying from Airbus and Boeing instead.

---

#### Force 5: 🔴 Competitive Rivalry — EXTREMELY HIGH

COMAC seeks to erode Western dominance on key routes, build market confidence, and create a parallel commercial aviation ecosystem that could one day rival the Airbus–Boeing duopoly. The incumbents — Airbus and Boeing — are deeply entrenched, with order backlogs stretching years into the future. COMAC is betting that the backlog for Airbus/Boeing will give it room to offer earlier deliveries. This is a shrewd competitive insight: the duopoly's own production constraints create a window of opportunity.

In 2024, Boeing had a poor year, delivering only 348 commercial aircraft, while Airbus delivered 766 aircraft. They will need to ramp up production, or airlines may feel like they need to look elsewhere.

## PART 4: RECOMMENDATIONS AND ACTION PLAN

The following strategic recommendations are sequenced by priority and time horizon.

---

### 🔴 RECOMMENDATION 1: ACCELERATE SUPPLY CHAIN INDIGENISATION — "OPERATION SOVEREIGN SKY"
**Priority: Critical | Timeframe: Immediate–5 years**

COMAC must treat supply-chain sovereignty as a national security imperative, not merely a commercial objective. The July 2025 LEAP-1C suspension demonstrated that Washington could halt C919 production at will. With the CJ-1000A in mass production, that lever breaks.

**Actions:**
- **Fast-track CJ-1000A certification:** Dedicate sovereign R&D funding to achieve entry into service by 2028, not 2030. Chinese state planners view engine independence as essential for sustaining the COMAC C919 programme's viability, enabling production scalability without reliance on licensed foreign parts that constitute bottlenecks in supply.
- **Establish a Domestic Avionics Ecosystem:** Partner with AVIC and Huawei to develop indigenous flight management and avionics systems, reducing the 48 US supplier dependencies.
- **Dual-sourcing strategy:** For components where indigenisation is not yet feasible, establish parallel supply agreements with European suppliers to reduce sole-source US dependency.
- **Build strategic stockpiles:** Maintain a minimum 18-month buffer of critical imported components to insulate production from geopolitical shocks.

**KPI:** Reduce Western component dependency from ~60% to below 30% by 2030.

---

### 🔴 RECOMMENDATION 2: FIX PRODUCTION BEFORE SCALING — "QUALITY FIRST" PROGRAMME
**Priority: Critical | Timeframe: 0–3 years**

COMAC's credibility is being destroyed by its inability to meet its own delivery targets. Initially targeting 75 deliveries in 2025, COMAC has slashed its goal to 25 units. This is not just a commercial failure — it is a reputational one that undermines every other strategic initiative.

**Actions:**
- **Implement Lean Manufacturing principles** modelled on Toyota Production System — eliminate bottlenecks in final assembly at Pudong.
- **Hire and retain world-class production engineers** through competitive compensation benchmarked against global aerospace peers.
- **Establish a dedicated Production Excellence Centre** with real-time supply chain visibility and predictive analytics.
- **Set credible, conservative public targets** — under-promise and over-deliver to rebuild stakeholder confidence.
- **Commission an independent operational audit** by a respected international aerospace consultancy to identify and resolve root-cause production constraints.

**KPI:** Achieve 50 deliveries in 2026 and 80 in 2027, with zero missed committed delivery slots.

---

### 🟡 RECOMMENDATION 3: PURSUE A SEQUENCED INTERNATIONAL CERTIFICATION STRATEGY — "EASA FIRST"
**Priority: High | Timeframe: 3–7 years**

COMAC should prioritise EASA certification over FAA, given the severe geopolitical tensions with the US. COMAC has mobilised extensive resources, including enhanced technical exchanges, to support the EASA team's work. The current phase of EASA engagement marks the third stage in a structured four-phase certification process.

**Actions:**
- **Embed a dedicated EASA Liaison Team** in Brussels and Cologne — full-time, senior engineers whose sole mandate is certification progress.
- **Proactively share safety and operational data** from China Eastern's C919 operations (now over 1 million passengers carried) to build EASA confidence.
- **Target EASA certification by 2029** as the primary international milestone, unlocking European and Middle Eastern markets.
- **Pursue bilateral airworthiness agreements** with ASEAN, Gulf Cooperation Council, and African aviation authorities as stepping stones before EASA/FAA.
- **Defer FAA certification pursuit** until US-China geopolitical relations stabilise — investing resources there now is strategically premature.

**KPI:** Achieve EASA type certificate by 2029; secure first non-Chinese airline order by 2028.

---

### 🟡 RECOMMENDATION 4: BUILD INTERNATIONAL MARKET CREDIBILITY THROUGH A "BELT & ROAD AVIATION ALLIANCE"
**Priority: High | Timeframe: 2–6 years**

COMAC cannot win in the West before it wins in the Global South. Selling aircraft cheaply in developing markets enables COMAC to establish an operational footprint for Chinese-made products and foster brand familiarity before profitability becomes a possibility.

**Actions:**
- **Create a formal "Belt & Road Airline Partnership Programme"** offering bundled C919 packages — aircraft financing, pilot training academies, MRO centres, and spare parts warehousing — to airlines in Southeast Asia, South Asia, the Middle East, and Africa.
- **Establish regional MRO hubs** in Dubai, Singapore, and Nairobi to provide genuine after-sales support and reduce airline operational risk.
- **Publish transparent safety and reliability data** on a public COMAC Safety Dashboard — Mean Time Between Failures (MTBF), dispatch reliability, and incident reports — to build international trust.
- **Partner with a respected international airline** (e.g., a Gulf carrier or Singapore Airlines) as a "launch international customer" — even for a small order — to signal global acceptance.

**KPI:** Secure 50 firm international orders (non-Chinese) by 2027; achieve 99%+ dispatch reliability across the fleet.

---

### 🟢 RECOMMENDATION 5: RATIONALISE THE PRODUCT PORTFOLIO — "FOCUS TO WIN"
**Priority: Medium | Timeframe: 3–8 years**

COMAC is attempting to develop the C919, a stretched C919+, the C929 wide-body, and the CJ-1000A engine simultaneously. This is strategically overextended. The BCG analysis shows the C919 is the Star that deserves the most investment.

**Actions:**
- **Concentrate resources on the C919 programme** until it achieves production stability and international certification — delay C929 wide-body development by 3–5 years.
- **Develop the C919+ stretched variant** as the next product priority, directly targeting the A321neo segment where Airbus has a multi-year backlog.
- **Licence or co-develop the C929** with a trusted international partner (e.g., a Russian, Brazilian, or ASEAN partner) to share development risk, rather than pursuing it entirely indigenously.
- **Treat the CJ-1000A as a strategic infrastructure investment**, ring-fenced from commercial pressures, with its own dedicated programme office.

**KPI:** C919 achieves 100+ annual deliveries before C929 enters full development; C919+ enters service by 2032.

## CONCLUSION

COMAC represents one of the most strategically significant industrial projects of the 21st century. Its ambition — to break a decades-old Western duopoly in commercial aviation — is audacious, and its state-backed resources are formidable. Yet the frameworks applied in this analysis reveal a company whose strategic ambitions consistently outrun its operational capabilities.

The PESTLE analysis identifies geopolitical supply-chain dependency as an existential threat. Porter's Five Forces reveals that supplier power and competitive rivalry are the dominant forces constraining COMAC's position. The VRIO analysis confirms that state backing and the captive domestic market are genuine sources of sustained competitive advantage — but that technology and brand credibility are currently liabilities. The Ansoff Matrix shows COMAC is fighting on too many fronts simultaneously, and the 7-S analysis identifies a dangerous misalignment between strategy and systems.

The path forward is clear: **fix production, indigenise the supply chain, certify internationally, and build credibility market by market — before scaling globally.** COMAC has the resources, the mandate, and the market opportunity. What it needs now is strategic discipline.

> *"The race to break the Boeing-Airbus duopoly will not be won in the boardrooms of Shanghai — it will be won on the runways of Dubai, Singapore, and Nairobi, one certified, on-time delivery at a time."*
