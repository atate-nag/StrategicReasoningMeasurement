# SLB (Schlumberger): A Strategic Case Study

## Navigating the Trilemma — Sustaining Core Dominance, Monetising Digital Disruption, and Seeding New Energy in an Era of Structural Uncertainty

---

## 1. Introduction and Case Framing

This case examines the strategic situation facing SLB (formerly Schlumberger Limited) as it attempts to execute a simultaneous three-front transformation: defending and growing its incumbent oilfield services ("Core") business, scaling a high-margin digital platform, and building a credible New Energy portfolio ahead of long-run demand erosion for hydrocarbons. The central strategic tension is that each "engine" competes for capital, management attention, and organisational identity, while the macro environment in 2024–2025 is deteriorating (OPEC+ oversupply, tariff uncertainty, softening upstream capex). The case asks: **Is SLB's three-engine strategy internally coherent and competitively defensible, and what must it do to resolve the tensions between engines before the window of profitable transition narrows?**

The analysis proceeds through: (1) industry and macro context (PESTEL + Porter's Five Forces); (2) internal resource and capability audit (VRIO / Resource-Based View); (3) strategic position mapping (Ansoff Matrix); (4) coherence assessment of the three-engine model; and (5) a set of actionable recommendations.

---

## 2. Company Background

Founded in 1926, SLB has evolved from pioneering well logging to becoming the world's largest oilfield services provider. With $36.3 billion in revenue for 2024 and 111,000 employees globally, the company's scale is immense. In October 2022, the world's largest oilfield services provider announced a historic transformation: by shedding its nearly century-old name and rebranding as SLB, the company signalled a pivot that extended far beyond a simple logo change — it was a declaration of a new identity, moving from a traditional "oil and gas" company to a global energy technology firm focused on decarbonisation and the digital future.

With a balanced energy transition in mind, SLB's strategy is focused on three engines of growth: Core, Digital, and New Energy. Consisting of its Reservoir Performance, Well Construction and Production Systems Divisions, Core remains SLB's largest engine of growth.

The strategic situation is urgent: the industry is navigating global economic uncertainty stemming from the supply-demand imbalance and recent tariff announcements. In this environment, commodity prices are challenged, and until they stabilise, customers are likely to take a more cautious approach to near-term activity and discretionary spending. This creates a stress-test for whether the three-engine model is robust or merely aspirational.

## 3. External Environment Analysis

### 3.1 PESTEL Analysis

**Political / Geopolitical.** SLB's risks include changing global economic and geopolitical conditions; changes in exploration and production spending by customers; and the ongoing conflict in Ukraine. Geopolitical fragmentation directly affects SLB because its revenue is disproportionately international: SLB continued to benefit from elevated activity in the Middle East & Asia, particularly in gas, and clients' increased investments in deepwater basins, exploration, and digital, achieving its highest quarterly international revenue since 2014. The concentration of growth in the Middle East — while currently positive — also creates geopolitical exposure if regional tensions escalate.

**Economic.** While capex cuts can provide short-term financial relief, they often have long-term implications for the oil and gas industry. Reduced investment in exploration and development can lead to a decline in production capacity, potentially resulting in supply shortages when demand rebounds. The cyclical nature of capex cuts and subsequent supply constraints has been observed in past downturns, for example the significant capex reductions during the 2014–2015 downturn led to a broader downturn in ancillary industries such as oilfield services and offshore services. This structural cyclicality is the defining economic hazard for SLB's Core business.

**Social.** With a growing emphasis on sustainability, many customers are prioritising suppliers that offer environmentally friendly solutions. In 2023, Schlumberger reported that around 30% of its new contracts included sustainability clauses, reflecting the shift in customer preferences. This social pressure both threatens the Core business long-term and creates demand for SLB's New Energy and Transition Technologies offerings.

**Technological.** SLB's customers are increasingly embracing digital technology to shorten planning cycle times, boost automation, and improve production efficiency. SLB launched the Lumi™ data and AI platform, which integrates advanced artificial intelligence capabilities — including generative AI — with workflows across the energy value chain. The open, secure, and modular platform unlocks access to high-quality data across subsurface, surface, planning, and operations, increasing cross-domain collaboration and releasing new intelligence and insights to improve the quality and speed of decision-making at the enterprise level. Technological disruption is therefore simultaneously a threat (if competitors lead) and an opportunity (if SLB retains its platform advantage).

**Environmental.** SLB continues to expand its exposure beyond oil and gas with significant growth momentum in the low-carbon markets, where SLB will continue building businesses and forging partnerships across three key areas: industrial decarbonisation, with a focus on carbon capture and sequestration and low-carbon hydrogen for hard-to-abate industries; renewables and energy efficiency, with a focus on geothermal, geoenergy, and energy storage; and critical minerals, such as lithium from brine deposits, which will be required to support the energy transition.

**Legal / Regulatory.** SLB's risks include changes in government regulations and regulatory requirements, including those related to offshore oil and gas exploration, radioactive sources, explosives, chemicals, and climate-related initiatives. Regulatory tightening on carbon emissions accelerates the New Energy opportunity but simultaneously increases compliance costs in the Core business.

### 3.2 Porter's Five Forces

**Competitive Rivalry — HIGH.** The oilfield services sector is characterised by intense competition, with several key players vying for market share. As of 2024, SLB's total revenue reached approximately $27 billion [nine-month figure], while Halliburton reported $23 billion and Baker Hughes approximately $20 billion in revenue. The competitive dynamics often lead to price wars, which can significantly erode profit margins across the industry. This intense rivalry means that SLB's scale advantage is necessary but not sufficient; technology differentiation is required to avoid commoditisation.

**Threat of New Entrants — LOW.** In summary, Schlumberger Limited faces substantial barriers deterring new entrants because the industry is capital-intensive, technology-patent-protected, and relationship-dependent. SLB owns or controls the industry's leading portfolio of intellectual property, including but not limited to patents, proprietary information, trade secrets, and software tools and applications that, in the aggregate, are material to SLB's business. The combination of capital requirements, accumulated IP, and long-term customer relationships constitutes a durable moat against new entrants. This warrants the conclusion that SLB's Core business is structurally protected from disruption by start-ups, even as digital-native firms (e.g., cloud providers) could potentially encroach on the Digital engine.

**Bargaining Power of Buyers — MODERATE TO HIGH.** The oilfield services market exhibits moderate to high switching costs for customers. However, dissatisfaction with pricing or service quality can lead customers to consider alternatives. SLB's main competitors, such as Halliburton and Baker Hughes, are constantly vying for market share. In 2023, SLB's customer retention rate was approximately 85%, indicating a competitive landscape where maintaining customer satisfaction is crucial for reducing churn. An 85% retention rate is high but imperfect; it signals that roughly 15% of the customer base actively tests the market each year, giving SLB limited pricing power in commoditised service lines.

**Bargaining Power of Suppliers — MODERATE.** The bargaining power of suppliers is moderated by strong relationships and high switching costs. SLB's global scale gives it leverage over most component suppliers, but specialised raw materials (e.g., rare earths for energy storage, specialised steel for subsea equipment) represent pockets of supplier power that are growing in importance as the New Energy portfolio expands.

**Threat of Substitutes — MODERATE AND RISING.** Schlumberger faces a moderate threat from substitutes as energy transition advances. In the long run, electrification and renewable energy reduce demand for the upstream services that constitute SLB's Core. In the near term, the substitution threat is manageable; however, the strategic implication is that SLB has a finite window — perhaps one to two decades — to diversify its revenue base before structural substitution materially erodes Core volumes.

**Summary of Five Forces:** The industry structure is moderately attractive for incumbents (high barriers, moderate buyer power), but the trajectory is adverse (rising substitution, intensifying rivalry in digital). This warrants the conclusion that SLB must use its current period of structural advantage to build durable positions in Digital and New Energy before the competitive dynamics of the Core business deteriorate.

## 5. Strategic Position: Ansoff Matrix

The Ansoff Matrix (Ansoff, 1957) classifies growth strategies along two dimensions: existing vs. new markets, and existing vs. new products/services. SLB is simultaneously pursuing all four quadrants, which is unusual and creates resource allocation tension.

| | **Existing Products/Services** | **New Products/Services** |
|---|---|---|
| **Existing Markets** | **Market Penetration** — Core: deepwater, Middle East gas | **Product Development** — Digital: Lumi AI, Delfi platform enhancements |
| **New Markets** | **Market Development** — Core: new geographies (e.g., Central Asia, Africa) | **Diversification** — New Energy: CCS, geothermal, hydrogen, critical minerals |

**Market Penetration (Core, lowest risk).** International revenue grew 18% year on year, compensating for a softer North American market where revenue declined 6%. During the quarter, SLB continued to benefit from its favourable exposure to the international markets, with remarkable year-on-year growth of 29% in the Middle East & Asia, in addition to growth of 18% in Europe & Africa. This confirms that the Core penetration strategy is delivering, particularly in long-cycle international markets. However, the North America weakness (-6%) signals a structural shift away from US shale — the market that drove SLB's prior growth cycle — which warrants caution about over-reliance on Middle East concentration.

**Product Development (Digital, medium risk).** Digital revenue grew 20% for the full year, exceeding targets of high-teens growth. Customers continue to embrace the power of cloud computing, AI and digital operations to shorten cycle times and improve operating efficiencies. This strong performance validates the product development strategy. The key risk is that digital solutions are being sold primarily to existing oil and gas customers, meaning the revenue base remains correlated with hydrocarbon capex cycles — limiting the diversification benefit of the Digital engine.

**Market Development (Core into new geographies, medium risk).** In Asia, SLB saw strong activity across the region led by offshore, notably in China, Indonesia, Malaysia, the Philippines, and India. Geographic expansion into new offshore basins extends the addressable market for Core services. The risk is geopolitical: operating in fragmented emerging markets exposes SLB to regulatory and currency volatility.

**Diversification (New Energy, highest risk).** The New Energy unit is expected to bring in around $3 billion by the end of this decade and at least $10 billion by the end of the next decade by focusing on five areas: carbon solutions, hydrogen, geothermal and geoenergy, energy storage, and critical minerals. Diversification is the highest-risk quadrant in Ansoff's framework because it involves both new products and new markets. The strategic logic — that SLB's subsurface expertise and global footprint transfer to CCS, geothermal, and critical minerals — is sound, but the revenue targets require significant organisational scaling. Combined revenue from CCS, geothermal, critical minerals, and data centre solutions is on pace to visibly exceed $1 billion in 2025. This milestone is encouraging but represents only ~2.8% of SLB's 2024 revenue, confirming that New Energy remains embryonic relative to the ambition.

**Ansoff synthesis:** SLB's simultaneous pursuit of all four quadrants is strategically ambitious and financially stressful. The Ansoff framework implies that risk increases as firms move from penetration toward diversification, and that managing multiple quadrants simultaneously strains capital and management bandwidth. This is the core strategic tension that the recommendations must address.

## 6. Coherence Assessment: The Three-Engine Model

### 6.1 Engine 1 — Core: Structurally Strong but Cyclically Vulnerable

The Core business is performing well in the current upcycle. Full-year 2024 revenue of $36.3 billion grew 10% year on year, with the acquired Aker Subsea business accounting for half of the growth. Organic revenue grew 5%, entirely driven by the international markets. This was led by the Middle East, which grew 19% year on year, to reach a record. The acquisition of OneSubsea strengthens the Core's offshore offering. On October 2, 2023, SLB, Aker, and Subsea7 closed their previously announced joint venture. The new business, OneSubsea, will drive innovation and efficiency in subsea production by helping customers unlock reserves and reduce cycle time. OneSubsea now comprises SLB's and Aker's subsea businesses, which include an extensive complementary subsea production and processing technology portfolio, world-class manufacturing scale and capacity, access to industry-leading reservoir and digital domain expertise, unique pore-to-process integration capabilities, and strengthened research and development capabilities.

The ChampionX acquisition further extends the Core into the production and recovery segment — a strategically astute move because it shifts exposure toward OpEx-funded production chemistry and artificial lift, which are less cyclical than exploration-driven capex. SLB's core strategy remains centred on meeting growing energy demand while accelerating decarbonisation and emissions reduction through innovation, scale and digitalisation in its core oil and gas business. This acquisition will expand SLB's presence in the less cyclical and growing production and recovery space that is closely aligned with its returns-focused, capital-light strategy.

**Coherence verdict:** The Core engine is coherent. The strategic logic of moving from exploration-heavy to production-heavy services reduces cyclicality, and the OneSubsea and ChampionX acquisitions execute this logic. The risk is Middle East concentration: if Saudi Arabia or UAE cut capacity expansion programmes, Core growth would stall abruptly.

### 6.2 Engine 2 — Digital: High Momentum but Correlated Risk

Digital continued its strong growth momentum, delivering more than $2 billion in revenue in 2023. SLB's success in Digital was driven by further adoption of its new digital technology platforms — comprised of cloud, edge, and AI — as more customers embrace the integration of connected and autonomous, data, and AI solutions. The Q1 2025 data confirms acceleration: SLB's Digital & Integration segment experienced a significant 17% year-on-year revenue growth in Q1 2025, underscoring the strong customer adoption of its digital technologies aimed at enhancing upstream lifecycle efficiency and performance.

The critical coherence question is whether Digital revenue is genuinely diversifying SLB's risk profile or merely amplifying it. The evidence suggests the latter: digital solutions are sold primarily to the same oil and gas customers who buy Core services. When upstream capex contracts (as in Q1 2025), digital discretionary spending is also cut. Upstream investment has remained constrained by the over-supplied oil market. This has been amplified over the past few weeks with additional economic uncertainty stemming from the acceleration of supply releases by OPEC+ and recent tariff announcements. This correlation means the Digital engine does not provide the countercyclical buffer that its "technology company" framing implies.

The path to genuine diversification lies in selling digital solutions to non-oil-and-gas energy operators — a market SLB has not yet fully penetrated. Combined revenue from CCS, geothermal, critical minerals, and data centre solutions is on pace to visibly exceed $1 billion in 2025, suggesting that digital tools are beginning to flow into New Energy contexts, but this is nascent.

**Coherence verdict:** The Digital engine is strategically sound but not yet structurally diversifying. Its value proposition is well-evidenced; its risk-reduction claim is overstated. SLB must accelerate the sale of digital platforms to non-oil-and-gas customers to validate the diversification logic.

### 6.3 Engine 3 — New Energy: Strategically Necessary but Organisationally Underdeveloped

The New Energy unit is focused on five areas: carbon solutions, hydrogen, geothermal and geoenergy, energy storage, and critical minerals. The strategic rationale is compelling: SLB's subsurface characterisation, drilling, and reservoir management capabilities transfer directly to CCS (injecting CO₂ underground), geothermal (drilling and reservoir management), and critical minerals (lithium brine extraction). In 2024, SLB successfully injected 100% of captured carbon dioxide (CO₂) underground at the Cygnus Hub CCS project in Western Australia, demonstrating strong operability and readiness to scale. This proof-of-concept is important because it converts a theoretical capability claim into an evidenced operational track record.

However, the revenue scale remains small relative to the ambition. Combined revenue from CCS, geothermal, critical minerals, and data centre solutions is on pace to visibly exceed $1 billion in 2025, against a target of $3 billion by the end of this decade and at least $10 billion by the end of the next decade. Reaching $10 billion by 2035 from a ~$1 billion base in 2025 implies a 10-fold increase over ten years — a compound annual growth rate of approximately 26%. This is achievable only if SLB makes large-scale acquisitions, forms transformative joint ventures, or the policy environment (e.g., carbon pricing, IRA-style subsidies) dramatically accelerates demand. The current trajectory does not guarantee this outcome.

**Coherence verdict:** New Energy is strategically necessary (to hedge long-run substitution risk) but organisationally underdeveloped. The five-area focus risks spreading resources too thinly. SLB must prioritise within New Energy rather than maintaining equal investment across all five verticals.

## 7. Synthesis: The Strategic Problem

The three-engine model is directionally correct but structurally fragile in three specific ways:

1. **The Core and Digital engines are positively correlated**, meaning a hydrocarbon demand shock hits both simultaneously rather than allowing one to offset the other.
2. **The New Energy engine is too small and too broad** to provide meaningful near-term diversification, and its growth targets require either a step-change in policy support or large M&A.
3. **The Middle East concentration** within the Core creates a single-point-of-failure risk: organic revenue grew 5%, entirely driven by the international markets, led by the Middle East, which grew 19% year on year. If Middle East capacity expansion programmes moderate — as OPEC+ production discipline implies — Core growth stalls.

## 8. Recommendations

The following recommendations are sequenced by urgency and connected explicitly to the diagnostic findings above.

### Recommendation 1: Decouple Digital Revenue from the Hydrocarbon Cycle (Urgent, 12–24 months)

**Rationale:** The analysis shows that the Digital engine is currently correlated with Core revenues because it sells primarily to oil and gas operators. This undermines the diversification logic of the three-engine model. To create genuine countercyclical resilience, SLB must expand its digital customer base beyond upstream oil and gas.

**Action:** Accelerate the commercialisation of the Lumi/Delfi platform to power companies, utilities, and industrial emitters (cement, steel, chemicals) who need subsurface and operational AI for CCS, geothermal, and energy efficiency. The Lumi data and AI platform is open, secure, and modular, unlocking access to high-quality data across subsurface, surface, planning, and operations — this architecture is sector-agnostic and can be adapted to non-oil-and-gas clients. The NVIDIA partnership should be leveraged to co-develop industry-specific AI models for these adjacent sectors, replicating the energy-domain data moat in new verticals. This is warranted because digital tools sold to power and industrial customers are not correlated with oil prices, providing the countercyclical buffer the current model lacks.

### Recommendation 2: Concentrate the New Energy Portfolio on CCS and Geothermal (Urgent, 12–36 months)

**Rationale:** The Ansoff analysis shows that diversification is the highest-risk quadrant, and SLB is currently pursuing five New Energy verticals simultaneously. The VRIO analysis shows that CCS and geothermal are the two areas where SLB's existing capabilities (subsurface characterisation, well construction, reservoir management) are most directly transferable — making them the least diversification-risky of the five. Spreading resources equally across hydrogen, energy storage, and critical minerals dilutes focus and slows the path to scale.

**Action:** Designate CCS and geothermal as "priority" New Energy verticals and redirect a disproportionate share of New Energy R&E and M&A budgets toward them. The CCS track record is already evidenced: SLB entered into an agreement with Aramco and Linde that paves the way for the development of a carbon capture and storage hub in Jubail, Saudi Arabia, expected to become one of the largest globally, with a first-phase capacity to store up to nine million metric tons of CO₂ annually. Geothermal is similarly aligned with SLB's drilling capabilities. Hydrogen and energy storage, by contrast, require manufacturing and electrochemical capabilities that SLB does not currently possess at scale, making them lower-priority until the CCS and geothermal platforms are self-sustaining. This concentration is warranted because it aligns investment with inimitable capabilities, reducing execution risk while accelerating the path to the $3 billion target.

### Recommendation 3: Reduce Middle East Concentration by Accelerating Deepwater Diversification (Medium-term, 24–48 months)

**Rationale:** The financial analysis shows that SLB's Core organic growth is almost entirely driven by the Middle East. Organic revenue grew 5%, entirely driven by the international markets, led by the Middle East, which grew 19% year on year. This concentration creates a structural vulnerability: any moderation in Middle East capex — which OPEC+ supply discipline could trigger — would disproportionately harm SLB's Core revenues. The Five Forces analysis identified geopolitical risk as a material threat; concentration amplifies it.

**Action:** Reinvest a portion of Middle East cash flows into deepwater basin development in West Africa (Angola, Nigeria), Latin America (Guyana, Brazil), and Southeast Asia (Indonesia, Malaysia). These markets are already growing — SLB continued to benefit from long-cycle developments, capacity expansions, and exploration and appraisal activities across offshore basins in Brazil, Angola, US Gulf of Mexico, Guyana, and Norway — but they remain secondary to the Middle East. The OneSubsea joint venture provides the subsea technology platform to compete in these deepwater markets. Deepwater projects are long-cycle and therefore less sensitive to short-term oil price volatility than Middle East onshore capacity expansions, providing more durable revenue streams. This is warranted because geographic diversification within the Core reduces the single-point-of-failure risk without requiring new capabilities.

### Recommendation 4: Institutionalise the "Returns-Focused" Capital Discipline as a Structural Guardrail (Ongoing)

**Rationale:** The Ansoff analysis shows that SLB is simultaneously pursuing all four growth quadrants. Without explicit capital allocation guardrails, the three-engine model risks becoming a capital-allocation free-for-all in which New Energy consumes cash that should be returned to shareholders or reinvested in high-return Core and Digital projects. SLB's core strategy is aligned with a returns-focused, capital-light strategy.

**Action:** Establish explicit hurdle rates for each engine: Core projects must meet a minimum ROCE threshold (reflecting the mature, lower-risk nature of the business); Digital investments must demonstrate a clear path to margin expansion above the current Digital & Integration pretax operating margin of ~33%; New Energy investments must show a credible route to the $3 billion revenue target within five years. Any New Energy venture that cannot demonstrate billion-dollar scale potential should be divested or partnered. SLB New Energy explicitly targets billion-dollar opportunities only: "We look for two things: impact and scale. If the scale is not there, it is not really an energy transition business." This discipline, already articulated, must be operationalised through formal investment governance rather than remaining a rhetorical commitment. This is warranted because the current macro headwinds — OPEC+ oversupply, tariff uncertainty, softening upstream capex — mean that capital efficiency is now more important than growth optionality.

### Recommendation 5: Use the ChampionX Integration to Validate the "Production and Recovery" Thesis (Short-term, 12 months)

**Rationale:** The ChampionX acquisition is strategically sound — it shifts SLB toward less cyclical OpEx-funded production chemistry — but integration risk is real. The acquisition will expand SLB's presence in the less cyclical and growing production and recovery space that is closely aligned with SLB's returns-focused, capital-light strategy. However, the strategic benefit only materialises if cross-selling (SLB's digital platforms + ChampionX's production chemicals) is executed effectively.

**Action:** Establish a dedicated integration team tasked with three specific cross-selling objectives within 12 months: (i) deploying Lumi/Delfi AI optimisation tools to ChampionX's existing customer base; (ii) bundling ChampionX production chemicals with SLB's artificial lift portfolio in North America (where ChampionX has its strongest position); and (iii) using ChampionX's production data to train domain-specific AI models that deepen the Lumi platform's moat. This is warranted because the acquisition's financial rationale depends on synergies that require active integration management — passive co-existence of two portfolios will not generate the promised value.

## 10. Conclusion

SLB's three-engine strategy is the right directional response to the structural challenge of energy transition: it preserves the profitable Core, monetises the digital disruption of its own industry, and seeds the New Energy portfolio before hydrocarbon demand peaks. However, the strategy contains three structural weaknesses — Core-Digital correlation, New Energy breadth without depth, and Middle East concentration — that must be resolved to make the model robust rather than merely aspirational. The five recommendations above are sequenced to address the most urgent coherence failures first (decoupling Digital, concentrating New Energy) before addressing the medium-term structural risks (geographic diversification, capital discipline, integration execution). Executed together, they convert a directionally correct but fragile strategy into a resilient multi-decade competitive position.
