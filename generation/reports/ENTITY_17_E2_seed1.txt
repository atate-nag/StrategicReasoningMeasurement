# GE HealthCare Technologies: Navigating the Post-Spin-Off Strategic Inflection Point

## A Strategic Case Study

---

## 1. Introduction and Case Overview

On 4 January 2023, GE HealthCare Technologies Inc. (GEHC) completed its separation from General Electric and began trading on NASDAQ as an independent, publicly listed company. Within the proxy statement, the transaction is referred to as the "Spin-Off," which resulted in GE HealthCare becoming an independent publicly traded company. The separation was a deliberate act of strategic focus: GE HealthCare is driving precision care globally, with a clear strategy for value creation that will benefit patients, customers, and shareholders under the leadership of CEO Peter Arduini and his team, with GE retaining up to 19.9% of the outstanding shares of GE HealthCare common stock.

Independence, however, is not a strategy in itself—it is an enabling condition. The strategic question this case examines is: **Having achieved independence, can GE HealthCare convert its formidable installed-base advantage and AI innovation pipeline into durable competitive advantage, while managing a simultaneous set of structural threats—Chinese market erosion, post-spin-off cost burdens, and intensifying rivalry from Siemens Healthineers—before those threats compound?**

This question matters because the decisions made in the 2023–2026 window will determine whether GEHC consolidates its position as a global medtech leader or becomes a high-revenue, margin-constrained follower. The case proceeds through: (§2) situational context and financial performance; (§3) macro-environmental analysis (PESTEL); (§4) industry structure analysis (Porter's Five Forces); (§5) internal capability audit (Resource-Based View/VRIN); (§6) strategic position synthesis (SWOT); and (§7) strategic options and recommendations using Ansoff's Growth Matrix and the SAF (Suitability, Acceptability, Feasibility) test.

## 2. Company Profile and Financial Context

Founded from GE's X-ray unit in 1896, the company progressed from early radiography to CT, MRI, and AI-enabled platforms, serving over 160 countries with an installed base exceeding 4 million devices and revenue near $19.9 billion in 2024.

GEHC organises its business into four segments: Imaging (CT, MRI, PET); Advanced Visualization Solutions (AVS); Patient Care Solutions (PCS); and Pharmaceutical Diagnostics (PDx). GE HealthCare is among the top three global players in diagnostic imaging, with Imaging contributing roughly 54% of 2024 revenue and a 20–25% share in high-end MRI and CT modalities.

**Financial trajectory post-spin-off.** The company's first year as a public entity was operationally creditable. GE HealthCare delivered strong financial and operating performance in its first year as a public company, with fourth-quarter 2023 revenue growth of 5% year-over-year and organic revenue growth of 5%. However, 2024 told a more cautious story: revenues declined 1% year-over-year in Q1 2024, with organic revenue growth approximately flat, though the adjusted EBIT margin improved to 14.7% versus 14.1% in the prior year. By Q3 2024, revenue growth recovered to 1% year-over-year, with adjusted EBIT margin expanding to 16.3% versus 15.4%, and cash flow from operating activities rising to $742 million versus $650 million for the prior year.

The pattern—flat-to-modest top-line growth combined with improving margins—signals a company successfully executing cost discipline but facing a structural revenue headwind. The principal culprit is China.

GE HealthCare's China revenue declined by 15% in 2024 due to weakened sales and tariffs, prompting supply chain adjustments to tariff-friendly locations. This is not a cyclical blip but a structural dislocation rooted in geopolitics, as analysed in §3 below.

## 3. Macro-Environmental Analysis: PESTEL

The PESTEL framework (Johnson, Scholes & Whittington, 2017) maps the six macro-environmental forces that set the boundary conditions within which any firm's strategy must operate. Crucially, PESTEL forces do not merely describe context—they reveal where the firm is exposed and where it has room to manoeuvre.

### 3.1 Political

The most acute political risk for GEHC is the deterioration of the US–China trade relationship. Throughout 2025, the US has imposed a variety of new tariffs on most imports from all countries in the world, which in turn prompted several countries to announce tariffs on US imports; tariffs materially impacted GE HealthCare's current-year profitability and cash flows, primarily the bilateral US and Chinese tariffs and US tariffs on all other global import suppliers. The financial consequence is severe: bilateral tariffs have already cost the company $375 million in 2025, with impacts escalating to $200 million per quarter by year-end, and a cumulative toll of a $0.85 per share earnings hit.

Beyond tariffs, the Chinese government's domestic policy is equally disruptive. The Chinese Government cracked down on corrupt clinicians and senior hospital staff involved in medical equipment and drug procurement in July 2023. The result was a near-freeze in health sector sales that sent financial shockwaves through the medical technology sector. This matters strategically because China had historically been a key growth market for capital-intensive imaging equipment.

**Analytical implication:** Political risk has bifurcated GEHC's geographic portfolio. The US, Europe, and the rest of Asia remain stable or growing, but China—which had been a high-growth market—has become a drag. This is not merely a revenue problem; it forces a strategic reallocation of commercial resources and supply chain investment.

### 3.2 Economic

At the macro level, the International Monetary Fund (IMF) projected global growth to be 3.2% in 2024, a slight slowdown from 2023, but still indicative of continued demand, with a healthy economic environment typically translating into higher government spending on healthcare infrastructure.

More specifically, the structural economics of healthcare spending are favourable. Powerful, secular global growth drivers—an aging population, the prevalence of chronic disease, rising middle classes around the world—are shaping the industry and increasing the demand for improved patient care. These drivers are independent of short-term economic cycles and underpin long-run demand for GEHC's core products.

**Analytical implication:** The macro-economic environment is broadly supportive of demand, but the China-specific economic disruption creates a near-term drag that must be managed without sacrificing investment in the markets that will drive the next decade of growth.

### 3.3 Social

The global burden of chronic disease—cancer, cardiovascular disease, diabetes—is rising, directly increasing the demand for diagnostic imaging. Growth in the medical imaging market is driven by rising chronic disease incidence, an aging population, and technological innovations, notably AI integration, portable and point-of-care systems, 3D/4D imaging, and hybrid modalities.

There is also a growing societal expectation of precision medicine—the idea that diagnosis and treatment should be tailored to the individual patient. GEHC's stated corporate strategy, "precision care," is a direct response to this social trend. GE HealthCare's strategy prioritises precision care through connected care pathways and digitisation of healthcare, growth acceleration through commercial capabilities and product leadership, and business optimisation through sustainable margin expansion and strong cash flow.

### 3.4 Technological

The technological environment is the most dynamic dimension of GEHC's PESTEL. The AI in Medical Imaging market recorded revenue of US$1.70 billion in 2024 and is projected to expand at a CAGR of 25.8%, reaching an estimated value of US$16.88 billion by 2034. This extraordinary growth rate means that AI is not a peripheral feature but a core competitive battleground.

Strategic partnerships between imaging OEMs and cloud providers such as Microsoft, Google, and AWS are becoming increasingly influential, as competition now extends beyond hardware superiority to the ability to deliver integrated ecosystems. This represents a platform shift: the basis of competition is moving from device performance to data network effects and software lock-in.

GEHC has responded with its Edison AI platform. The Edison Developer Program is based on Edison, GE Healthcare's secure intelligence platform, and helps healthcare providers gain easier access to market-ready algorithms and applications by directly integrating these technologies into existing workflows.

**Analytical implication:** Technological change is simultaneously GEHC's greatest opportunity (AI-enabled differentiation) and its greatest threat (new entrants from software and cloud sectors that do not carry the capital cost of hardware). This tension is central to the strategic choices in §7.

### 3.5 Environmental

Healthcare is under increasing pressure to reduce its environmental footprint. GEHC has embedded sustainability into its corporate narrative, referencing "resilient, sustainable practices and products" in its investor communications. The company serves customers in approximately 160 countries with a global team of 9,900 sales professionals and 8,100 field service engineers, and a network of 43 manufacturing, assembly, and pharmaceutical facilities. This extensive physical footprint creates both environmental compliance costs and an opportunity to differentiate through energy-efficient product design—a factor increasingly relevant to hospital procurement decisions.

### 3.6 Legal

Regulatory compliance is a structural feature of the medical device industry. The US FDA and the EU's Medical Device Regulation (MDR) set the approval requirements for GEHC's hardware and software products. Forward-thinking companies like GE Healthcare have put Regulatory Information Management (RIM) systems in place to build their submissions, track and manage their global product registrations, and handle their healthcare correspondence.

Critically, the US, European Union, and China are GE HealthCare's most significant regions based on revenue, and the regulatory landscape within these regions is a key risk factor. Divergent regulatory regimes across jurisdictions create compliance costs and can delay product launches, disadvantaging GEHC relative to smaller, more nimble competitors in specific markets.

**PESTEL Summary:** The macro-environment is characterised by strong secular demand tailwinds (ageing population, chronic disease, AI growth), offset by acute geopolitical headwinds (US–China trade war, Chinese anti-corruption freeze) and structural regulatory complexity. The net implication is that GEHC must reduce its dependence on China-derived revenue while accelerating its AI and digital platform strategy to capture the technological opportunity before rivals do.

## 4. Industry Structure Analysis: Porter's Five Forces

Porter's Five Forces (Porter, 1980) analyses the structural attractiveness of an industry by examining five competitive pressures. Applied to the global medical imaging and healthcare technology sector, the framework reveals why this is a structurally attractive industry—but one in which the rules of competition are shifting.

### 4.1 Threat of New Entrants — **LOW to MODERATE**

The capital intensity of medical imaging hardware, the regulatory approval requirements (FDA 510(k), EU MDR), and the long-term service relationships required to maintain hospital trust create formidable entry barriers. GE HealthCare holds top-three global positions: often No.1–2 in MRI and CT, top-2 in ultrasound, and market leadership in contrast media, supplying an estimated 110+ million patient doses annually. These positions took decades to build and cannot be replicated quickly.

However, the threat from software-native entrants is rising. NVIDIA and Google Health shift competitive dynamics as software-first AI companies enter the diagnostic imaging space, eroding the assumption that hardware incumbents automatically control the AI layer. These entrants do not need to manufacture scanners—they can partner with hospitals directly, potentially commoditising the hardware layer. This is why GEHC's Edison platform strategy is strategically essential: it is a defensive moat against software-native entrants.

### 4.2 Bargaining Power of Buyers — **MODERATE to HIGH**

Hospital systems and health networks are sophisticated, large-volume buyers with significant negotiating leverage. Procurement decisions for capital-intensive imaging equipment involve multi-year budget cycles, clinical evaluation committees, and competitive tendering. However, switching costs temper buyer power significantly. Scale and installed base support resilient services and parts logistics, which are high-attach, high-retention profit pools central to GE HealthCare's competitive position. Once a hospital has standardised on GEHC imaging systems, integrated PACS (Picture Archiving and Communication Systems), and trained its radiologists on GEHC interfaces, switching to Siemens or Philips carries substantial operational disruption.

Cloud-based enterprise imaging and command-centre software are shifting CAPEX to OPEX, enabling subscription models and recurring revenue, with enterprise imaging spend growth outpacing capital equipment in several health systems in 2024–25. This transition to SaaS-like models further embeds GEHC into hospital workflows, reducing buyer power over time—but only if GEHC executes the platform strategy successfully.

### 4.3 Bargaining Power of Suppliers — **LOW to MODERATE**

GEHC's global manufacturing network across 43 facilities and its scale in contrast media production give it leverage over most input suppliers. However, the concentration of semiconductor and rare-earth magnet supply chains creates pockets of vulnerability. There is a trend toward regional production and supply chain diversification, as companies want to avoid being too exposed to tariff shocks or political risk. This is precisely the risk GEHC faces with its China-exposed supply chain, and the tariff impact of $375 million in 2025 is a direct consequence of insufficient supply-chain diversification.

### 4.4 Threat of Substitutes — **LOW**

There is no functional substitute for MRI, CT, or PET imaging in the diagnosis of cancer, neurological conditions, or cardiovascular disease. Point-of-care ultrasound and AI-assisted diagnostics from smartphone-based tools represent partial substitutes at the lower end of the market, but they cannot replace high-resolution cross-sectional imaging for complex diagnosis. The threat of substitutes is therefore structurally low, which is a key reason the medical imaging industry generates persistently high margins.

### 4.5 Competitive Rivalry — **HIGH**

The intensity of rivalry among the top three incumbents—GEHC, Siemens Healthineers, and Philips—is high and increasing. Siemens Healthineers excels in medical imaging, laboratory diagnostics, and advanced therapies, reporting net revenue of €22.36 billion (approximately $24.44 billion USD) for fiscal year 2024, a 3% increase from FY2023. Siemens is larger than GEHC by revenue and has been investing aggressively in software. The Varian acquisition strengthened Siemens' integrated oncology workflows, creating a hardware-plus-software edge over GE HealthCare's partnership-based approach.

At the lower end, Fujifilm and Mindray compete aggressively on price and portable ultrasound solutions, eroding premium share in emerging and cost-sensitive markets.

**Five Forces Summary:** The medical imaging industry is structurally attractive (low substitution threat, moderate entry barriers, moderate supplier power), but rivalry is intensifying at both the premium end (Siemens, Philips) and the value end (Mindray, Fujifilm). The critical strategic insight from this analysis is that **the basis of competition is shifting from hardware performance to platform ecosystem**—and the firm that wins the software and AI layer will determine the distribution of industry profits over the next decade.

## 5. Internal Capability Audit: The Resource-Based View

The Resource-Based View (Barney, 1991; Wernerfelt, 1984) holds that sustainable competitive advantage derives from resources and capabilities that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN). The following audit applies this framework to GEHC's key assets.

### 5.1 Installed Base — VRIN ✓✓✓✓

GE HealthCare's installed base exceeds 4 million systems, driving high-margin recurring revenue that represents nearly 50% of total revenue. This is arguably GEHC's most powerful strategic asset. It is:

- **Valuable**: It generates recurring service and consumable revenue and creates the data network effects that power AI model training.
- **Rare**: Siemens and Philips have comparable scale, but no new entrant can replicate a 4-million-device installed base.
- **Inimitable**: Building this base took 130 years of commercial relationships, service infrastructure, and clinical trust.
- **Non-substitutable**: No alternative asset provides equivalent access to hospital workflows and patient data.

GE HealthCare's route to winning is based on breadth, installed-base leverage, workflow integration, and clinical credibility, aiming to win by offering hospitals and clinicians a combination of advanced hardware, service reliability, digital tools, AI-enabled workflow, and diagnostic agents that fit together in real clinical use.

### 5.2 AI and Digital Innovation Capability — VRIN ✓✓✓~

The company has secured over 80 FDA 510(k) clearances for AI-enabled devices as of early 2025. The flagship product, AIR Recon DL, exemplifies this capability: as of mid-2024, at least 3.5 million patients worldwide have been scanned using AIR Recon DL, while a study of its use with 3D and PROPELLER sequences showed reductions in exam times by as much as 50%. A 50% reduction in scan time is a clinically and economically significant outcome that directly addresses hospital capacity constraints.

A substantial R&D investment of over $1.3 billion annually in 2024 fuels a robust innovation pipeline, with over 25 new product launches planned through 2028. This investment rate sustains the innovation pipeline but is not fully "inimitable" in isolation—Siemens and Philips invest at comparable rates. The differentiating factor is the combination of AI capability with the installed base's data advantage: GEHC's models are trained on a larger and more diverse dataset than any new entrant can access.

### 5.3 Pharmaceutical Diagnostics (PDx) Segment — VRIN ✓✓✓✓

The PDx segment—contrast media and radiopharmaceuticals—is frequently underappreciated but represents a powerful moat. GE HealthCare holds market leadership in contrast media, supplying an estimated 110+ million patient doses annually. Pharmaceutical Diagnostics produced approximately $2.7 billion USD (~13% of revenue), reflecting high barriers to entry and steady reagent demand.

This segment is strategically valuable for three reasons: (i) it generates recurring consumable revenue independent of capital equipment cycles; (ii) it has high barriers to entry due to manufacturing complexity and regulatory approval; and (iii) it positions GEHC in the fast-growing radiopharmaceutical therapeutic space. The Sumitomo agreement is expected to bolster GE HealthCare's Pharmaceutical Diagnostics segment and demonstrates its commitment to shaping the future of Molecular Imaging.

### 5.4 Post-Spin-Off Structural Weaknesses

Not all of GEHC's internal characteristics are strengths. The spin-off created structural costs that a fully integrated parent would not have borne. In connection with the Spin-Off, GE HealthCare entered into a Transition Services Agreement, a Tax Matters Agreement, an Employee Matters Agreement, a Trademark License Agreement, and Intellectual Property Cross-Licence Agreements with GE. These agreements impose ongoing costs and dependencies that erode the financial independence the spin-off was intended to create.

Furthermore, GEHC carries significant debt taken on at separation, serviced by operating cash flows, and material pension obligations—creating financial constraints on the pace of investment and acquisition.

**RBV Summary:** GEHC's installed base and PDx segment are genuinely VRIN-compliant resources that underpin durable competitive advantage. Its AI capability is valuable and rare but not yet fully inimitable, since rivals are investing comparably. The strategic imperative, therefore, is to **fuse** the AI capability with the installed base's data advantage to create a network-effect moat that rivals cannot replicate—before they close the gap.

## 6. Strategic Position Synthesis: SWOT

Drawing together the PESTEL and Five Forces analyses with the RBV audit, the following SWOT matrix synthesises GEHC's strategic position. The key analytical discipline here is that each SWOT element must be connected to the preceding analysis—not listed in isolation.

| | **Helpful** | **Harmful** |
|---|---|---|
| **Internal** | **Strengths** | **Weaknesses** |
| | 4M+ installed base → recurring revenue moat (RBV) | China revenue concentration → 15% decline in 2024 |
| | 80+ FDA-cleared AI tools → clinical differentiation (Tech PESTEL) | Post-spin-off debt and TSA costs → constrained capital allocation |
| | PDx segment: 110M+ contrast doses → high-barrier recurring revenue | Margin gap vs. Siemens Healthineers → pressure on profitability |
| | $1.3B+ annual R&D → 25+ new products through 2028 | Implementation lag in complex multi-site AI deployments |
| **External** | **Opportunities** | **Threats** |
| | AI imaging market CAGR 25.8% → platform monetisation | US–China tariffs: $375M cost in 2025 (Political PESTEL) |

| | Emerging markets (India, Southeast Asia) → geographic diversification | Regulatory divergence (FDA vs. EU MDR) → product launch delays |

**The core strategic tension** that emerges from this SWOT is a **time-pressure problem**: GEHC has the assets (installed base, AI pipeline, PDx moat) to win the platform competition, but the China headwind and tariff costs are eroding the financial capacity to invest at the speed required. If GEHC does not accelerate its platform strategy now, Siemens—with greater scale and a fully integrated oncology suite—will establish a software ecosystem lock-in that GEHC's hardware advantages cannot overcome.

## 7. Strategic Options and Recommendations

### 7.1 Strategic Options: Ansoff's Growth Matrix

Ansoff's matrix (Ansoff, 1957) provides a structured framework for evaluating growth options along two dimensions: markets (existing vs. new) and products/services (existing vs. new). Applied to GEHC's situation, four strategic options emerge.

**Option A — Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets):** Deepen service contract penetration and AI software upsell within the existing 4-million-device installed base. Nearly 45% of revenue is recurring from long-term service contracts, contrast media and consumables, and SaaS. Raising this to 55–60% would materially improve revenue quality and margin resilience. This is the lowest-risk option and should be pursued as a baseline.

**Option B — Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets):** Accelerate the Edison AI platform from device-level tools to an enterprise-wide clinical intelligence platform. GE HealthCare's Command Centre Software Platform provides a real-time and predictive control system for ongoing patient care orchestration, integrating streaming data from the EMR and other source systems and applying AI to put crucial insights at the hands of caregivers and leaders. Scaling this from individual hospitals to integrated health systems is the highest-leverage product development opportunity.

**Option C — Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets):** Redirect commercial investment from China toward high-growth emerging markets. Emerging markets like India, Southeast Asia, and other parts of Asia might benefit from companies rethinking where and how they serve global markets. GEHC already has partnerships in Brazil and Saudi Arabia; expanding this model to India and Southeast Asia would diversify geographic risk.

**Option D — Diversification (New Products, New Markets):** Expand into radiopharmaceutical therapeutics—using the PDx segment's manufacturing and regulatory expertise as a platform for therapeutic (not just diagnostic) radiopharmaceuticals. The Sumitomo agreement bolsters GE HealthCare's Pharmaceutical Diagnostics segment and demonstrates its commitment to shaping the future of Molecular Imaging. This is the highest-risk option but also the highest-potential, as the radiopharmaceutical therapeutics market is growing rapidly.

### 7.2 Evaluating Options: SAF Test

Each option is evaluated against Johnson, Scholes & Whittington's (2017) SAF criteria: **Suitability** (does it address the strategic situation?), **Acceptability** (is it acceptable to stakeholders?), and **Feasibility** (can it be executed with available resources?).

| Option | Suitability | Acceptability | Feasibility | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| A: Market Penetration | ✓ Directly monetises installed base | ✓ Low risk; shareholders supportive | ✓ Requires commercial execution, not capital | **Pursue immediately** |
| B: Product Development | ✓✓ Addresses platform competition threat | ✓ High R&D spend already committed | ~ Requires technology integration capability | **Priority investment** |
| C: Market Development | ✓✓ Reduces China concentration risk | ✓ Investors want geographic diversification | ✓ Existing manufacturing and partnerships | **Pursue in parallel** |
| D: Diversification | ✓ Captures radiopharmaceutical growth | ~ Requires investor patience for long payback | ~ Requires regulatory and manufacturing investment | **Selective/phased** |

## 8. Integrated Recommendations

The following action plan is designed as a coherent chain, not a list of independent suggestions. Each recommendation flows from the preceding analysis and connects to the next.

### Recommendation 1: Accelerate the "Platform-First" Revenue Model (Options A + B)

**The logic chain:** The Five Forces analysis shows that the basis of competition is shifting to software ecosystems (§4). The RBV analysis shows GEHC's installed base is its most defensible asset (§5.1). The SWOT identifies software-native entrants as an existential threat (§6). Therefore, the highest-priority strategic action is to convert the installed base from a hardware revenue source into a software and data platform—before rivals establish ecosystem lock-in.

**Specifically:** GEHC should invest to accelerate the Edison platform into a full enterprise clinical intelligence subscription, targeting the Command Centre and AI diagnostics layer. Cloud-based enterprise imaging and command-centre software are shifting CAPEX to OPEX, enabling subscription models and recurring revenue, with enterprise imaging spend growth outpacing capital equipment in several health systems in 2024–25. This transition is already underway in the market; GEHC must lead it, not follow it.

**Target:** Raise recurring revenue from ~45% to 55% of total revenue within three years, primarily through multi-year AI platform subscriptions attached to existing hardware contracts.

### Recommendation 2: Execute Deliberate China De-risking and Geographic Rebalancing (Option C)

**The logic chain:** The PESTEL analysis identifies China as a compound political and economic risk (§3.1). The financial data confirms a 15% revenue decline in 2024 (§2). The company is already exploring a partial divestiture of its China unit. GE Healthcare is exploring selling a stake in its China unit amid economic and political challenges affecting foreign businesses, with the company's China revenue declining by 15% in 2024 due to weakened sales and tariffs, prompting supply chain adjustments to tariff-friendly locations. This is the right direction, but it must be paired with active redeployment of commercial resources toward India, Southeast Asia, and the Middle East.

**Specifically:** (i) Proceed with a partial stake sale in the China joint venture to Sinopharm (the existing JV partner), retaining a minority position that preserves market access without full exposure; (ii) simultaneously establish dedicated commercial teams for India and Southeast Asia, leveraging existing partnerships in Brazil and Saudi Arabia as a template; (iii) accelerate supply chain diversification away from China-dependent components to mitigate the $375 million tariff impact.

### Recommendation 3: Invest Selectively in Radiopharmaceutical Therapeutics (Option D, phased)

**The logic chain:** The PDx segment is identified in the RBV analysis as a VRIN-compliant asset (§5.3). The Sumitomo agreement signals GEHC's intent to expand in molecular imaging. The strategic logic is that GEHC can leverage its existing regulatory expertise, manufacturing network, and clinical relationships to enter radiopharmaceutical therapeutics—a market where it already has a beachhead through diagnostics.

**Specifically:** Allocate a defined portion of the $1.3 billion R&D budget to radiopharmaceutical therapeutics, prioritising oncology applications where GEHC's imaging installed base provides a natural commercial pathway. Structure this as a phased investment with clear milestones, avoiding the capital overcommitment that would strain the post-spin-off balance sheet.

### Recommendation 4: Resolve the Post-Spin-Off Cost Structure (Enabling Condition)

**The logic chain:** All three strategic recommendations above require capital. The post-spin-off TSA costs and debt burden constrain capital allocation. This is not a growth strategy but an enabling condition: without resolving the cost structure, GEHC cannot fund the platform investment or geographic expansion at the required pace.

**Specifically:** (i) Accelerate the exit from remaining Transition Services Agreements with GE to eliminate the associated costs; (ii) pursue the margin expansion programme ("Heartbeat" lean operating system) with discipline—GE HealthCare's broad service capabilities and dedication to quality and integrity are supported by a strong operational culture and lean business system, Heartbeat, which generates revenue from the sale of medical devices, consumable products, service capabilities, and AI-enabled solutions. Heartbeat should be used to fund the platform investment through productivity savings, not just to improve margins for their own sake.

## 9. Conclusion

GE HealthCare Technologies enters the mid-2020s with a rare combination of structural advantages—a 4-million-device installed base, a leading AI imaging portfolio, and a high-barrier pharmaceutical diagnostics segment—and a set of structural vulnerabilities that could, if unaddressed, convert those advantages into liabilities. The China revenue erosion is not a temporary setback; it is a signal that geographic concentration is a strategic risk. The tariff-driven cost shock is not an anomaly; it is a preview of a world in which geopolitical fragmentation will persistently complicate global medtech supply chains.

The analytical chain of this case study leads to a single, coherent strategic conclusion: **GEHC must use its window of installed-base advantage to win the platform competition before software-native rivals commoditise the hardware layer.** This means accelerating the Edison AI platform into a full enterprise subscription model, rebalancing geographic exposure away from China toward India and Southeast Asia, and selectively investing in radiopharmaceutical therapeutics as a long-term growth vector.

The suitability of these recommendations is established by the PESTEL and Five Forces analyses. Their acceptability is supported by the alignment with shareholder expectations for margin expansion and revenue quality improvement. Their feasibility is grounded in the RBV audit, which confirms GEHC has the installed base, AI capability, and PDx infrastructure to execute—provided it resolves the post-spin-off cost structure that currently constrains capital allocation.

The strategic situation is urgent but not dire. The assets are there. The question is whether management will deploy them with sufficient speed and coherence to convert a formidable position into an enduring one.
