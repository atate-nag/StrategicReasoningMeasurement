# STRATEGIC CASE STUDY
## **"Diamonds in the Dust: Anglo American's De Beers Divestiture and the Quest for Portfolio Coherence"**

---

## ABSTRACT

This case study examines the strategic situation facing Anglo American plc following its rejection of BHP Group's £39 billion takeover bid in May 2024 and its subsequent announcement that it would divest De Beers — its iconic diamond subsidiary — as part of the most radical restructuring in the company's century-long history. Using Porter's Five Forces, VRIN/VRIO resource analysis, the BCG portfolio matrix, PESTEL, stakeholder mapping, and the SAF framework, the analysis demonstrates that divestiture is the correct strategic choice — but that the *manner* of divestiture carries risks that, if mismanaged, could undermine Anglo American's broader transformation and destabilise a fragile diamond market. A concrete set of recommendations is offered to manage this transition.

---

## 1. INTRODUCTION AND SITUATIONAL OVERVIEW

Anglo American plc, headquartered in London, is one of the world's largest diversified mining companies, with operations spanning copper, iron ore, platinum group metals, diamonds, and crop nutrients. For decades, its 85% ownership of De Beers — the world's most famous diamond company — was considered a crown jewel of its portfolio, a source of brand prestige, and a significant revenue contributor.

That relationship unravelled with startling speed between 2023 and 2025. As part of a strategy to fend off an approach from BHP Group in 2024, the company unveiled a radical restructuring plan that included exiting diamonds, coal and platinum. Anglo American CEO Duncan Wanblad confirmed the sale of De Beers is "progressing" with multiple consortium bids. Anglo expects completion within six months as the company refocuses on copper and iron ore.

This case study analyses that strategic situation in depth, using De Beers as the primary unit of analysis while situating it within Anglo American's broader corporate strategy.

## 2. BACKGROUND: DE BEERS AND ANGLO AMERICAN

### 2.1 Historical Context

De Beers was founded in 1888 and, for most of the twentieth century, operated as one of the most powerful monopolies in commercial history, at one point controlling up to 85% of the world's rough diamond supply. Its "A Diamond is Forever" campaign, launched in 1948, is widely regarded as the most successful advertising slogan of the twentieth century, fundamentally shaping consumer psychology around diamonds as symbols of love, permanence, and status.

### 2.2 The Debswana Complexity

A critical structural feature of De Beers is its joint venture with the Government of Botswana: Debswana Diamond Company, in which both parties hold a 50% share. Debswana controls all diamond mining in Botswana and combined production of the company's four mines totalled 30 million carats, nearly a quarter of the world's annual production. Separately, Botswana holds a 15% direct equity stake in De Beers through its Debswana joint venture, which controls approximately 70% of De Beers' total output. For Botswana, diamonds are not merely a commercial commodity — they represent approximately 80% of the country's total export earnings and contribute roughly 30% of national GDP, making De Beers ownership a question of economic sovereignty.

### 2.3 The Sightholder System

Trading of rough diamonds takes place through two channels — De Beers Global Sightholder Sales (GSS) and De Beers Auction Sales. GSS sells about 90% of De Beers's rough diamonds by value, featuring wholly owned and joint venture operations in South Africa, Botswana, and Namibia. This system historically gave De Beers extraordinary pricing power and market intelligence.

## 3. THE STRATEGIC PROBLEM

The strategic problem facing Anglo American can be articulated as follows:

> **How should Anglo American manage the separation of De Beers — a structurally impaired but historically iconic asset — in a way that maximises value for its shareholders, preserves its relationship with the Botswana government, and enables its transformation into a focused, high-margin mining company, all while navigating a deeply distressed diamond market?**

This problem has four interlocking dimensions:
1. **Portfolio logic** — Does De Beers still fit within Anglo American's strategic architecture?
2. **Market disruption** — Is the natural diamond industry structurally impaired or merely cyclically depressed?
3. **Stakeholder complexity** — How should Anglo American manage Botswana's sovereign interests in the sale process?
4. **Execution risk** — What are the risks of selling a loss-making asset in a distressed market, and how can they be mitigated?

## 4. EXTERNAL ENVIRONMENT: PESTEL ANALYSIS

### Political
Resource nationalism is intensifying across sub-Saharan Africa. Botswana's president reiterated his plan to acquire a majority stake in De Beers, as Anglo American looks to offload its controlling interest in the iconic diamond company. "Government will leverage a majority stake," Duma Boko said, adding: "Concrete steps are under way towards the acquisition of Anglo-American shares in De Beers." Simultaneously, Angola has formally offered to acquire Anglo American Plc's 85% stake in De Beers, positioning itself against Botswana and global investor groups in one of the most high-stakes resource negotiations on the continent. State-owned gem producer Endiama EP confirmed that it submitted "a concrete and well-defined proposal" to Anglo American.

### Economic
The financial damage has been severe and compounding. Anglo American booked a $2.3 billion impairment on De Beers in 2025, following a $2.9 billion writedown in 2024. The group plans to complete a structured sale of De Beers in 2026 as part of a broader portfolio overhaul. The impairment slashed De Beers' carrying value to $2.3 billion from over $4 billion, bringing total writedowns over three years to $6.8 billion. Anglo posted a USD3.1 billion net loss in 2024, against a $283 million profit in 2023. Despite the results, shares in the company rose on the news, increasing as much as 4% during early trading in London. Anglo American values De Beers at about $5 billion, though UBS analysts estimate the final sale price could range from $3 billion to $4 billion given soft market conditions.

### Socio-Cultural
Consumer preferences are shifting in key markets. Between 2021 and 2024, India's diamond exports to China declined from over $6.5 billion to around $3.3 billion. The fall was driven by a combination of factors — including a consumer shift towards gold, macroeconomic uncertainty, and post-pandemic slowdowns in retail spending. However, there are nascent recovery signals: Chinese and Indian demand for luxury diamonds is projected to grow by 8–10% annually, driven by rising disposable incomes.

### Technological
Lab-grown diamonds (LGDs) represent the most disruptive technological force in the industry's history. Launched in 2018, Lightbox distinguished LGDs from natural diamonds, highlighting their unique attributes and differing values by offering transparent linear pricing of $800/carat. However, LGD wholesale prices in the jewellery sector have since plummeted by 90%, aligning more with a cost-plus model. Paradoxically, this price collapse has clarified the market: "Customers clearly see now that natural diamonds and lab-grown diamonds are two entirely different things." In response, De Beers Group has decided to discontinue Lightbox, its lab-grown diamond jewellery brand. The decision reflects the company's clear shift back to focusing on natural diamonds — where it believes long-term value still holds strong. The planned shutdown of Lightbox is a strategic step in De Beers Group's Origins Strategy, introduced in May 2024, to concentrate on high-return activities and streamline operations.

### Environmental
Anglo American's Sustainable Mining Plan commits to net-zero carbon emissions by 2040, and De Beers' Tracr blockchain provenance platform differentiates its natural diamonds as ethically sourced — a potential competitive advantage as ESG scrutiny intensifies.

### Legal / Regulatory
Any sale of De Beers requires regulatory approval across multiple jurisdictions (South Africa, Botswana, Namibia, Canada, and potentially the EU and US). President Duma Boko's commitment to "full control over this strategic national asset and the entire value chain including marketing" represents a fundamental challenge to Anglo American's preferred exit strategy. Botswana's position reflects broader resource nationalism trends where commodity-rich nations seek greater control over value chains and revenue streams.

**PESTEL Summary:** The macro-environment for De Beers is characterised by severe near-term economic headwinds, structural technological disruption from LGDs, intensifying political complexity driven by resource nationalism, and only modest medium-term demand recovery prospects. The overall environment strongly supports Anglo American's decision to exit the diamond business.

## 5. INDUSTRY ANALYSIS: PORTER'S FIVE FORCES

Porter's Five Forces is applied to the *natural rough diamond industry* in which De Beers operates.

### Force 1: Threat of New Entrants — MODERATE TO HIGH
Historically, this threat was low due to enormous capital requirements and De Beers' near-monopolistic supply control. This barrier has been fundamentally eroded. The rise of lab-grown diamond producers and online retailers poses a significant threat to De Beers' traditional business model. The company faces increasing challenges from disruptive innovation, emerging markets, and changing consumer preferences.

### Force 2: Bargaining Power of Suppliers — LOW TO MODERATE (but politically elevated)
De Beers is vertically integrated, reducing dependence on external suppliers. However, the Debswana JV structure means that Botswana, as a 50% JV partner controlling 70% of production, holds extraordinary quasi-supplier power — a form of political leverage that no commercial supplier could match.

### Force 3: Bargaining Power of Buyers — INCREASING
The sightholder system historically suppressed buyer power. However, as De Beers' market share has declined and alternative rough diamond sources have expanded, sightholders now have credible alternatives. The "bullwhip" effect occurred as producers and traders frantically restocked depleted inventories following strong demand in 2021 and early 2022. The oversupply of new goods led rough diamond buyers to drastically reduce purchases by the end of 2023.

### Force 4: Threat of Substitutes — HIGH (Most Transformative Force)
LGDs are chemically and physically identical to natural diamonds. De Beers reported a 21% year-on-year drop in first-half rough sales to $2.83 billion, while its sales volume was flat. In the first half of 2024, De Beers' sales were 20% lower than the same time the previous year. It has "cut production from its mines by about 20% from [2023] levels and reduced prices at its most recent sale."

### Force 5: Competitive Rivalry — INCREASING
The market endured a second consecutive year of crisis, indicating that its problems were deep-seated. Anglo American put De Beers up for sale, signaling the parent company's poor outlook for the industry.

**Five Forces Summary:** The structural attractiveness of the natural diamond industry has deteriorated significantly. The combination of a high substitution threat, increasing buyer power, and intensifying rivalry — against a backdrop of a declining market share for De Beers — strongly supports Anglo American's decision to exit.

5. **Stakeholder Analysis** identifies Botswana as the pivotal stakeholder whose alignment is necessary — and whose economic dependency on diamonds makes it simultaneously the most powerful and most motivated party in the transaction.

6. **SAF Framework** validates the trade sale as the preferred strategic option, while identifying the valuation gap and Botswana's governance requirements as the principal execution risks.

The logical conclusion is that Anglo American must complete the De Beers divestiture — but the *manner* of execution is as strategically important as the decision itself.

## 11. RECOMMENDATIONS AND ACTION PLAN

### Recommendation 1: Pursue a Structured Trade Sale with Botswana as Anchor Partner
**Rationale:** The SAF analysis confirms the trade sale as the only viable option. The key to unlocking a transaction at an acceptable valuation is to resolve the Botswana question first, before running a competitive bidding process.

**Action Steps:**
- Engage Botswana in a bilateral pre-negotiation to establish the governance structure for a post-sale De Beers *before* inviting third-party bids. This prevents Botswana from using its veto power as a negotiating weapon mid-process.
- Structure the deal as a **consortium**: Botswana acquires a majority stake (e.g., 51%), with Anglo American retaining a transitional minority stake of 10–15% to provide continuity, and a third-party financial investor (e.g., the Qatari-backed Gareth Penny consortium) providing capital and commercial expertise.
- Include a **"governance compact"** that protects the sightholder system and De Beers' marketing independence from political interference — a critical safeguard given that Botswana's interest is in revenue maximisation, not brand stewardship.

### Recommendation 2: Protect the Sightholder System and Brand Architecture During Transition
**Rationale:** The sightholder distribution system and the De Beers brand are the two most inimitable assets in the business (as confirmed by VRIN analysis). Any disruption to the sightholder network during the ownership transition would destroy value that cannot be recovered.

**Action Steps:**
- Include **binding covenants** in the sale agreement requiring the new owner to maintain the sightholder system for a minimum of five years post-completion.
- Establish a **De Beers Brand Council** (including independent directors and Botswana government representatives) to oversee brand strategy and prevent short-term value extraction at the expense of long-term brand equity.
- Protect the **Origins Strategy** — De Beers' refocus on natural diamond provenance and the discontinuation of Lightbox — as a non-negotiable element of the sale framework. The planned shutdown of Lightbox is a strategic step in De Beers Group's Origins Strategy, introduced in May 2024, to concentrate on high-return activities and streamline operations. Lower demand and uncertainties regarding tariffs also contributed to the company's decision. This strategy is the correct response to LGD disruption and must be preserved under new ownership.

### Recommendation 3: Reinvest Divestiture Proceeds in Copper Growth
**Rationale:** The BCG analysis identifies copper as Anglo American's Star business. The energy transition is creating a structural demand uplift for copper that is likely to persist for decades.

**Action Steps:**
- Allocate the majority of De Beers sale proceeds (estimated $3–4 billion) to accelerating the copper growth pipeline. Anglo will allocate US$678 million in 2024 to ensure the start-up of the desalination plant in 2026 and advance studies that seek to add 25,000 tonnes per year of copper to its attributable production between 2025 and 2028.
- Pursue the Woodsmith crop nutrients project as a secondary investment priority, consistent with the food security megatrend and Anglo's stated portfolio focus.
- Resist the temptation to return all sale proceeds to shareholders via buybacks; the copper growth opportunity is a higher-return use of capital in the current environment.

### Recommendation 4: Bridge the Valuation Gap Through Creative Deal Structuring
**Rationale:** The gap between Anglo American's ~$5 billion valuation and the market's $3–4 billion estimate is a significant obstacle to completing the transaction. Rather than accepting a distressed price, Anglo American should use creative deal structures to bridge this gap without delaying the strategic transformation.

**Action Steps:**
- Include an **earnout mechanism** linked to diamond price recovery: the buyer pays a base price of $3.5 billion now, with contingent consideration of up to $1.5 billion payable if diamond prices recover to specified benchmarks within five years. This aligns buyer and seller incentives and avoids crystallising the full loss at the trough of the market cycle.
- Retain a **royalty interest** on De Beers' production revenues, giving Anglo American upside participation in any market recovery without the operational burden of ownership.
- Consider a **vendor financing arrangement** for Botswana's portion of the acquisition, funded by De Beers' future dividends, reducing the immediate cash requirement for the government and making the transaction more politically viable.

### Recommendation 5: Communicate the Strategic Transformation Clearly to Capital Markets
**Rationale:** Anglo posted a USD3.1 billion net loss in 2024, against a $283 million profit in 2023. Despite the results, shares in the company rose on the news, increasing as much as 4% during early trading in London. This signals that capital markets are rewarding the strategic direction, not the near-term financials. This investor confidence must be maintained through consistent, transparent communication.

**Action Steps:**
- Provide quarterly updates on the De Beers sale process to prevent information vacuums that invite speculation and depress the share price.
- Articulate a clear **"New Anglo American" investment thesis** centred on copper, iron ore, and crop nutrients — three commodities with compelling demand drivers (energy transition, urbanisation, food security).
- Set specific, measurable targets for the post-transformation portfolio: e.g., copper production growth of 15–20% by 2027, a structurally lower cost base, and EBITDA margin improvement targets.
- Address the **BHP overhang** explicitly: demonstrate through the transformation that Anglo American's standalone value exceeds what BHP's bid implied, thereby reducing the risk of a renewed hostile approach.

## 13. CONCLUSION

The strategic situation facing Anglo American with respect to De Beers is one of the most consequential corporate divestiture decisions in the global mining industry in recent years. The analysis presented in this case study demonstrates, through a coherent chain of evidence-based reasoning, that:

1. The natural diamond industry has undergone a **structural — not merely cyclical — deterioration**, driven by LGD disruption, demand shifts in China, and the collapse of De Beers' market share from historical dominance to approximately 30–35% of global rough diamond supply by value.

2. De Beers, while possessing genuinely rare and inimitable resources (brand, sightholder system, world-class mining assets), is **no longer organisationally well-served by Anglo American's ownership**, which has become misaligned with De Beers' strategic needs as Anglo pivots to copper and iron ore.

3. Anglo American's **portfolio logic** — as confirmed by BCG analysis — demands divestiture of this "Dog" asset and redeployment of capital into "Star" businesses, particularly copper, which sits at the intersection of the energy transition megatrend and a structural supply deficit.

4. The **trade sale is the only viable strategic option**, but its execution requires sophisticated stakeholder management — particularly with Botswana — and creative deal structuring to bridge the valuation gap.

5. The recommendations offered — a Botswana-anchored consortium structure, sightholder system protection covenants, earnout mechanisms, reinvestment in copper, and transparent capital markets communication — constitute an **integrated action plan** that addresses both the strategic imperative and the execution risks.

Anglo American CEO Duncan Wanblad articulated the goal as "a highly attractive and differentiated investment proposition with a structurally lower cost base" and "a higher-margin and more cash generative Anglo American" that offers "greater resilience through the cycle." The analysis in this case study supports that vision entirely — and provides a rigorous, evidence-based roadmap for realising it. Sometimes the most value-creating decision a company can make is to let go of an iconic asset that no longer belongs in its portfolio, and to deploy that capital where the structural tailwinds are strongest. For Anglo American, that place is copper — not diamonds.
