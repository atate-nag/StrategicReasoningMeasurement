# Diageo PLC: Strategic Case Study
## *From Inventory Hangover to Structural Renewal — Navigating the Post-Pandemic Spirits Correction*

---

## 1. Introduction and Case Overview

Diageo was created in 1997 through the merger of two British companies: Guinness, known for its iconic stout beer, and Grand Metropolitan, a conglomerate. From that foundation, it grew into the world's largest premium spirits company. Yet by 2024, the company faced one of the most serious strategic crises in its history — a convergence of self-inflicted supply-chain misjudgements, macroeconomic headwinds, and a structural shift in consumer behaviour that together caused its share price to halve from its 2021 peak.

Diageo DGE stock is now worth half what it was at the end of 2021, when its shares peaked at over £40. Since that high, the company has been on a downward tear through 2023 and 2024, with shares falling 54.45% since then.

This case study examines the strategic situation Diageo faces, analyses it through a suite of established frameworks — PESTEL, Porter's Five Forces, the Resource-Based View (RBV/VRIN), the Ansoff Growth Matrix, and McKinsey's Three Horizons — and concludes with a coherent, evidence-based set of strategic recommendations.

The central strategic problem is this: **Diageo over-indexed its growth strategy on a single high-growth category (premium tequila) and a single high-growth region (Latin America & Caribbean), mismanaged the resulting inventory cycle, and now faces both a near-term recovery challenge and a longer-term structural question about whether its premiumisation-led strategy remains viable in an era of consumer downtrading and moderation.**

## 2. Company Background and Financial Snapshot

Diageo's strategic report 2024 notes that it holds number one global positions in scotch, vodka, tequila, Canadian whisky, liqueurs and gin, with iconic brands including Johnnie Walker, Smirnoff, Guinness, Don Julio, Crown Royal, Baileys and Tanqueray.

Diageo is the third largest player in total beverage alcohol (TBA) by value globally, number one in International Spirits in retail sales value globally, 1.4x larger than its nearest International Spirits competitor, and in the premium plus price tiers even larger still. It has thirteen billion-dollar brands across its largest categories.

Despite this formidable position, FY2024 (year ended 30 June 2024) told a sobering story. Diageo's FY24 sales were dragged down by its Latin America and Caribbean region (LAC), where reported net sales declined 15% and organic net sales declined 21%. Organic net sales declined $129 million or 0.6%, with positive price/mix of 2.9 percentage points more than offset by a 3.5% volume decline, primarily driven by a 21.1% decline in LAC. Organic operating profit declined by $304 million or 4.8%, of which $302 million was attributable to LAC; organic operating margin contracted 130 basis points.

By FY2025, a modest recovery was underway, but the structural questions remained. In FY25, Diageo reported 1.7% organic net sales growth and a 0.7% organic operating profit decline, in line with guidance.

## 3. The Strategic Situation: A Multi-Layered Crisis

### 3.1 The Proximate Cause: The Latin America Inventory Debacle

The most visible element of Diageo's crisis was the collapse in its Latin America and Caribbean (LAC) region. The root cause was a strategic miscalculation during the pandemic boom.

In 2021, Diageo committed $500 million to expand tequila production in Mexico, aiming to grow in a category where it saw demand rising. But the company's expectations proved too optimistic. By 2023, excess inventory had built up in Latin America — particularly in Mexico and Brazil — as consumers shifted toward cheaper products.

This was a textbook case of the **bullwhip effect**: pandemic-era demand spikes led distributors to over-order, Diageo responded by ramping production capacity, and when underlying consumption normalised, the entire channel was left holding excess stock. The decline in LAC was driven by a strong double-digit net sales growth comparator as well as lower consumption and consumer downtrading due to macroeconomic pressures in the region.

Diageo's FY24 sales were hit by poor performance for Scotch (particularly Buchanan's, Johnnie Walker Black Label and Johnnie Walker Red Label) and a double-digit decline in tequila in Mexico. The market-level consequences were severe: Diageo PLC shares staggered over 13% lower to 2,808.5p, around three-year lows, as the Smirnoff and Guinness maker warned of slower growth, with organic sales in LAC, which represent nearly 11% of Diageo's net sales value, expected to decline by more than 20% year on year in the first half of financial year 2024.

### 3.2 The Deeper Problem: A Maturing Premiumisation Thesis

Beyond the LAC inventory crisis lies a more structurally significant challenge: the global premiumisation trend that underpinned Diageo's entire strategic logic since the late 1990s is showing signs of fatigue.

According to Euromonitor's global insight manager, the industry has seen a "continued deterioration" in 2025, as macroeconomic and lifestyle factors impact the sector, with a reversal of the "almost untouchable" premiumisation trend of the past two decades in the spirits sector.

According to IWSR data, in 2024, total volumes of alcoholic beverages fell by 1%, with growth in value limited to 1%. For the first time in nearly 30 years, IWSR recorded a decline of 2% in 2023 in the volume of spirits sold in the key US market.

This matters enormously for Diageo because its entire portfolio strategy — from the 2017 acquisition of Casamigos for $1 billion to the heavy investment in Don Julio — was predicated on a secular upward shift in consumer willingness to pay for premium spirits. Sales of Casamigos Tequila fell by 20% in Diageo's full-year FY2024 results. By FY2025, the picture had not improved: Casamigos' net sales declined by 18% as a result of increased category competition driving lower demand.

### 3.3 Leadership Instability

Adding to the strategic uncertainty, Diageo suffered significant leadership disruption. Former CEO Debra Crew stepped down with immediate effect in mid-July 2025, after more than two years of relatively underwhelming group performance, with CFO Nik Jhangiani replacing her on an interim basis.

## 4. External Environment Analysis: PESTEL

**Political:** Diageo faces ongoing tariff headwinds, which were expected to add around $150 million in annual costs, with the company planning to absorb half through operational efficiencies and pass the rest on through price increases. US tariffs on UK and EU spirits imports represent a direct threat to Diageo's Scotch and Irish whiskey exports.

**Economic:** Macroeconomic pressures are bifurcating Diageo's market landscape. In developed markets, the IWSR report paints a picture of a beverage alcohol market at a crossroads — shaped by consumer downtrading, selective premiumisation, health-driven moderation, and geopolitical complexities. In emerging markets, however, the picture is more positive. In regions such as India, Diageo continued to benefit from consumer premiumisation, particularly in the prestige and above whisky segment, aided by smaller pack sizes and the potential tailwind of the UK-India Free Trade Agreement.

**Social:** A structural shift in consumer behaviour is underway. The no/low alcohol category grew +9% in volumes in 2024, gaining 61 million new consumers in two years, while RTD (ready-to-drink) cocktails are expected to grow by +400% in North America by 2029. Younger consumers are increasingly "zebra striping" — alternating between alcoholic and non-alcoholic drinks across an evening — challenging the traditional consumption model on which Diageo's volume assumptions rest.

**Technological:** AI and digital tools are reshaping marketing efficiency. Diageo achieved productivity savings in fiscal 2025 driven by AI-driven content production, Agile Brand Communities, and supply chain optimisations, with non-working development costs in advertising and promotion falling from 21% to 14%.

**Environmental:** Climate risk is a material concern. Climate-driven yield variability affected agave and barley prices in 2024–2025; tequila production costs rose materially amid significant agave price swings. Diageo's dependence on water-intensive agricultural inputs (agave, barley, grain) makes it structurally exposed to climate volatility.

**Legal/Regulatory:** Advertising restrictions, minimum unit pricing legislation, and health labelling requirements continue to tighten across key markets, most notably in Ireland and the UK, adding compliance costs and constraining marketing reach.

## 5. Industry Analysis: Porter's Five Forces

**Threat of New Entrants — LOW to MODERATE:** The premium spirits industry has formidable barriers to entry: brand heritage takes decades to build, aged spirits require long lead times (Scotch whisky must mature for at least three years, often much longer), and distribution networks are capital-intensive. Diageo is 1.4x larger than its nearest International Spirits competitor in retail sales value, and bigger than four of its top ten competitors combined — scale advantages that new entrants cannot easily replicate. However, the craft spirits movement and direct-to-consumer digital channels are gradually lowering barriers in niche categories.

**Bargaining Power of Suppliers — MODERATE:** Diageo sources agricultural products from a concentrated supplier base. The agave crisis illustrates supplier power in practice: tequila production costs rose sharply when agave yields were disrupted. However, Diageo's scale gives it leverage in most commodity markets, and long-term supply contracts partially insulate it from short-term price volatility.

**Bargaining Power of Buyers — HIGH:** Walmart, Costco, and Tesco control 59.2% of global alcohol retail distribution channels and negotiate prices aggressively, demanding volume discounts of up to 18–22% from Diageo. The consolidation of retail grocery and the growing power of on-trade chains give buyers significant leverage. This dynamic intensifies when consumer confidence is weak and retailers seek margin protection.

**Threat of Substitutes — INCREASING:** This is the most strategically significant force for Diageo's long-term positioning. The growth of no/low alcohol beverages, cannabis-infused drinks in legalised markets, premium soft drinks, and RTDs all represent substitution threats. RTD cocktails are expected to grow by +400% in North America by 2029, with Gen Z representing 15% of RTD consumers versus 12% for wine. Diageo has some exposure to RTDs through Smirnoff Ice but lacks a dominant position in this fast-growing category.

**Competitive Rivalry — HIGH:** Diageo's most prominent direct competitors include Pernod Ricard, Bacardi, Beam Suntory, Brown-Forman, Constellation Brands, LVMH's Moët Hennessy Division, Campari Group, William Grant & Sons, and Rémy Cointreau. The industry experienced broad-based destocking challenges, with Brown-Forman's CEO noting that "while our fiscal 2024 organic results reflect the inventory reductions across the entire spirits value chain, when you adjust for the changes in distributor inventory, we feel good about the results." This confirms that the destocking challenge was sector-wide, not unique to Diageo — but Diageo's LAC exposure made its experience disproportionately severe.

**Summary:** The Five Forces analysis reveals a structurally attractive industry (high barriers, scale advantages) that is currently experiencing a cyclical downturn amplified by structural headwinds (substitution, buyer power, moderation trends). The key strategic implication is that Diageo's competitive position remains fundamentally strong, but it must adapt its strategy to a market that is growing more slowly and more selectively than the post-pandemic boom suggested.

## 6. Internal Analysis: The Resource-Based View (VRIN Framework)

Barney's (1991) VRIN framework asks whether a firm's resources are **V**aluable, **R**are, **I**nimitable, and **N**on-substitutable. Diageo's resource base scores highly on most dimensions, but with important caveats.

| Resource | Valuable? | Rare? | Inimitable? | Non-substitutable? | Assessment |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Brand portfolio (Johnnie Walker, Guinness, Don Julio) | ✅ High | ✅ High | ✅ Very high | ⚠️ Moderate | Core SCA |
| Global distribution network | ✅ High | ✅ Moderate | ✅ High | ⚠️ Moderate | Core SCA |
| Aged spirit inventories | ✅ High | ✅ High | ✅ High | ✅ High | Core SCA |
| Marketing capabilities | ✅ High | ⚠️ Moderate | ⚠️ Moderate | ⚠️ Moderate | Important but replicable |
| Distributor relationships | ✅ High | ⚠️ Moderate | ⚠️ Moderate | ⚠️ Moderate | Vulnerable to mismanagement |
| Supply chain (demand forecasting) | ⚠️ Moderate | ❌ Low | ❌ Low | ❌ Low | **Demonstrated weakness** |

**Brand Portfolio as the Core SCA:** Diageo's strategic report notes that Johnnie Walker's retail sales value has increased over 400% since 2002, and Diageo brands have driven around 17% of total absolute dollar growth in the international spirits category since 2018. This is a compelling demonstration of brand-driven value creation that is genuinely hard to imitate — the heritage, liquid quality, and cultural associations of Johnnie Walker or Guinness cannot be manufactured quickly.

According to Tracksuit's data, Guinness leads the UK beer category for usage, sitting at 36%, with this figure having increased six percentage points since October 2024. Guinness's resurgence — entering its eighth consecutive half of double-digit growth, delivering 17% organic net sales growth — is the clearest current demonstration of Diageo's brand-building capability as a genuine VRIN resource.

**The Supply Chain as a Revealed Weakness:** The LAC crisis exposed a critical gap in Diageo's dynamic capabilities: its ability to sense and respond to demand signals across complex, multi-tier distribution chains. The $500 million committed to tequila production expansion in 2021 was not matched by equivalent investment in demand-sensing capability. This is not a VRIN resource; it is a strategic liability.

**Dynamic Capabilities (Teece et al., 1997):** Beyond static VRIN, firms need the ability to sense opportunities, seize them, and reconfigure resources as markets evolve. Diageo's sensing capability failed in LAC; its seizing capability was demonstrated by the Don Julio acquisition and Guinness brand investment; its reconfiguring capability is now being tested by the Accelerate programme and the Nigeria business model restructuring.

## 8. McKinsey's Three Horizons Framework

The Three Horizons framework (Baghai, Coley & White, 1999) provides a useful lens for organising Diageo's strategic priorities across different time frames.

**Horizon 1 — Defend and Extend the Core (0–2 years):**
The immediate priority is operational recovery. The Accelerate programme's savings come from reduced overheads, more targeted trade investment and A&P and supply chain efficiencies. In North America, opportunities identified through Accelerate had actually led Diageo to "put more feet on the street" to help its distributors and customers grow sales. This is the right Horizon 1 move: fix the execution before seeking new growth.

Diageo has set a target of achieving USD 625 million in cost savings over the next three years, up from an initial goal of USD 500 million. The increase in the savings target signals growing confidence in execution.

**Horizon 2 — Build Emerging Businesses (2–5 years):**
The India and Africa market development plays are classic Horizon 2 initiatives — not yet at scale, but with clear momentum and structural tailwinds. Africa delivered organic net sales growth across all markets, led by Ghana, while Latin America and the Caribbean saw improving conditions in Brazil and premiumisation-led growth in Mexico.

The no/low alcohol category also belongs here. Diageo's investment in Guinness 0.0 is a Horizon 2 bet: the 2024 annual report notes that during the 2023 St Patrick's Day push for Guinness 0.0, Diageo reported a 50% surge in sales and permanent listings in 150 additional pubs.

**Horizon 3 — Create Viable Options (5+ years):**
Diageo must monitor and selectively invest in the RTD category, AI-driven personalisation, and the intersection of spirits with wellness (functional beverages, adaptogens). These are not yet strategic priorities but represent genuine options value that should be preserved through small, exploratory investments.

## 9. The Coherent Chain: Connecting the Analysis

The analytical frameworks, taken together, tell a coherent story:

1. **The PESTEL analysis** identifies structural headwinds (moderation, downtrading, tariffs) that are compressing the total addressable market for premium spirits in developed markets, while simultaneously identifying significant growth opportunities in emerging markets.

2. **Porter's Five Forces** confirms that the industry remains structurally attractive (high barriers, brand moats) but that buyer power and substitution threats are intensifying — meaning Diageo cannot simply rely on category growth to lift all boats.

3. **The RBV/VRIN analysis** reveals that Diageo's brand assets are genuine, durable sources of competitive advantage — but that its supply chain and demand-sensing capabilities are a critical weakness that amplified the LAC crisis.

4. **The BCG Matrix** shows a portfolio with clear Stars (Don Julio, Guinness) that deserve disproportionate investment, and clear Dogs/declining Question Marks (Casamigos, Cîroc) that are consuming resources without generating adequate returns.

5. **The Ansoff Matrix** points to Market Penetration (US execution) and Market Development (India, Africa) as the optimal strategic positions — building on existing brand strengths rather than diversifying into unfamiliar territory.

6. **The Three Horizons framework** provides the temporal architecture: fix the core now (Horizon 1), build the emerging market and no/low alcohol businesses (Horizon 2), and preserve options in RTD and wellness (Horizon 3).

The chain of reasoning is: *Diageo's core problem is not its brands — it is the gap between its brand strength and its operational/commercial execution capability. The solution is not portfolio transformation but operational renewal, disciplined capital allocation, and geographic rebalancing toward markets where the premiumisation thesis still holds.*

## 10. Strategic Recommendations and Action Plan

The following recommendations are sequenced by time horizon and ordered by strategic priority.

---

### Recommendation 1: Invest Decisively in Demand-Sensing Infrastructure (Horizon 1 — Immediate)

**The Problem:** The LAC crisis was fundamentally a failure of demand visibility. Diageo did not have real-time sight of inventory levels at the distributor and retailer level in Mexico and Brazil. It continued shipping product into a channel that was already saturated.

**The Recommendation:** Diageo should implement a global **Integrated Business Planning (IBP)** system with mandatory distributor inventory reporting as a condition of trade terms. This is not a novel idea — it is standard practice in FMCG companies like Unilever and P&G. The investment should be funded from the Accelerate programme savings.

**Evidence Base:** By 2023, excess inventory had built up in Latin America — particularly in Mexico and Brazil — as consumers shifted toward cheaper products. Reports of the oversupply surfaced shortly after Debra Crew became chief executive officer in mid-2023. The fact that the problem only "surfaced" when a new CEO arrived suggests that real-time demand data was not reaching the executive level. This must change.

**Success Metric:** Distributor inventory visibility across 90% of net sales value within 18 months; early-warning triggers that automatically flag inventory-to-depletion ratios exceeding 1.3x historical norms.

---

### Recommendation 2: Rationalise the Portfolio — Divest or Restructure Casamigos (Horizon 1–2)

**The Problem:** Casamigos is consuming management attention and marketing spend in a position of declining relevance. Casamigos' net sales declined by 18% as a result of increased category competition driving lower demand. Meanwhile, Don Julio is thriving in the same category.

**The Recommendation:** Diageo faces a binary choice on Casamigos: either (a) execute a genuine turnaround by repositioning it at a lower price point to compete in the growing "accessible premium" tequila tier, or (b) divest the brand and redeploy capital into Don Julio's global rollout and the Africa/India growth agenda. Given that Diageo already has a dominant tequila brand in Don Julio, the rational choice is divestment. Running two competing premium tequila brands in the same portfolio is strategically incoherent and operationally wasteful.

**Evidence Base:** The BCG analysis places Casamigos in the "Question Mark" or declining "Dog" quadrant. The RBV analysis confirms that Casamigos does not possess the heritage and cultural embeddedness of Don Julio. The Ansoff logic of focusing on existing strengths (Don Julio) rather than attempting to revive a weakening brand (Casamigos) is compelling. Don Julio's net sales grew by 41.9% in FY2025, pushed by its cultural relevance and successful activation — demonstrating that the tequila category opportunity is real, but concentrated in brands with genuine authenticity.

**Success Metric:** A decision on Casamigos within 12 months; if retained, a clear repositioning plan with measurable market share targets; if divested, proceeds reinvested in Don Julio global expansion and emerging market infrastructure.

---

### Recommendation 3: Accelerate the Emerging Markets Rebalancing — Prioritise India and Africa (Horizon 2)

**The Problem:** Diageo is over-dependent on mature developed markets (North America ~35% of net sales) where the premiumisation thesis is stalling, and under-indexed in emerging markets where it is still structurally intact.

**The Recommendation:** Diageo should explicitly rebalance its capital allocation toward India and Africa, targeting these two regions to represent 25% of net sales by 2030 (from a combined ~15% today). This requires investment in local production capacity, route-to-market infrastructure, and locally relevant innovation (smaller pack sizes, price-accessible premium variants).

**Evidence Base:** In regions such as India, Diageo continued to benefit from consumer premiumisation, particularly in the prestige and above whisky segment, aided by smaller pack sizes and the potential tailwind of the UK-India Free Trade Agreement. Africa also delivered organic net sales growth across all markets, led by Ghana. When recovery comes, growth is expected to be relatively modest, with volume and value both rising at a CAGR of +1% between 2023 and 2028 — with the main value growth coming from India, China and the US. India is the most attractive of these three, given Diageo's existing strong position through its United Spirits subsidiary.

The Nigeria business model restructuring is a useful template: Diageo is creating a new model for Guinness and its locally manufactured RTD and MSS products in Nigeria, partnering with local expertise — an asset-light, locally anchored model that reduces capital exposure while maintaining brand presence.

**Success Metric:** India and Africa combined to reach 20% of organic net sales growth contribution by FY2027; at least two new local production partnerships established in Africa by FY2026.

### Recommendation 4: Build a Credible No/Low Alcohol Strategy (Horizon 2)

**The Problem:** Diageo's long-term volume assumptions depend on consumers continuing to drink alcohol. The moderation trend is structural, not cyclical. The no/low alcohol category grew +9% in volumes in 2024, gaining 61 million new consumers in two years. Diageo's current no/low portfolio is limited primarily to Guinness 0.0.

**The Recommendation:** Diageo should systematically extend its top five brands into the no/low alcohol category, using Guinness 0.0 as the proof-of-concept model. The brand architecture already exists; the challenge is liquid development and consumer education. Diageo should also consider a targeted acquisition in the premium non-alcoholic spirits category (e.g., Seedlip-type brands) to accelerate capability building.

**Evidence Base:** During the 2023 St Patrick's Day push for Guinness 0.0, Diageo reported a 50% surge in sales and permanent listings in 150 additional pubs, with that number set to reach over 2,000 outlets. This demonstrates that brand equity transfers effectively to no/low variants when execution is strong. The Guinness 0.0 success is not merely a product win — it is a proof point that Diageo's brand-building VRIN resource is applicable to the no/low category.

**Success Metric:** No/low alcohol variants available for at least five of Diageo's top ten brands by FY2027; no/low to represent 5% of net sales by FY2028.

---

### Recommendation 5: Resolve the Leadership Vacuum and Establish a Clear Long-Term Strategic Narrative (Horizon 1 — Urgent)

**The Problem:** After a maelstrom of problems, including profit warnings, leadership uncertainty, and economic headwinds, Diageo stock has fallen 27.49% in the year to date, heading for its worst one-year share price performance ever. Leadership instability is not merely a governance issue — it is a strategic one. Investors, employees, and distribution partners all require strategic clarity.

**The Recommendation:** The Board should appoint a permanent CEO within six months, with a mandate to articulate a five-year strategic plan that clearly answers three questions: (1) What is Diageo's theory of growth in a post-premiumisation-boom world? (2) How will it rebalance its geographic exposure? (3) What is its response to the moderation trend? The Accelerate programme is a necessary but insufficient answer — it is an operational plan, not a strategic vision.

**Evidence Base:** Investors are keen to get updates on the search for a longer-term successor, with full-year underlying operating profits expected to decline slightly before returning to growth territory in the new financial year. The market is pricing in uncertainty; a credible permanent appointment with a clear strategic narrative would reduce the risk premium embedded in Diageo's valuation.

## 11. Conclusion

Diageo's strategic situation is serious but not existential. The company retains genuinely world-class brand assets — Guinness entered its eighth consecutive half of double-digit growth, delivering 17% organic net sales growth — and operates in an industry that, despite current headwinds, has grown at a 4.4% CAGR over the past decade as measured by IWSR, with international spirits growing at 5.1% over the same period.

The crisis of 2023–2025 was partly cyclical (post-pandemic normalisation, distributor destocking) and partly self-inflicted (over-investment in LAC tequila capacity without adequate demand-sensing). But it has also revealed a structural challenge: the premiumisation thesis that drove Diageo's strategy for two decades is maturing in developed markets, even as it remains vibrant in emerging ones.

The five recommendations above form a coherent strategic response:
1. **Fix the operational foundation** (demand-sensing infrastructure)
2. **Sharpen the portfolio** (Casamigos rationalisation)
3. **Rebalance geographically** (India and Africa acceleration)
4. **Hedge the moderation risk** (no/low alcohol strategy)
5. **Restore strategic clarity** (permanent leadership and a coherent long-term narrative)

Together, these moves would allow Diageo to defend its core brand-based competitive advantage while repositioning for the next phase of growth — one defined not by the indiscriminate premiumisation of everything, but by the selective, disciplined deployment of iconic brands in the markets and occasions where they genuinely win.

The analytical chain is clear: Diageo's brands are its moat; its operational execution and geographic mix are its vulnerabilities; and the path to recovery runs through fixing execution first, then using that platform to pursue the genuine long-run growth opportunities that India, Africa, and the moderation trend all represent.
