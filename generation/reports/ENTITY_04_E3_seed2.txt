# STRATEGIC CASE STUDY: LOCKHEED MARTIN CORPORATION

## *Fixed-Price Development Contracts, Classified Programme Losses, and the Structural Dilemma at the Heart of the World's Largest Defence Contractor*

---

## PART I — CASE BACKGROUND AND SITUATIONAL OVERVIEW

### 1.1 The Company

Lockheed Martin operates in a highly competitive aerospace and defence industry, facing competition from major players such as Boeing, Northrop Grumman, Raytheon Technologies, and BAE Systems. Despite this competition, Lockheed Martin has maintained a strong market position, with a market share of 33.56% in the aerospace sector as of Q2 2024. The company leads the defence industry with approximately $71 billion in 2024 sales and a record backlog of $160.6 billion in 2023, showcasing its strong market position and diversified portfolio. Structurally, Lockheed Martin operates in four business segments: Aeronautics, Missiles and Fire Control (MFC), Rotary and Mission Systems (RMS), and Space, generating net sales of $67,571 million in fiscal year 2023.

### 1.2 The Strategic Situation

Between 2023 and 2025, Lockheed Martin faced a mounting crisis that exposed a deep structural tension at the core of its business model: the firm's pursuit of large, fixed-price development contracts to win strategic programmes has generated catastrophic financial losses precisely when technical complexity is highest and cost predictability is lowest. This tension is not peripheral — it strikes at the company's profitability, its relationship with its sole customer (the U.S. Government), and its long-term competitive positioning against an emerging cohort of technology-native rivals.

The world's largest defence contractor recorded total year-end losses of $1.4 billion on a classified programme in its Missiles and Fire Control (MFC) portfolio as well as a $555 million overrun on a programme in its aeronautics division in 2024. In the second quarter of 2025, the situation worsened: Lockheed Martin logged a $1.8 billion loss in the second quarter of 2025, $950 million of which came from a "critical" classified aeronautics programme. The penalties were assessed under the classified contract's fixed-price structure, which generally speaking allows for little deviation from initially agreed-upon cost figures and progress milestones. CEO Jim Taiclet said he hopes the Pentagon will be open to restructuring the contract in a way that allows for a more sustainable development path, while still meeting national security commitments.

This situation is simultaneously a product of external industry dynamics, internal capability gaps, and a procurement policy environment that systematically misallocates risk — making it an ideal subject for multi-framework strategic analysis.

## PART II — MACRO-ENVIRONMENTAL ANALYSIS (PESTEL)

A PESTEL analysis establishes the macro-environmental context within which Lockheed Martin's strategic situation is embedded. Each factor is assessed for its direct bearing on the core problem.

### 2.1 Political

The defence sector is uniquely political: the government is simultaneously regulator, customer, and policy-setter. Opinions vary throughout the Defense Department on the proper role of fixed-price contracts. Air Force Secretary Frank Kendall has warned over the years that by placing the risk on contractors under such a deal, the government is essentially relegated to a "hands off" role as the programme proceeds. This political ambiguity over contract type creates strategic uncertainty for Lockheed Martin: it cannot predict whether future development awards will carry cost-plus or fixed-price structures, making long-term financial planning difficult. The political significance is that the choice of contract type is not purely a commercial decision — it is a policy instrument, subject to shifts in administration priorities, congressional oversight, and procurement reform agendas.

### 2.2 Economic

World military expenditure reached $2,718 billion in 2024, an increase of 9.4% in real terms from 2023, the steepest year-on-year rise since at least the end of the Cold War. Estimated global military expenditure rose for the tenth consecutive year in 2024, driven by the Russia–Ukraine war and other armed conflicts and geopolitical tensions. This macro-economic tailwind creates demand-side opportunity for Lockheed Martin. However, it is offset by inflationary pressures on the cost side: companies faced rising production costs due to inflation, labour shortages, and supply chain constraints, forcing the Pentagon to rethink procurement strategies and industrial base resilience. The economic warrant is therefore double-edged: rising demand increases Lockheed's backlog and revenue potential, but rising input costs exacerbate losses on fixed-price contracts where cost growth cannot be passed to the customer.

### 2.3 Social/Workforce

Critical talent shortages in engineering and skilled trades continue to be a major constraint, limiting industry expansion and innovation. Attrition remains high, holding steady at nearly 15% in 2024 — more than double the average across other U.S. industries. McKinsey's 2024 A&D talent gap report noted that frontline and middle managers are two times more likely to leave their employer than individual contributors. That is concerning given that about one-third of all A&D manufacturing and engineering roles are filled by workers who are 55 or older. The strategic significance of this data is that workforce attrition directly amplifies the technical risk that generates cost overruns: when experienced engineers leave mid-programme, the institutional knowledge embedded in complex development work is lost, increasing the probability of the "design, integration, and test challenges" that caused Lockheed's classified programme losses.

### 2.4 Technological

Technological change is simultaneously an opportunity and a threat. The F-35's TR-3 upgrade illustrates how technology risk materialises in practice: software instability and testing challenges meant TR-3 was not ready on schedule. As a result, the Pentagon was forced to pause deliveries of new jets with TR-3 installed from mid-2023 until July 2024. More broadly, SpaceX in just a few years upended the military launch business, capturing contracts that were once the exclusive domain of United Launch Alliance, the Boeing-Lockheed joint venture. Now, a new wave of commercial tech firms pushes into the defence sector, with Silicon Valley's biggest players challenging traditional prime contractors across defence and space programmes. The technological warrant is that Lockheed's competitive moat — built on decades of platform incumbency — is being eroded by software-native competitors who do not carry the legacy cost structures of traditional primes.

### 2.5 Environmental/Supply Chain

Materials on which Lockheed's products rely include aluminium, titanium, specialty steel, carbon fibre, and advanced microelectronics such as semiconductors. Supply chain challenges, supplier disputes, regulatory restrictions, and inflationary pressures have caused parts shortages, extended lead times, and pricing escalations affecting certain sources of supply. A specific geopolitical dimension amplifies this: China produces over 60% of global rare-earth minerals and controls over 85% of refining, creating a single-point-of-failure dependency that is both an environmental and a national security risk for a contractor whose products depend on rare-earth-based magnets and advanced materials.

### 2.6 Legal/Regulatory

As DoD contracting outlays grew, critics called for fixing the defence procurement system alleged to be wasteful and lacking proper oversight. A GAO study found that in 2008, approximately 70% of 96 Major Defence Acquisition Programmes were experiencing significant cost overruns, reaching over $295 billion — a 26% overrun — over the life of the projects. This regulatory history explains why the DoD has periodically mandated fixed-price contracts as a cost-control mechanism — but as the evidence below shows, this legal/regulatory solution has itself created a new category of financial risk for contractors.

**PESTEL Summary Warrant:** The PESTEL analysis establishes that Lockheed Martin faces a favourable demand environment (rising global defence budgets) simultaneously with structurally adverse supply-side conditions (workforce attrition, material cost inflation, supply chain fragility, and technology disruption). This asymmetry — where revenue opportunity is high but execution risk is also high — means the company's contract type strategy is the critical variable determining whether the macro-environment translates into profit or loss.

## PART III — INDUSTRY STRUCTURE ANALYSIS (PORTER'S FIVE FORCES)

Porter's Five Forces (Porter, 1979) provides a framework for assessing the structural attractiveness and competitive dynamics of the defence industry. The analysis reveals an industry that is highly attractive on most dimensions — but with one force, buyer power, that is structurally dominant and directly causes the core strategic problem.

### 3.1 Threat of New Entrants — *Moderate and Rising*

Historically, the defence industry has been characterised by exceptionally high barriers to entry: security clearances, long programme incumbency, capital intensity, and specialised engineering talent. Today, just five prime contractors receive 86% of the Pentagon's spending compared to 51 contractors being allocated 6% of defence spending at the end of the Cold War. This concentration reflects decades of consolidation behind high barriers.

However, the barrier structure is changing. Notable among new entrants are Palantir, Anduril Industries, and SpaceX, which have been securing substantial government contracts and building extensive networks within the U.S. defence establishment. Anduril and Palantir are forming a consortium with fellow defence tech upstarts including SpaceX, OpenAI, Saronic, and Scale AI to jointly bid for military contracts. The strategic implication is that barriers to entry for *software-defined* capabilities are lower than for hardware platforms: a consortium of technology firms can bid on AI, autonomous systems, and command-and-control contracts without the manufacturing infrastructure that traditional primes require. This selectively erodes Lockheed's positional advantage in the segments most relevant to future warfare.

### 3.2 Bargaining Power of Suppliers — *Moderate but Increasing*

With more than 13,000 suppliers, Lockheed Martin is safeguarding critical production lines by diversifying suppliers, adding dual-source options and insourcing where needed, while continuously monitoring the supplier network to spot and correct bottlenecks before they impact production. The breadth of the supplier base dilutes individual supplier power. However, for *critical* inputs — titanium, semiconductors, rare-earth magnets — supplier concentration is high and geopolitically exposed. The IPerionX partnership illustrates the dependency: Lockheed Martin agreed an order with IPerionX for the delivery of titanium plate components produced using IPerionX's U.S.-manufactured titanium. Lockheed makes heavy use of titanium alloys for critical structures and other key components across the breadth of its products. When critical materials are single-sourced or geopolitically constrained, supplier bargaining power rises sharply — and this power asymmetry becomes cost overrun risk on fixed-price contracts.

### 3.3 Bargaining Power of Buyers — *Very High (Dominant)*

This is the structurally defining force in the defence industry, and the direct cause of the strategic situation under analysis. The U.S. Government is effectively Lockheed Martin's monopsonistic buyer: over two decades of post-9/11 wars, a full $2 trillion went to just the top five contractors: Lockheed Martin, RTX, Boeing, General Dynamics, and Northrop Grumman. The customer's concentration of purchasing power allows it to dictate contract terms. Under fixed-price agreements, meant to secure the least risk for taxpayers, companies pick up the bill when costs run higher than expected.

The buyer's power is further amplified by its regulatory authority: it sets procurement rules, determines which contract types are permissible, and can — as the F-35 TR-3 case shows — pause programme deliveries unilaterally. The strategic warrant is that Lockheed Martin's losses on classified programmes are not primarily a management failure; they are the predictable consequence of a buyer-imposed contract structure that transfers all cost risk to the contractor in programmes characterised by genuine technological uncertainty.

### 3.4 Threat of Substitutes — *Low for Platforms, Rising for Services*

For major weapons platforms — fighter aircraft, missile systems, naval vessels — there are no commercial substitutes. The F-35 has no civilian analogue; HIMARS has no commercial equivalent. This makes substitution risk structurally low for Lockheed's core hardware business. However, for services, intelligence, and command-and-control — increasingly important segments of defence spending — software-based substitutes are emerging rapidly. The formation of the Palantir-Anduril-SpaceX consortium is precisely a substitution threat: it offers the Pentagon an alternative to buying integrated systems from traditional primes, instead purchasing modular software capabilities that can be layered onto existing hardware.

### 3.5 Competitive Rivalry — *Moderate but Oligopolistic*

Lockheed Martin operates in a highly competitive aerospace and defence industry, facing competition from major players such as Boeing, Northrop Grumman, Raytheon Technologies, and BAE Systems. The oligopolistic structure means rivalry is intense at the bid stage but diminishes once a contract is awarded — incumbents rarely lose programmes mid-execution. The F-35 programme's long, slow development illuminates the core of Lockheed Martin's business strategy: the company is patient, quietly matching its engineering talent against the desires of the Pentagon until it achieves the right fit. This "patient incumbency" strategy generates long-term revenue streams but requires absorbing short-term losses on development contracts — a model that is sustainable only if the losses are bounded.

**Five Forces Summary Warrant:** The industry is structurally attractive — high barriers, low substitution, oligopolistic rivalry — except for the dominant buyer power of the U.S. Government, which is the root cause of the fixed-price contract problem. This means Lockheed's strategic challenge is not competitive rivalry in the conventional sense, but rather the management of its relationship with a monopsonistic buyer that has the power to impose loss-generating contract terms.

## PART V — STRATEGIC POSITION ANALYSIS (PORTER'S GENERIC STRATEGIES)

Porter (1985) identifies three generic strategies: cost leadership, differentiation, and focus. Lockheed Martin's position is ostensibly one of **differentiation** — competing on the basis of superior technical capability, platform integration, and programme management expertise rather than lowest price. The company is patient, quietly matching its engineering talent against the desires of the Pentagon until it achieves the right fit.

However, the evidence reveals a critical strategic inconsistency. A differentiation strategy requires that the premium earned from differentiated capabilities exceeds the cost of delivering them. On fixed-price development contracts, Lockheed is being forced to absorb cost overruns that eliminate the margin that differentiation should generate. The world's largest defence contractor recorded total year-end losses of $1.4 billion on a classified MFC programme and a $555 million overrun on an aeronautics programme in 2024. In Q2 2025, a further $950 million loss on the classified aeronautics programme contributed to an overall $1.8 billion quarterly loss.

This constitutes what Porter would identify as being "stuck in the middle" — not in the sense of pursuing neither cost leadership nor differentiation, but in the sense that the company's differentiation premium is being systematically extracted by buyer power through fixed-price contract terms. The strategic warrant is: *differentiation without pricing power is not a viable generic strategy*. Lockheed differentiates technically but cannot price accordingly when contract terms are imposed by a monopsonistic buyer.

## PART VIII — SYNTHESIS: THE CORE STRATEGIC PROBLEM

The multi-framework analysis converges on a single coherent diagnosis:

**Lockheed Martin's core strategic problem is a structural mismatch between its differentiation strategy and the buyer-imposed contract terms under which it must execute that strategy.** The company possesses genuine, rare, and inimitable capabilities in complex defence system development (VRIO). The macro-environment is favourable, with record global defence spending (PESTEL). The industry is structurally attractive except for dominant buyer power (Five Forces). Yet the monopsonistic buyer uses fixed-price development contracts to extract the value of Lockheed's differentiation, converting technical complexity into financial losses rather than premium margins (Porter's Generic Strategies).

This diagnosis is supported by the convergent evidence: $1.4 billion in MFC classified programme losses and $555 million in aeronautics overruns in 2024; a further $950 million classified aeronautics loss in Q2 2025; and a pattern of F-35 overruns where the programme is 80% over budget and 10 years late — all of which flow from the same structural cause: fixed-price contracts applied to genuinely novel, high-complexity development work.

Three compounding vulnerabilities reinforce this core problem:
1. **Workforce erosion** (15% attrition, ageing workforce) reduces the engineering capability that is the source of Lockheed's differentiation advantage.
2. **Supply chain fragility** (titanium, semiconductors, rare earths) increases input cost volatility on fixed-price contracts where cost growth cannot be recovered.
3. **New entrant disruption** (Anduril, Palantir, SpaceX) threatens to capture future programme awards in software-defined domains where Lockheed's incumbency advantage is weakest.

### Recommendation 1: Proactive Contract Restructuring — Negotiate the Transition from Fixed-Price to Hybrid/Cost-Plus on High-TRL-Risk Development Programmes

**The Problem it Addresses:** The immediate financial crisis — $2B+ in classified programme losses — flows directly from fixed-price contract structures applied to development work characterised by genuine technological uncertainty.

**The Evidence Base:** Air Force Secretary Frank Kendall has warned that fixed-price contracts can be good deals for the Pentagon, but the conditions have to be right. The conditions are *not* right for classified, first-of-kind development programmes. CEO Taiclet characterised the approach to pricing as "middle of the road," noting that a fixed-price approach would incur "a risk premium that's significantly higher" than a cost-plus contract to mitigate the chance of future losses. Taiclet has already signalled his intent, saying he hopes the Pentagon will be open to restructuring the contract in a way that allows for a more sustainable development path, while still meeting national security commitments.

**The Warrant:** The evidence warrants this recommendation because the losses are not random — they follow a predictable pattern on fixed-price development contracts where technical complexity is high. A general cost-plus framework is preferred to a fixed-price one for Major Defence Acquisition Programmes in order to keep risk-sharing benefits. Restructuring to hybrid contracts (fixed-price for production lots once designs are mature; cost-plus with incentive fees for development phases) would align contract type with the actual risk profile of each programme phase, restoring the margin that Lockheed's differentiation strategy requires.

**Action Steps:**
- Engage DoD procurement leadership with a structured proposal distinguishing development-phase risk (cost-plus appropriate) from production-phase risk (fixed-price appropriate).
- Commission an independent GAO-acknowledged analysis quantifying the total cost to the government of contractor losses (including programme delays, management disruption, and renegotiation costs) to build the evidential case for contract reform.
- Coordinate with industry peers (Boeing, Northrop Grumman) to present a unified industry position on contract type reform — collective action reduces the risk that any single firm is penalised for raising the issue.

**The Problem it Addresses:** Attrition at nearly 15% in 2024 — more than double the average across other U.S. industries — directly erodes the engineering capability that is the source of Lockheed's VRIO-identified differentiation advantage. For Lockheed Martin — the industry's largest employer — the aggregate cost is substantially higher.

**The Warrant:** The evidence warrants this recommendation because the classified programme losses are not purely a contract-type problem — they are also an execution problem. Even under a cost-plus contract, poor engineering execution generates delays and cost growth. Stabilising the workforce that executes complex development programmes is therefore a necessary complement to contract restructuring, not a substitute for it.

**Action Steps:**
- Implement structured knowledge-transfer programmes pairing retiring engineers (55+) with mid-career successors on active classified programmes, with formal documentation of design decisions and test methodologies.
- Introduce retention incentives (deferred compensation, programme completion bonuses) specifically tied to critical classified programme milestones, directly addressing the attrition risk at the points of maximum programme vulnerability.
- Partner with universities and community colleges to build a pipeline for the skilled trades (welders, electricians, precision machinists) where NDIA has reported a persistent gap between supply and demand for welders, technicians, electricians, maintainers, and other skilled workers to meet the defence industrial base's manufacturing needs.
