# **DIAGEO PLC: A STRATEGIC CASE STUDY**
## *Navigating the Structural Shift in Global Spirits: Portfolio Rebalancing, Demand Erosion, and the Path to Sustainable Growth*

---

## 1. INTRODUCTION AND CASE OVERVIEW

Diageo is a global leader in Total Beverage Alcohol (TBA) and one of the world's most successful brand builders, with products sold in nearly 180 countries around the world. Its portfolio spans iconic names including Johnnie Walker, Guinness, Smirnoff, Don Julio, Tanqueray, Crown Royal, Captain Morgan, and Baileys. Yet beneath this apparent strength, the company faces a compounding strategic crisis that is simultaneously structural, competitive, and organisational.

Diageo's FY2025 preliminary results (year ended 30 June 2025) reported net sales of $20,245m, with organic net sales growth of only 1.7%, while reported operating profit plunged 27.8% to $4,335m. Reported operating profit declined 27.8% and reported operating profit margin declined 819bps, primarily due to exceptional impairment and restructuring costs, unfavourable foreign exchange and a decline in organic operating margin. This performance follows a FY2024 in which organic net sales were down 0.6%, with positive price/mix performance mostly mitigating a decline in volume.

The strategic situation is one of multi-front pressure: secular demand erosion in core Western markets, portfolio concentration risk in declining categories, geographic over-dependence on a weakening North America, a leadership vacuum following a CEO departure, and the looming threat of trade tariffs. The case argues that Diageo must execute a deliberate portfolio rebalancing strategy — investing behind high-growth "Stars," repositioning declining "Cash Cows," and decisively entering the no/low-alcohol space — to restore sustainable growth.

## 2. EXTERNAL ENVIRONMENT ANALYSIS

### 2.1 PESTEL Analysis

**Political / Legal**

Major spirits companies such as Diageo, Pernod Ricard, and Rémy Cointreau are grappling with a volatile mix of economic and regulatory challenges in 2025. The company estimated the measure would cut annual operating profit by about $150 million, down from earlier forecasts of roughly $200 million, after threats of a 25% tariff on Mexican tequila and Canadian whisky failed to materialise. This tariff risk is strategically significant because Diageo's highest-growth brands — Don Julio (Mexican tequila) and Crown Royal (Canadian whisky) — are precisely those most exposed to US trade policy. The political environment therefore creates a direct threat to the brands that are currently carrying the portfolio.

**Economic**

Organic net sales declined 7.5% in Greater China, driven by weakness in Chinese white spirits (CWS), partially offset by double-digit growth in India and growth across other markets in the region. In North America, the decline was attributable to a cautious consumer environment and the impact of lapping inventory replenishment in the prior year. These twin pressures — a squeezed Western consumer and a structurally challenged China — remove two of the company's historically most reliable growth engines simultaneously, creating an acute earnings gap.

**Social**

30% of Americans are participating in Dry January in 2025, representing a 36% increase from 2024. This trend has measurable market impact, with purchases of spirits dropping 39%, wine falling 36%, and beer/cider/hard seltzer decreasing 21% in January 2024 compared to the previous month. More structurally, recent data from Gallup shows that Millennials and Gen Z are upending long-standing trends in alcohol consumption in the U.S., with their preference for moderation, wellness, and value fuelling a multiyear decline in beer, wine, and spirits consumption. According to IWSR's No/Low Alcohol Strategic Study, 75% of Gen Z consumers said they had moderated their alcohol intake over the past six months. This is not a cyclical blip but a generational reorientation of consumption norms — a structural threat to the entire sector's volume base.

**Technological**

By shifting 14% of its 2025 marketing budget from development to consumer-facing activities, Diageo is leveraging AI-driven "virtual content studios" to tailor global campaigns to local markets. Digital and AI capabilities are becoming a source of competitive advantage in marketing efficiency, allowing Diageo to do more with reduced advertising spend under the Accelerate programme.

**Environmental**

Climate-driven yield variability affected agave and barley prices in 2024–2025; tequila production costs rose materially amid significant agave price volatility. This creates a cost-side risk for Diageo's fastest-growing category (tequila), compressing margins precisely when volume growth is most needed.

**Legal / Regulatory**

Alcohol faces increasing regulatory scrutiny globally, from minimum unit pricing in the UK and Scotland to advertising restrictions in emerging markets. The WHO's global campaign to reduce harmful alcohol use represents a long-run headwind to market expansion, particularly in developing markets where Diageo is seeking growth.

### 2.2 Porter's Five Forces Analysis

**Force 1: Rivalry Among Existing Competitors — HIGH**

Diageo's most prominent direct competitors in the spirits sector include Pernod Ricard, Bacardi, Beam Suntory, Brown-Forman, Constellation Brands, LVMH's Moët Hennessy Division, Campari Group, William Grant & Sons, and Rémy Cointreau. Beam Suntory and Brown-Forman push whisky premiumisation; Pernod Ricard accelerates innovation in single malts and blended whiskies. Rivalry is intensified by the fact that the overall market is declining in volume in key developed markets, meaning competitors are fighting for share of a shrinking pool — a classic condition for intensified rivalry (Porter, 1980). In tequila specifically, while tequila's U.S. growth slowed to 0.5% last year, the category has made enormous long-term strides, more than doubling in size since 2015 to reach nearly 32 million cases. As category growth slows, competition for Don Julio's and Casamigos' share intensifies.

**Force 2: Threat of New Entrants — MODERATE**

The spirits industry has high barriers to entry in premium segments (brand heritage, aged inventory, regulatory compliance, distribution scale). However, changes in consumer preferences are occurring particularly away from larger producers in favour of small distilleries or local producers, or away from brown spirits, premium products, or spirits generally. The craft distillery movement, celebrity-backed brands, and direct-to-consumer digital channels lower barriers at the premium-niche level, creating a persistent entry threat from below.

**Force 3: Threat of Substitutes — HIGH AND RISING**

The RTD cocktail market is expected to more than double globally, from $1.01 billion to $2.23 billion by 2029. Non-alcoholic beverages are also anticipated to grow at a CAGR of 17% through 2028 in the U.S. Substitution is occurring not merely from competitor spirits but from adjacent categories — cannabis beverages, functional wellness drinks, premium soft drinks, and RTD formats — which are capturing the social drinking occasion without the alcohol content. This represents a structural demand displacement, not merely competitive switching.

**Force 4: Bargaining Power of Buyers — MODERATE TO HIGH**

In the on-trade (bars and restaurants), large chains have significant bargaining power over listing fees and promotional support. In the off-trade (retail), major supermarket groups (Tesco, Walmart, Costco) exert price pressure. The shift in consumption from on-trade to off-trade — accelerated post-COVID — has structurally increased buyer power because off-trade buyers are more price-sensitive and concentrated.

**Force 5: Bargaining Power of Suppliers — MODERATE**

Key inputs (agave, barley, glass, oak casks) are subject to commodity price volatility. Climate-driven yield variability affected agave and barley prices in 2024–2025, with tequila production costs rising materially. However, Diageo's scale provides some countervailing power, and its diversified portfolio reduces dependence on any single input.

**Five Forces Synthesis:** The industry structure is deteriorating. Rivalry is intensifying in a flat-to-declining volume environment; substitution threats are structural and accelerating; buyer power is rising. This means industry-average profitability is under sustained pressure, and only firms with genuinely differentiated, difficult-to-imitate positions will earn above-average returns. This directs analysis toward Diageo's internal resources.

## 3. INTERNAL ANALYSIS

### 3.1 VRIO Analysis (Resource-Based View)

The Resource-Based View (Barney, 1991) holds that sustained competitive advantage derives from resources that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Organised for exploitation. The following applies this to Diageo's key resources.

| Resource | Valuable? | Rare? | Inimitable? | Organised? | Competitive Implication |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Brand Portfolio (Johnnie Walker, Guinness, Don Julio) | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ (heritage, provenance) | ✓ | **Sustained Competitive Advantage** |
| Global Distribution (180 countries) | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ (scale, relationships) | ✓ | **Sustained Competitive Advantage** |
| No/Low Alcohol Position (Seedlip, Guinness 0.0) | ✓ | ✓ | Partially | ✓ | **Temporary Advantage (eroding)** |
| Scotch Whisky Aged Inventory | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ (time-locked) | Partially | **Sustained Advantage (underutilised)** |
| Marketing & Brand-Building Capability | ✓ | Partially | Partially | ✓ | **Competitive Parity** |
| Financial Scale / Balance Sheet | ✓ | Partially | Partially | Partially | **Competitive Parity** |

**Brand Portfolio:** Diageo's global distribution network spans nearly 180 countries, providing unparalleled access to consumers worldwide, enabling scale efficiencies in production, marketing, and sales. A critical element of Diageo's strength lies in its robust global distribution network, reaching over 180 countries with 132 operational sites. The combination of globally recognised brand equities with this distribution infrastructure constitutes a genuinely inimitable resource: no competitor can replicate 200+ years of brand heritage or rebuild a 180-country distribution network from scratch. This is Diageo's core strategic asset.

**No/Low Alcohol:** Diageo's long-term conviction that consumers want to "drink better, not more" remains one of the key building blocks of its strategy. Diageo is now the world's largest non-alcoholic spirits player, more than four times bigger than any of its competitors in this space. This is a valuable and currently rare position, but it is not yet fully inimitable as competitors are investing rapidly in this space. Diageo must deepen this moat before rivals close the gap.

**Scotch Aged Inventory:** Aged Scotch whisky inventory is time-locked — it cannot be manufactured on demand. This is one of the most genuinely inimitable resources in the industry. However, with Scotch in category decline in North America, this resource is currently underutilised strategically.

## 4. THE STRATEGIC SITUATION: DIAGNOSIS

The foregoing analyses converge on a single diagnostic conclusion: **Diageo is experiencing a simultaneous portfolio-mix problem and a geographic concentration problem, set against a deteriorating industry structure.**

**Portfolio-Mix Problem:** Diageo's two largest categories by revenue — Scotch whisky and vodka — are in structural decline in its most important market. Johnnie Walker net sales declined 10.6% in North America due to overall Scotch category weakness. Vodka net sales declined 10% in North America, due to increased competition in the category from ready-to-drink (RTD) formats and overall category weakness. These are not execution failures; they reflect structural demand shifts. The implication is that no amount of marketing investment will fully reverse these trends — the strategic response must be portfolio rebalancing, not merely better execution in declining categories.

**Geographic Concentration Problem:** Diageo aims to rebalance growth beyond a soft North America in FY2024–FY2025 toward Europe, Africa, India and Travel Retail, targeting mid- to high-single-digit organic net sales growth over the cycle. Yet North America remains the single largest region. When it declines — as it has — the group-level numbers are dragged down regardless of performance elsewhere. This is a classic portfolio concentration risk.

**Leadership Vacuum:** Diageo announced on July 16, 2025 that CEO Debra Crew stepped down after two challenging years in the top role, by mutual agreement. The Accelerate initiative was described as "a shift in how we do business", including developing "a more agile global operating model." A CEO transition during a period of structural industry change and ongoing restructuring creates execution risk — strategy is most vulnerable to drift precisely when leadership continuity is absent.

## 5. BCG MATRIX ANALYSIS: PORTFOLIO PRIORITISATION

The BCG Growth-Share Matrix (Henderson, 1970) provides a framework for allocating investment across Diageo's portfolio based on market growth rate and relative market share.

Stars in Diageo's portfolio include tequila (Don Julio, Casamigos), Tanqueray, Guinness, and Seedlip — operating in high-growth segments; tequila grew at approximately 18% CAGR 2020–25, with Don Julio up 22% in FY25. These brands warrant continued heavy investment to defend and extend their market positions.

**Cash Cows — Johnnie Walker, Smirnoff, Captain Morgan:** These brands generate substantial revenue but operate in low-growth or declining categories. They should be managed for cash extraction to fund investment in Stars and Question Marks, rather than receiving disproportionate marketing spend in an attempt to reverse structural category trends.

**Question Marks — No/Low Alcohol (Guinness 0.0, Tanqueray 0.0, Seedlip), RTD formats:** These operate in high-growth markets but Diageo has not yet achieved dominant share. Guinness 0.0 net sales and volume more than doubled in the year in Europe. Diageo is now the world's largest non-alcoholic spirits player, more than four times bigger than any of its competitors in this space. The strategic imperative is to invest aggressively to convert these Question Marks into Stars before the window closes.

**Dogs — Casamigos (declining), Cîroc:** Sales of Casamigos tumbled 21% in H1 FY2025, while Don Julio soared. Casamigos net sales declined 30.9% in H1 FY2026, reflecting increased competition and distributors reducing inventories. Casamigos, once a Star, has deteriorated rapidly. The BCG logic suggests either a turnaround investment programme or divestiture to free capital for higher-return uses.

**BCG Implication for Strategy:** Diageo must resist the organisational temptation to defend every brand equally. The BCG matrix makes explicit that resource allocation should be asymmetric: Stars (Don Julio, Guinness) deserve disproportionate investment; Cash Cows (Johnnie Walker, Smirnoff) should be harvested efficiently; Question Marks (no/low, RTD) need decisive investment bets; Dogs (Casamigos, Cîroc) need hard decisions.

## 6. ANSOFF MATRIX: GROWTH VECTOR ANALYSIS

Ansoff's (1957) Growth Matrix maps strategic options across existing/new products and existing/new markets, providing a framework for evaluating Diageo's growth options.

**Market Penetration (Existing Products / Existing Markets):** In North America, Diageo is already pursuing this through its US route-to-market transformation. In the US, Diageo's spirits route-to-market has benefited from increased investment in capability building, commercial execution and investing in key accounts, with early signs of incremental growth. However, given structural category decline in Scotch and vodka, pure market penetration has limited upside. This is a necessary but insufficient growth strategy.

**Market Development (Existing Products / New Markets):** Diageo's 2025 expansion prioritises Asia-Pacific and Africa, with a major scale-up in India to capture premium Scotch and local craft spirit demand, including expanded manufacturing in Rajasthan to support luxury brands. Africa delivered organic net sales growth across all markets, led by Ghana, while Latin America and the Caribbean saw improving conditions in Brazil and premiumisation-led growth in Mexico. Market development in India and Africa is the most strategically compelling near-term growth lever, because it deploys Diageo's existing brand assets (Scotch, gin, beer) into markets where the middle class is expanding and alcohol consumption is growing — the inverse of the Western demographic dynamic.

**Product Development (New Products / Existing Markets):** Diageo is accelerating into the super-premium plus segment — underscored by its 2024 Don Papa Rum acquisition — as it chases higher margins amid shifting consumer tastes toward quality over quantity. The Don Papa acquisition was described as "in line with our strategy to acquire high-growth brands with attractive margins that support premiumisation, and enables us to participate in the fast-growing super-premium plus segment." Product development through premiumisation and no/low alcohol extension is a critical hedge against volume decline: if consumers drink less, Diageo must ensure they drink better (and more expensively).

**Diversification (New Products / New Markets):** The RTD category represents a limited diversification move. The RTD cocktail market is expected to more than double globally, from $1.01 billion to $2.23 billion by 2029. Diageo's Crown Royal RTD and Smirnoff RTD extensions represent early moves into this space, leveraging existing brand equity in a new format.

**Ansoff Implication:** Diageo's optimal growth vector is a combination of Market Development (India, Africa) and Product Development (no/low, RTD, super-premium), rather than doubling down on Market Penetration in structurally declining Western categories. The risk level increases as one moves away from the core (Ansoff's "diversification" quadrant carries the most risk), but Diageo's brand assets and distribution network provide a platform for controlled expansion.

## 7. PORTER'S GENERIC STRATEGIES

Porter (1980) identifies three generic strategies: Cost Leadership, Differentiation, and Focus. A firm that fails to commit to one risks being "stuck in the middle."

Diageo has historically pursued a **Broad Differentiation** strategy — competing across the full price spectrum of TBA with differentiated, premium-positioned brands. The strategic question is whether this remains viable or whether a sharper **Differentiation Focus** on the premium and super-premium tier is now more appropriate.

The evidence supports a tightening of focus. Consumers are increasingly prioritising quality over quantity, a trend known as selective premiumisation. Despite economic pressures, premium and above spirits and wine volumes saw a 3% decline in the first half of 2024, while premium beer experienced a 2% volume increase. This bifurcation — value and ultra-premium holding up while the middle declines — suggests that Diageo's mid-tier brands (standard Smirnoff, standard Captain Morgan) face the most structural pressure. A Differentiation Focus strategy would concentrate resources on the premium-and-above tier, accepting lower volume but higher margin and stronger brand equity.

The Diageo Luxury Group supports the premiumisation strategy, with luxury experiences and Casks of Distinction enhancing appeal. Premium spirits sales growth in 2024 indicates success. The November 2024 launch of the Diageo Luxury Group is a structural signal that management has partially recognised this strategic logic — but implementation must be more aggressive and the resource reallocation more explicit.

## 8. THE ACCELERATE PROGRAMME: ASSESSMENT

The Accelerate programme was announced in May 2025 and is a three-year programme aiming to create a more agile global operating model with cash delivery, cost savings and deleveraging targets. Total implementation cost is expected to be up to $500 million over the three-year period. Diageo is now eyeing cost savings of $625m over the next three years from Accelerate, up from the $500m target announced in May. The savings would come from reduced overheads, more targeted trade investment and A&P, and supply chain efficiencies.

**Assessment:** The Accelerate programme addresses the cost side of the strategic equation but does not, by itself, resolve the demand-side structural problem. Cost savings improve margins but do not grow the top line. The programme is a necessary condition for restoring profitability, but it is not sufficient for restoring growth. The risk is that cost-cutting, if too aggressive, damages the brand-building investment that is Diageo's primary source of competitive advantage. The strategic logic must be: use Accelerate savings to *fund* redeployment of resources into Stars and Question Marks, not merely to improve reported margins.

By shifting 14% of its 2025 marketing budget from development to consumer-facing activities and leveraging AI-driven "virtual content studios" to tailor global campaigns to local markets, Diageo is attempting to make marketing spend more efficient rather than simply cutting it — this is the right approach, as it preserves brand investment while improving return on that investment.

## 9. RECOMMENDATIONS AND ACTION PLAN

The analysis above generates a coherent chain of strategic recommendations. Each is grounded in evidence and connected to the analytical frameworks applied.

### Recommendation 1: Asymmetric Portfolio Investment — Double Down on Stars, Harvest Cash Cows

**Rationale:** The BCG analysis demonstrates that Diageo's Stars (Don Julio, Guinness, Tanqueray) are growing strongly while its Cash Cows (Johnnie Walker in North America, Smirnoff) are in structural category decline. The Porter Five Forces analysis shows that industry-average profitability is declining — only brands with genuinely differentiated positions will earn above-average returns. The VRIO analysis confirms that Don Julio and Guinness possess sustained competitive advantage through brand heritage and provenance.

**Action:** Increase A&P investment behind Don Julio, Guinness, and Tanqueray by 15–20% in absolute terms, funded by reducing support for structurally declining mid-tier brands. Don Julio gained share in markets representing over 90% of its net sales, with tequila organic net sales growing 21% in the first half of 2025. This momentum must be protected and extended.

**On Casamigos:** Casamigos has ceded roughly 800,000 cases since its peak in 2023. Diageo should conduct a strategic review of Casamigos with a clear decision point: either a focused turnaround investment (new positioning, new marketing platform) or a divestiture to unlock capital. Continuing to support a declining brand in a competitive category without a clear recovery thesis destroys shareholder value.

---

### Recommendation 2: Accelerate Market Development in India and Africa

**Rationale:** The Ansoff analysis identifies Market Development as the highest-return growth vector, because it deploys existing brand assets into structurally growing markets. The PESTEL analysis highlights that Western markets face structural demand erosion from the sober-curious movement and demographic shifts, while emerging markets offer the inverse dynamic — growing middle classes, expanding legal-age populations, and rising disposable incomes.

**Action:** Diageo should continue the major scale-up in India to capture premium Scotch and local craft spirit demand, including expanded manufacturing in Rajasthan. In Africa, organic net sales growth across all markets, led by Ghana, should be supported with increased distribution investment and local brand development. The strategic logic is that Diageo's Scotch whisky aged inventory — identified in the VRIO analysis as a genuinely inimitable resource — finds its highest-value deployment not in a declining North American Scotch market but in a growing Indian premium spirits market where Scotch carries aspirational cachet.

**Specific target:** India and Africa combined should represent 20%+ of group net sales within five years (vs. current low-teens), reducing dependence on North America below 30%.

---

### Recommendation 3: Commit to No/Low Alcohol as a Strategic Platform, Not a Niche

**Rationale:** The PESTEL analysis identifies the sober-curious movement as a structural social trend, not a cyclical one. 75% of Gen Z consumers said they had moderated their alcohol intake over the past six months. If Diageo's consumer base is moderating, the company faces a binary choice: lose those consumers to competitors or retain them through no/low alternatives. The VRIO analysis shows that Diageo's current position — the world's largest non-alcoholic spirits player, more than four times bigger than any competitors — is valuable and currently rare, but not yet inimitable. The window to build an unassailable position is open but closing.

**Action:** Elevate no/low alcohol from a sub-brand strategy to a standalone strategic platform with dedicated P&L accountability, separate marketing budgets, and explicit growth targets. Guinness 0.0 net sales and volume more than doubled in the year in Europe — this is the proof of concept. The Ansoff logic here is Product Development: use existing brand equity (Guinness, Tanqueray, Captain Morgan) to extend into the no/low format, capturing the moderation occasion without losing the consumer relationship.

**Target:** No/low alcohol to represent 5% of group net sales by FY2028, growing to 10% by FY2030, in line with projected category CAGR of 17%.

---

### Recommendation 4: Build Tariff Resilience Through Supply Chain Localisation

**Rationale:** The PESTEL (Political) and Five Forces (Supplier Power) analyses both identify US tariff risk as a material threat to Diageo's most valuable growth brands. The company estimated the measure would cut annual operating profit by about $150 million, and this estimate was based on tariffs that were ultimately less severe than threatened. A return to 25% tariffs on Mexican tequila would materially impair Don Julio's economics.

**Action:** Diageo has announced a new manufacturing and warehouse facility in Montgomery, Alabama, to increase efficiency and sustainability. This US supply chain investment should be accelerated and expanded. Additionally, Diageo should explore contract manufacturing arrangements for RTD formats within the US, reducing the proportion of finished goods crossing tariff-exposed borders. The strategic logic is to convert a political risk into an operational advantage: US-manufactured products are tariff-immune and carry "local" credentials that resonate with certain consumer segments.

---

### Recommendation 5: Resolve the Leadership Vacuum Urgently and Appoint a Transformational CEO

**Rationale:** Strategy without leadership is inert. Diageo's CEO Debra Crew stepped down on July 16, 2025, after two challenging years in the top role. Without a permanent CEO, investor confidence may waver, particularly in a sector facing existential risks and transformative opportunities. The Accelerate programme, portfolio rebalancing, and emerging market expansion all require sustained, committed leadership to execute. An interim CEO managing a restructuring programme while simultaneously conducting a CEO search creates strategic drift at precisely the moment when decisiveness is most needed.

Diageo's board unanimously felt that the new CEO appointment required extensive CEO experience and proven leadership skills in building and marketing world-leading brands. The new CEO must be empowered to make the asymmetric portfolio allocation decisions recommended above, including the politically difficult ones (harvesting Johnnie Walker in North America, divesting Casamigos if the turnaround case is weak).

## 11. CONCLUSION

This case study has argued that Diageo faces a strategic situation defined by structural demand erosion in its core Western markets, portfolio concentration in declining categories, and geographic over-dependence on a weakening North America — all set against a deteriorating industry structure and a leadership vacuum.

The analytical chain is as follows: the PESTEL analysis establishes that the sober-curious movement, tariff risk, and emerging market growth are the three most consequential macro forces. Porter's Five Forces demonstrates that industry-average profitability is under sustained pressure, meaning Diageo must rely on its unique resources rather than industry tailwinds. The VRIO analysis confirms that Diageo's brand portfolio, distribution network, and no/low alcohol position are the resources most capable of generating sustained competitive advantage — but only if they are actively invested in and defended. The BCG Matrix reveals that Diageo's portfolio contains genuine Stars (Don Julio, Guinness) that deserve asymmetric investment, and declining Cash Cows and Dogs that should be harvested or divested. The Ansoff Matrix identifies Market Development (India, Africa) and Product Development (no/low, super-premium) as the highest-return growth vectors. Porter's Generic Strategies analysis supports a tightening toward Differentiation Focus on the premium-and-above tier.

Together, these frameworks generate a coherent strategic prescription: invest asymmetrically behind Stars, develop emerging markets aggressively, build the no/low platform into a structural moat, localise supply chains against tariff risk, and resolve the leadership vacuum urgently. Diageo possesses the brand assets to execute this strategy. The question is whether its leadership and organisation have the strategic clarity and decisiveness to do so.
