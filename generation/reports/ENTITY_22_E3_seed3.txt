# STRATEGIC CASE STUDY: ASTRAZENECA PLC
## *Navigating the Patent Cliff While Pursuing the $80 Billion Ambition*

---

## 1. INTRODUCTION AND CASE OVERVIEW

This case study examines the strategic situation facing **AstraZeneca PLC** (LSE/STO/Nasdaq: AZN), one of the world's largest biopharmaceutical companies, headquartered in Cambridge, United Kingdom. AstraZeneca operates across three core therapy areas—Oncology, BioPharmaceuticals (comprising Cardiovascular, Renal & Metabolism [CVRM] and Respiratory & Immunology [R&I]), and Rare Disease—and is listed on the London Stock Exchange, Stockholm Stock Exchange, and Nasdaq.

The strategic situation is defined by a fundamental tension: AstraZeneca is simultaneously at the peak of its recent growth trajectory and facing structural revenue threats that could undermine its most ambitious financial target in company history. In May 2024, AstraZeneca unveiled an ambitious plan to deliver $80 billion in total annual revenue by 2030. AstraZeneca reported Total Revenue up 21% to $54,073m in FY 2024, driven by a 19% increase in Product Sales. Yet two of its highest-revenue products face imminent loss of exclusivity, a geopolitical crisis is unfolding in its second-largest market, and the broader industry faces what analysts describe as a structural "super-cliff." The central strategic question is: **Can AstraZeneca bridge the revenue gap created by patent expirations and geopolitical risk to reach $80 billion by 2030, and what must it do to sustain growth beyond that horizon?**

## 2. EXTERNAL ENVIRONMENT ANALYSIS

### 2.1 PESTEL Analysis

**Political / Regulatory**

The Medicare Drug Price Negotiation Program, enacted as part of the Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 (IRA), was designed to help lower Medicare spending on prescription drugs by requiring the federal government to negotiate the price of some high-cost drugs covered by Medicare. This is directly material to AstraZeneca: while the IRA targets Medicare-specific cost reductions through direct negotiation, the Executive Order introduces a broader international pricing benchmark that could influence both public and private markets. Despite ongoing legal challenges from the pharmaceutical industry—particularly around the IRA—courts have largely upheld the government's authority to implement these reforms. This regulatory pressure constrains pricing power on mature blockbusters precisely when AstraZeneca needs those revenues to fund its pipeline.

**Economic**

The economics of pharmaceutical R&D are deteriorating. In 2024, the FDA approved 38 new molecular entities for therapeutic use, a decline from 47 in the previous year. Clinical trials now demand greater complexity, as well as increased data requirements. Furthermore, rising costs per new drug approval encompass the expense of conducting clinical trials, prolonged timelines, and the commercial performance of the average new launch on the market, which is shrinking. This means the cost of replacing lost revenue from patent expiries is rising.

**Social / Demographic**

Ageing populations in AstraZeneca's key markets—the US, Europe, and Japan—structurally increase demand for oncology, cardiovascular, and rare-disease treatments. Each year an estimated 2.4 million people are diagnosed with lung cancer globally, with 80–85% of patients diagnosed with NSCLC, the most common form of lung cancer. This demographic tailwind supports the long-term commercial rationale for AstraZeneca's oncology-heavy portfolio.

**Technological**

Antibody-drug conjugates (ADCs) represent a paradigm shift in oncology drug delivery. ADCs are next-generation treatments that deliver highly potent cancer-killing agents directly to cancer cells through a targeted antibody. AstraZeneca's collaboration with Daiichi Sankyo on Enhertu and Datroway positions it at the frontier of this platform. More broadly, AI-enabled drug discovery and manufacturing optimisation are becoming sources of competitive differentiation.

**Environmental / Legal**

The legal environment around patents is intensifying. Samsung's biosimilar eculizumab product received FDA approval on 22 July 2024, placing Soliris—AstraZeneca's rare-disease flagship—in active biosimilar competition. Meanwhile, the primary patent for the dapagliflozin molecule (Farxiga) expired in late 2025, though secondary patents extend brand protection.

### 2.2 Porter's Five Forces Analysis

**Threat of New Entrants: LOW–MODERATE**

The pharmaceutical industry's high R&D costs and lengthy development cycles create formidable barriers to entry. In 2024, AstraZeneca spent $8.4 billion on R&D. Such capital requirements, combined with regulatory approval timelines of 10–15 years, mean that new entrants cannot rapidly replicate AstraZeneca's pipeline. However, platform biotechs (e.g., those specialising in ADCs or RNA therapeutics) represent a new breed of entrant that can develop targeted therapies faster than traditional routes, moderating this force upward.

**Bargaining Power of Buyers: INCREASING**

Governments and managed-care organisations are consolidating purchasing power. The IRA's Medicare negotiation programme is the most prominent example: while the IRA targets Medicare-specific cost reductions through direct negotiation, courts have largely upheld the government's authority to implement these reforms. In parallel, hospital formulary committees and pharmacy benefit managers exert downward price pressure on branded drugs as soon as biosimilars or generics enter the market.

**Bargaining Power of Suppliers: LOW**

Suppliers of raw materials, contract research organisations (CROs), and contract manufacturing organisations (CMOs) are numerous, and AstraZeneca's scale gives it leverage. However, the specialised nature of ADC manufacturing—requiring highly skilled labour and specialised facilities—creates pockets of supplier power in this emerging modality.

**Threat of Substitutes: HIGH**

Between 2025 and 2030, more than $300 billion in prescription drug revenues will lose patent exclusivity, about one-sixth of the industry's annual revenue. Generic and biosimilar substitutes represent the most immediate threat: branded revenues can collapse with historical data showing declines of 80–90% within just 12 to 18 months of generic or biosimilar entry.

**Competitive Rivalry: INTENSE**

AstraZeneca competes with Pfizer, Merck, Roche, Novartis, Bristol-Myers Squibb, and Johnson & Johnson across all its therapy areas. In oncology specifically, several companies, including Amgen, Samsung Bioepis, and Novartis, are developing biosimilars to Soliris. In EGFR-mutated lung cancer, Johnson & Johnson's EGFR-targeted combination of Rybrevant (amivantamab) and Lazcluze (lazertinib) represents a direct competitive challenge to Tagrisso.

**Summary:** The Five Forces analysis reveals that AstraZeneca operates in a structurally challenging environment where buyer power is rising (IRA, formulary pressure), substitutes are imminent (patent cliff, biosimilars), and rivalry is intensifying. This warrants a strategic response focused on differentiation through pipeline depth, platform leadership, and geographic diversification—which is precisely what the "2030 Ambition" attempts.

**Oncology Pipeline:** AstraZeneca's 2024 commercial delivery and sales performance was driven by five multiblockbuster medicines: Tagrisso, Lynparza, Calquence, Imfinzi, and Enhertu. This breadth is rare: few competitors can claim five concurrent blockbusters in a single therapy area. The combination of Tagrisso's EGFR dominance, Enhertu's HER2 platform, and the emerging Datroway asset creates a portfolio that is difficult to replicate.

**ADC Platform:** Combined sales of Enhertu, recorded by Daiichi Sankyo and AstraZeneca, amounted to $3,754 million in FY 2024 (FY 2023: $2,566 million), with growth primarily attributed to its strong performance in second-line treatment of HER2-positive breast cancer and its expanding indications in HER2-low breast cancer. The ADC platform is inimitable in the near term because the Daiichi Sankyo collaboration embeds proprietary DXd payload and linker technology that competitors cannot readily replicate. Rare diseases represent a high-growth opportunity with rapid innovation and significant unmet medical needs. **Critical Vulnerability—Soliris:** Soliris, a key rare disease asset that brought in nearly $2.6 billion in 2024, is also facing loss of exclusivity and is expected to face biosimilar challenge. AstraZeneca's mitigation strategy—transitioning patients to Ultomiris, a next-generation C5 inhibitor with more convenient dosing—is a textbook "evergreening" move, but its success depends on switching rates before biosimilars erode the franchise.

### 3.2 Dynamic Capabilities Framework

Teece, Pisano, and Shuen (1997) define dynamic capabilities as an organisation's ability to **sense** opportunities and threats, **seize** them through resource mobilisation, and **reconfigure** assets to maintain competitive fitness. This framework is particularly apt for AstraZeneca, which must continuously reinvent its portfolio as patents expire.

**Sensing:** AstraZeneca has demonstrated strong sensing capability. The company identified the ADC revolution early, entering the Daiichi Sankyo collaboration in 2019. It identified the obesity/metabolic opportunity and is now developing an oral GLP-1 (AZD5004). AstraZeneca's new Virginia facility will produce drug substance for its weight management and metabolic portfolio, including oral GLP-1 (AZD5004), baxdrostat, oral PCSK9 (laroprovstat), and combination small molecule products. This represents proactive sensing of a multi-hundred-billion-dollar market before it becomes crowded.

**Seizing:** The $39 billion acquisition of Alexion (2021) was a decisive seizing move. Rare diseases represent a high-growth opportunity with rapid innovation and significant unmet medical needs. Shareholders of both companies overwhelmingly voted in support of the transaction. More recently, AstraZeneca announced $50 billion of investment in the United States by 2030, building on America's global leadership in medicines manufacturing and R&D, with the investment expected to create tens of thousands of new, highly skilled direct and indirect jobs. This seizing move simultaneously addresses manufacturing capacity for new platforms and signals political commitment in a regulatory environment where US domestic investment is increasingly rewarded.

**Reconfiguring:** AstraZeneca has reconfigured its portfolio over the past decade from a respiratory/cardiovascular company (with AstraZeneca's old blockbusters such as Crestor and Seroquel) to an oncology-led, multiplatform biopharma. Total Revenue growth from Oncology was 24%, CVRM 20%, R&I 25%, V&I 8%, and Rare Disease 16% in FY 2024—demonstrating that revenue is now diversified across therapy areas rather than concentrated in any single franchise.

## 4. IDENTIFICATION OF THE STRATEGIC SITUATION

Synthesising the external and internal analyses, AstraZeneca's strategic situation can be characterised as a **"Bridge and Build" dilemma**: the company must simultaneously defend revenues from two major assets facing loss of exclusivity (Farxiga and Soliris) while building the next generation of blockbusters (ADCs, oral GLP-1, baxdrostat) to reach $80 billion by 2030. Three compounding strategic risks make this dilemma acute:

### Risk 1: The Patent Cliff — Revenue Exposure of ~$10 Billion

Farxiga generated $7.7 billion in 2024 and faces key patent expirations in 2025. Soliris brought in nearly $2.6 billion in 2024 and is in active biosimilar competition. Together, these two assets represent approximately $10.3 billion in annual revenue—roughly 19% of AstraZeneca's FY 2024 total revenue of $54 billion—that is structurally at risk. The industry context amplifies this: between 2025 and 2030, more than $300 billion in prescription drug revenues will lose patent exclusivity, about one-sixth of the industry's annual revenue, with nearly 200 drugs seeing their patents expire, including about 70 blockbusters generating over $1 billion each in annual sales. AstraZeneca is not unique in facing this challenge, but the scale of exposure relative to its $80 billion target is strategically significant.

**Why this warrants strategic action:** If Farxiga revenues decline by 80% within 18 months of generic entry (consistent with historical patterns cited above), AstraZeneca would lose approximately $6.2 billion in annual revenue from that drug alone. Combined with Soliris erosion, the company could lose $8–9 billion in annual revenues by 2027—equivalent to needing to find entirely new revenue streams of that magnitude to stay on the $80 billion trajectory.

### Risk 2: The China Crisis — Governance and Geopolitical Exposure

The executive in charge of AstraZeneca operations in China is under detention by Chinese authorities who are investigating employees there. Leon Wang, Executive Vice President of International, is on extended leave from the Company while under investigation in China. Sales in China rose by 15% to $1.67bn, accounting for 12% of total revenue in Q3 2024. China is therefore not a peripheral market—it contributes approximately 12% of total revenue. The detention of the China president and ongoing government investigation creates reputational risk, regulatory uncertainty, and potential market access disruption in a market that has been a key growth driver. This is compounded by broader geopolitical tensions between China and Western economies, which create systemic risk to AstraZeneca's China operations beyond the immediate governance issue.

**Why this warrants strategic action:** If China revenue were to stagnate or decline (from 15% growth to, say, flat growth) due to regulatory or reputational fallout, AstraZeneca would lose approximately $800–900 million in incremental annual revenue it had been counting on. More critically, China has historically been a key market for Tagrisso (given that 30–40% of NSCLC patients in Asia have EGFR mutations), meaning disruption in China could disproportionately affect the company's most important oncology asset.

### Risk 3: Pipeline Execution Risk — The $80 Billion Gap

AstraZeneca's ambition to deliver $80 billion in Total Revenue by 2030 will be achieved through significant growth in its existing oncology, biopharmaceuticals, and rare disease portfolio, and by launching an expected 20 new medicines before the end of the decade. Starting from $54 billion in FY 2024, reaching $80 billion requires approximately $26 billion in additional annual revenue over six years—a compound annual growth rate of roughly 6.7%. With ~$10 billion at risk from patent expiries, the actual growth requirement from new and expanding products is closer to $36 billion. This is an extraordinarily demanding pipeline execution requirement.

**Why this warrants strategic action:** The commercial performance of the average new launch on the market is shrinking, and diminishing performance is coupled with rising costs per new drug approval. This means AstraZeneca cannot assume that its 20 planned new medicines will each achieve peak revenues comparable to legacy blockbusters. The pipeline must be actively managed for commercial impact, not just regulatory approval.

## 5. STRATEGIC ANALYSIS: CONNECTING EVIDENCE TO CONCLUSIONS

### 5.1 The Core Strategic Tension

The VRIO analysis reveals that AstraZeneca's most durable competitive advantages lie in its **ADC platform** (inimitable, organised, rare) and its **complement rare-disease platform** (inimitable, but under biosimilar threat). Its **SGLT2 franchise** (Farxiga) is valuable but no longer inimitable. The strategy must therefore accelerate the transition from the former to the latter.

The dynamic capabilities framework explains *how* this transition is being managed. AstraZeneca's sensing capability is evidenced by its early entry into ADCs and its identification of the oral GLP-1 opportunity. Its seizing capability is evidenced by the Alexion acquisition and the $50 billion US investment commitment. However, the reconfiguration capability—the ability to shift revenue dependency from expiring assets to new platforms—is the critical execution challenge.

### 5.2 Portfolio Concentration Risk

Despite impressive diversification across therapy areas, AstraZeneca's oncology portfolio carries its own concentration risk. Emerging resistance mutations, competitive agents, pricing pressures, and patent expirations pose significant risks to Tagrisso; strategic innovation and pipeline development are essential responses. Tagrisso faces competition from Johnson & Johnson's amivantamab/lazertinib combination in first-line EGFR-mutated NSCLC. While Tagrisso's patents are estimated to be protected until 2042, competitive erosion from next-generation agents could reduce its market share before patent expiry.

### 5.3 The ADC Platform as Strategic Anchor

The Daiichi Sankyo ADC collaboration is the single most strategically important asset in AstraZeneca's portfolio because it satisfies all four VRIO criteria. Enhertu has demonstrated improved outcomes in six Phase III breast cancer trials across different subtypes and stages of disease. The expanding clinical evidence base—across HER2-positive, HER2-low, and HER2-ultralow populations, as well as lung and gastric cancers—creates a platform with multiple successive indication approvals, each adding incremental revenue. The breadth of positive Phase III data across multiple platforms reduces single-asset execution risk and supports the case that the pipeline can bridge the patent cliff.

## 6. RECOMMENDATIONS AND ACTION PLAN

The following recommendations are derived directly from the strategic analysis above. Each is connected to a specific analytical finding and carries an explicit warrant for why the recommended action follows from the evidence.

### Recommendation 1: Execute a "Managed Retreat" Strategy on Farxiga, Reinvesting Margin into Next-Generation CVRM Assets

**Analytical basis:** Farxiga's primary patent has expired, and method-of-use patents covering specific uses such as chronic kidney disease and heart failure prolong Farxiga's exclusivity, potentially into the 2040s. This means AstraZeneca has a window—not a cliff—in which to manage the transition. The authorised generic strategy already underway is the correct first step, as it captures generic market share before third-party generics erode it entirely.

**Warrant:** The RBV analysis shows Farxiga is no longer inimitable (primary patent expired), so defending it as a branded asset is strategically inefficient. Capital and management attention should be redirected to baxdrostat (a next-generation aldosterone synthase inhibitor for hypertension and CKD) and the oral GLP-1 programme, which are early-stage but address large unmet needs. AstraZeneca's planned Virginia facility will produce drug substance for its weight management and metabolic portfolio, including oral GLP-1 (AZD5004), baxdrostat, and oral PCSK9. This infrastructure investment is the correct reconfiguration move—it institutionalises the transition from Farxiga to next-generation CVRM assets.

**Action:** Accelerate Phase III enrolment for baxdrostat in hypertension and CKD; fast-track the oral GLP-1 (AZD5004) programme; and use Farxiga's remaining branded revenues (protected by indication-specific patents) to fund this transition without requiring external capital.

### Recommendation 2: Defend the Soliris/Ultomiris Franchise Through Accelerated Patient Switching and Indication Expansion

**Analytical basis:** Several companies, including Amgen, Samsung Bioepis, and Novartis, are developing biosimilars to Soliris. However, Ultomiris (ravulizumab) offers more convenient dosing (every eight weeks versus every two weeks for Soliris) and is already approved across multiple indications. The strategic logic of migrating patients from Soliris to Ultomiris before biosimilar entry—a classic "next-generation molecule" defence—is sound, but execution speed is critical.

**Warrant:** The dynamic capabilities framework (reconfiguration) predicts that firms that proactively reconfigure assets before competitive threats materialise outperform those that react. Historical data shows branded revenues can decline 80–90% within just 12 to 18 months of biosimilar entry. AstraZeneca therefore has a narrow window to complete the Soliris-to-Ultomiris patient transition. Failure to do so would mean losing the $2.6 billion Soliris revenue base to biosimilars rather than retaining it within the Ultomiris franchise.

**Action:** Implement a physician education programme emphasising Ultomiris's clinical and dosing advantages; negotiate formulary preference for Ultomiris ahead of biosimilar Soliris entry; and accelerate Ultomiris indication expansion (particularly in neurology indications) to grow the addressable market beyond the current Soliris patient base.

### Recommendation 3: Institutionalise the ADC Platform as the Core Growth Engine Through Expanded Manufacturing and Indication Breadth

**Analytical basis:** The VRIO analysis identifies the ADC platform as AstraZeneca's most durable competitive advantage—valuable, rare, inimitable, and organised. AstraZeneca's planned greenfield facility in Singapore will be its first end-to-end ADC production site, fully incorporating all steps of the manufacturing process at a commercial scale. This manufacturing investment is essential because ADC supply chain complexity is itself a barrier to imitation—competitors cannot simply replicate the Daiichi Sankyo DXd payload/linker technology.

**Warrant:** Porter's Five Forces analysis shows that the threat of new entrants is moderated by high capital requirements. By investing in proprietary ADC manufacturing infrastructure, AstraZeneca is raising its own barriers to imitation and ensuring that supply constraints do not become the bottleneck for commercial scale-up of Enhertu and Datroway. The commercial evidence supports this: combined Enhertu sales grew from $2,566 million in FY 2023 to $3,754 million in FY 2024—a 46% increase—demonstrating the platform's commercial velocity.

**Action:** Complete the Singapore ADC manufacturing facility on schedule; expand the Daiichi Sankyo collaboration to include additional ADC candidates beyond Enhertu and Datroway; and invest in the next-generation ADC pipeline (e.g., Dato-DXd in NSCLC and triple-negative breast cancer) to ensure the platform has a succession of indication approvals beyond the current Enhertu indications.

### Recommendation 4: Restructure the China Operating Model to Reduce Governance Risk While Protecting Market Access

**Analytical basis:** The executive in charge of AstraZeneca's China operations was detained by Chinese authorities, and Leon Wang is on extended leave while under investigation. China accounts for approximately 12% of AstraZeneca's total revenue. This is not a peripheral risk: the combination of governance failure and geopolitical tension creates a compound risk to a market that is structurally important for EGFR-mutated lung cancer (given Asia's higher EGFR mutation prevalence) and emerging markets growth.

**Warrant:** The PESTEL analysis identifies geopolitical risk as a macro-level threat that cannot be managed through product strategy alone. The China investigation signals a governance failure that, if unaddressed, could trigger regulatory sanctions, market access restrictions, or reputational damage that compounds the commercial impact. The appointment of Iskra Reic as replacement is a necessary but insufficient response.

**Action:** Commission an independent governance review of China operations, with findings reported to the Board; establish a China-specific compliance framework with enhanced oversight; develop contingency scenarios for China revenue disruption (ranging from 10% to 50% decline) and identify offsetting growth in other emerging markets (India, Southeast Asia, Latin America); and reduce structural dependence on China by diversifying the emerging-markets revenue base.

### Recommendation 5: Leverage the $50 Billion US Investment as Both a Strategic and Regulatory Hedge

**Analytical basis:** AstraZeneca announced $50 billion of investment in the United States by 2030, building on America's global leadership in medicines manufacturing and R&D. This investment simultaneously addresses three strategic needs: manufacturing capacity for new platforms (oral GLP-1, ADCs), political capital in a regulatory environment where US domestic investment is rewarded, and a hedge against IRA-driven pricing pressure by demonstrating US economic contribution.

**Warrant:** The Five Forces analysis shows that buyer power (government as payer) is the most immediately threatening force. By making a credible commitment to US investment and employment, AstraZeneca is engaging in a form of political capital accumulation that reduces the likelihood of punitive pricing interventions. In October 2025, AstraZeneca announced a historic agreement with the US administration to lower the cost of prescription medicines for American patients, voluntarily agreeing to a range of measures which will enable American patients to access medicines at prices equalised with those available in other countries. This voluntary price concession—while commercially costly in the short term—is strategically rational as it pre-empts more coercive government action and preserves market access.

**Action:** Ensure the Virginia manufacturing facility and Maryland R&D expansion are executed on schedule; communicate the jobs and economic impact of the $50 billion investment to key Congressional and regulatory stakeholders; and use the voluntary pricing agreement as a template for negotiating favourable formulary positions for new launches (Datroway, baxdrostat, oral GLP-1) in the US Medicare and commercial markets.

## 8. CONCLUSION

AstraZeneca's strategic situation in 2024–2025 is one of managed transition under pressure. The company has built a genuinely differentiated portfolio—anchored by its ADC platform, oncology multiblockbuster franchise, and Alexion rare-disease capabilities—that provides a credible foundation for the $80 billion 2030 Ambition. However, the simultaneous loss of exclusivity on Farxiga ($7.7 billion) and Soliris ($2.6 billion), compounded by the China governance crisis and rising US pricing pressure, creates a revenue gap that requires precise execution across multiple strategic fronts.

The VRIO analysis warrants confidence in the ADC platform and next-generation oncology pipeline as sources of durable competitive advantage. The dynamic capabilities framework explains the logic of AstraZeneca's sensing (oral GLP-1, ADCs), seizing (Alexion acquisition, $50 billion US investment), and reconfiguring (portfolio shift from SGLT2 to next-generation CVRM) moves. Porter's Five Forces analysis identifies buyer power and substitutes as the dominant threats, which the recommended actions directly address.

The critical execution risks are: (1) the pace of Farxiga revenue decline relative to next-generation CVRM ramp-up; (2) the depth of China disruption; and (3) whether 20 new medicine launches can each achieve sufficient commercial scale to collectively bridge a ~$10 billion revenue gap. If these risks are managed through the recommendations above, the $80 billion target is achievable. If they compound simultaneously, AstraZeneca will need to either revise the target or accelerate inorganic growth through further M&A.
