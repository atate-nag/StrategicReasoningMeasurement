# BAYER AG: THE MONSANTO LEGACY AND THE CRISIS OF STRATEGIC COHERENCE
## A Strategic Case Study

---

## ABSTRACT

This case study examines Bayer AG's strategic predicament following its 2018 acquisition of Monsanto. The central argument is that Bayer's crisis is not merely a legal or financial problem but a *strategic coherence failure*: the Monsanto deal destroyed the internal logic of the portfolio, generated liabilities that now constrain every other strategic choice, and produced a conglomerate structure that markets persistently discount. The analysis proceeds through PESTEL, Porter's Five Forces, a VRIO resource audit, and a dynamic capabilities lens, culminating in a sequenced five-part action plan. The order of execution is argued to matter as much as the content of each recommendation.

---

## 1. SITUATIONAL OVERVIEW

### 1.1 The Acquisition That Defines the Crisis

Bayer completed the acquisition of Monsanto on June 7, 2018, for $63 billion including debt—the largest acquisition in Bayer's history. The stated strategic rationale was complementarity: Bayer's innovative chemical and biological crop-protection portfolio combined with Monsanto's exceptional expertise in seeds and traits. The projected cost synergies were cited at up to $1.9 billion. To obtain regulatory approval from the US Department of Justice and the European Commission, Bayer divested significant seed and herbicide assets to BASF for €7.6 billion, materially reducing the synergy base before integration had begun.

What Bayer's due diligence either failed to quantify or chose to discount was Monsanto's glyphosate liability. Glyphosate is the active ingredient in Roundup, the world's most widely used herbicide. When the deal was announced in September 2016, there were approximately 120 glyphosate-related claims pending. About 200,000 Roundup-related claims have now been made against Bayer. The company has paid about $11 billion to settle nearly 100,000 Roundup lawsuits, with roughly 61,000 still pending. As of September 30, 2025, Bayer's provision for the glyphosate litigation totalled US$7.6 billion (€6.5 billion). In February 2026, Bayer proposed a $7.25 billion class-action settlement to resolve current and future claims from users of its glyphosate-based products who have cancer now or develop cancer in the next several years.

Beyond glyphosate, Bayer also inherited Monsanto's legacy liability for polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs). In December 2024, Bayer took a $618.3 million provision to deal with its liabilities for PCBs once manufactured by Monsanto. In July 2024, Bayer agreed to pay $160 million to resolve a claim by the city of Seattle that Monsanto's unit had fouled the municipal storm-water drainage system and a local river.

The combined effect on market value has been catastrophic. The litigation has cast such a massive cloud over Bayer's stock, which is down more than 70% since the Monsanto deal, that Chief Executive Officer Bill Anderson is considering whether the company should even make glyphosate anymore.

### 1.2 Compounding Structural Pressures

The litigation crisis sits atop two further structural problems that compound the strategic difficulty.

**The Xarelto patent cliff.** Xarelto (rivaroxaban), Bayer's blockbuster anticoagulant co-developed with Johnson & Johnson, has faced progressive patent erosion. The primary composition-of-matter patent for rivaroxaban in the United States expired in July 2023. Sales of Xarelto, its blood thinner that prevents clotting, dropped by 33% to €2.34 billion in 2025. By 2025, Bayer's pharmaceutical division expected Xarelto's EBITDA contribution to drop from €4.7 billion in 2023 to €3.5 billion—a 25% erosion of the division's most important profit engine.

**Crop Science earnings deterioration.** Sales at Crop Science fell slightly compared to the prior year and earnings declined significantly in 2024. More precisely, EBITDA before special items at Crop Science decreased 14.2% to €4.3 billion, with the EBITDA margin falling to 19.4%, driven by significant price reductions in crop protection, inflationary cost increases, and ongoing market challenges.

**Net financial debt.** Group sales came in at €46.606 billion in 2024, up 0.7% on a currency- and portfolio-adjusted basis, while EBITDA before special items decreased by 13.5% to €10.123 billion. Net financial debt amounted to €32.626 billion as of December 31, 2024, down 5.4% from year-end 2023. In Q1 2026, net financial debt of the Bayer Group increased by €2.7 billion to €32.5 billion, mainly due to negative operating cash flow and the PCB and glyphosate litigations. In order to further reduce debt and gain greater flexibility, the company intends to pay out the legal minimum in dividends.

## 2. STRATEGIC ANALYSIS

### 2.1 PESTEL Analysis

**Political.** The Environmental Protection Agency does not require a cancer warning label on products that use glyphosate. The Trump administration has sided with Bayer, reversing the position of the Biden administration. However, state courts continue to award large verdicts—in March 2025, a jury in the state of Georgia ordered Bayer to pay almost $2.1 billion to a plaintiff who claimed Roundup caused cancer. State legislatures have resisted Bayer's lobbying campaign for legislative immunity. After years of large jury awards, in 2024 Bayer launched a campaign to stop the company's financial haemorrhaging with a state-by-state push for legislation to prohibit lawsuits for failure to warn. This campaign has stalled in the House.

**Economic.** The 2023–2024 period saw inventory destocking across the global crop-protection market, compressing Bayer's pricing power. Inflationary cost pressures on raw materials and energy further squeezed margins. The €33 billion debt load means that rising interest rates directly increase the cost of capital at a time when earnings are under pressure. The priority is to reduce the debt level so that Bayer can boost its financial flexibility.

**Social.** Consumer and activist concern about glyphosate's safety—amplified by the Roundup litigation narrative—has reputational consequences beyond the courtroom. Bayer has already stopped making glyphosate-based Roundup for home use. The broader ESG agenda creates both pressure (on chemical-intensive farming) and opportunity (for regenerative agriculture solutions).

**Technological.** Digital farming represents a genuine growth vector. Bayer's Climate FieldView platform is already being used on more than 150 million subscribed acres and its adoption continues to grow around the world. AI is described as "a core technology in powering the digital transformation of agriculture, which helps tackle climate change and ensure global food security." In pharmaceuticals, the shift to targeted therapies creates both pipeline opportunities and competitive threats from specialist biotechs.

**Environmental.** Climate change is a structural tailwind for Bayer's Crop Science division: extreme weather variability increases demand for resilient seed genetics and adaptive agronomic tools. Bayer aims to launch ten blockbuster agricultural products in the next ten years to support farmers worldwide. Simultaneously, regulatory pressure on chemical-intensive farming creates a transition risk for the conventional crop-protection portfolio.

**Legal.** The legal environment is the dominant strategic constraint. A ruling in favour of Bayer at the US Supreme Court could effectively eliminate many of the tens of thousands of remaining Roundup lawsuits. SCOTUS is expected to deliver a decision by late June 2026. A ruling against Bayer would extend the liability horizon indefinitely. The PCB litigation adds a further layer of open-ended environmental liability inherited from Monsanto.

### 2.2 Porter's Five Forces: Crop Science Division

**Threat of New Entrants: LOW.** The agricultural inputs industry requires enormous R&D investment, decade-long regulatory approval processes, and extensive seed germplasm libraries. The consolidation of the industry following the 2015–2018 merger wave—Dow/DuPont/Pioneer, Syngenta/ChemChina, and Monsanto/Bayer—has reshaped the global agricultural input sector, signalling the final stages of a long process of market concentration. The capital and time barriers are prohibitive for new entrants, and the four-player oligopoly (Bayer, Corteva, Syngenta/ChemChina, BASF) has further entrenched incumbents.

**Bargaining Power of Suppliers: MODERATE.** Key inputs include chemical precursors, energy, and proprietary biotechnology licences. While Bayer has significant scale, energy market volatility and raw-material inflation have given suppliers episodic leverage, contributing to the cost inflation that compressed Crop Science margins in 2024.

**Bargaining Power of Buyers: INCREASING.** Farmers are price-sensitive and increasingly sophisticated. The 2023–2024 inventory destocking demonstrated that when prices are perceived as excessive, distributors and farmers defer purchases. Retail consolidation in agricultural distribution increases buyer concentration and negotiating power. Bayer held approximately 23% of the global seeds market as of 2025, but this market leadership does not insulate it from price competition in crop protection.

**Threat of Substitutes: MODERATE AND RISING.** Biological crop-protection products (biopesticides, biofungicides) represent a growing substitute category. Regenerative agriculture practices that reduce chemical inputs are gaining regulatory and consumer support. Bayer is seeking to scale regenerative agriculture, reflecting strategic awareness of this substitution risk.

**Competitive Rivalry: HIGH.** Corteva, as a global pure-play agriculture company, combines industry-leading innovation, high-touch customer engagement, and operational execution. As a pure-play, Corteva has a focused management attention and capital allocation advantage that Bayer, as a conglomerate, structurally lacks. BASF and Syngenta compete with equivalent scale in crop protection.

**Five Forces Summary.** The Crop Science competitive environment is structurally unattractive in the short term (high rivalry, increasing buyer power, rising substitution) but defensible in the long term for incumbents with strong IP and digital platforms. Bayer's position is competitively sound but strategically encumbered by the litigation overhang and debt load that constrain R&D investment and management attention.

### 2.3 Porter's Five Forces: Pharmaceuticals Division

**Threat of New Entrants: LOW TO MODERATE.** Drug development requires decade-long timelines and billion-dollar investment, but the rise of biotech and platform technologies has lowered barriers in specific therapeutic niches, creating "biotech disruption" risk in innovative segments.

**Bargaining Power of Buyers: HIGH AND RISING.** The US Inflation Reduction Act (IRA), enacted in 2022, gives Medicare the right to negotiate drug prices directly, representing a structural shift in US pharmaceutical pricing power. European health technology assessment bodies (NICE, G-BA) already apply rigorous cost-effectiveness thresholds. This buyer-power increase disproportionately affects companies with ageing, high-revenue products facing price negotiation.

**Threat of Substitutes: HIGH.** Generic and biosimilar competition is the defining feature of pharmaceutical economics after patent expiry. The expiration of US patents in 2024 is expected to reduce Xarelto revenue significantly due to increased generic competition, with estimates indicating a decline to under $500 million by 2026. This trajectory illustrates the structural vulnerability of any company dependent on a small number of blockbusters.

**Competitive Rivalry: HIGH.** Bayer competes against global pharma giants with larger R&D budgets, deeper pipelines, and stronger balance sheets. In cardiovascular and oncology—Bayer's core therapeutic areas—competition is intense.

**Five Forces Summary.** The pharmaceutical environment is structurally challenging for Bayer specifically because of its narrow blockbuster dependence (Xarelto has accounted for a disproportionate share of pharmaceutical EBITDA) and its relatively thin pipeline compared to large-cap peers. The Five Forces analysis therefore reinforces the imperative to accelerate pipeline development as a strategic priority.

### 2.4 VRIO Resource Analysis

The VRIO framework (Barney, 1991) assesses resources on four dimensions—Value, Rarity, Imitability, Organisation—to identify sources of sustained competitive advantage. Resources satisfying all four criteria generate sustained competitive advantage (SCA); those failing on Organisation are potentially exploitable but currently underutilised.

| Resource | V | R | I | O | Competitive Implication |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Crop Science seed/trait IP | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | Partial | Potential SCA—organisational constraints limit exploitation |
| Climate FieldView digital platform | ✓ | ✓ | Partial | Partial | Temporary advantage—competitors building equivalents |
| Pharma pipeline (Nubeqa, Kerendia) | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | Partial | Potential SCA if pipeline execution improves |
| Brand equity (Aspirin, Bayer cross) | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | SCA in consumer health—but being monetised via divestiture |
| Manufacturing/distribution scale | ✓ | Partial | Partial | ✓ | Competitive parity, not advantage |
| Glyphosate franchise | ✓ | Partial | Partial | ✓ | Eroding asset—litigation, reputational, and substitution risks |
| Financial capacity (balance sheet) | ✗ | — | — | — | Competitive *dis*advantage: €33bn net debt constrains strategy |

**Key VRIO insight.** Bayer's most genuinely rare and inimitable resources—seed genetics IP, pharmaceutical pipeline, and digital farming platform—score only "Partial" on the Organisation dimension. This pattern is not coincidental: it reflects management attention consumed by litigation, a complex three-division conglomerate structure, and the bureaucratic layers that the DSO restructuring is designed to remove. The implication is that the competitive advantage gap is as much an *organisational* problem as a resource problem—addressing it requires both portfolio simplification and cultural transformation.

### 2.5 Dynamic Capabilities Analysis

Teece, Pisano, and Shuen (1997) define dynamic capabilities as a firm's ability to *sense* opportunities, *seize* them, and *reconfigure* assets accordingly. Applied to Bayer, the three sub-capabilities are uneven.

**Sensing.** Bayer's sensing of agricultural megatrends appears strong: the Climate FieldView platform, the ten-blockbuster agricultural pipeline, and the integration of AI into precision agriculture all reflect genuine foresight. In pharmaceuticals, the pivot from cardiovascular blockbusters to oncology (Nubeqa) and cardio-renal (Kerendia) demonstrates adaptive sensing of therapeutic area opportunities. However, Bayer's sensing of *legal and reputational risk* in the Monsanto acquisition was catastrophically poor. The glyphosate litigation was a known and pending risk at the time of the 2018 close—approximately 120 claims were already filed when the deal was announced—and the failure to adequately provision for the subsequent explosion to 200,000 claims represents a fundamental sensing failure that undermines confidence in Bayer's risk-assessment processes.

**Seizing.** CEO Bill Anderson's "Dynamic Shared Ownership" (DSO) restructuring, launched in January 2024, represents a genuine seizing attempt. The new operating model aims to reduce hierarchies, eliminate bureaucracy, streamline structures, and accelerate decision-making processes to make the company much more agile and significantly improve its operational performance. Management layers have been cut from 12–13 to 6–7, management roles reduced by roughly 50%, approximately 12,000 positions eliminated, and some 2,000 autonomous teams created, each with 6–10 people and the authority to make decisions. The DSO model targets €2 billion in annual organisational cost savings from 2026.

**Reconfiguring.** Reconfiguration is where Bayer is most visibly active. In May 2025, Bayer agreed to sell its consumer health brands Aspirin and Bepanthen to Haleon for around €8.2 billion, while continuing to prepare a potential separation of the Consumer Health division. CEO Anderson has tabled a full split-up for now, noting that a spinoff would require at least two years to execute and that Bayer cannot simultaneously tackle its many other problems while executing a business breakup.

**Dynamic capabilities verdict.** Sensing is strong in technology and market trends but demonstrated a catastrophic failure in legal risk. Seizing is improving through DSO. Reconfiguration is active but incomplete. The core constraint is that reconfiguration requires financial flexibility that the €33 billion debt load and ongoing litigation provisions deny—a conclusion that directly motivates the sequencing of the recommendations below.

### 2.6 The Conglomerate Discount Problem

A critical strategic issue that cuts across all frameworks is the *conglomerate discount*. Financial theory and empirical evidence consistently show that diversified conglomerates trade at a discount to the sum-of-parts value of their constituent businesses, because investors are forced to take unwanted exposure to segments outside their mandate, capital allocation opacity makes divisional performance difficult to assess, and management attention is divided across unrelated competitive environments.

Persistent disappointment and a clunky conglomerate structure has hurt Bayer's stock, prompting analysts at Bernstein to argue for a breakup. Elliott Advisors took a stake in 2019 making the same argument. Bayer's three divisions—Pharmaceuticals, Consumer Health, and Crop Science—operate in fundamentally different competitive environments, have different capital requirements, different regulatory frameworks, different customer relationships, and different investor bases. The strategic logic for keeping them together rests on cash-flow diversification and modest cross-divisional capability sharing. Neither rationale is compelling enough to offset the conglomerate discount, particularly when the debt load means that cash-flow diversification is consumed by debt service rather than reinvested in growth.

## 3. SYNTHESIS: THE STRATEGIC PROBLEM STATED PRECISELY

The foregoing analysis converges on a single coherent diagnosis:

**Bayer is trapped in a strategic debt spiral.** The Monsanto acquisition generated liabilities (glyphosate, PCB) that consume cash flows that should fund pharmaceutical R&D and agricultural innovation. The debt load from the acquisition constrains the portfolio restructuring (breakup, divestiture) that would release the conglomerate discount. The conglomerate discount suppresses the equity value that would otherwise allow Bayer to raise capital more cheaply to fund R&D and litigation settlement. Meanwhile, the Xarelto patent cliff is eroding the pharmaceutical cash engine that partly funds debt service. Each problem makes the others worse.

The chain of causation runs: *Monsanto acquisition* → *litigation liability + debt* → *constrained cash flow* → *reduced R&D investment capacity* → *weaker pipeline* → *lower equity value* → *higher cost of capital* → *further constrained strategic options*. Breaking this spiral requires attacking debt and litigation simultaneously, because neither can be resolved independently: paying down debt requires selling assets (requiring strategic clarity about what to keep), and settling litigation requires cash (requiring debt headroom). This structural interdependence is why the *sequence* of the following recommendations matters as much as their content.

## 4. RECOMMENDATIONS AND ACTION PLAN

### Recommendation 1: Achieve Definitive Litigation Resolution (Immediate Priority, 2025–2026)

**The warrant.** The litigation overhang is the primary suppressor of equity value and the primary consumer of management attention. The stock is down more than 70% since the Monsanto deal, and this cannot be fully explained by earnings weakness (EBITDA of €10 billion on sales of €46 billion is not catastrophic). The persistent discount reflects a market that cannot price the terminal value of the company while the litigation tail is open-ended. Until the litigation is bounded, no strategic restructuring can be fully valued by the market—making litigation resolution a precondition for every other strategic action.

**The action.** Bayer should pursue the proposed $7.25 billion class-action settlement as its primary legal strategy, combined with the Supreme Court appeal. The settlement would eliminate some risk from an eventual Supreme Court ruling: patients would be assured of receiving settlement money even if the Supreme Court rules in Bayer's favour, and Bayer would be protected from potentially larger costs if the high court rules against it. These are, as Bayer has described them, "independently necessary and mutually reinforcing elements in a multi-pronged strategy." For PCBs, Bayer should accelerate settlement of municipal and state claims—following the model of the Seattle and Los Angeles settlements—rather than litigating each case individually, since litigation cost is itself a liability.

### Recommendation 2: Execute a Disciplined Portfolio Simplification (2025–2027)

**The warrant.** The VRIO analysis shows that Bayer's strongest resources are in Crop Science genetics/digital farming and in the pharmaceutical pipeline. Consumer Health, while profitable, does not share the R&D intensity or the innovation moat of the other two divisions. The conglomerate discount analysis shows that the market persistently undervalues the portfolio as a whole. The sale of Aspirin and Bepanthen to Haleon for approximately €8.2 billion in May 2025 is a correct first step but is insufficient to resolve the structural problem.

**The action.** Bayer should pursue a full separation of the Consumer Health division, either through a trade sale or a capital-markets demerger. CEO Anderson has noted the two-year execution timeline as a constraint; the correct response is to begin preparation immediately so that separation can be completed by 2027. The proceeds should be applied to reducing net financial debt below €20 billion—a level consistent with investment-grade credit metrics that would restore financial flexibility. A further separation of Pharmaceuticals and Crop Science should be considered only in a second phase (2028+), after the balance sheet is repaired, because premature breakup while the balance sheet is stressed risks each separated entity carrying a portion of the debt at a higher cost than the consolidated group.

### Recommendation 3: Accelerate and Focus Pharmaceutical Pipeline Investment (2025–2030)

**The warrant.** The Xarelto patent cliff is an irreversible structural decline. Nubeqa and Kerendia grew by 80% year-over-year in 2024, more than offsetting the decline of Xarelto. Nubeqa achieved blockbuster status in September 2024, with annual sales reaching €1.52 billion for the full year of 2024. This demonstrates that the pipeline transition is achievable if sustained—the inference from past performance to future feasibility is stronger than a purely forward-looking claim because it is grounded in observed outcomes.

**The action.** Bayer should protect pharmaceutical R&D investment at or above €5 billion per year. The €2 billion in annual DSO cost savings targeted for 2026 should be redirected to pipeline investment, making this a reallocation rather than an addition to total spend. Specifically: (a) secure label expansions for Kerendia into heart failure, a market that could add €1–2 billion in annual sales by 2030; (b) achieve regulatory approval for asundexian in secondary stroke prevention; and (c) continue EU launches of Beyonttra for transthyretin-mediated amyloidosis with cardiomyopathy and Lynkuet for menopause management, building out the commercial infrastructure beyond the Nubeqa/Kerendia core.

### Recommendation 4: Reposition Crop Science Around Digital Farming and Biologicals (2024–2030)

**The warrant.** The Five Forces analysis shows that conventional crop-protection chemistry faces increasing buyer power, rising substitution from biologicals, and intensifying rivalry. The VRIO analysis shows that Bayer's strongest agricultural assets are seed genetics IP and the Climate FieldView digital platform—not its herbicide/pesticide portfolio. The strategic logic therefore points toward accelerating the transition from chemistry to biology and data.

**The action.** Bayer should continue to invest in the Climate FieldView platform and in biological crop-protection alternatives, while managing the conventional crop-protection portfolio for cash rather than for growth. The ten-blockbuster-in-ten-years agricultural pipeline commitment should be resourced and tracked as a strategic KPI. The glyphosate franchise—which remains commercially important in the near term—should be managed defensively: continuing to exit home-consumer markets where reputational risk is highest while maintaining the professional/agricultural market where regulatory approval is clearest and commercial volumes are largest.

### Recommendation 5: Sustain the DSO Organisational Transformation (Ongoing)

**The warrant.** The dynamic capabilities analysis identifies organisational complexity as a binding constraint on Bayer's ability to exploit its resources—evidenced by the "Partial" Organisation score on multiple VRIO dimensions. Anderson described Bayer as a "badly broken" business, underperforming due to patent losses, litigation, debt levels, and bureaucracy. The DSO model has cut management layers from 12–13 to 6–7 and reduced management roles by roughly 50%. Organisational transformations of this scale typically take three to five years to embed, and the risk of reverting to bureaucratic norms under financial stress is real.

**The action.** Anderson should maintain DSO as a non-negotiable organisational commitment and resist the temptation to re-centralise decision-making in response to the crisis. The €2 billion cost savings should be treated as a minimum. The organisational simplification should be reinforced by the portfolio simplification: a two-division company (Pharma + Crop Science) is inherently simpler to manage under a flat DSO model than a three-division conglomerate, creating a reinforcing dynamic between Recommendations 2 and 5.

2. **From VRIO "Partial Organisation" scores to "organisational constraint" conclusion.** The warrant is that multiple of Bayer's strongest resources score "Partial" on Organisation—specifically the Climate FieldView platform, the pharmaceutical pipeline, and the Crop Science IP. 3. **From conglomerate discount theory to portfolio simplification recommendation.** The warrant is that the 70%+ stock decline cannot be fully explained by earnings weakness alone—EBITDA of €10 billion on sales of €46 billion represents an EBITDA margin of approximately 22%, which is not catastrophic for a life-sciences company. 4. **From Nubeqa/Kerendia 80% growth to "pipeline transition is achievable" conclusion.** The warrant is that the inference is grounded in observed 2024 outcomes rather than projections alone. The fact that these products already more than offset Xarelto's decline in 2024 makes the claim that the transition is achievable a description of what has already occurred, not merely a forecast—a significantly stronger inferential basis.

5. **From DSO cost savings to "protect R&D without increasing total spend" recommendation.** The warrant is arithmetic: the €2 billion in targeted DSO savings is approximately equal to the incremental R&D investment that would meaningfully accelerate the pipeline. Redirecting DSO savings to R&D is therefore financially feasible as a reallocation, not an addition—making the recommendation internally consistent with the balance-sheet repair objective of Recommendation 2.

## 6. CONCLUSION

Bayer's strategic crisis is a case study in acquisition-driven value destruction. The Monsanto deal was strategically coherent in its ambition—integrating seed genetics with crop protection and digital farming—but was executed without adequate due diligence of the legal liability, at a price that left no margin for error, and into a conglomerate structure that markets persistently discount. The result is a company whose strongest competitive assets (seed IP, digital farming platform, pharmaceutical pipeline) are being held hostage by a legacy liability that pre-dates Bayer's ownership.

The path forward requires disciplined sequencing: resolve the litigation to bound the liability; simplify the portfolio to release the conglomerate discount; accelerate the pharmaceutical pipeline to replace Xarelto; reposition Crop Science around digital farming and biologicals; and sustain the organisational transformation to ensure that the resources Bayer possesses are actually deployed competitively. None of these steps is individually sufficient. Together, executed in sequence, they offer a credible route to the "improved performance from 2026 onwards" that CEO Anderson has promised—but only if litigation resolution comes first, because without it, the financial constraints that bind every other strategic choice remain in place.
