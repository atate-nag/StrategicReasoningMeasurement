# BAE Systems plc: Navigating the Dual-Horizon Strategic Dilemma — Scaling for the Present While Transforming for the Future

## A Strategic Case Study and Analysis

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

BAE Systems plc, the United Kingdom's largest defence contractor and the world's seventh-largest by revenue, stands at a pivotal strategic crossroads. A decade-long surge in global defence spending, accelerated by Russia's invasion of Ukraine and rising great-power competition, has generated record order backlogs and double-digit revenue growth. Yet beneath this favourable demand environment lies a cluster of structural tensions: a supply chain dangerously exposed to Chinese rare-earth export controls, an intensifying war for STEM talent, the emergence of software-first "neo-prime" competitors such as Anduril and Palantir, and near-total dependence on government customers whose budgets remain politically volatile. This case study applies Porter's Five Forces, a SWOT analysis, the Resource-Based View (RBV) with dynamic capabilities, and the Ansoff Growth Matrix to diagnose BAE Systems' strategic position and to derive a coherent, prioritised action plan.

---

## 1. BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### 1.1 Company Overview

BAE Systems plc is a British multinational defence, security, and aerospace company headquartered in London, listed on the FTSE 100, and operating in more than 40 countries. Its portfolio spans combat aircraft (Typhoon, F-35 components, GCAP/Tempest), naval vessels (Type 26 frigate, nuclear submarines), armoured vehicles (CV90, Bradley IFV, AMPV), electronic warfare systems, cyber security, and space. BAE Systems is the largest foreign investor in the U.S. defence industry and plays a crucial role in international defence programmes like the Global Combat Air Programme (GCAP).

### 1.2 The Macro-Demand Environment

The external environment is, by historical standards, exceptionally favourable. World military expenditure rose to $2,718 billion in 2024, meaning that spending has increased every year for a full decade, going up by 37 per cent between 2015 and 2024. The 9.4 per cent increase in 2024 was the steepest year-on-year rise since at least 1988. The global military burden—the share of the world's GDP devoted to military expenditure—increased to 2.5 per cent in 2024.

Within Europe specifically, in 2024, EU member states' defence expenditure reached €343 billion, rising for the tenth consecutive year. In 2025, it is expected to reach an estimated €381 billion, representing an increase of 11% compared to the previous year and 62.87% compared to 2020. At the 2025 NATO Summit, all Allies met or exceeded the pre-summit target of investing at least 2% of GDP in defence, compared to only three Allies in 2014.

### 1.3 BAE Systems' Financial Performance

Annual sales reached £28.3 billion, a ~14% year-on-year increase in constant currency terms. Underlying EBIT climbed to £3.0 billion, up 14% in constant currency, and operating profit was £2.69 billion. Order intake in 2024 was £33.7 billion, but the order backlog swelled to a record £77.8 billion, indicating decades of future revenue. Free cash flow remained strong at £2.5 billion, supporting continued investment and shareholder returns. Management reaffirmed a positive outlook for 2025, forecasting high-single-digit growth in sales, EBIT and earnings, with guidance unchanged after a "strong start to 2025."

### 1.4 The Strategic Problem

Despite this favourable backdrop, BAE Systems faces what can be termed a **dual-horizon dilemma**: it must simultaneously *scale* to meet surging near-term demand while *transforming* its technology base, supply chain, and talent model to remain competitive in the 2030s and beyond. Failure on either horizon — execution breakdown today, or technological obsolescence tomorrow — carries existential strategic risk. The case study analyses the forces shaping this dilemma and proposes a resolution.

## 2. INDUSTRY ANALYSIS: PORTER'S FIVE FORCES

Porter's (1980) Five Forces framework examines the structural attractiveness of an industry by assessing the competitive intensity arising from rivalry, buyer power, supplier power, substitutes, and new entrants. In the defence sector, these forces take unusual and often inverted forms compared to commercial markets.

### 2.1 Rivalry Among Existing Competitors — **Moderate**

The defence industry is a rational oligopoly. The trend toward fewer and larger prime contractors has the potential to affect innovation, limit the supply base, pose entry barriers to small, medium, and large businesses, and ultimately reduce competition — resulting in higher prices. During the 1990s, the U.S. defence industry underwent drastic consolidation; the number of aerospace and defence prime contractors shrank from 51 to 5: Lockheed Martin, Raytheon, General Dynamics, Northrop Grumman, and Boeing.

BAE Systems' most significant direct competitors include major global players like Lockheed Martin, RTX Corp, Northrop Grumman, and General Dynamics. RTX Corp reported revenues of $80.7 billion in 2023, employing around 186,000 individuals. Northrop Grumman, with 97,000 employees, generated $41.0 billion in revenue during the same period. European rivals include Thales and Leonardo in radar, avionics, and regional export markets with strong local partnerships, and Anduril Industries as an emergent threat in autonomous systems and software-first capabilities funded by venture capital.

Rivalry is moderated because long-term contracts reduce immediate price competition, programmes are segmented by national industrial policy, and co-operation (e.g., F-35 consortium) often coexists with competition. However, rivalry intensifies at the programme-capture stage, where winner-takes-most dynamics apply.

### 2.2 Bargaining Power of Buyers — **High**

Governments are monopsonistic or oligopsonistic buyers. BAE Systems' significant reliance on government contracts, which accounted for around 92% of its revenue in recent reporting periods, presents a notable weakness. This heavy dependence exposes the company to the inherent volatility of political landscapes and shifts in national defence priorities. Changes in government spending or policy can directly impact BAE Systems' revenue streams, creating uncertainty.

Buyer power is further amplified by the DoD's shift in priorities: shifts to unmanned, cyber, and space are reallocating U.S. defence spending — FY2024 topline ~$858 billion — pressuring legacy programme budgets. Affordability caps and portfolio reviews constrain price escalation; multi-year procurements secure volume but demand firm cost commitments.

### 2.3 Bargaining Power of Suppliers — **High and Rising**

The supply chain is the industry's Achilles heel. China produces over 60% of global rare-earth minerals and controls over 85% of refining, especially for heavy rare-earth elements, using export controls and licensing as geopolitical leverage, most recently tightening regulations in July 2024. Since December 2024, China has banned the sale of germanium, gallium, and antimony to the United States — three key minerals heavily used in defence applications — causing major U.S. defence contractors to seek a diverse supply.

The IEA reinforces the severity: for a remarkable 19 out of 20 important strategic minerals, China is the leading refiner, with an average market share of 70%. Moreover, this concentration has only intensified in recent years. Critically, there is no heavy rare earths separation happening in the United States at present. The development of these capabilities is currently underway; in its 2024 National Defence Industrial Strategy, the DoD set a goal to develop a complete mine-to-magnet REE supply chain that can meet all U.S. defence needs by 2027.

### 2.4 Threat of Substitute Products — **Low to Moderate**

There are no commercial substitutes for a nuclear submarine or a fifth-generation fighter aircraft. However, at the mission-system level, software-defined solutions (AI-driven surveillance, cyber-attack tools, drone swarms) increasingly substitute for expensive hardware platforms — a structural shift that poses a medium-term threat to BAE's hardware-intensive revenue base.

### 2.5 Threat of New Entrants — **Low Traditionally, but Rising Disruptively**

Traditional barriers to entry are formidable. Barriers to entry only appear to be increasing as defence technology continues to get more complex and specialised. Today's incumbents are likely to become further entrenched. Security clearances, long certification cycles, and government relationships create near-impenetrable moats.

However, a new class of entrant is bypassing these barriers. Palantir, Anduril, OpenAI, and SpaceX are joining forces to bid for U.S. government defence contracts. By leveraging cutting-edge AI and autonomous technologies, this consortium aims to compete directly with long-standing defence contractors like Lockheed Martin and Boeing. The Trump administration has been explicit: Secretary of the Army Dan Driscoll articulated the administration's position: "We are going to completely disrupt the system that held the Army back for decades." Pentagon Chief Technology Officer Emil Michael's call for "5 more Andurils, Palantirs, and SpaceXs" constitutes perhaps the most unambiguous statement of the administration's industrial vision.

**Porter's Five Forces Summary:**

| Force | Intensity | Key Driver |
|---|---|---|
| Rivalry | Moderate | Rational oligopoly; programme-level winner-takes-most |
| Buyer Power | High | Government monopsony; shifting priorities |
| Supplier Power | High & Rising | Chinese REE dominance; specialised sub-tier fragility |
| Substitutes | Low–Moderate | Software/AI displacing hardware at mission level |
| New Entrants | Low (traditional) / Rising (tech) | Anduril/Palantir bypassing legacy barriers |

**Analytical inference:** The industry's structural attractiveness is eroding at the margins. Buyer power and supplier power are both elevated, and a new competitive dynamic from tech-native entrants is emerging. BAE must act on both supply chain resilience and technology transformation to defend its position.

## 3. INTERNAL ANALYSIS: SWOT

The SWOT framework (Andrews, 1971) connects internal capabilities with external conditions, providing the bridge between the industry analysis above and the strategic choices below.

### 3.1 Strengths

**Scale and Portfolio Breadth.** BAE Systems boasts a formidable global market leadership, operating in over 40 countries. This extensive reach, coupled with a diverse portfolio covering air, land, naval, electronic systems, and cybersecurity, significantly reduces its dependence on any single market or project.

**Technological Capability and R&D Investment.** BAE Systems' technological prowess is a significant advantage, with substantial investments in innovation across defence, aerospace, and security. The company is a leader in integrating advanced technologies such as AI, autonomous systems, and electronic warfare, exemplified by a 9% increase in R&D spending to £1.8 billion in 2023.

**Long-term Programme Visibility.** Order intake in 2024 was £33.7 billion, and the order backlog swelled to a record £77.8 billion, indicating decades of future revenue. This gives BAE exceptional revenue visibility unavailable to most industrial companies.

**Workforce and Human Capital.** The company's workforce of approximately 107,000 skilled employees globally is a critical asset for delivering complex, multi-domain programmes. BAE spent £230 million on education and skills in 2024.

**GCAP/Tempest Flagship Programme.** The Global Combat Air Programme (GCAP) is a multi-national collaborative project involving Italy, the United Kingdom, and Japan, with the shared ambition of producing the next-generation fighter aircraft by 2035. In December 2024, Leonardo, BAE Systems, and Japan Aircraft Industrial Enhancement Co. Ltd. reached a landmark agreement to establish an international strategic partnership to deliver the next-generation aircraft for GCAP.

### 3.2 Weaknesses

**Government Revenue Concentration.** BAE Systems' significant reliance on government contracts, which accounted for around 92% of its revenue in recent reporting periods, presents a notable weakness. This heavy dependence exposes the company to the inherent volatility of political landscapes and shifts in national defence priorities.

**Regulatory and Export Complexity.** BAE Systems navigates a labyrinth of international regulations, a significant weakness. Operating in over 40 countries means grappling with ITAR, UK export licensing, and sanctions regimes simultaneously — increasing compliance costs and slowing contract execution.

**Supply Chain Fragility.** BAE's broad spread of projects contributed to double-digit growth in 2024 despite supply chain challenges and inflation — a tacit admission that supply chain stress is a live operational constraint.

**Execution Risk on Complex Programmes.** Execution risks on complex programmes represent a key challenge, as delays or cost overruns can pressure margins. Progress on high-profile projects like submarines, where supply chain issues persist, must be tracked.

### 3.3 Opportunities

**Sustained NATO Rearming.** At the 2025 NATO Summit in The Hague, Allies made a commitment to investing 5% of GDP annually on core defence requirements and defence- and security-related spending by 2035. This trajectory represents a structural, multi-decade demand tailwind directly aligned with BAE's product portfolio.

**European Rearmament and AUKUS.** BAE won orders worth ~$2.5 billion to supply upgraded CV9035 MkIIIC vehicles to Sweden and Denmark — a significant boost driven by NATO countries rearming. AUKUS submarine commitments similarly provide long-horizon revenue.

**AI and Autonomous Systems.** With contracts totalling over $1.3 billion in 2024–2025 alone, the Department of Defense is doubling down on artificial intelligence to streamline operations, enhance cybersecurity, and modernise decision-making.

**Supply Chain Reshoring.** The REE crisis is also an opportunity: firms that invest early in alternative supply chains will achieve a durable cost and resilience advantage over competitors who act late.

### 3.4 Threats

**Chinese Rare Earth Export Controls.** China has imposed its most stringent rare earth and magnet export controls yet, restricting products with even trace Chinese content. This bolsters its leverage ahead of strategic negotiations and heightens risks to U.S. defence and semiconductor supply chains.

**Tech-Native Competitors.** As detailed in the Five Forces analysis, Anduril, Palantir, and their consortium represent a fundamentally different competitive model — faster, software-centric, and now politically favoured by the current U.S. administration.

**STEM Talent Shortage.** Risk executives are struggling to keep up with an increasingly complex threat environment due to a shortage of cybersecurity professionals and critical skills gaps on existing teams. The problem is systemic across the defence-industrial base.

**Geopolitical Volatility in Spending.** U.S. defence budget uncertainty — including DOGE-driven efficiency reviews — introduces risk to BAE's substantial U.S. revenues, which represent its largest single-country market.

## 4. RESOURCE-BASED VIEW AND DYNAMIC CAPABILITIES

While Porter's framework explains *industry* attractiveness and SWOT maps *position*, the Resource-Based View (Barney, 1991) and Teece's Dynamic Capabilities framework (2007) explain *why* some firms sustain competitive advantage even as conditions change. Grounded in the RBV, dynamic capabilities theory assumes that companies require certain capabilities to be successful and responsive to dynamic changes in their environment by creating, integrating, and modifying their resource base.

### 4.1 Applying the VRIN Framework

For a resource to generate sustained competitive advantage, it must be **V**aluable, **R**are, **I**mperfectly imitable, and **N**on-substitutable (Barney, 1991).

| Resource / Capability | Valuable? | Rare? | Inimitable? | Non-substitutable? | Assessment |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Nuclear submarine design and build (Astute, AUKUS) | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ (decades of tacit knowledge) | ✓ | **Core SCA** |
| F-35 electronic warfare integration | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ (sole-source) | Partial | **Core SCA** |
| GCAP/Tempest design leadership | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ (government-backed) | Partial | **Emerging SCA** |
| Scale manufacturing (CV90, Bradley) | ✓ | Partial | Partial | Partial | **Competitive parity** |
| AI/Autonomous R&D capability | ✓ | No (Anduril, Palantir) | No | No | **At risk** |
| Supply chain (REE-dependent) | ✓ | No | No | No | **Vulnerability** |

### 4.2 Dynamic Capabilities Gap

Teece (2007) identifies three clusters of dynamic capabilities: **sensing** (identifying threats/opportunities), **seizing** (mobilising resources to capture value), and **reconfiguring** (transforming the asset base). A capability is something valuable that the organisation has learned to do that is subject to management control — a type of intangible asset.

BAE's sensing capability is strong: it correctly identified the AI/autonomy shift and has invested in R&D. Its seizing capability is moderate: GCAP and the F-35 demonstrate the ability to win and execute large, complex programmes. However, its **reconfiguring** capability — the ability to rapidly reshape the organisation, supply chain, and talent base — is the weakest link. The company's 107,000-person workforce, legacy procurement processes, and government-contract compliance culture create organisational inertia that tech-native entrants do not face.

**Analytical inference:** BAE's most durable competitive advantages reside in regulated, tacit-knowledge-intensive domains (nuclear submarines, classified electronic warfare). Its vulnerability is greatest where speed and software agility matter most. Strategy must protect the former and urgently build the latter.

## 5. GROWTH STRATEGY ANALYSIS: ANSOFF MATRIX

The Ansoff (1957) Product/Market Expansion Grid structures four growth options by risk level: Market Penetration (existing products, existing markets), Market Development (existing products, new markets), Product Development (new products, existing markets), and Diversification (new products, new markets).

The Ansoff Matrix, often called the Product/Market Expansion Grid, is a two-by-two framework used by management teams and the analyst community to help plan and evaluate growth initiatives. In particular, the tool helps stakeholders conceptualise the level of risk associated with different growth strategies.

BAE's current strategic activity maps across all four quadrants:

| Quadrant | BAE Activity | Risk Level | Assessment |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Market Penetration** | Scaling CV90 production for Sweden/Denmark; increased Typhoon sustainment | Low | Strong — exploiting existing NATO rearming demand |
| **Market Development** | AUKUS submarine export to Australia; Hunter-class frigates for RAN | Medium | Strong — leveraging existing nuclear/naval IP in new geographies |
| **Product Development** | GCAP next-generation fighter; AI-enabled electronic warfare systems | High | Critical — requires sustained R&D investment |
| **Diversification** | Cyber security services for non-defence government clients | Very High | Nascent — under-exploited given cyber talent base |

The strategic implication of the Ansoff analysis is that BAE is appropriately concentrated in the lower-risk quadrants (penetration and market development) to fund the higher-risk quadrants (product development and selective diversification). The risk is that the pace of investment in product development is insufficient to counter the speed of tech-native entrants in the AI/autonomous systems space.

## 6. COHERENT STRATEGIC DIAGNOSIS

The three frameworks above generate a coherent, interlocking diagnosis:

1. **Porter** reveals that the industry's structural attractiveness is being eroded by rising supplier power (REE), sustained buyer power (government monopsony), and a disruptive new-entrant threat (tech-natives). BAE cannot rely on industry structure alone to protect margins.

2. **SWOT** confirms that BAE's strengths (scale, backlog, GCAP) are real but that its weaknesses (supply chain exposure, government dependence, execution risk) and threats (REE controls, Anduril/Palantir, talent shortage) are material and interrelated.

3. **RBV/Dynamic Capabilities** reveals *why* the threats are serious: BAE's core sustainable competitive advantages are in tacit, regulated domains, but its dynamic reconfiguring capability — the ability to rapidly adapt supply chains, absorb AI talent, and pivot to software-defined products — is underdeveloped relative to the pace of environmental change.

4. **Ansoff** shows that the company is correctly prioritising near-term penetration and market development to generate cash, but must accelerate product development investment or risk being outpaced in the next-generation technology cycle.

The **root strategic tension** is therefore: *BAE's greatest strengths (scale, complexity, tacit knowledge) are also the source of its greatest inertia, precisely when the competitive environment rewards speed and software agility.*

## 7. RECOMMENDATIONS AND ACTION PLAN

The following recommendations are ordered by strategic priority and time horizon.

---

### Recommendation 1: Build a Sovereign Critical Minerals Supply Chain (Immediate — 0–18 months)

**Rationale:** For 19 out of 20 important strategic minerals, China is the leading refiner, with an average market share of 70%. Since December 2024, China has banned the sale of germanium, gallium, and antimony to the United States — three key minerals heavily used in defence applications. This is not a speculative risk; it is an active supply disruption. Given that allied collaboration is necessary for creating resilient supply chains, BAE should act as a first mover.

**Actions:**
- Establish a **Critical Minerals Consortium** with Rolls-Royce, Leonardo UK, and MBDA (GCAP partners) to pool purchasing power and co-invest in alternative REE processing capacity in allied nations (Canada, Australia, Greenland).
- Map the entire Tier 1–3 supply chain for REE dependency within 90 days; publish a Board-level supply-chain resilience KPI.
- Lobby the UK Ministry of Defence to include REE supply chain investment as an allowable cost under cost-plus contracts, as the DoD has done under its 2024 National Defence Industrial Strategy.
- Maintain a 90-day strategic buffer stock of the six most critical minerals used in guidance systems and electronic warfare.

**Expected Outcome:** Reduce supply disruption risk, lower supplier bargaining power, and position BAE as the most resilient prime contractor — a competitive differentiator in contract bids.

---

### Recommendation 2: Establish a "Digital Battlespace" Internal Venture Unit (Immediate–Medium — 6–24 months)

**Rationale:** Palantir, Anduril, OpenAI, and SpaceX are joining forces to bid for U.S. government defence contracts, leveraging cutting-edge AI and autonomous technologies to compete directly with long-standing defence contractors. BAE cannot match the speed of venture-backed software firms through its existing programme management culture. However, it possesses something they lack: decades of classified domain knowledge, security clearances, and trusted government relationships.

**Actions:**
- Create a ring-fenced **"BAE Digital Battlespace" (BDB) unit** — structurally separate from the main business, with its own P&L, agile development processes, and a remuneration structure competitive with Silicon Valley (equity-like instruments, milestone bonuses).
- Staff BDB with a target of 2,000 AI/software engineers within 24 months, recruited from universities, tech companies, and through the existing DoD cyber talent pipeline.
- Give BDB a mandate to build **software-defined electronic warfare** and **AI-enabled autonomous systems** products that can be integrated into existing hardware platforms (Typhoon, CV90, Type 26) — exploiting BAE's hardware base rather than competing with it.
- Structure BDB as a potential future spin-off or strategic partnership vehicle, preserving optionality.

**Expected Outcome:** Closes the dynamic capability gap identified in the RBV analysis; creates a credible counter to Anduril/Palantir; retains AI talent that would otherwise be lost to tech firms.

---

### Recommendation 3: Accelerate the GCAP Programme as a Strategic Anchor (Medium — 12–36 months)

**Rationale:** The Global Combat Air Programme (GCAP) is a multi-national collaborative project involving Italy, the United Kingdom, and Japan, with the shared ambition of producing the next-generation fighter aircraft by 2035. In December 2024, Leonardo, BAE Systems, and Japan Aircraft Industrial Enhancement Co. Ltd. reached a landmark agreement to establish an international strategic partnership to deliver the next-generation aircraft for GCAP. GCAP is BAE's most important product-development investment and, if executed well, will be the cornerstone of its competitive position for 40 years.

**Actions:**
- Secure UK government commitment to a firm production contract and export financing facility to enable third-nation sales (India, Saudi Arabia, potential ASEAN customers) — transforming GCAP from a three-nation programme into a wider alliance platform.
- Invest in **digital twin and model-based systems engineering** to reduce development time and cost overrun risk — the programme's greatest execution vulnerability.
- Pursue an active **IP licensing and workshare** strategy: rather than competing with Leonardo and JAIEC, create a collaborative IP pool that makes the GCAP consortium more attractive to export customers than competing programmes (NGAD, FCAS).
- Establish a dedicated GCAP Supply Chain Resilience Office to pre-qualify alternative suppliers for all critical components before production ramp.

**Expected Outcome:** GCAP becomes a 40-year revenue anchor equivalent to the F-35 programme's value to Lockheed Martin; the workshare model converts potential rivals into locked-in partners.

---

### Recommendation 4: Reduce Government Revenue Concentration through Cyber and Space Diversification (Medium–Long — 18–60 months)

**Rationale:** BAE Systems' reliance on government contracts, which accounted for around 92% of its revenue, presents a notable weakness. This heavy dependence exposes the company to the inherent volatility of political landscapes and shifts in national defence priorities. The Ansoff diversification quadrant offers a path to reducing this concentration risk.

**Actions:**
- Scale the **cyber security division** to serve critical national infrastructure operators (energy, finance, transport) — sectors where BAE's classified threat intelligence provides a genuine edge over commercial cyber firms.
- Pursue **bolt-on acquisitions** of mid-sized cyber and space analytics firms (sub-£500m enterprise value) to accelerate capability building rather than organic growth alone.
- Develop a **"Defence-as-a-Service"** recurring revenue model for sustainment, training simulation, and predictive maintenance — converting one-time hardware contracts into multi-year software/service streams that improve revenue quality and reduce cyclicality.
- Target a medium-term goal of reducing government contract concentration from 92% to 80% of revenue within five years.

**Expected Outcome:** Improved revenue resilience; higher earnings quality (recurring software/service revenues trade at higher multiples); reduced political risk.

---

### Recommendation 5: Implement a Strategic Workforce Transformation Programme (Ongoing — 0–60 months)

**Rationale:** Risk executives are struggling to keep up with an increasingly complex threat environment due to a shortage of cybersecurity professionals and critical skills gaps on existing teams. BAE's own data shows the scale of the investment required: BAE spent £230 million on education and skills in 2024. This is substantial but must be directed more strategically.

**Actions:**
- Establish **BAE Systems University Partnerships** with at least ten leading STEM institutions globally (MIT, Imperial College, Tokyo Tech, ETH Zurich), providing funded PhD and Masters programmes with guaranteed placement — building a proprietary talent pipeline.
- Introduce a **"Tour of Duty" model**: partner with the DoD and UK MoD to create structured secondment programmes where government cyber professionals rotate into BAE and vice versa, building mutual trust and shared capability without permanent recruitment.
- Create an internal **"AI Academy"** to upskill existing engineers in machine learning, digital engineering, and model-based systems — converting the existing 107,000-person workforce into a competitive asset rather than an inertial constraint.
- Tie senior executive compensation explicitly to workforce transformation KPIs (AI-qualified engineers as % of workforce; retention rate of top-quartile STEM talent).

**Expected Outcome:** Addresses the talent shortage identified as a systemic threat; builds the human capital base required to execute the Digital Battlespace unit; reduces dependence on external hiring in a tight market.

## 9. CONCLUSION

BAE Systems enters the mid-2020s from a position of genuine strength: annual sales reached £28.3 billion, a ~14% year-on-year increase in constant currency terms, and the order backlog swelled to a record £77.8 billion. Yet the strategic analysis reveals that this strength is neither automatic nor permanent. The Five Forces analysis shows a structurally challenging industry where supplier power (REE), buyer power (government monopsony), and disruptive new entrants (tech-native firms) are all intensifying. The SWOT analysis confirms that BAE's 92% government revenue concentration and supply chain exposure are material vulnerabilities. The RBV analysis identifies a dynamic capability gap — particularly in reconfiguring speed — that, if unaddressed, will allow software-first competitors to capture the next technology cycle. The Ansoff analysis shows that BAE is correctly harvesting near-term demand but must accelerate product development investment to sustain long-run competitiveness.

The recommended action plan — supply chain resilience, a ring-fenced digital innovation unit, GCAP acceleration, controlled diversification, and workforce transformation — is not a set of isolated initiatives. Each recommendation addresses a specific gap identified in the analysis, and together they form a coherent strategic programme to resolve the dual-horizon dilemma: scale for the present, transform for the future.

The reasoning chain is explicit: structural industry forces create the threats; SWOT maps them onto BAE's specific position; RBV explains why they are difficult to address quickly; and Ansoff structures the growth response. The recommendations follow directly and necessarily from this chain of analysis. BAE Systems has the financial resources, the programme portfolio, and the institutional relationships to execute this transformation. The question is whether it has the organisational urgency to act before the window narrows.
