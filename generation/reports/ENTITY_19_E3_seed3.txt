# Diageo: Navigating the Premiumisation Paradox — A Strategic Case Study

## 1. Introduction and Strategic Situation

Diageo plc, headquartered in London, is the world's largest premium spirits company by revenue. Diageo is a global leader in beverage alcohol with an outstanding collection of brands including Johnnie Walker, Crown Royal, J&B and Buchanan's whiskies, Smirnoff, Cîroc and Ketel One vodkas, Captain Morgan, Baileys, Don Julio, Tanqueray and Guinness, with products sold in nearly 180 countries around the world.

The strategic situation under examination is this: Diageo built its entire growth architecture on **premiumisation** — the thesis that consumers would consistently trade up to higher-priced spirits — yet the external environment between 2023 and 2025 has systematically undermined that thesis. The result is a company caught between its long-run strategic identity and a short-run structural demand shock, simultaneously facing: (1) a stalling US spirits market; (2) a collapsing Chinese white spirits (CWS) position; (3) the rise of the no- and low-alcohol movement; and (4) geopolitical tariff risk. The strategic question is therefore: **how should Diageo reconfigure its portfolio, geographic footprint, and operating model to restore profitable growth without abandoning the premium positioning that constitutes its core competitive advantage?**

## 2. Situational Analysis

### 2.1 PESTEL Analysis

**Political/Legal**

Diageo has suggested the US government consider tougher rules of origin requirements in trade agreements as an alternative to tariffs, signalling that the Trump administration's tariff agenda represents a material political risk to the business. The company's own guidance explicitly acknowledges this: Diageo's fiscal 26 operating profit guidance includes "the impact of tariffs as at this time," with organic operating profit expected to be low to mid-single digit, including the impacts of Chinese white spirits and a weaker US consumer environment. This matters analytically because tariffs act as a cost shock on Diageo's cross-border supply chains (e.g., Scotch whisky exported to the US) and a demand shock if retaliatory tariffs are imposed on American-made spirits sold abroad, compressing margins at both ends simultaneously.

**Economic**

The macroeconomic environment has directly suppressed Diageo's core US market. In 2024, spirits depletions fell by 3.7%, with revenue down 4.3%. Critically, this is not merely a volume story: the once-reliable premiumisation trend is "all but dead," according to the WSWA, with spirits revenue declining -4.3% in 2024. Further: super-premium spirits fell by 5.6% in revenue and 4.8% in volume, while value spirits grew by 3.5% in revenue and 4.6% in volume. This "trading down" dynamic is the precise inverse of Diageo's strategic bet, meaning the company's premium-heavy portfolio is disproportionately exposed to the economic cycle. The inference is clear: Diageo's strategy generates outsized returns during consumer confidence upswings but creates outsized downside during contractions, revealing a structural cyclicality risk that management must address.

**Social/Consumer**

Two social trends compound the economic headwinds. First, the "sober curious" movement: all signs indicate that low and no-alcohol drinks are here to stay and set to claim a lasting share of the market, with IWSR analysts describing the current environment as a "transformative period." Second, and more structurally threatening, is the rise of GLP-1 weight-loss medications: analysis of third-party studies points to a potential 75% reduction in alcohol consumption among all patients taking GLP-1 drugs, driven by 50% fewer drinking occasions and 50% fewer drinks per occasion. While GLP-1 adoption remains a minority phenomenon today, the directionality of this trend represents a secular demand headwind that no pricing strategy can fully offset.

**Technological**

The Ready-to-Drink (RTD) category is reshaping the competitive landscape. Big players including Diageo are investing in the RTD space, with Diageo's The Cocktail Collection among the spirits-based RTD offerings, as a new generation of consumers demand solutions on their terms. RTDs represent both a threat (cannibalising traditional spirit occasions) and an opportunity (extending brand reach to new consumption contexts). The analytical implication is that Diageo must treat RTD not as a peripheral product line but as a strategic battleground for occasion ownership.

**Environmental**

Climate-driven yield variability affected agave and barley prices in 2024–2025; tequila production costs rose materially amid significant agave scarcity. Since Don Julio (tequila) is one of Diageo's highest-growth brands, agave cost inflation directly compresses the margin on its most strategically important asset, creating a tension between volume growth and profitability.

**Legal/Regulatory**

The three-tier US distribution system remains a structural constraint on Diageo's route-to-market efficiency. The company's own reporting acknowledges progress on "US route-to-market enhancements" as a priority, while consumers continue to be cautious, Diageo is focused on strengthening the resilience of its business through operational excellence, productivity and strategic investments to win quality market share, including US route-to-market enhancements.

### 2.2 Porter's Five Forces

**Threat of New Entrants — Low–Moderate**

The spirits industry is characterised by high barriers to entry: brand equity accumulated over decades, aged inventory requirements, complex global distribution networks, and regulatory licensing. However, the craft spirits movement has lowered barriers at the premium niche level. Brown-Forman leverages Jack Daniel's brand extensions and aggressive distribution to contest Diageo in American whiskey, impacting Diageo's US market share in key categories. The relevant insight is that while new entrants cannot replicate Diageo's scale, they can erode share in specific high-margin sub-categories (e.g., craft bourbon, artisanal gin), threatening the long-tail of Diageo's portfolio.

**Bargaining Power of Suppliers — Moderate**

Agave scarcity (noted above) illustrates how commodity suppliers can exert power during supply shocks. Diageo's scale generally provides procurement leverage, but single-source agricultural inputs (agave, specific barley varieties for Scotch) create pockets of supplier power that cannot be fully mitigated.

**Bargaining Power of Buyers — High and Rising**

In the on-trade (bars, restaurants), large hospitality chains can negotiate listing fees and promotional support from Diageo, reducing margin. In the off-trade (retail), consolidation among global grocery and liquor retail chains (e.g., Walmart, Costco, Total Wine in the US) concentrates purchasing power. More importantly, the end consumer's bargaining power has increased because the economic environment has made them more price-sensitive: the trading-down data cited above (super-premium volumes down 4.8%, value volumes up 4.6%) reflects consumers exercising their substitution power.

**Threat of Substitutes — High**

Substitution threats operate at multiple levels: beer and wine substituting for spirits; RTDs substituting for poured cocktails; non-alcoholic alternatives substituting for all alcohol categories. The total beverage alcohol market has grown at 4.4% CAGR in the 10 years through 2023, and international spirits has grown at 5.1% over the same period, suggesting spirits have historically *been* the substitute that consumers switched *to*. But the sober-curious and GLP-1 trends represent a potential structural break in this pattern, making the substitution threat more acute than the historical data implies.

**Competitive Rivalry — High**

Major competitors in the premium spirits market include Pernod Ricard (France), Brown-Forman (USA), Bacardi (Bermuda), Beam Suntory (Japan), and Campari Group (Italy), all of whom are pursuing similar premiumisation strategies and competing for the same shrinking pool of consumer spending. Pernod Ricard intensifies premium whisky competition in India, vying for leadership with Diageo. The implication is that rivalry is most intense precisely where Diageo's growth aspirations are highest — premium and super-premium tiers in emerging markets — creating a competitive squeeze.

### 2.3 SWOT Analysis

**Strengths**

*Brand Portfolio Depth:* Diageo commands immense brand equity through its diverse portfolio, featuring globally recognised names, allowing it to cater to a wide spectrum of consumer tastes and preferences. This is not merely a marketing asset; it confers pricing power, retailer shelf space priority, and consumer loyalty that competitors cannot replicate quickly.

*Scale and Distribution:* Operating in nearly 180 countries gives Diageo unmatched distribution infrastructure, enabling it to shift investment toward high-growth geographies when developed markets slow.

*Innovation Capability:* Guinness 0.0 net sales and volume more than doubled in the year, demonstrating that Diageo can successfully extend its most iconic brands into the no/low alcohol category — a capability that most pure-play spirits rivals lack.

**Weaknesses**

*Over-Concentration in Premiumisation:* The company's strategic identity is so tightly coupled to premiumisation that it lacks a credible mid-tier value proposition. When consumers trade down, Diageo has limited portfolio assets to capture that demand shift.

*China Exposure:* The CWS (Chinese white spirits) business has become a material drag. Excluding CWS, organic net sales for the group would have been approximately 2% higher in FY2025, with volume down approximately 0.5% and price/mix broadly flat. This quantifies the cost of the CWS position: it subtracted roughly 2 percentage points from group growth, turning modest organic growth into near-stagnation.

*Leverage:* Net debt as at 30 June 2025 was $21.9 billion, with a leverage ratio of 3.4x net debt to adjusted EBITDA. This constrains Diageo's financial flexibility for acquisitions and investment precisely when it most needs to pivot its portfolio.

**Opportunities**

*Emerging Market Growth:* Diageo's fiscal 2025 results underscored the diverging fortunes of its portfolio across developed and emerging markets, with overall organic sales growth of 1.7% disproportionately supported by emerging markets, with Africa delivering organic net sales growth across all markets, led by Ghana, while Latin America and the Caribbean saw premiumisation-led growth in Mexico.

*No/Low Alcohol:* Diageo's broader non-alcoholic portfolio delivered strong performance in fiscal 25, growing approximately 40%, with particularly strong momentum in Guinness 0.0, which delivered double-digit net sales growth with standout performance in Great Britain and Ireland. This growth rate, from an admittedly small base, signals a category that could become material.

*Luxury Consolidation:* The Diageo Luxury Group, announced in November 2024, is a new global division which unites Diageo's luxury assets into one portfolio including key luxury brands Brora and Port Ellen, iconic Scottish brand homes including Johnnie Walker Princes Street, and Justerini & Brooks. Consolidating luxury assets under a single organisational unit enables focused investment and coherent positioning at the ultra-premium tier, where margins are highest and consumer loyalty most durable.

**Threats**

*US Tariffs:* As noted in the PESTEL, tariffs threaten both cost and demand simultaneously.

*GLP-1 Drugs:* The potential 75% reduction in alcohol consumption among users is a structural secular threat that Diageo cannot price or market its way out of.

*Competitive Intensification:* All major rivals are pursuing the same emerging-market premiumisation strategy, meaning Diageo's first-mover advantage in markets like India is being eroded.

## 3. The Core Strategic Problem: A Coherent Chain of Reasoning

The analysis above, taken together, supports the following chain of inference:

1. **Diageo's strategy is built on premiumisation** — the expectation that consumers will consistently trade up — which drove exceptional performance from 2020–2022 when post-pandemic revenge spending inflated demand for premium goods.

2. **The premiumisation thesis has stalled in Diageo's largest market** (the US): consumers are not trading up as they consume less — the premiumisation trend is all but dead, according to WSWA data. This is not a temporary blip but a structural consequence of cost-of-living pressures and changed consumption habits.

Organic growth of 1.7% against a company that set an ambition to grow TBA market share from 4% to 6% by 2030 represents a material shortfall.

4. **The CWS problem compounds the US weakness:** the two largest headwinds — US consumer caution and Chinese white spirits — are simultaneously depressing the company's two most strategically important markets, creating a dual-market drag that portfolio diversification cannot easily offset.

5. **Secular trends (sober curious, GLP-1) threaten to make the demand shortfall permanent rather than cyclical**, meaning a purely tactical response (wait for the cycle to turn) is insufficient.

6. **Therefore**, Diageo needs a structural portfolio and geographic rebalancing, not merely operational efficiency improvements, to restore the growth trajectory required to reach its 2030 ambitions.

The following recommendations are sequenced by urgency and derive directly from the analytical findings above.

### Recommendation 1: Divest CWS (Shui Jing Fang) and Redeploy Capital to India and Africa

### Recommendation 2: Accelerate the No/Low Alcohol Portfolio as a Strategic Pillar, Not a Niche

**Warrant:** The PESTEL analysis identifies the sober-curious movement and GLP-1 drugs as secular demand headwinds. **Action:** Double marketing investment behind Guinness 0.0 and Tanqueray 0.0 over the next 18 months; establish a dedicated no/low innovation fund within the Diageo Luxury Group structure to develop new premium non-alcoholic expressions; set an explicit revenue target (e.g., 5% of net sales from no/low by FY2028) to force organisational accountability.

### Recommendation 3: Introduce a Tiered Value Architecture in the US to Capture Trading-Down Consumers

**Warrant:** The SWOT analysis identifies Diageo's over-concentration in premiumisation as a structural weakness. The market data shows value spirits growing 3.5% in revenue and 4.6% in volume while super-premium fell 5.6%. A company with no credible mid-tier offering loses these consumers entirely to competitors. However, Diageo cannot simply launch cheap products without damaging its premium brand equity — the solution is a tiered architecture that uses separate sub-brands or existing "cash cow" brands (Smirnoff, Captain Morgan) as the accessible entry point, while keeping the premium tier intact. This is consistent with the Porter Five Forces finding that buyer bargaining power has increased: Diageo must give price-sensitive consumers a reason to remain within the Diageo portfolio ecosystem rather than switching to private label or competitor value brands.

**Action:** Reposition Smirnoff and Captain Morgan explicitly as "quality-accessible" brands in US retail with targeted price promotions; invest in RTD formats for these brands (lower price point, higher convenience) to capture the on-the-go consumption occasion. Diageo's The Cocktail Collection is already in the RTD space; this should be expanded with more accessible price-point variants.

### Recommendation 4: Leverage the Diageo Luxury Group to Insulate Margin at the Ultra-Premium Tier

**Warrant:** While the mass premium segment is under pressure, the ultra-luxury segment has historically shown more resilience to economic cycles (luxury goods consumers are less price-sensitive). The introduction of the Diageo Luxury Group in November 2024, targeting brands priced at $100 and above, signifies this premiumisation strategy. The analytical logic is that Diageo should pursue a "barbell" portfolio strategy: invest in accessible value at the bottom (Recommendation 3) and ultra-luxury at the top (Recommendation 4), while accepting that the middle-premium tier will face the most competitive pressure. This directly addresses the BCG finding that Johnnie Walker (a cash cow) needs to be managed efficiently while ultra-premium brands like Brora and Port Ellen are invested for growth.

**Action:** Expand the Diageo Luxury Group's experiential offering (distillery visits, rare cask programmes, private client services) as a high-margin revenue stream that is insulated from commodity pricing pressures; target the ultra-high-net-worth consumer segment in Asia (particularly India and Southeast Asia) where Diageo's 2025 expansion prioritises Asia-Pacific, with a major scale-up in India to capture premium Scotch and local craft spirit demand.

### Recommendation 5: Accelerate the Accelerate Programme and Reduce Leverage to Restore Strategic Flexibility

**Warrant:** The SWOT analysis identifies Diageo's 3.4x leverage ratio as a constraint on strategic optionality. The Accelerate programme's cost savings target has been increased to approximately $625 million, up from approximately $500 million, which is the right directional move. However, cost savings alone will not resolve the strategic situation — they must be combined with asset disposals (Recommendation 1) to reduce leverage and free capital for redeployment. The rationale is that a highly leveraged Diageo cannot respond opportunistically to M&A opportunities (e.g., acquiring a fast-growing no/low alcohol brand or an emerging market distributor) and is more vulnerable to credit rating pressure if organic performance continues to disappoint.

**Action:** Target leverage ratio of 2.5x within 24 months through a combination of Accelerate savings, CWS disposal proceeds, and disciplined capital allocation; suspend share buybacks until leverage target is achieved.

## 5. Conclusion

Diageo's strategic situation is not a crisis of brand equity or competitive position — its brands remain among the world's most valuable — but a crisis of strategic fit between its portfolio architecture and the current demand environment. The premiumisation thesis that drove a decade of outperformance has been disrupted by a confluence of macroeconomic, social, and geopolitical forces simultaneously. Organic net sales growth of 1.7% and EPS pre-exceptionals down 8.6% in FY2025 are the financial expression of this misalignment.

The recommendations above form a coherent strategic response: divest the CWS drag and redeploy capital to high-growth emerging markets (correcting geographic misallocation); build the no/low portfolio from a Question Mark to a Star (correcting category misallocation); introduce a value tier to retain trading-down US consumers (correcting price-tier misallocation); and use the Luxury Group to protect margin at the ultra-premium end (preserving the core identity). The Accelerate programme provides the operational efficiency foundation that makes all of the above financially viable. Taken together, these moves constitute a "barbell" strategy that preserves Diageo's premium identity while building resilience against the cyclical and secular forces that have exposed the fragility of a single-minded premiumisation approach.
