# SLB (Schlumberger): A Strategic Case Study
## Navigating the Dual Imperative — Sustaining Oilfield Leadership While Transitioning to an Energy Technology Company

---

## 1. Introduction and Case Overview

SLB (formerly Schlumberger) occupies a paradoxical strategic position in 2024–2025. Founded in 1926, SLB has evolved from pioneering well logging to becoming the world's largest oilfield services provider, with $36.3 billion in revenue for 2024 and 111,000 employees globally. Yet this very dominance creates a strategic dilemma: the industry that built SLB is under structural pressure from the global energy transition, while the digital and low-carbon sectors that represent its future require capabilities and business models that differ fundamentally from its legacy core.

This case study examines the central strategic situation facing SLB: **how to simultaneously defend and monetise its position in a cyclically volatile but still-growing traditional oilfield services market, while successfully pivoting towards digital technology and new energy—before the window of financial strength to fund that pivot narrows.** The analysis applies PESTEL, Porter's Five Forces, a Resource-Based View (VRIO), and Ansoff's Growth Matrix to generate a coherent set of strategic recommendations.

## 2. Background and Financial Context

### 2.1 Recent Financial Performance

SLB's financial trajectory over 2022–2024 has been one of strong recovery and growth. In 2023, SLB fulfilled the company's full-year financial targets, surpassing its revenue target and generating exceptional cash flows, posting $33.14 billion in revenue—up 18% year on year—and $4.20 billion in net income attributable to SLB, up 22% year on year. Cash flow from operations grew 78% year on year to $6.64 billion, and adjusted EBITDA reached $8.11 billion, up 25% year on year.

In 2024, growth moderated but remained positive. Nine-month 2024 revenue of $27.0 billion increased 12% year on year, with approximately 50% of the year-on-year revenue increase coming from the acquisition of the Aker Solutions subsea business in the fourth quarter of 2023. By Q3 2024, international revenue grew by 16% year on year, while North America revenue decreased by 3% due to lower drilling activity.

By Q1 2025, the macro environment had deteriorated. SLB reported revenue of $8.49 billion, a year-on-year decline of 2.5%, driven by weaker activity in international markets, though management emphasised continued execution discipline and margin resilience, delivering an adjusted EBITDA margin of 23.8%, up 18 basis points year on year.

### 2.2 The Three-Engine Strategic Architecture

SLB articulates its strategy through three growth engines. The Core business, comprising Reservoir Performance, Well Construction, and Production Systems, was fuelled by strong growth in the international and offshore markets, with SLB continuing to benefit from long-cycle developments, capacity expansions, and exploration and appraisal activities, particularly in the Middle East, where revenue reached a record high, and across offshore basins in Brazil, Angola, the US Gulf of Mexico, Guyana, and Norway. Digital continued its strong growth momentum, delivering more than $2 billion in revenue in 2023, driven by further adoption of new digital technology platforms comprising cloud, edge, and AI, as more customers embraced the integration of connected and autonomous, data, and AI solutions. The third engine, New Energy, targets carbon capture, geothermal, critical minerals, energy storage, and hydrogen.

## 3. External Environment Analysis: PESTEL

### 3.1 Political

SLB operates across more than 100 countries, making it acutely exposed to geopolitical volatility. As a global entity operating in over 100 countries, SLB faces regulatory and geopolitical risks that can affect its operations. The company's significant receivables in Mexico, accounting for 15% of the net accounts receivable balance, illustrate the concentration risk and the potential for exposure to local economic and political instability.

Sanctions regimes represent a further constraint. SLB completed the wind-down of its Iranian operations in 2013 and continues to manage residual tax obligations there. More broadly, the conflict in Ukraine is explicitly listed in SLB's risk disclosures as a material source of uncertainty. In the current environment, commodity prices are challenged, and until they stabilise, customers are likely to take a more cautious approach to near-term activity and discretionary spending. OPEC+ has announced plans to increase production beginning in May 2025, which comes at a time when the macroeconomic picture remains uncertain due to global trade concerns that have the potential to result in lower liquid demand than originally expected for the year.

### 3.2 Economic

The oilfield services industry is structurally tied to upstream capital expenditure cycles. Over the last four years, the industry's capital expenditures have increased by 53%, while net profit has risen by nearly 16%, with oilfield services reporting its best performance for the 2023 to 2024 period in the past 34 years. However, this cycle is showing signs of maturation. Taken together, OPEC+ supply increases and macroeconomic uncertainty are resulting in an uncertain market backdrop, with global upstream investment expected to decline compared to 2024, with customer spending in the Middle East and Asia being more resilient than other regions.

The North America–international divergence is a structural economic feature. International deepwater and Middle East gas projects are long-cycle and relatively price-inelastic, while North American shale activity is highly sensitive to short-term oil prices—a dynamic that increasingly disadvantages SLB's competitors with greater North American exposure.

### 3.3 Social

Societal pressure on the energy industry to decarbonise is reshaping customer priorities and investor expectations. Investors increasingly assess SLB on ESG and leadership diversity; in 2024, SLB linked 10% of executive compensation to ESG metrics, reflecting pressure from ESG-focused funds holding roughly $1.2 trillion in oilfield services exposure. The workforce dimension is equally important: SLB's global footprint across 85+ countries creates labour-market complexity, and its ability to attract digital and data science talent is critical to the digital engine's success.

### 3.4 Technological

Technology is both SLB's primary competitive asset and the most disruptive force in its environment. Three technological trends are particularly significant:

**AI and cloud computing** are transforming subsurface workflows. Increased adoption and integration of digital technologies remains one of the most significant opportunities for growth. Over the past few years, customers have made investments to modernise their digital infrastructures, recognising the pivotal role that digital plays in increasing efficiency, enhancing production, and elevating performance. SLB has responded with its Delfi platform and partnerships with NVIDIA and Amazon Web Services. These include a collaboration with NVIDIA to develop generative AI solutions for energy, as well as a partnership with Amazon Web Services to expand access to applications from the Delfi platform and to evaluate decarbonisation solutions for Amazon's digital infrastructure.

**Automation and autonomous operations** threaten to reduce the labour-intensive field-service work that underpins much of SLB's revenue—but also create opportunities for SLB to offer higher-margin software and remote-monitoring services.

**Energy transition technologies** are creating new markets. Some oilfield services companies are developing solutions focused on electrification, carbon capture, and hydrogen generation, with Schlumberger and Baker Hughes collaborating with Genvia and Air Products, respectively, to create new solutions for producing clean hydrogen.

### 3.5 Environmental

Climate regulation is reshaping both SLB's customers' investment plans and SLB's own obligations. The COP28 commitments place national oil companies (NOCs) under particular pressure to reduce their carbon footprints. National oil companies, particularly those in the Middle East and members of OPEC, face challenges including balancing crude oil supply and demand, fulfilling COP28 commitments to reduce the industry's carbon footprint, and helping to sustain their economies if oil prices remain below their fiscal breakeven. This creates a dual demand signal for SLB: customers need both efficiency in existing operations and decarbonisation technologies.

### 3.6 Legal

SLB's global operations expose it to a complex web of environmental regulations, export controls, and data sovereignty laws. The expansion into digital services introduces new legal dimensions around data privacy (particularly in the EU and Middle East) and intellectual property protection. SLB's 2025 proxy explicitly lists "the inability to achieve net-zero carbon emissions goals or interim emissions reduction goals" as a material risk, signalling that regulatory exposure is increasingly tied to the company's own ESG commitments.

## 4. Industry Analysis: Porter's Five Forces

### 4.1 Competitive Rivalry (HIGH)

SLB competes with companies like Halliburton, Baker Hughes, and Weatherford. The competition is mainly based on pricing, quality of services, and technology. Schlumberger has a significant advantage due to its scale and financial resources, which enable it to invest in research and development and enhance its technology. The rivalry is intensifying in digital, where Baker Hughes has partnered with Microsoft and Saudi Aramco to develop competing platforms. Baker Hughes' digital solutions encompass a wide range of applications, from asset performance management and predictive maintenance to supply chain optimisation and workforce management, and by partnering with Microsoft, Baker Hughes has been able to leverage the expertise and resources of a leading technology company to accelerate its digital transformation journey.

### 4.2 Threat of New Entrants (LOW–MODERATE)

Capital intensity, regulatory complexity, and proprietary technology create substantial barriers. The supply of high-precision subsea and drilling components is concentrated among a few specialised vendors, with the top five suppliers estimated to cover approximately 60% of SLB's critical parts market in 2024, reflecting high quality barriers, as these suppliers hold moderate leverage because their engineering underpins SLB's proprietary tool reliability and performance. However, in the digital sub-segment, the barriers are lower: cloud-native software companies could potentially enter with AI-driven subsurface analytics tools without the capital burden of physical hardware.

### 4.3 Bargaining Power of Buyers (MODERATE–HIGH)

SLB's customers—predominantly IOCs (international oil companies) and NOCs—are large, sophisticated, and increasingly consolidating. The mega-mergers in the Permian Basin in 2023–2024 have created larger, more powerful counterparties. Considering that large upstream customers have completed megamergers in the Permian region in 2023 and 2024 and will require scalable and tech-powered oilfield services, many small-sized companies could seek exits at favourable valuations, spurring consolidation across the sector. Buyer power is partially offset by the high switching costs embedded in long-term integrated contracts and proprietary digital platforms.

### 4.4 Bargaining Power of Suppliers (MODERATE)

Supplier power is mixed: few specialised vendors supply approximately 60% of critical parts in 2024, giving moderate leverage, while bulk-input suppliers are weaker, but commodity swings raised costs, with alloy premiums up 12% in 2023. SLB hedges and uses multi-year contracts to cover 40–60% of inputs, protecting 150–200 basis points of margin; cloud providers and niche heavy-lift carriers retain pockets of leverage.

### 4.5 Threat of Substitutes (HIGH AND RISING)

This is the most strategically significant force for SLB's long-term positioning. In the oil and gas industry, the threat of substitution is high due to the availability of alternative sources of energy such as renewable energy, natural gas, nuclear energy, and coal. For Schlumberger Limited, the threat of substitution is significant and is accelerating as renewable energy costs continue to fall. Simultaneously, national oil companies are building internal capabilities, reducing reliance on external OFS providers and intensifying competition. NOC insourcing represents a substitute for SLB's services at the institutional level, distinct from the energy-source substitution threat.

**Five Forces Summary:** The overall industry structure is moderately attractive in the short term but faces structural deterioration over a 5–10 year horizon, driven by rising substitution threats and NOC capability-building. SLB's scale and technology provide partial insulation, but not immunity.

## 6. Strategic Situation: The Core Tension

The PESTEL and Five Forces analyses converge on a single core tension: **SLB's current financial strength derives overwhelmingly from the Core business (oil and gas services), but its long-term viability depends on successfully building the Digital and New Energy engines before the Core business faces structural decline.**

This tension has three specific manifestations:

**A. The Revenue Concentration Risk:** Despite the three-engine narrative, the overwhelming majority of SLB's revenue remains in Core services. Digital revenues, while growing rapidly, represented approximately $2 billion out of $33 billion in 2023—roughly 6% of total revenue. SLB's new technology platforms comprising Cloud, AI, and Edge have grown at a CAGR of nearly 60% since 2021, and SLB remains on track to reach approximately $3 billion in Digital revenue annually by 2025. Even at $3 billion, Digital would represent less than 9% of total revenue at 2024 scale—insufficient to offset a significant decline in Core.

**B. The Cyclicality Trap:** With oil prices holding steady at approximately $70 per barrel, SLB is implementing profound structural changes designed to optimise operational efficiency, reduce costs, and position itself for long-term growth. However, the tempered outlook for organic revenue, especially in light of expectations to remain flat in 2025 (excluding certain acquisitions), has led analysts to revise their operating margin forecasts downward. The very cycle that funds SLB's diversification investment is the one that, when it turns down, will reduce the capital available to execute that diversification.

**C. The Credibility Gap in New Energy:** SLB's New Energy vertical is projected to reach a $1 billion run-rate by end-2025 across carbon capture, hydrogen, geothermal, and storage. While strategically significant, $1 billion represents less than 3% of group revenue. The risk is that New Energy remains a portfolio of options rather than a scaled business—consuming management attention and capital without achieving the critical mass needed to attract best-in-class talent, customers, and partners.

## 8. Critical Evaluation of SLB's Current Strategy

### 8.1 Strengths of the Current Approach

The three-engine strategy is coherent in its internal logic. The Core business generates the cash flows that fund Digital and New Energy investment. Digital, in turn, makes the Core business stickier (higher switching costs through embedded platforms) and more margin-accretive. New Energy provides long-term optionality and regulatory legitimacy.

Schlumberger recently finalised its acquisition of ChampionX for approximately $7.75 billion. This strategic move is expected to yield around $400 million in annual pre-tax synergies within three years. The ChampionX deal is strategically sound: it adds recurring, production-phase revenue that is less sensitive to the exploration cycle, and it brings artificial lift and production chemistry capabilities that can be cross-sold with Delfi's digital monitoring tools.

### 8.2 Strategic Vulnerabilities

**Vulnerability 1: Digital monetisation lag.** The gap between SLB's technology investment and its digital revenue realisation is wide. SLB's strategic investment of $1.5 billion in digital technology for 2024, with a particular focus on AI and machine learning, further solidifies this advantage. Yet digital revenues of ~$3 billion represent a return of only 2x on a cumulative multi-year investment base. The platform must scale faster to justify its capital consumption.

**Vulnerability 2: New Energy scale.** SLB is aiming for billion-dollar business opportunities only under its New Energy unit, with the stated aim of looking for two things: impact and scale. While this selectivity is financially disciplined, it risks missing the window for early-mover advantages in CCUS and geothermal markets, where regulatory frameworks and project pipelines are being established now.

**Vulnerability 3: North America structural weakness.** International revenue grew 18% year on year, compensating for a softer North American market. North America's structural shift away from large integrated service contracts—driven by operator self-performance and technology commoditisation in shale—means SLB cannot rely on its home market as a growth driver. The ChampionX acquisition partially addresses this through its North American production chemistry presence, but the strategic question of SLB's positioning in the US shale market remains unresolved.

**Vulnerability 4: Macro-cyclical dependence.** SLB's financial performance is closely tied to the health of the energy industry and the demand for oilfield services. Recent financial results reflect the challenges of a volatile market, with SLB expecting flat revenues for 2025, excluding the ChampionX acquisition. A prolonged period of low oil prices—possible given OPEC+ supply increases and demand uncertainty from trade tensions—would constrain the capital available to fund the digital and new energy pivots precisely when those investments are most needed.

## 9. Recommendations and Action Plan

The following recommendations are structured around three strategic horizons (McKinsey's Three Horizons framework), ensuring SLB protects its current core while building the businesses that will sustain it.

---

### Recommendation 1 (Horizon 1 – 0–2 years): Accelerate Digital Monetisation Through a "Platform-as-a-Standard" Strategy

**The problem:** Delfi's user growth (28% YoY) and compute metrics are impressive, but revenue conversion remains slow because many customers adopt the platform incrementally rather than committing to enterprise-wide contracts.

**The recommendation:** SLB should pursue a "Platform-as-a-Standard" strategy, analogous to Salesforce's enterprise licensing model, by:
- Bundling Delfi access into all major Core service contracts as a default (not an add-on), thereby accelerating adoption while building switching costs;
- Establishing a "Digital Performance Guarantee"—contractually linking Delfi-enabled efficiency gains to performance-based pricing, reducing customer risk and accelerating commitment;
- Deepening the NVIDIA and AWS partnerships to co-develop generative AI applications that are exclusive to the Delfi ecosystem for a defined period, reinforcing platform differentiation.

**Rationale:** Many of SLB's digital offerings are delivered through Delfi, its cloud-native software-as-a-service solution that generates revenue based on the number of user licences and connected assets. This SaaS model has the highest margin profile in SLB's portfolio—the Digital & Integration pretax operating margin of 36% expanded 353 basis points year on year due to increased profitability in digital. Accelerating the adoption curve is therefore the single highest-return action available to SLB's management.

**Target:** Reach $4 billion in annual Digital revenue by 2026 (vs. the current $3 billion 2025 target), with Digital & Integration segment margins sustained above 35%.

---

### Recommendation 2 (Horizon 1–2 – 1–3 years): Restructure the Core Business Around "Integrated Performance Contracts" to Defend Margin Against NOC Insourcing

**The problem:** National oil companies are building internal capabilities, reducing reliance on external OFS providers and intensifying competition. This is SLB's most structurally dangerous competitive threat: if NOCs replicate even 20% of SLB's service capabilities in-house, the pricing and volume pressure on Core could be severe.

**The recommendation:** SLB should reframe its Core offering from "services vendor" to "integrated performance partner" by:
- Offering Integrated Asset Management contracts in which SLB takes responsibility for reservoir performance outcomes (production volumes, recovery rates) rather than individual service line delivery—making it economically irrational for NOCs to insource;
- Deploying the ChampionX production chemistry portfolio as the "last mile" of these integrated contracts, cementing SLB's presence through the full production lifecycle;
- Localising manufacturing and talent in key NOC markets (Saudi Arabia, UAE, Brazil, Angola) to pre-empt local-content regulations that could otherwise advantage domestic competitors.

**Rationale:** SLB's core oil and gas business will continue to be a key engine of growth, and deliberately increasing exposure to the production and recovery space will align it with a growing and resilient OpEx spend category into the next decade. Integrated contracts are harder to disaggregate and insource than individual service lines, making them structurally more defensible.

---

### Recommendation 3 (Horizon 2 – 2–5 years): Scale New Energy Through a "Brownfield CCUS" Beachhead Strategy

**The problem:** SLB's New Energy portfolio is broad but shallow. Spreading investment across CCUS, geothermal, hydrogen, critical minerals, and energy storage risks achieving scale in none of them.

**The recommendation:** SLB should concentrate its New Energy investment on a single beachhead: **brownfield CCUS for industrial emitters and NOCs**, for three reasons:

1. *Capability fit:* SLB's subsurface expertise (reservoir characterisation, well construction, monitoring) maps directly onto CO₂ storage site assessment and injection—it is not entering an adjacent market but exploiting existing competencies in a new application.

2. *Customer overlap:* SLB's NOC and IOC customers are also the largest industrial CO₂ emitters and face the strongest regulatory pressure to decarbonise. SLB's CEO noted: "This is presenting an exciting new market for lower-carbon technologies and carbon capture and sequestration, where we are positioned very well, as exemplified by the expansion of our CCS portfolio with our recent announcement of our agreement to acquire a majority ownership stake in Aker Carbon Capture."

3. *Market timing:* Global clean energy investment reached $2.2 trillion in 2025, spurred by supportive policies. The regulatory and financial infrastructure for CCUS is being built now; SLB must establish reference projects and IP leadership before the market matures and competition intensifies.

**Specific actions:**
- Fully integrate Aker Carbon Capture's technology with SLB's subsurface and well construction capabilities to create end-to-end CCUS project delivery;
- Target 3–5 flagship brownfield CCUS projects with major NOC partners (Aramco, Petrobras, Equinor) by 2027, using performance-based contracts to demonstrate commercial viability;
- Divest or de-prioritise early-stage New Energy verticals (e.g., critical minerals, energy storage) where SLB lacks differentiated capabilities, redirecting capital to CCUS scale-up.

**Target:** Reach $1.5 billion in New Energy revenue by 2027, with CCUS representing at least 60% of that total.

---

### Recommendation 4 (Horizon 1–3 – Ongoing): Implement Structural Cost Resilience to Protect the Investment Capacity Through the Cycle

**The problem:** What matters in this environment is SLB's ability to continue to generate strong margins and cash flows, deliver resilient returns to shareholders, and come out stronger. A prolonged oil price downturn would reduce the operating cash flows that fund Recommendations 1–3.

**The recommendation:** SLB should build a "variable cost architecture" that can flex with the cycle without cutting investment in Digital and New Energy:
- Ring-fence Digital and New Energy R&D budgets as protected capital, immune to Core-driven cost reduction programmes;
- Accelerate the shift to asset-light delivery models in Core (remote operations, digital twins, third-party manufacturing partnerships), reducing fixed cost exposure;
- SLB remains on track to return $4 billion to shareholders in 2025 and expects to realise annual pre-tax synergies from the ChampionX acquisition of approximately $400 million within the first three years post-closing through revenue growth and cost savings—these synergies should be directed to Digital and New Energy investment rather than shareholder returns, to the extent capital allocation flexibility allows.

## 10. Conclusion

SLB's strategic situation is one of the most complex in the global industrial landscape: it must simultaneously defend a $33+ billion legacy business against cyclical and structural headwinds, scale a digital platform from $3 billion to a transformative revenue engine, and build credible new energy businesses from near-zero—all while managing the financial risks of a major acquisition and a deteriorating macro environment.

The analysis presented here, moving from PESTEL (macro threats and opportunities) through Porter's Five Forces (industry structure) to VRIO (internal competitive advantage) and Ansoff (growth path risk), yields a coherent strategic logic: **SLB's greatest competitive assets—its subsurface domain expertise, its global customer relationships, and its proprietary technology platform—are most powerfully deployed not by spreading investment broadly across all three engines simultaneously, but by using the Digital engine to deepen and defend the Core, while concentrating New Energy investment on the one domain (CCUS) where SLB's existing capabilities create a genuine and rare advantage.**

The four recommendations—accelerating digital monetisation, restructuring Core around integrated performance contracts, building a CCUS beachhead, and protecting investment capacity through structural cost flexibility—form a coherent and sequenced action plan. Executed together, they offer SLB a credible path from "world's largest oilfield services company" to "world's leading energy technology company"—a transformation that is strategically necessary, financially feasible, and grounded in the firm's deepest competitive strengths.
