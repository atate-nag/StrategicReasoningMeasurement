# Shell plc: Navigating the Strategic Tension Between Shareholder Value and Energy Transition (2021–2024)

## A Strategic Case Study

---

## 1. Introduction and Case Overview

Shell plc is one of the world's largest integrated energy companies, operating across upstream oil and gas, liquefied natural gas (LNG), refining, chemicals, and increasingly in low-carbon energy solutions. Between 2021 and 2024, Shell faced a defining strategic dilemma: how to generate the short-term financial returns demanded by shareholders while credibly transitioning toward a net-zero business model in response to climate regulation, litigation, and shifting energy markets. This tension came to a head when incoming CEO Wael Sawan, who took office in January 2023, reoriented Shell's strategy around the mantra of "Performance, Discipline, Simplification" — a pivot that involved weakening climate targets, retreating from renewable energy assets, and doubling down on LNG and oil — while simultaneously maintaining a commitment to net-zero by 2050. This case study analyses the strategic situation using Porter's Five Forces, the VRIO framework, a stakeholder analysis, and Bowman's Strategy Clock, and concludes with a set of strategic recommendations.

## 2. The Strategic Situation

### 2.1 The Dual Mandate Problem

Shell's core strategic problem is a **dual mandate tension**: it must simultaneously satisfy investors demanding competitive returns and stakeholders (regulators, NGOs, institutional ESG investors, and the public) demanding credible decarbonisation. This is not merely a communications problem — it is a structural strategic conflict over resource allocation.

Shell's CEO Wael Sawan has described the company's strategy as delivering "more value with less emissions," and has argued that Shell's "focus on performance, discipline and simplification is driving clear choices about where we can have the greatest impact through the energy transition and create the most value for our investors and customers." This framing implies the two goals are complementary. The evidence, however, reveals significant trade-offs.

### 2.2 The Strategic Pivot Under Sawan

Sawan replaced Ben van Beurden in January 2023, stripping away what critics called the "green-at-all-costs" rhetoric of his predecessor. His "Performance, Discipline, Simplification" strategy has been executed with precision, with the leadership team flattened and the board refreshed to prioritise "value over volume."

The most concrete expression of this pivot was a retreat from renewables. Shell stepped back from new offshore wind investments and split its power division following a review launched in 2023 aimed at reducing costs. A Shell spokesperson confirmed: "We will be focusing on maximising the value of our existing renewable generation platforms. While we will not lead new offshore wind developments, we remain interested in offtakes where commercial terms are acceptable."

Divestments followed swiftly. Shell subsidiaries Shell Wind Energy and Savion Equity agreed to sell their stakes in two US renewable energy projects — a 60% stake in Brazos Wind Holdings (a 182 MW onshore wind farm in Texas) and a 50% stake in Madison Fields Class B Member (a 180 MW solar development in Ohio) — to InfraRed Capital Partners. CEO Wael Sawan had previously announced his intention for the company to focus on activities with the highest returns, which in many cases has meant reducing spending on low-carbon and renewable businesses.

The financial data confirms the directional shift. Shell's spending in its renewables and energy solutions division fell 23% in 2023 compared with the previous year, according to a Reuters analysis.

### 2.3 Weakening of Climate Targets

The pivot was codified in Shell's Energy Transition Strategy 2024. Shell abandoned a key climate target for 2035 and weakened another goal for 2030. The company updated its target to cut the "net carbon intensity" of all the energy products it sells by 20% between 2016 and 2030; the reduction is now set at between 15–20%. Within Shell's strategy, CEO Wael Sawan wrote that this change reflects "a strategic shift" to focus less on selling electricity, including renewable power. Instead, the company says investment in oil and gas "will be needed" due to sustained demand for fossil fuels, and it emphasises the importance of LNG as "critical" for the energy transition, planning to grow its LNG business by up to 30% by 2030.

### 2.4 The LNG Bet

At the heart of Shell's revised strategy is a major commitment to LNG. Shell aims to reinforce its leadership position in LNG by growing sales by 4–5% per year through to 2030, arguing that gas is "a stabilising force in energy systems because it is versatile, flexible and reliable." At Capital Markets Day 2023, Shell announced a target of 10% per annum free cash flow (FCF) per share growth through to 2025, enabled by a structural cost reduction of $2–3 billion by end-2025 and a lowering of capital spend to $22–25 billion per annum.

### 2.5 Financial Performance

Shell's financial results for 2023 revealed a significant profit drop to $28 billion, down from the record year of 2022. However, the company exceeded market expectations through robust LNG trading results. Shell's performance enabled it to return $23 billion to shareholders through $15 billion in share buybacks and $8 billion in dividends — 42% of cash flow from operations, around the upper end of its 30–40% through-the-cycle target range.

## 3. External Environment Analysis: Porter's Five Forces

Porter's Five Forces (1979) provides a framework for assessing the structural attractiveness and competitive intensity of the industry in which Shell operates. The purpose here is to determine whether Shell's strategic pivot is warranted by the external environment, and what forces most threaten or support its chosen position.

### Force 1: Threat of New Entrants — **Low to Moderate**

The oil and gas upstream sector has extremely high barriers to entry: capital intensity, geological expertise, regulatory licensing, and long lead times. The capital expenditures for specialised oil and gas equipment are formidable — offshore drilling rigs cost $650 million to $1.2 billion per unit, and subsea production systems run $200–500 million per project. These barriers protect Shell's upstream and LNG positions. However, in renewable energy generation — the segment Shell is retreating from — barriers to entry are falling rapidly as technology costs decline, meaning Shell's exit from offshore wind cedes ground to well-capitalised new entrants (e.g., Ørsted, RWE, and sovereign wealth fund-backed developers). **Implication:** Shell's retreat from renewables is strategically coherent only if it does not expect that segment to become a high-return competitive space; the falling barriers in renewables challenge that assumption.

### Force 2: Bargaining Power of Suppliers — **Moderate**

The supply chain for oil and gas services is dominated by a small number of large equipment and services providers (Schlumberger/SLB, Halliburton, Baker Hughes). During periods of high industry activity, these suppliers gain pricing power. However, Shell's scale and long-term contracting relationships give it leverage. In LNG, Shell's integrated model — controlling liquefaction, shipping, and trading — reduces its dependence on any single supplier node. **Implication:** Shell's vertical integration in LNG is a structural hedge against supplier power, reinforcing the strategic logic of doubling down on integrated gas.

### Force 3: Bargaining Power of Buyers — **Moderate to High**

Individual consumers exert influence through price sensitivity and low switching costs, as seen in fluctuating gasoline prices in 2024. Large industrial clients hold substantial sway due to their massive procurement volumes, as evidenced by Shell's $47.8 billion revenue from Integrated Gas in 2023, which includes LNG sales to major buyers. In retail energy markets, Shell's sale of its UK and German energy retail operations to Octopus Energy in September 2023 reflects the recognition that retail customers have low switching costs and thin margins. **Implication:** Shell's strategic shift toward commercial and industrial LNG customers — rather than retail electricity consumers — is consistent with a move toward higher-margin, stickier buyer relationships.

### Force 4: Threat of Substitutes — **High (Long-term)**

This is the most existential force facing Shell. The IEA's Net Zero Emissions scenario makes clear that oil declines from around 100 million barrels per day to 77 mb/d by 2030 and 24 mb/d by 2050, and natural gas demand drops from 4,150 billion cubic metres in 2022 to 3,400 bcm in 2030 and 900 bcm in 2050. Even in the IEA's more moderate Stated Policies Scenario, a peak in coal demand and stable oil consumption around 2030 is forecast. Renewables, electrification, and energy efficiency are structural substitutes for Shell's core products. **Implication:** The threat of substitution is high over a 10–30 year horizon, meaning Shell's LNG bet is a medium-term play that must be managed as a cash-generative "bridge" rather than a permanent strategic anchor. Shell's own net-zero-by-2050 commitment implicitly acknowledges this.

### Force 5: Competitive Rivalry — **High**

Shell competes with BP, ExxonMobil, TotalEnergies, Chevron, and national oil companies (Saudi Aramco, QatarEnergy) for upstream resources, LNG market share, and customer relationships. Rival BP made a similar move to Shell in 2023, rowing back on oil production and emission reduction targets in the face of growing investor pressure to boost returns. This indicates that the competitive rivalry is not only about market share in hydrocarbons but also about which company can most credibly position itself as the "transition champion" for institutional capital. QatarEnergy's massive LNG expansion (North Field expansion) is a direct competitive threat to Shell's LNG growth ambitions, as Qatar can produce LNG at a fraction of Shell's cost. **Implication:** The Five Forces analysis suggests that Shell's industry is structurally attractive in the medium term but faces long-run erosion from substitutes. Its competitive position is defensible in LNG and integrated trading, but rivalry is intensifying precisely in the segments where Shell is concentrating.

## 4. Internal Capabilities Analysis: The VRIO Framework

The VRIO framework (Barney, 1991) asks whether a firm's resources and capabilities are **V**aluable, **R**are, difficult to **I**mitate, and supported by **O**rganisation — the conditions for sustainable competitive advantage. This analysis identifies which of Shell's capabilities justify its strategic choices.

### 4.1 LNG Trading and Optimisation Capability

- **Valuable?** Yes. Shell's trading capabilities are described as "optimization and not speculation," deeply integrated with the company's asset base and physical flows, creating value through optimisation across the entire value chain from production through refining to customer delivery.
- **Rare?** Yes. Few competitors combine Shell's scale in LNG liquefaction, a global fleet of LNG carriers, long-term offtake contracts across Asia, Europe, and the Americas, and a sophisticated trading desk.
- **Inimitable?** Largely yes. The trading capabilities become increasingly valuable as energy markets become more differentiated, and Shell's downstream marketing operations provide access to more customers than any competitor, creating opportunities to introduce low-carbon solutions as market demand develops. Replicating this integrated network would take decades and billions of dollars.
- **Organised?** Yes. Shell's Integrated Gas segment is structured to exploit this capability, and the 2023 strategy explicitly centres on it.

**Conclusion:** LNG trading and optimisation is a source of **sustainable competitive advantage** and the VRIO analysis supports Shell's decision to double down on it.

### 4.2 Global Downstream and Retail Network

- **Valuable?** Yes, in terms of customer reach.
- **Rare?** Moderately — BP, TotalEnergies, and ExxonMobil have comparable networks.
- **Inimitable?** Partially — brand, network density, and convenience retail integration create some lock-in.
- **Organised?** Increasingly less so, as Shell is rationalising its refinery portfolio and exiting retail energy.

**Conclusion:** This capability provides **competitive parity** rather than sustainable advantage, justifying Shell's strategy of rationalising its downstream footprint.

### 4.3 Balance Sheet and Capital Allocation Discipline

- **Valuable?** Yes. Between 2023 and 2025, Shell plans to invest around $40 billion in oil and gas, while cash capital expenditure of $24 billion in 2023 was at the lower end of its $23–27 billion range. Disciplined capital allocation in a capital-intensive industry is a genuine advantage.
- **Rare?** Moderately — all majors exercise capital discipline, but Shell's post-2023 rigor is notable.
- **Inimitable?** Partially — it depends on organisational culture and governance, which are hard to replicate quickly.
- **Organised?** Yes, with the enhanced shareholder distribution target of 30–40% of CFFO and $3.5 billion quarterly buyback programmes.

**Conclusion:** Capital allocation discipline provides a **temporary to sustainable advantage**, particularly as it enables Shell to fund both returns and transition investments simultaneously.

### 4.4 Renewable Energy Generation Capability

- **Valuable?** Potentially, but Shell has concluded it is not currently value-accretive at scale.
- **Rare?** No — renewable generation is increasingly commoditised.
- **Inimitable?** No — low barriers to entry mean many players can generate renewable power.
- **Organised?** No — Shell's exit from offshore wind and sale of wind/solar assets signals organisational misalignment.

**Conclusion:** Renewable generation is **not a source of competitive advantage** for Shell, and the VRIO analysis supports the divestment decision. However, this analysis is static; if renewable energy becomes a high-margin, differentiated market (e.g., through grid-scale storage or green hydrogen), Shell's exit could prove premature.

## 5. Stakeholder Analysis

A stakeholder analysis (Freeman, 1984; Mendelow's Power-Interest Matrix) maps the competing pressures Shell faces, revealing why its dual mandate is so difficult to resolve.

### 5.1 Shareholders (High Power, High Interest)

Shell's largest institutional shareholders have historically supported the energy transition strategy. In 2021, 89% of Shell's shareholders voted in support of its Energy Transition Strategy. However, the composition of shareholder pressure is bifurcating. A group of investors backed a resolution at Shell's 2024 AGM calling for the company to align its medium-term emissions reductions targets with the Paris Agreement. Simultaneously, activist value investors (including Elliott Management, which built a stake in Shell in 2023) push for higher returns and asset disposals. This bifurcation means Shell cannot satisfy all shareholders simultaneously — a structural tension that its "more value, less emissions" framing attempts, but does not fully resolve.

### 5.2 NGOs and Climate Litigants (High Interest, Escalating Power)

In 2021, the District Court in The Hague ordered Shell to reduce its group-wide CO₂ emissions by 45% compared to 2019 levels by 2030 — the first time a national court had ordered a private company to reduce its emissions to meet the targets of the Paris Climate Agreement. Shell appealed, and on 12 November 2024, the Dutch Court of Appeals overturned the ruling, holding that while Shell is legally obliged to reduce its CO₂ emissions, a civil court cannot require it to meet specific reduction targets. While Shell won on the specific percentage, the appeals court held that Shell is legally obligated to reduce its Scope 3 emissions — a significant residual legal risk that constrains Shell's strategic freedom.

### 5.3 Governments and Regulators (High Power, Variable Interest)

Shell operates across over 70 countries with varying regulatory environments. The EU's Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism, tightening methane regulations, and the US Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) — which incentivises domestic clean energy investment — all shape Shell's operating context. The IRA in particular creates a competitive disadvantage for Shell relative to US-based energy companies that can access federal clean energy subsidies.

### 5.4 Employees (Moderate Power, High Interest)

Several high-profile executives from Shell's renewables division departed in 2024, citing a lack of ambition in the energy transition. This signals a talent risk: Shell's ability to attract and retain professionals with expertise in clean energy technologies may be compromised by its strategic pivot, creating a capability gap precisely in the areas where future value may lie.

## 6. Competitive Positioning: Bowman's Strategy Clock

Bowman's Strategy Clock (Bowman & Faulkner, 1996) maps competitive positions along two axes: perceived value to the customer and price. It extends Porter's generic strategies by identifying eight positions, of which positions 1–5 are broadly sustainable.

Shell's current strategy corresponds most closely to **Position 4: Differentiation** — offering high perceived value at a premium price, without necessarily being the cost leader. In LNG, Shell differentiates on reliability of supply, trading sophistication, and the breadth of its global network. Customers — particularly Asian utilities and European energy companies — pay a premium for Shell's supply security and flexibility.

However, Shell's downstream oil products business is drifting toward **Position 3: Hybrid** — moderate price with moderate perceived value — as commodity refining margins compress and customers increasingly have alternatives. The strategic logic of rationalising Shell's refinery portfolio (converting to "energy and chemicals parks" focused on biofuels and hydrogen) is consistent with moving this segment back toward a differentiated position.

The critical risk is that Shell's retreat from renewable electricity generation leaves it without a credible position in the fastest-growing energy market segment. Competitors such as TotalEnergies and Ørsted are pursuing **Position 5: Focused Differentiation** in renewables — high perceived value in a specific segment (clean energy for corporate buyers). Shell's exit from this space, justified by the VRIO analysis today, may create a strategic gap if the energy transition accelerates faster than Shell's scenario planning assumes.

## 7. Strategic Tensions and Coherence Assessment

Having applied four analytical frameworks, three core strategic tensions emerge:

**Tension 1: Short-term returns vs. long-term positioning.** The VRIO analysis confirms that Shell's LNG and trading capabilities are genuinely superior. The Five Forces analysis confirms that these capabilities are defensible in the medium term. However, the threat of substitution is high over 20–30 years, and Shell's own net-zero-by-2050 commitment implies that its current core business model must eventually be transformed. The risk is that the current strategy optimises for the next 5–7 years at the cost of the capabilities needed for 2035–2050.

**Tension 2: Credibility of climate commitments.** Shell has abandoned its 2035 emissions target and weakened its 2030 goal, while simultaneously claiming that this makes its climate targets "more, not less, likely" to be achieved. This is logically strained: weakening a target cannot, by definition, make the original ambition more achievable. The credibility gap exposes Shell to continued litigation risk (the Milieudefensie case established a legal obligation to reduce Scope 3 emissions, even if no specific percentage was mandated), reputational risk with ESG-focused investors, and regulatory risk as climate disclosure rules tighten globally.

**Tension 3: The talent and capability paradox.** Shell's exit from renewable generation and the departure of renewables executives creates a capability gap. If Shell's strategy is to remain relevant through the energy transition by pivoting from generation to trading and optimisation of clean energy — as its "commercial realism" framing suggests — then it needs people who understand clean energy markets. The current strategic signal may be undermining the talent pipeline required to execute the long-term strategy.

## 8. Recommendations and Action Plan

The following recommendations are grounded in the analytical findings above and are designed to resolve the strategic tensions identified. They are sequenced by time horizon.

### Recommendation 1: Anchor the LNG Strategy with an Explicit Sunset Framework (Immediate, 2024–2026)

**What:** Shell should publicly commit to a date range (e.g., 2038–2042) beyond which it will not sanction new long-life LNG upstream projects, consistent with IEA demand scenarios. **Why:** The VRIO analysis confirms LNG is Shell's strongest competitive advantage, and the Five Forces analysis confirms it is defensible in the medium term. However, under the IEA's Net Zero scenario, natural gas demand drops from 4,150 bcm in 2022 to 3,400 bcm by 2030 and 900 bcm by 2050 — meaning LNG is a bridge, not a destination. An explicit sunset framework would allow Shell to maximise cash generation from its LNG position while providing credible signal to investors, regulators, and employees that it is managing the asset for value rather than perpetuating it indefinitely. This resolves Tension 1 by making the short-term/long-term trade-off explicit rather than obscuring it.

### Recommendation 2: Redirect Renewable Energy Strategy from Generation to Integration (Immediate–Medium Term, 2024–2028)

**What:** Rather than re-entering commodity wind and solar generation, Shell should deploy capital into clean energy trading, optimisation, and origination — specifically green hydrogen offtake agreements, corporate power purchase agreement (PPA) intermediation, and battery storage optimisation. **Why:** The VRIO analysis shows that Shell's trading and optimisation capability is rare, valuable, and inimitable — but its renewable *generation* capability is not. Shell's trading capabilities are deeply integrated with its asset base and physical flows, creating value through optimisation across the entire value chain, and the company's downstream marketing operations provide access to more customers than any competitor, creating opportunities to introduce low-carbon solutions as market demand develops. The strategic logic is to apply Shell's genuine competitive advantage (trading and integration) to the clean energy market, rather than competing in commodity generation where it has no structural edge. This resolves Tension 1 by investing in transition capabilities that build on existing strengths.

### Recommendation 3: Restore a Credible, Absolute Scope 3 Emissions Trajectory (Medium Term, 2025–2030)

**What:** Shell should replace its weakened net carbon intensity (NCI) metric — which is a relative measure and can be met even as absolute emissions rise — with a supplementary absolute Scope 3 emissions reduction pathway, expressed in megatonnes of CO₂e, declining year-on-year from 2025. **Why:** The Hague Court of Appeals confirmed that Shell is legally obligated to reduce its Scope 3 emissions, even though no specific percentage was mandated. An NCI target alone does not satisfy this obligation if Shell's total energy sales volumes increase. Furthermore, a group of investors backed a 2024 AGM resolution calling for Shell to align its medium-term emissions reductions targets with the Paris Agreement — indicating that institutional shareholder pressure on absolute targets is growing. A credible absolute trajectory would reduce litigation risk, satisfy ESG-focused institutional investors, and restore strategic coherence between Shell's net-zero-by-2050 commitment and its near-term actions. This resolves Tension 2 directly.

### Recommendation 4: Invest in Clean Energy Talent and Capability Retention (Medium Term, 2024–2027)

**What:** Shell should establish a dedicated "Energy Transition Ventures" unit with ring-fenced capital (c.$2–3 billion per annum), its own P&L accountability, and a distinct talent strategy — including competitive compensation benchmarked against clean energy pure-plays and technology firms. **Why:** Several high-profile executives from Shell's renewables division departed in 2024, citing a lack of ambition in the energy transition. If Shell's long-term strategy depends on pivoting its trading and optimisation capabilities into clean energy markets, then losing the people who understand those markets is a direct threat to strategic execution. Ring-fencing the unit signals organisational commitment, attracts talent, and creates an internal innovation engine. This resolves Tension 3.

### Recommendation 5: Deepen Stakeholder Engagement on a Managed Transition Narrative (Ongoing)

**What:** Shell should proactively convene a multi-stakeholder advisory panel — including climate NGOs, institutional investors, and government representatives — to co-develop scenario-based disclosure on how its portfolio will evolve under different energy transition speeds. **Why:** Shell's current communications strategy attempts to satisfy all stakeholders simultaneously with the "more value, less emissions" framing, but the evidence shows this is increasingly unconvincing. Shell's Energy Transition Strategy 2024 states that it leads in the energy transition in areas where it has competitive strengths, strong customer demand, and clear regulatory support from governments — but the strategy does not specify what happens when these three conditions diverge, as they currently do in offshore wind (strong customer demand, regulatory support, but no competitive strength for Shell). Transparent scenario-based disclosure would allow different stakeholder groups to assess Shell's strategy against their own assumptions, reducing the credibility gap and pre-empting future litigation.

## 10. Conclusion

Shell's strategic situation between 2021 and 2024 represents one of the most consequential examples of a firm navigating a structural industry transition in real time. The analytical frameworks applied in this case study converge on a consistent finding: Shell's pivot toward LNG and away from renewable generation is defensible on the basis of its current competitive capabilities and the medium-term industry structure, but it creates three compounding risks — a long-term capability gap, a credibility deficit with climate stakeholders, and a talent drain — that, if unaddressed, will undermine the very financial performance the strategy is designed to protect. The five recommendations proposed are designed not to reverse Shell's strategic direction, but to make it coherent across all time horizons: maximising the value of today's competitive advantages while building the capabilities needed for the energy system of 2035–2050.
