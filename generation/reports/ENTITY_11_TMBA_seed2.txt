# 💎 STRATEGIC CASE STUDY
# De Beers Group: Navigating Existential Disruption in the Global Diamond Industry
### A Strategic Analysis of Anglo American's Diamond Subsidiary

---

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

De Beers Group — the world's most iconic diamond company and a wholly-owned subsidiary of Anglo American plc — faces an unprecedented strategic crisis. Once commanding over 80% of the global rough diamond supply, the company has seen its market position eroded by the explosive growth of laboratory-grown diamonds (LGDs), a prolonged demand slump in China, and structural shifts in consumer values. Three consecutive write-downs totalling over $4 billion between 2022 and 2025, and a net loss of $511 million in 2025, combined with Anglo American's publicly confirmed plans to demerge or divest De Beers as part of a larger restructuring to focus on "green" energy operations, signal the depth of the challenge.

This case study analyses De Beers' strategic situation using PESTEL, Porter's Five Forces, VRIO, Value Chain Analysis, SWOT, the Ansoff Matrix, and SAFe criteria to diagnose root causes and generate actionable recommendations.

---

## 2. COMPANY BACKGROUND & CONTEXT

**De Beers Group** was founded in 1888 by Cecil Rhodes in Kimberley, South Africa. For over a century, it operated as a near-monopoly in the global diamond industry, controlling supply through its "Central Selling Organisation" and shaping global demand through the iconic 1947 slogan "A Diamond is Forever." Anglo American acquired an 85% stake in De Beers in 2012, with the Government of Botswana retaining the remaining 15%.

De Beers operates across the full diamond value chain: mining (Botswana, Namibia, South Africa, Canada), sorting, valuation, and sales through its proprietary **"sightholder" system** — a selective distribution mechanism through which it sells rough diamonds to approximately 80 accredited buyers globally.

Key subsidiaries include:
- **Debswana** – 50/50 JV with the Government of Botswana; operates Jwaneng, the world's richest diamond mine by value
- **Namdeb** – 50/50 JV with the Namibian government; includes marine diamond operations
- **De Beers Jewellers** – luxury retail brand with flagship stores in London, New York, and Paris
- **Forevermark** – branded diamond certification programme, recently refocused on India
- **Lightbox** – lab-grown diamond brand launched in 2018, shuttered in May 2025

### 2.1 The Corporate Trigger

In May 2024, BHP made three successive unsolicited takeover bids for Anglo American, all of which were rejected. The Board of Anglo American unanimously rejected BHP's third unsolicited, non-binding and highly conditional takeover proposal on the basis that it did not meet the Board's expectations of value delivered to shareholders. However, the episode catalysed Anglo's own strategic review. Anglo American's fourth quarter 2024 production report reveals a comprehensive strategic transformation, focusing on copper, premium iron ore, and crop nutrients, with major portfolio restructuring including a $4.9 billion Steelmaking Coal business sale, increased Anglo American Platinum free float, and planned De Beers separation.

## 4. ENVIRONMENTAL ANALYSIS: PESTEL

### 4.1 Political

Botswana's diamond revenues account for approximately 70–80% of its export earnings and one-third of GDP. The government, under President Duma Boko, is actively positioning to acquire a majority stake in De Beers during the divestiture process. Securing majority ownership would provide Botswana with direct governance over De Beers' global sightholder allocation system, determining which cutting and polishing centres receive rough stone parcels and on what terms. Resource nationalism is rising across Africa, with beneficiation requirements mandating local processing before export.

### 4.2 Economic

China's demand slump is critical — the country was the world's second-largest diamond jewellery market, but economic slowdown, a property market crisis, and shifting consumer sentiment have driven a sharp contraction. De Beers recorded an underlying EBITDA loss of $189 million in the first half of 2025, compared with income of $300 million in the same period of 2024. Full-year revenue totalled $1.95 billion, down 13% year-over-year. Rising interest rates (2022–2024) also increased the cost of holding diamond inventory for midstream players, depressing rough diamond demand.

### 4.3 Social

Lab-grown diamonds now account for 52% of US engagement ring centre stones — a stunning reversal from just 3% in 2018 — driven by Millennial and Gen Z consumers who prioritise sustainability, ethical sourcing, and value-for-money. The Indian market, however, offers a counterpoint, with an expanding middle class and deep cultural affinity for jewellery representing a significant growth opportunity.

### 4.4 Technological

Lab-grown diamond prices have declined to $500 per carat — a 90% decline since 2018 — as CVD and HPHT manufacturing technologies have been commoditised. Conversely, De Beers' **Tracr blockchain platform** enables mine-to-finger provenance verification, a potential differentiator. De Beers is also introducing a new retail-facing diamond verification instrument, bringing its leading lab-grown diamond detection technology to retail counters.

### 4.5 Environmental

Climate change poses physical risks to mining operations (water scarcity in Botswana), while the carbon footprint of diamond mining is under scrutiny. Lab-grown diamonds, increasingly produced using renewable energy, are gaining an environmental marketing advantage. ESG expectations from investors and consumers are rising sharply.

### 4.6 Legal

De Beers' antitrust history — it was banned from operating directly in the US for decades due to price-fixing allegations, settling with the US DOJ in 2004 — constrains certain market strategies. Beneficiation legislation across Botswana, Namibia, and South Africa legally requires local value-addition. Evolving FTC guidelines in the US on diamond labelling are also tightening.

## 5. INDUSTRY ANALYSIS: PORTER'S FIVE FORCES (1979)

### 5.1 Threat of New Entrants — **MEDIUM-HIGH** ⬆️

Traditional mining barriers (capital intensity, permitting, geological expertise) remain high. However, the threat of new entrants is HIGH when the competitive frame is broadened to include lab-grown diamond producers, for whom CVD reactor technology is commercially available, with dozens of Indian and Chinese manufacturers having entered the market in recent years.

### 5.2 Bargaining Power of Buyers — **HIGH** ⬆️

De Beers' sightholder system historically gave it enormous power over buyers, but this has eroded significantly. With lab-grown alternatives available at 80–90% lower prices, end consumers now have a genuine substitute. De Beers' sightholders — the select group of diamond buyers who purchase directly from the company — might face new selection criteria or purchasing requirements as the company restructures, but their leverage has increased materially.

### 5.3 Bargaining Power of Suppliers — **LOW-MEDIUM** ➡️

De Beers is vertically integrated, owning its mines and controlling its supply chain. Labour in Southern Africa has increasing bargaining power through unionisation, and the Botswana and Namibian governments, as JV partners, represent a unique form of supply-side political risk. Overall, supplier power remains relatively contained.

### 5.4 Threat of Substitute Products — **VERY HIGH** ⬆️⬆️

This is the **dominant force** reshaping the industry. Laboratory-grown diamonds now command approximately 50% of the US engagement ring market by volume and 14% of overall US jewelry sales, representing a structural shift that fundamentally alters industry economics. De Beers faces an existential competitive threat that extends far beyond traditional cyclical market pressures. Other substitutes, including coloured gemstones (moissanite, sapphires), are also gaining popularity among younger consumers.

### 5.5 Competitive Rivalry — **HIGH** ⬆️

The natural diamond mining industry includes ALROSA (~30% of global production), Lucara Diamond Corp, Petra Diamonds, and Gem Diamonds. In the broader competitive landscape (including LGDs), rivalry is intense and growing, with Indian and Chinese manufacturers competing aggressively on price.

### Five Forces Summary

| Force | Intensity | Trend |
|---|---|---|
| Threat of New Entrants | Medium-High | ⬆️ Increasing |
| Bargaining Power of Buyers | High | ⬆️ Increasing |
| Bargaining Power of Suppliers | Low-Medium | ➡️ Stable |
| **Threat of Substitutes** | **Very High** | **⬆️⬆️ Rapidly Increasing** |
| Competitive Rivalry | High | ⬆️ Increasing |

**Overall Industry Attractiveness: LOW** — The structural forces are increasingly unfavourable for De Beers' current business model.

## 9. RECOMMENDATIONS & ACTION PLAN

The following integrated three-horizon action plan is proposed for De Beers under its new ownership structure.

---

### 🔴 HORIZON 1: STABILISE (0–18 Months)

**Recommendation 1.1 — Operational Right-Sizing**

Reduce rough diamond production to align with structural (not cyclical) demand. Jwaneng and Orapa should continue at optimised levels; higher-cost South African underground operations should be reviewed for suspension. The consolidated average realised price for rough diamonds fell to $155/carat — down 5% from $164/carat a year earlier, and more ominously, the average rough price index declined 14% as the company resorted to selling assortments at lower margins to clear excess inventory. Disciplined supply reduction will support price recovery, as De Beers demonstrated successfully in 2015–2016.

- **KPI:** Achieve positive EBITDA within 18 months; reduce unit cost of production by 15%.

**Recommendation 1.2 — Sightholder System Reform**

Restructure the sightholder allocation system to prioritise buyers who commit to downstream branding and provenance certification. De Beers tweaked its distribution system to provide more bespoke supply by classifying sightholders according to their business type: manufacturer, dealer, or retailer — a move seen as an attempt to reduce the flipping of boxes on the secondary market and to bring more efficiency to the supply chain. This reform should now go further, reducing the sightholder pool to ~40 strategic partners and requiring Tracr certification for all allocations.

- **KPI:** 100% of sightholder allocations include Tracr provenance certification within 12 months.

**Recommendation 1.3 — "Natural Diamond Origin" Campaign**

Launch a global campaign clearly differentiating natural diamonds from LGDs on the basis of rarity, geological heritage, and cultural meaning. De Beers' "Origins" strategy aims to enhance value and revitalize natural diamond appeal, with collaborations with retailers demonstrated by the success of the Seize the Day campaign, which involved over 22,000 retail stores. This should be scaled urgently and co-funded with the Natural Diamond Council.

- **KPI:** Measurable improvement in consumer perception of natural diamond uniqueness in US, UK, and India within 12 months.

---

### 🟡 HORIZON 2: REPOSITION (18 Months – 4 Years)

**Recommendation 2.1 — Luxury Retail Expansion (De Beers Jewellers)**

Accelerate the expansion of De Beers Jewellers to 30+ flagship stores globally (London, New York, Paris, Dubai, Mumbai, Shanghai, Singapore). De Beers will launch its first flagship store in Paris, on the prestigious Rue de la Paix. The CEO of De Beers' brands noted the brand is "a bit cold today — a little bit engineered" and needs to be made "much more emotional." Develop an exclusive "Tracr Certified" product line sold exclusively through De Beers Jewellers.

- **KPI:** De Beers Jewellers revenue to reach $500 million by Year 4; gross margin >60%.

**Recommendation 2.2 — India Market Acceleration (Forevermark)**

De Beers will refocus its Forevermark brand on the growing Indian market and scale its luxury De Beers Jewellers brand globally, with strategic relationships with major retailers like Signet Jewelers in the US and Chow Tai Fook in China. A dedicated India marketing budget of $50 million per annum should be established, with partnerships with Tanishq, Kalyan Jewellers, and PC Jeweller.

- **KPI:** Forevermark India revenues to grow 40% year-on-year for three consecutive years; achieve 15% market share of India's premium diamond jewellery segment.

**Recommendation 2.3 — Tracr Platform Commercialisation**

Position Tracr as the industry standard for diamond provenance verification. De Beers is introducing a new retail-facing diamond verification instrument, bringing its leading lab-grown diamond detection technology to retail counters. License the Tracr platform to competing natural diamond miners on a fee-per-carat basis and deploy verification instruments to 10,000+ retailers globally by Year 3.

- **KPI:** Tracr to achieve $100 million in licensing and service revenues by Year 4.

**Recommendation 2.4 — Southeast Asia Market Entry**

Global competitive pressures add complexity, as other diamond-producing nations like Russia, Canada, and Angola have strengthened their positions in recent years. Establishing Forevermark distribution partnerships in Vietnam, Indonesia, and Thailand — markets with growing middle classes and strong jewellery cultures — offers first-mover advantage.

- **KPI:** Establish distribution in all three markets within 24 months; combined revenues of $150 million by Year 4.

---

### 🟢 HORIZON 3: SUSTAIN (4–10 Years)

**Recommendation 3.1 — Vertical Integration into Polished Diamonds**
Gradually increase De Beers' direct involvement in cutting and polishing, particularly in Botswana (Gaborone is already a significant polishing centre), to capture more of the value chain. This aligns with Botswana's beneficiation requirements, strengthening the government partnership.

**Recommendation 3.2 — "Rarity Index" Pricing Model**
Develop a transparent, scientifically-grounded "Rarity Index" for natural diamonds — a proprietary scoring system quantifying geological rarity, provenance, and ethical credentials — as the basis for premium pricing. In a world where LGDs are physically identical, the value of natural diamonds must be anchored in verifiable, non-replicable attributes.

**Recommendation 3.3 — Governance & Ownership Alignment**
Work constructively with the incoming ownership consortium to establish a governance structure that aligns Botswana's national development goals with commercial imperatives. As negotiations intensify, the century-old diamond empire's future hangs in the balance between financial pragmatism and political complexity, with implications extending far beyond Anglo American's balance sheet to the economic destinies of diamond-dependent African nations. Proactive governance design can convert political risk into a source of competitive legitimacy — "proudly African diamonds."

## 10. CONCLUSION

De Beers stands at a genuine strategic crossroads. The divestment timeline — completion by end-2025 — reflects management's recognition that market conditions may deteriorate further. Better to transact during a cyclical downturn than wait for potential structural decline to accelerate.

Yet De Beers is not without hope. It possesses assets that no competitor can replicate: world-class geological assets, a brand with 135 years of heritage, proprietary traceability technology, and the emotional resonance of the world's most successful marketing idea. The strategic analysis — drawing on PESTEL, Porter's Five Forces, VRIO, Value Chain Analysis, SWOT, the Ansoff Matrix, and SAFe criteria — converges on a clear conclusion: **De Beers must abandon its identity as a volume commodity supplier and embrace its destiny as the world's defining luxury natural diamond brand.**

This means moving decisively down the value chain, investing in consumer-facing brands and retail, accelerating into high-growth markets like India, and commercialising its technological advantages in provenance verification. The alternative — continuing to compete on volume in a market being structurally disrupted by laboratory-grown diamonds — leads to a slow but inevitable decline.

A diamond may be forever. De Beers' future, however, depends entirely on the strategic decisions made in the next three to five years.
