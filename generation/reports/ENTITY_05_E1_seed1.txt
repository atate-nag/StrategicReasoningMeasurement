# BAYER AG: FROM ACQUISITION TO CRISIS — A STRATEGIC ANALYSIS

## Strategic Management Case Study

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Bayer AG, a 160-year-old German life sciences conglomerate, stands at one of the most consequential strategic crossroads in its history. The 2018 acquisition of Monsanto for $63 billion — intended to create a global agricultural powerhouse — instead triggered a cascade of litigation, financial distress, and reputational damage that has destroyed more than half of the company's enterprise value. Simultaneously, Bayer faces a pharmaceutical patent cliff as its two top-selling drugs lose exclusivity, and its Crop Science division has recorded billions in impairment charges. This case study analyses Bayer's strategic situation using **Porter's Five Forces**, the **Resource-Based View (RBV)**, a **SWOT analysis**, and the **Ansoff Growth Matrix**, arriving at a coherent set of strategic recommendations designed to restore financial stability, rebuild competitive advantage, and re-earn stakeholder trust.

---

## SECTION 1: BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### 1.1 The Company

Bayer is a global enterprise with core competencies in the life science fields of health care and nutrition, with products and services designed to benefit people by supporting efforts to overcome the major challenges presented by a growing and aging global population. The company operates through three divisions: **Pharmaceuticals**, **Consumer Health**, and **Crop Science**. Bayer's sales came in at approximately €47 billion in 2024, having been impacted by considerable negative currency effects, with EBITDA before special items declining by 13.5 percent to around €10 billion.

### 1.2 The Monsanto Acquisition: Strategic Logic and Execution

In 2018, German pharmaceutical and life sciences giant Bayer AG completed its $63 billion acquisition of Monsanto, the infamous agrochemical and biotechnology company behind Roundup, a glyphosate-based herbicide. On paper, it looked like a bold strategic move to create an agricultural powerhouse. The stated strategic rationale was explicit: "The acquisition of Monsanto is a strategic milestone in strengthening our portfolio of leading businesses in health and nutrition. We will double the size of our agriculture business and create a leading innovation engine in agriculture."

Without the agreed-to divestitures, the proposed merger would likely have resulted in higher prices, lower quality, and fewer choices across a wide array of seed and crop protection products. To secure regulatory approval, Bayer agreed to the divestiture of businesses which generated EUR 2.2 billion in sales in 2017 for an aggregate base purchase price of EUR 7.6 billion. The US Department of Justice described this as the largest negotiated merger divestiture in US history.

### 1.3 The Strategic Situation: A Three-Front Crisis

What followed was not the anticipated value creation, but a three-front strategic crisis:

**1. Legal liability:** Shortly after the acquisition, Bayer found itself engulfed in a wave of litigation alleging that Roundup causes cancer. Despite previously settling thousands of lawsuits, Bayer is still facing approximately 58,000 active Roundup cancer claims. The company agreed to settle mass litigation in multiple states for $10 billion in 2020 and received preliminary court approval for a $7.25 billion settlement. Individual verdicts have been staggering: in March 2025, the jury in the Barnes Roundup trial in the State Court of Cobb County, Georgia reached a verdict in favour of the plaintiff of 2.1 billion US dollars (2 billion in punitive damages and 65 million in compensatory damages).

**2. Financial distress:** The acquisition of Monsanto increased Bayer's leverage, and while the company has worked to reduce debt over time, the combination of litigation, restructuring, and investment needs still requires careful financial management. Net financial debt amounted to 32.626 billion euros as of December 31, 2024, down 5.4 percent from year-end 2023. In order to further reduce debt and gain greater flexibility, the company intends to pay out the legal minimum in dividends. Bayer AG's enterprise value was at its highest in the June 2018 quarter at €133.54 billion, falling to a low of approximately €53.59 billion in the December 2024 quarter.

**3. Pharmaceutical patent cliff:** Bayer lost its US patent on Xarelto, its factor Xa inhibitor, in 2025, which alone accounted for an estimated €1bn–€1.5bn of lost 2025 revenues. The company is set to lose its patent on Eylea in 2027. Meanwhile, in 2024, Bayer Crop Science recorded significant impairment charges totalling €3.78 billion, primarily in crop protection. The majority of this was a €3.27 billion impairment on goodwill, reflecting weaker-than-expected agricultural market conditions.

## SECTION 2: EXTERNAL ENVIRONMENT ANALYSIS — PORTER'S FIVE FORCES

Porter's Five Forces framework (Porter, 1980) provides a systematic tool for assessing the competitive intensity and structural attractiveness of Bayer's two primary industries.

### 2.1 Threat of New Entrants — LOW to MODERATE

The pharmaceutical and agrochemical industries are characterised by formidable barriers to entry. A robust R&D pipeline, backed by a €6.2 billion investment in 2024, fuels innovation in pharmaceuticals and agriculture. Regulatory approval timelines and deep scientific expertise further deter entry. However, the threat is moderate rather than negligible because AgTech investments hit $10.5 billion in 2024, pressuring Bayer to scale digital spend to protect margins. Additionally, generic price pressure in crop protection is already cited as a material challenge for Bayer's Crop Science division in 2025.

### 2.2 Bargaining Power of Suppliers — MODERATE to HIGH

High switching costs significantly bolster supplier bargaining power for Bayer. For instance, replacing a supplier of highly specialised active pharmaceutical ingredients requires extensive re-validation and new regulatory approvals, a process that can cost millions and delay product launches. In logistics, consolidation in global shipping and cold-chain logistics has cut Bayer's carrier options, raising supplier power; the top 10 ocean carriers now control approximately 85% of capacity, enabling price hikes for temperature-controlled pharma and seed shipments.

### 2.3 Bargaining Power of Buyers — MODERATE

In 2024, online agricultural marketplaces and health information portals provided farmers with data on crop protection products, enabling them to benchmark Bayer's offerings against competitors. In pharmaceuticals, national health systems and insurers exercise significant pricing pressure through reference pricing and reimbursement negotiations. However, the switching costs associated with adopting an entirely new seed-and-herbicide system moderate buyer power in agriculture.

### 2.4 Threat of Substitute Products — MODERATE to HIGH

In pharmaceuticals, the loss of patent exclusivity on Xarelto and Eylea exposes Bayer to direct generic substitution. In crop science, biological crop protection products represent a growing substitute for chemical herbicides, driven by regulatory tightening on synthetic chemicals and farmer demand for sustainable inputs. Bayer's CEO has laid out priorities for the troubled German conglomerate, noting that it doesn't involve an immediate business split-up as many investors had hoped. Glyphosate faces ongoing regulatory scrutiny in the EU, adding an existential dimension to the substitution threat for Roundup.

### 2.5 Competitive Rivalry — HIGH

Bayer Crop Science offers a comprehensive range of crop protection products, seeds, and digital farming solutions, with its acquisition of Monsanto bolstering its position, particularly in genetically modified seeds. However, it competes directly with Syngenta (ChemChina), Corteva Agriscience, and BASF. In 2024, BASF's Agricultural Solutions segment reported sales of €9,798 million. In pharmaceuticals, Bayer competes with global giants across cardiovascular, oncology, and ophthalmology therapeutic areas. The market continues to associate Bayer with legal risk and value destruction before looking at operational progress, and multiples remain lower than those of several comparable players in agrochemicals or healthcare.

**Five Forces Summary:** The overall structural attractiveness of Bayer's industries is moderate-to-low, with high rivalry, growing substitution threats, and significant buyer power. This makes it imperative that Bayer competes on the basis of proprietary, hard-to-replicate resources rather than industry structure.

## SECTION 3: INTERNAL ANALYSIS — THE RESOURCE-BASED VIEW (RBV)

The Resource-Based View (Barney, 1991; Wernerfelt, 1984) holds that sustained competitive advantage derives from resources and capabilities that are **Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN)**. Bayer's internal audit reveals a mixed picture.

### 3.1 Strengths (VRIN Resources)

**Pharmaceutical Pipeline:** Bayer's most credible VRIN asset is its emerging pharmaceutical pipeline. Nubeqa achieved global blockbuster status in September 2024 after crossing the threshold of one billion euros in annual sales. Additionally, Nubeqa is now the fastest growing androgen receptor inhibitor in the US, with nearly 45,000 patients in the US and 100,000 patients globally treated as of the end of 2024. The robust data from Kerendia's clinical development programme underscore its potential to become a pillar of therapy for patients with chronic kidney disease associated with Type 2 Diabetes and patients with heart failure. Regulatory filings are underway for a third indication for darolutamide in prostate cancer and a new indication for finerenone in patients with heart failure. Furthermore, Bayer's pharmaceutical division delivered an 80% sales surge in Q1 2025, driven by Nubeqa and Kerendia.

**Seed Genetics and Digital Farming Platform:** The Crop Science division projects over €32 billion in peak sales from new products. In its Crop Science division, Bayer aims for above-market growth, targeting over €3.5 billion in incremental sales from innovation by 2029, including launching ten major products within the next decade, each with peak sales potential exceeding €1 billion.

**R&D Capability:** In 2024, Bayer's R&D expenses were approximately €5.5 billion, reflecting its commitment to innovation. This focus supports future revenue and market leadership, especially in pharmaceuticals and crop science.

### 3.2 Weaknesses (Resource Deficiencies)

Several critical lessons emerge from this high-profile case: Bayer appears to have underestimated the scale and durability of Monsanto's litigation risk. Publicly available studies and ongoing lawsuits at the time of acquisition should have triggered more extensive legal scenario planning.

Reputational liability has also functioned as financial liability: Monsanto's controversial public profile compounded Bayer's post-merger challenges. The company's balance sheet weakness — net debt of €32.6 billion — severely constrains strategic flexibility, while the €3.78 billion Crop Science impairment signals that the Monsanto acquisition premium has not been justified by operational performance.

## SECTION 4: SWOT ANALYSIS

Synthesising the external and internal analyses yields the following SWOT matrix:

### STRENGTHS
- Strong, diversified R&D pipeline with near-term blockbuster drugs (Nubeqa, Kerendia, elinzanetant)
- World-leading integrated seed-and-crop-protection platform
- Global regulatory expertise and established distribution networks
- Free cash flow more than doubled against the prior year, rising to 3.107 billion euros
- Dynamic Shared Ownership (DSO) targeting €2 billion of planned organisational savings between 2023 and 2026, with 3,500 DSO Champions embedding DSO principles globally

### WEAKNESSES
- €32.6 billion net financial debt severely limiting strategic flexibility
- ~58,000 active Roundup litigation claims representing open-ended financial liability
- Xarelto and Eylea patent cliff eroding pharmaceutical revenue base
- €3.78 billion Crop Science goodwill impairment signalling acquisition overpayment
- Bayer's CEO acknowledged the company still has too many layers of management and bureaucracy, despite prior cost-cutting measures

### OPPORTUNITIES
- Growing global demand for food security solutions driven by climate change and population growth
- Expansion of Nubeqa and Kerendia into new therapeutic indications
- Digital agriculture and precision farming as a platform for premium pricing
- The executive order President Trump signed in February protecting and encouraging the production of glyphosate is a major victory for Bayer AG — a potential regulatory tailwind
- Cell and gene therapy platform with long-term value crystallisation post-2027

### THREATS
- Generic competition following Xarelto (2025) and Eylea (2027) patent expiry
- Ongoing and escalating Roundup litigation with unpredictable jury verdicts
- Intensifying competition from Syngenta, Corteva, BASF in crop science
- EU regulatory tightening on glyphosate and synthetic crop protection chemicals
- AgTech disruption from digital-native entrants
- Credit rating pressure limiting refinancing flexibility

## SECTION 5: STRATEGIC POSITION ASSESSMENT — CONNECTING THE ANALYSIS

The Five Forces and RBV analyses connect into a coherent strategic diagnosis. Bayer operates in structurally competitive industries where sustained advantage requires proprietary, hard-to-imitate resources. Its VRIN assets — the pharmaceutical pipeline and the integrated seed-chemistry platform — are genuine, but they are being undermined by three structural problems that are **self-reinforcing**:

1. **The debt-litigation trap:** Litigation costs consume cash that would otherwise reduce debt; high debt constrains the R&D investment needed to replace revenues lost to patent expiry; the patent cliff reduces cash generation, making debt reduction slower. This is a negative feedback loop.

2. **The reputational discount:** The market continues to associate Bayer with legal risk and value destruction before looking at operational progress. This suppresses the share price and makes equity issuance expensive, preventing Bayer from raising capital to break the debt-litigation trap.

3. **The organisational drag:** Bureaucratic complexity slows Bayer's ability to commercialise its pipeline assets quickly enough to offset Xarelto/Eylea revenue losses.

The **Ansoff Matrix** (Ansoff, 1957) provides a further lens for evaluating growth options, given Bayer's constrained balance sheet:

| | **Existing Products** | **New Products** |
|---|---|---|
| **Existing Markets** | **Market Penetration** — Nubeqa growth in prostate cancer; Crop Science volume recovery | **Product Development** — elinzanetant, Kerendia new indications, new Crop Science traits |
| **New Markets** | **Market Development** — Nubeqa in Asia; Crop Science in emerging markets | **Diversification** — Cell & gene therapy; digital farming platforms |

Given Bayer's constrained balance sheet, the **highest-priority quadrant is Product Development** — extending existing pipeline assets into new indications within established markets (lowest risk, highest near-term return on existing R&D investment). **Market Development** in emerging markets offers medium-term upside. **Diversification** into cell and gene therapy is strategically important but must be sequenced carefully given capital constraints.

## SECTION 6: RECOMMENDATIONS AND ACTION PLAN

The following five recommendations are structured across three horizons and are designed to break the negative feedback loop identified above.

---

### RECOMMENDATION 1: RESOLVE THE LITIGATION OVERHANG DECISIVELY
**Horizon: Immediate (0–18 months)**

**Rationale:** With approximately 58,000 active Roundup claims outstanding, litigation uncertainty is the single largest destroyer of investor confidence and financial flexibility. The Roundup MDL in federal court has largely stalled, holding just over 4,400 pending cases with minimal monthly growth. In contrast, state court litigation has become the primary battleground in 2024 and 2025, resulting in nearly $5 billion in adverse verdicts. Continued litigation also consumes management attention and legal resources that should be directed at operational recovery.

**Action:** Bayer should pursue a **comprehensive global settlement framework** — negotiating a structured, time-limited claims resolution programme that caps total liability in exchange for a lump-sum fund, analogous to the asbestos trust fund model. The executive order President Trump signed in February protecting and encouraging the production of glyphosate is a major victory for Bayer AG and creates a political tailwind for pursuing federal pre-emption legislation that aligns US state court standards with the EPA's scientific finding that glyphosate is not a carcinogen.

**Connected logic:** Resolving litigation certainty is the prerequisite for the rest of the strategy. Without it, debt markets price in open-ended risk, equity markets apply a persistent discount, and management cannot focus on operational transformation.

---

### RECOMMENDATION 2: ACCELERATE DEBT REDUCTION THROUGH TARGETED ASSET DISPOSALS
**Horizon: Immediate to Medium-term (0–24 months)**

**Rationale:** Bayer executives considered a breakup option during a broader strategic review, but ultimately concluded it would take up too many resources without fixing the core issues. However, a targeted disposal of Consumer Health — which, while generating stable revenues, lacks the VRIN characteristics of Bayer's pharmaceutical pipeline and seed genetics IP — would generate significant proceeds for debt reduction without dismantling the core life sciences strategy. Bayer's strength lies in its diverse portfolio across pharmaceuticals, consumer health, and crop science, but this diversification does not equally reflect proprietary competitive advantage across all divisions.

**Action:** Conduct a **formal strategic review of the Consumer Health division** with a view to a partial IPO or trade sale. Proceeds should be directed entirely to debt reduction, targeting a net debt-to-EBITDA ratio below 3.0x. The proposed dividend of €0.11 per share reflects the company's priority to reduce debt level so as to boost financial flexibility. A Consumer Health disposal would accelerate this trajectory and unlock an improvement in Bayer's credit rating, reducing the cost of refinancing the remaining debt.

**Connected logic:** Debt reduction restores financial flexibility, enabling Bayer to invest more aggressively in R&D and pipeline commercialisation without the constraint of credit rating pressure.

---

### RECOMMENDATION 3: MAXIMISE PIPELINE VELOCITY IN PHARMACEUTICALS
**Horizon: Immediate to Medium-term (0–36 months)**

**Rationale:** Bayer lost its US patent on Xarelto in 2025, which alone accounted for an estimated €1bn–€1.5bn of lost 2025 revenues, and the company is set to lose its patent on Eylea in 2027. "We expect the full value of the cell and gene therapy platform to crystallise" beyond 2027, just as the sales decline from Xarelto bottoms out. Managing this transition requires pipeline velocity as the primary strategic priority.

**Action:**
- **Fast-track regulatory submissions** for new indications: darolutamide in a third prostate cancer indication, finerenone in heart failure (LVEF ≥40%), and acoramidis in transthyretin amyloid cardiomyopathy — all already in regulatory filing stages.
- **Invest in commercial launch excellence for elinzanetant**, which met all primary and secondary endpoints in Phase III study OASIS 4 for the treatment of moderate to severe vasomotor symptoms caused by breast cancer treatments — a large, underserved market with high unmet need.
- **Protect the Nubeqa franchise** globally, building on the momentum of 100,000 patients treated and blockbuster status achieved in 2024.
- **Ring-fence R&D investment** from cost-cutting pressures. The market continues to associate Bayer with legal risk and value destruction before looking at operational progress — only a credible pipeline narrative can shift this perception.

**Connected logic:** Pharmaceutical pipeline velocity is the primary revenue replacement mechanism for the patent cliff. The faster these assets reach market, the shorter the revenue trough and the faster debt can be serviced.

---

### RECOMMENDATION 4: STABILISE AND SELECTIVELY INVEST IN CROP SCIENCE
**Horizon: Medium-term (18 months–3 years)**

**Rationale:** While the Crop Science division faced a decline, Bayer is implementing a five-year plan to improve its mid-term position and enhance resilience. Abandoning this division entirely would destroy the strategic logic of the Monsanto acquisition and write off the remaining goodwill. The integrated seed-and-chemistry platform remains a VRIN asset, but capital must be deployed selectively.

**Action:**
- **Prioritise the innovation pipeline over volume defence:** Focus R&D on the ten blockbuster crop science products targeted for launch by 2029, rather than defending commoditised glyphosate volumes — a product development strategy in Ansoff terms.
- **Accelerate the shift to biological and precision agriculture** as regulatory pressure on synthetic chemicals intensifies in the EU, reducing regulatory risk while capturing premium pricing in sustainability-conscious markets.
- **Expand in emerging markets** (Brazil, India, Southeast Asia) where food security pressures are greatest and GM seed adoption is growing — a market development strategy.
- **Divest non-core Crop Science assets:** The Cotton Seed business suffered a €510 million write-down due to regulatory issues in the US and represents a non-core, capital-consuming asset that should be divested to focus the portfolio.

**Connected logic:** Crop Science stabilisation prevents further balance sheet damage from impairments and generates the cash flows needed to service debt and fund pharmaceutical R&D.

---

### RECOMMENDATION 5: COMPLETE THE ORGANISATIONAL TRANSFORMATION (DSO) WITH RIGOUR
**Horizon: Immediate to Medium-term (0–24 months)**

**Rationale:** CEO Bill Anderson's Dynamic Shared Ownership (DSO) operating model, rolled out in January 2024, aims to cut back on bloated bureaucracy and hierarchies and streamline the organisation. His bold response was a radical model replacing traditional hierarchies with self-organising teams. By 2025, Bayer was on track to cut €2 billion in costs, but questions remained — could fewer bosses truly drive innovation, or was this an unsustainable gamble?

**Action:**
- **Implement DSO with a dual mandate:** cost reduction AND innovation acceleration. The 3,500 DSO Champions already embedding DSO principles within their teams should be explicitly tasked with reducing time-to-market for pipeline assets, not just reducing headcount.
- **Protect scientific and regulatory talent** from restructuring cuts — these are the hardest-to-replace capabilities in both pharma and crop science, and the foundation of Bayer's VRIN resources.
- **Use the €2 billion savings target as a floor, not a ceiling:** Freed resources should be reallocated to R&D and commercial launch capabilities.

**Connected logic:** Organisational efficiency releases the capital and management bandwidth needed to execute Recommendations 1–4. Without DSO success, Bayer cannot generate the free cash flow required to service debt, fund litigation settlements, and invest in its pipeline simultaneously.

## SECTION 8: RISKS AND LIMITATIONS

No strategic recommendation is without risk. The key risks to the proposed action plan are:

1. **Litigation settlement failure:** If a comprehensive Roundup settlement cannot be negotiated — due to plaintiff attorney resistance or adverse Supreme Court rulings on federal pre-emption — the litigation overhang may persist indefinitely, undermining the entire strategy.

2. **Pipeline execution risk:** Pharmaceutical development carries inherent clinical and regulatory risk. If key pipeline assets fail in late-stage trials, the revenue replacement strategy collapses.

3. **Consumer Health valuation risk:** A disposal at a depressed valuation may generate less debt reduction than anticipated, and the loss of stable Consumer Health cash flows could increase earnings volatility.

4. **Agricultural market cycle:** Crop Science profitability is sensitive to commodity prices, weather, and farmer purchasing behaviour. A prolonged downturn would reduce cash generation and increase pressure on the balance sheet.

5. **Organisational disruption:** DSO is a radical organisational model. Excessive disruption — talent attrition, loss of institutional knowledge — could impair the very execution capabilities Bayer needs.

## CONCLUSION

Bayer's strategic situation is one of the most instructive corporate case studies of the 2020s. The Monsanto acquisition brought to light two major problems for the German giant: a collapse in investor confidence, with investors now seeing the Monsanto acquisition as one of the worst strategic blunders in Bayer's history, and persistent operational and legal challenges that continue to compound. The analysis in this case study demonstrates that Bayer's path to recovery is neither a simple financial restructuring nor a straightforward operational fix — it requires a **sequenced, integrated strategy** that first breaks the negative feedback loop between litigation and debt, then rebuilds the revenue base through pharmaceutical pipeline acceleration and disciplined Crop Science investment, and finally enables execution through organisational transformation. The VRIN assets are present; the task is to liberate them from the structural constraints that have suppressed their value. Bayer's mission — *Health for all, Hunger for none* — remains strategically coherent. The question is whether management can execute the sequence of moves required to make it financially viable.
