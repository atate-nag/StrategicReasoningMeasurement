# Strategic Case Study: Trustpilot and the Integrity–Monetisation Dilemma

## The Core Strategic Situation in One Sentence

Trustpilot's freemium, two-sided platform model generates revenue precisely from the businesses whose reviews it is supposed to police impartially, creating a structural conflict of interest that, if unresolved, will erode the consumer trust on which the entire platform's value rests.

---

## 1. Company Background

Trustpilot Group plc, founded in 2007, is a Danish consumer business that operates a review website hosting reviews of businesses worldwide. As of mid-2025, Trustpilot hosts more than 330 million reviews and has 60 million monthly active users. Founded by Peter Holten Mühlmann while studying at Aarhus University, the platform was originally conceived to address consumer distrust in early e-commerce, and its growth relied on a dual-sided network effect: more reviews created stronger social proof for businesses and safer choices for shoppers. Trustpilot was listed on the London Stock Exchange in March 2021.

Financially, the company has reached meaningful scale. Revenue grew 18% in constant currency to $210.7 million in 2024 (2023: $176.4 million), and the company delivered a record adjusted EBITDA of $24.1 million (2023: $15.5 million), with profit before tax of $5.2 million against a prior-year loss of $1.9 million. Bookings grew 21% in constant currency, and the company achieved a record net dollar retention rate of 103%. Despite this financial progress, a strategic tension sits at the heart of the model, which the following analysis unpacks.

---

## 2. Business Model Description

### 2.1 The Freemium, Two-Sided Architecture

Trustpilot operates a freemium business model and earns most of its revenue from subscription-based customers; the company freely allows businesses to collect and respond to reviews, and also sells marketing tools. The investor relations page describes a "dual-sided platform that leverages network effects and provides businesses with tools to invite reviews, respond to feedback, showcase content, and benchmark performance."

The platform has two distinct user groups: consumers who write reviews for free, and businesses that pay for premium tools. Its model centres on a tiered SaaS subscription framework and usage-based upsells, with paid tiers unlocking analytics, invitation automation, and marketing rights to display ratings. Subscribing businesses operate in more than 120 countries, and the platform is industry-agnostic, working with businesses ranging from small firms to enterprise.

### 2.2 Revenue and Scale

Trustpilot has a vast consumer audience with 160 billion annual TrustBox impressions and greater reach through off-platform impressions such as Google search results; consumers recognise the Trustpilot brand, which influences purchasing decisions, so businesses value their Trustpilot score. Trustpilot reported approximately 60% of revenue from subscription products in 2024, highlighting dependence on SaaS monetisation. This dependence is the financial foundation of the strategic problem: the platform's primary paying customers are also the subjects of its reviews.

---

## 3. Strategic Problem Definition

### 3.1 The Integrity–Monetisation Conflict

The strategic situation is best framed as a **structural conflict of interest** embedded in the business model. The platform sells premium tools to businesses—tools that include the ability to flag and dispute reviews—while simultaneously claiming to be a neutral arbiter of consumer experience. The paid-tier model creates real bias concerns: subscribed businesses get more tools to flag and dispute reviews than free accounts, which critics say tilts outcomes.

This matters because the platform's commercial value to paying subscribers depends on its perceived credibility with consumers. If consumers cease to trust the reviews, businesses have no reason to pay for the platform's tools. The integrity problem is therefore not merely reputational—it is existential to the revenue model.

### 3.2 The Fake Review Problem

In 2023, 54 million reviews were published on Trustpilot globally, a 17% increase from 2022, and over a million businesses were reviewed. Despite the surge, the removal of fake reviews remained steady year-on-year, with Trustpilot removing 6% of total reviews in both 2023 (3.3 million) and 2022 (2.6 million). The majority of fake reviews—82%—were detected by Trustpilot's automated detection technology, up from 68% in 2022.

By 2024, the scale had grown further: Trustpilot removed 4.5 million fake reviews in 2024, with 90% of the fake reviews automatically taken down by AI tools. While Trustpilot frames these statistics as evidence of improving detection, a critic can read the same data differently: a platform that must remove 4.5 million fraudulent entries annually is a platform with a systemic fraud problem, not merely a fraud-detection success story. This inferential distinction is important because it determines whether the strategic response should be primarily technical (better detection) or structural (redesigning incentives).

### 3.3 Evidence of Structural Incentive Misalignment

Fake and misleading reviews remain a significant challenge for both businesses and consumers using Trustpilot; these dishonest practices affect star ratings and distort customer perception, often impacting purchasing decisions and reputations. The incentive structure explains why: businesses benefit from inflated scores (higher conversion rates), and Trustpilot benefits from business subscriptions. The platform therefore faces a principal–agent problem: it is simultaneously the agent of consumers (providing trustworthy information) and the agent of businesses (selling reputation management tools), and these roles conflict.

Trustpilot and other review platforms run on a two-sided model: appear neutral to users, keep paying clients happy behind the scenes. The Finance Magnates analysis adds that this dynamic is commercially productive in the short run—Trustpilot's net dollar retention is 103% and web traffic is soaring—but this short-run commercial success does not resolve the long-run structural risk.

## 4. Analytical Frameworks

### 4.1 Two-Sided Platform Theory (Rochet & Tirole, 2003)

Rochet and Tirole's theory of two-sided markets holds that a platform creates value by enabling interactions between two distinct user groups, and that the platform must price each side carefully to get both "on board." Cross-side network effects are central: the value to each side increases as the other side grows. Applied to Trustpilot:

- **Consumer side (free):** Consumers provide the raw content—reviews—that make the platform valuable. They are the supply side of the information good.
- **Business side (paid):** Businesses pay for tools to manage and leverage that content. They are the demand side.

The cross-side network effect is powerful: more consumer reviews make the platform more credible, which attracts more businesses to pay for subscriptions, which funds the platform's operations, which in turn enables more review collection infrastructure. This dual-sided network effect—more reviews creating stronger social proof for businesses and safer choices for shoppers—was the engine of Trustpilot's growth from its founding.

However, two-sided platform theory also identifies a critical vulnerability: **platform integrity is a public good**. If one side (businesses) can manipulate the content that the other side (consumers) relies upon, the cross-side network effect runs in reverse—deteriorating content quality reduces consumer trust, which reduces the value of the platform to businesses, which reduces subscription revenue. The fake review problem is therefore not an operational nuisance; it is a threat to the platform's core network effect.

The theory also illuminates the pricing problem. Because Trustpilot charges businesses but not consumers, it must be especially careful not to be perceived as favouring paying customers in content moderation decisions. Any perception of pay-to-play moderation destroys the consumer trust that is the platform's only durable asset.

### 4.2 Porter's Five Forces Analysis

**Threat of New Entrants (Moderate–High)**

The review platform market has low marginal costs of replication—any well-funded competitor can build a review aggregation site. The barriers to entry are not technical but reputational: the value of a review platform comes from the accumulated stock of reviews and the consumer habit of consulting it. Google Reviews and Amazon control distribution channels that generate more than 50% of first-touch review exposures for many categories. This means that the two most powerful potential entrants are already in the market, and they have structural advantages (search integration, existing customer relationships) that Trustpilot cannot easily replicate. The threat of new entrants is therefore high from platform giants, even if low from pure-play start-ups.

**Bargaining Power of Buyers (Businesses) (Moderate)**

Businesses have meaningful switching costs once they have accumulated a review history on Trustpilot—their star rating and review corpus are not portable to a competitor. However, Yotpo and Bazaarvoice integrations can boost on-site conversion by 5–15%, reducing merchant incentive to pay for external review platforms. This suggests that Trustpilot's value proposition is contestable: if on-site review tools deliver comparable conversion uplift, the marginal value of a Trustpilot subscription falls. Buyer power is therefore moderate and rising.

**Bargaining Power of Suppliers (Consumers) (Low–Moderate)**

Consumers who write reviews are the platform's content suppliers. Individually, they have no bargaining power—any single reviewer is replaceable. Collectively, however, consumer behaviour determines the platform's value. If consumers migrate to Google Reviews or social media for review-giving, Trustpilot's content pipeline degrades. Market dynamics, channel advantages, and vertical specialisation shape Trustpilot's competitive landscape: Google Reviews captures a large share of local feedback via search and Maps, impacting Trustpilot's organic traffic. Collective consumer migration is thus a latent supplier threat.

**Threat of Substitutes (High)**

Key competitors in the review platform space include Yelp, Google Reviews, and TripAdvisor. For consumers, Google Reviews is a near-perfect substitute for general business reviews, with the added benefit of integration into search and maps. For businesses, the choice between platforms depends on which delivers the highest conversion uplift and search visibility. The threat of substitutes is high and structurally worsening as Google integrates reviews more deeply into its search results.

**Competitive Rivalry (High)**

Consolidation continues: partnerships between e-commerce platforms and review providers are reshaping competitive dynamics. Trustpilot competes not only with direct review platforms but with any mechanism that provides social proof—including native product reviews on Amazon, Instagram social proof, and influencer marketing. Rivalry is intense.

**Porter's Five Forces Summary:** The industry structure is unfavourable. The two most significant threats—Google's integration of reviews into search, and the structural fake-review problem—are interconnected: if Trustpilot's review integrity deteriorates, Google's higher-trust reviews become an even more compelling substitute.

### 4.3 SWOT Analysis

**Strengths**
1. *Scale and network effects:* The platform hosts more than 330 million reviews and has 60 million monthly active users, a stock of content that is extremely difficult for a new entrant to replicate.
2. *Financial momentum:* The 2024 annual report recorded 21% constant-currency bookings growth and a record net dollar retention rate of 103%, demonstrating that paying customers are both growing in number and spending more over time.
3. *AI-powered fraud detection:* Trustpilot's models continuously learn and adapt, identifying new tactics used to manipulate trust ratings; more than 90% of fake reviews are now removed automatically, without manual intervention.
4. *Regulatory positioning:* Trustpilot's open platform model and transparency reporting position it favourably relative to closed or opaque competitors as regulation tightens.

**Weaknesses**
1. *Structural conflict of interest:* As established above, the freemium model creates an inherent tension between consumer neutrality and business-side revenue.
2. *Persistent fake review volume:* Removing 4.5 million fake reviews in a single year is simultaneously a detection achievement and evidence of a massive ongoing fraud problem.
3. *Subscriber tool asymmetry:* Subscribed businesses get more tools to flag and dispute reviews than free accounts, which creates a perception of unequal treatment that undermines platform neutrality.
4. *Dependence on SaaS monetisation:* Approximately 60% of revenue comes from subscription products, meaning any significant churn in the business subscriber base would materially damage financial performance.

**Opportunities**
1. *Regulatory tailwinds:* The EU Omnibus Directive imposes transparency obligations concerning the verification of online reviews, aligned with ISO 20488 standards, requiring robust procedures for reliability, traceability, and moderation. Platforms with credible verification processes gain a competitive advantage as regulation raises the bar for all players.
2. *AI-enhanced product development:* Trustpilot's 2024–2025 focus is expansion, product-data innovation, and profitable scale, with growth centred on geographic expansion, upselling subscriptions with analytics and service recovery, and embedding reputation signals across commerce.
3. *Enterprise upsell:* The improving net dollar retention rate (103%) suggests that existing customers are finding increasing value in the platform, supporting an enterprise upsell strategy.
4. *Emerging markets:* The platform operates across both online and offline verticals, with subscribing businesses in more than 120 countries, but significant geographic headroom remains.

**Threats**
1. *Google's structural advantage:* Google Reviews captures a large share of local feedback via search and Maps, impacting Trustpilot's organic traffic—a structural threat that cannot be addressed by product improvements alone.
2. *Escalating regulatory burden:* Coming into full effect for many platforms by 2024, the Digital Services Act introduces further obligations around transparency and online consumer protection, including new audit and reporting obligations for very large online platforms (VLOPs), further raising the stakes for those involved in review aggregation.
3. *Consumer trust erosion:* The erosion of trust in platforms like Trustpilot could lead to a more cautious approach to online reviews in general, reducing both the supply of consumer reviews and the demand from businesses for reputation tools.
4. *AI-generated fake reviews:* Generative AI dramatically lowers the cost of producing convincing fake reviews, threatening to overwhelm detection systems that were trained on human-generated fraud patterns.

### 4.4 The Strategic Tension as a Platform Governance Problem

The SWOT and Five Forces analyses converge on a single strategic insight: **Trustpilot's competitive moat is consumer trust, and its business model structurally undermines that moat.** This is not merely a risk management issue—it is a platform governance problem that requires a structural response.

The academic literature on platform governance (Eisenmann, Parker & Van Alstyne, 2006) identifies the core challenge for two-sided platforms as managing "envelopment" threats from larger platforms and "openness" decisions that affect the quality of the content ecosystem. Trustpilot faces both: Google threatens to envelop the review function within its own search ecosystem, while Trustpilot's openness (any consumer can write any review) creates the fake-review vulnerability. The governance challenge is to maintain sufficient openness to sustain the consumer network effect while imposing sufficient controls to maintain content integrity.

The current response—AI detection, transparency reports, litigation against fake-review sellers—is primarily technical and reactive. It addresses the symptoms (fake reviews) without resolving the structural cause (incentive misalignment between the platform's revenue model and its content integrity mission).

## 5. Strategic Options

Three strategic options are available, each with distinct trade-offs:

**Option A: Intensify Technical Defences (Incremental)**
Continue investing in AI detection and litigation, as currently pursued. This is the path of least commercial disruption but does not resolve the structural conflict. The fake review volume is likely to grow with the platform, and AI-generated fakes will test detection systems in ways human-generated fakes did not. This option preserves short-run revenue but accepts ongoing reputational risk.

**Option B: Structural Separation of Moderation and Monetisation**
Establish a genuinely independent content integrity function—operationally separated from the commercial team, with its own governance, budget, and public reporting—analogous to the editorial independence model in journalism. This directly addresses the structural conflict of interest. The cost is internal: it requires accepting that some paying customers will lose disputes they previously won, potentially increasing churn in the short run.

**Option C: Verified Review Model (Structural Upgrade)**
Move toward a verified-purchase or verified-transaction model, where reviews can only be submitted by consumers who can demonstrate a genuine transaction. The EU Omnibus Directive requires businesses to verify reviewer identities and purchases, disclosing this information clearly. Trustpilot could position itself ahead of regulatory requirements by making verification the default rather than the exception, differentiating from Google Reviews (which does not verify transactions) and from the open model that enables fake reviews.

## 6. Recommendations

The following action plan integrates elements of Options B and C, prioritised by urgency and strategic impact.

### Recommendation 1: Establish an Independent Content Integrity Board (Immediate – 0–6 months)

**What:** Create a Content Integrity Board with independent non-executive membership, separate from the commercial organisation, with authority over moderation policy and the power to publish its own annual integrity report.

**Why this is warranted:** The core strategic problem is a structural conflict of interest, not a technical one. The paid-tier model creates real bias concerns: subscribed businesses get more tools to flag and dispute reviews than free accounts, which critics say tilts outcomes. A technically independent board signals to consumers—and to regulators—that moderation decisions are not influenced by subscription revenue. This directly addresses the platform governance problem identified in Section 4.4. Without structural separation, even genuine improvements in detection will be read by sceptical consumers as commercially motivated.

### Recommendation 2: Introduce Tiered Review Verification as a Default (6–18 months)

**What:** Implement transaction-verified reviews as the standard for all categories where verification is technically feasible (e-commerce, SaaS, financial services), with unverified reviews clearly labelled as such. Invest in API partnerships with payment processors and e-commerce platforms to enable automated verification.

**Why this is warranted:** The EU Omnibus Directive imposes transparency obligations concerning the verification of online reviews, requiring robust procedures for reliability, traceability, and moderation. Moving to verified reviews ahead of regulatory requirements converts a compliance cost into a competitive advantage: Trustpilot becomes the high-integrity alternative to Google Reviews' unverified model. This directly addresses the fake review problem at the source—by raising the cost of submitting a fake review—rather than relying solely on post-submission detection. The trade-off is a reduction in review volume; however, a smaller corpus of verified reviews is strategically more valuable than a larger corpus of contested authenticity.

### Recommendation 3: Redesign Subscriber Tool Access to Remove Moderation Asymmetry (6–12 months)

**What:** Decouple review-flagging and dispute tools from subscription tier. All businesses—paying or free—should have equal access to the dispute mechanism. Premium tiers should be differentiated exclusively by analytics, marketing, and integration features, not by moderation access.

**Why this is warranted:** The current asymmetry—where paying subscribers have superior dispute tools—is the most visible manifestation of the structural conflict of interest and the most frequently cited source of consumer distrust. Businesses that invest in Trustpilot's premium services can benefit from features such as review removal and enhanced visibility, creating an environment where the loudest or wealthiest voices drown out genuine customer experiences, leading to a biased view of businesses. Removing this asymmetry reduces the commercial incentive to pay for subscriptions in the short run, but the long-run benefit is a more credible neutrality claim that sustains the consumer trust on which all subscription value ultimately depends.

### Recommendation 4: Proactively Shape Regulatory Standards (Ongoing)

**What:** Engage with the EU, UK Competition and Markets Authority, and equivalent bodies to co-author industry standards for review platform integrity, positioning Trustpilot as the standard-setter rather than a compliance subject.

**Why this is warranted:** From April 2025, a new UK regime makes it unlawful to publish or commission fake or misleading consumer reviews, and gives the Competition and Markets Authority stronger enforcement powers. The European Parliament has noted that around one in two consumers reported that they were unable to find information on whether a company ensures that the reviews published were submitted by real customers and on how reviews were collected. Regulatory pressure is therefore rising on both sides of the Atlantic. A platform that helps write the rules is better positioned than one that merely complies with them: it can shape standards to reflect its existing capabilities, raise the compliance costs for less-sophisticated competitors, and signal credibility to enterprise customers who face their own regulatory scrutiny.

### Recommendation 5: Invest in AI Integrity, Not Only AI Detection (12–24 months)

**What:** Redirect a portion of the AI investment budget from post-submission fake-review detection toward pre-submission integrity signals—behavioural biometrics, device fingerprinting, and reviewer identity verification—that make fake review submission harder rather than merely faster to detect after the fact.

**Why this is warranted:** In 2024, Trustpilot expanded its AI capabilities by deploying generative AI to help enforce platform policies, including impersonation, abuse, and misinformation. However, the asymmetry between attackers and defenders in the AI arms race favours attackers: generative AI reduces the cost of producing convincing fake reviews faster than it reduces the cost of detecting them. A pre-submission integrity architecture shifts the cost structure back toward attackers by requiring genuine behavioural signals before a review can be submitted. This is a more durable defence than post-submission detection alone.

## 8. Conclusion

Trustpilot's strategic situation is defined by a single, compounding tension: it has built a commercially successful platform on the premise of consumer trust, but its revenue model structurally incentivises the erosion of that trust. The financial results—18% revenue growth, 103% net dollar retention, record EBITDA—demonstrate that the current model is commercially viable in the short run. The fake review data, the consumer trust criticisms, and the rising regulatory pressure demonstrate that it is structurally fragile in the long run.

The recommended action plan addresses this tension at its source: by separating moderation governance from commercial governance (Recommendation 1), raising the cost of fake review submission through verification (Recommendation 2), removing the moderation asymmetry between paying and non-paying businesses (Recommendation 3), shaping the regulatory environment proactively (Recommendation 4), and shifting AI investment from detection toward prevention (Recommendation 5). Together, these measures convert Trustpilot's stated mission—to be "the universal symbol of trust"—from a marketing claim into a structural reality, and in doing so, protect the network effects on which its long-run competitive position depends.
