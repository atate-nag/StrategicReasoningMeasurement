# POLESTAR AUTOMOTIVE: BETWEEN AMBITION AND SURVIVAL
## A Strategic Case Study

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Polestar Automotive (Nasdaq: PSNY) occupies one of the most precarious positions in the global electric vehicle industry. Polestar has lost nearly all of its market value since 2022 and has accumulated USD $8 billion in losses even with its asset-light business model. Yet despite continued financial and market challenges in 2025, Polestar delivered over 60,000 vehicles to customers, a 34% increase from 2024. This case study examines the strategic situation facing Polestar through four analytical lenses — PESTEL, Porter's Five Forces, the Resource-Based View (RBV), and Porter's Generic Strategies — and derives a coherent set of strategic recommendations. The central strategic problem is this: Polestar possesses genuine differentiation assets — Scandinavian design, sustainability credentials, an asset-light manufacturing model — but has been unable to convert them into financial viability because of a structurally unfavourable cost position, a geopolitical manufacturing trap, weak brand awareness at scale, and an identity that sits uncomfortably between niche and volume.

---

## 1. COMPANY BACKGROUND

Polestar Automotive Holdings UK PLC was jointly formed in 2021 by Volvo Cars and Geely Holding Group. Polestar shares began trading on the Nasdaq exchange under the symbol PSNY on 24 June 2022. Its shareholding structure subsequently shifted dramatically: as of February 2024, Geely owns 72% of Polestar, with the remaining 18% controlled by Volvo. In parallel, Volvo Cars evaluated a potential adjustment to its shareholding in Polestar including a distribution of shares to its shareholders, with Geely Sweden Holding being the primary recipient, while Volvo Cars confirmed it would remain a strategic partner in areas across R&D, manufacturing, aftersales, and commercial activities.

Polestar has a capital-efficient, asset-light business model, with a lineup including the Polestar 2 (an electric performance fastback), Polestar 3 (the SUV for the electric age), and Polestar 4 (the SUV coupé transformed), with plans to add the Polestar 5 (electric four-door GT) and Polestar 6 (electric roadster).

Delivery volumes tell a story of ambition repeatedly outrunning execution. Polestar delivered approximately 51,500 cars in full year 2022, up 80% year-on-year. In 2023, Polestar delivered approximately 54,600 cars worldwide, a growth of 6% compared to 2022, but falling short of the annual target of 60,000 electric vehicles. The company delivered approximately 44,851 vehicles in all of 2024. Then came the 2025 recovery noted above — the brand's best-ever annual sales.

## 2. THE STRATEGIC SITUATION

The core strategic problem is a compound tension: Polestar is trying to operate as a focused premium differentiator while being structurally dependent on Chinese manufacturing (which exposes it to punitive tariffs), financially dependent on continuous external capital (which raises going-concern questions), and commercially dependent on a single volume model (the Polestar 2) that is losing ground to discounting pressure. These three tensions interact: tariff exposure forces price increases or margin compression, margin compression worsens the cash burn, and cash burn constrains the marketing investment needed to build the brand awareness that would justify premium pricing. The analysis below traces how each environmental and competitive force activates this compound tension, and why Polestar's existing resources partially — but not yet sufficiently — address it.

---

## 3. PESTEL ANALYSIS

### 3.1 Political

The most acute political risk Polestar faces is the imposition of trade tariffs on Chinese-manufactured EVs. Because Polestar's main production facility is in Chengdu, China, and this is where the majority of Polestar models are built, these measures directly compress its margins in its two largest Western markets. Polestar said it would need to introduce "mitigating measures" to achieve cashflow breakeven for 2025. This is not merely a cost inconvenience; it is a structural threat to the viability of the Polestar 2 as a volume product in Europe and North America. The political dimension also extends to the geopolitical optics of Geely's majority ownership: in an era of heightened scrutiny of Chinese investment in strategic industries, Polestar's Swedish identity is commercially important but increasingly difficult to sustain as a credible narrative.

### 3.2 Economic

According to the European Automobile Manufacturers' Association (ACEA), battery electric vehicles (BEVs) accounted for 13.6% of all new car registrations in the EU in 2024, down from 14.6% in 2023. However, the market rebounded significantly in 2025, with BEVs reaching a 17.4% market share. This deceleration in 2024 contributed to Polestar missing its volume targets and resorting to higher discounts on the Polestar 2 to maintain sales, which in turn eroded gross margins. For the first nine months of 2024, revenue fell 21% to $1.46 billion as global vehicle sales of the Polestar 2 declined amid higher discounts in a competitive market. The economic pressure is circular: volume shortfalls require discounting, discounting reduces margin, reduced margin increases cash burn, and increased cash burn raises the cost of capital.

### 3.3 Social

Consumer preferences are shifting in two directions that are relevant to Polestar. First, there is growing demand for vehicles that combine premium aesthetics with credible sustainability credentials — precisely the positioning Polestar occupies. Polestar's design philosophy emphasises Scandinavian minimalism: clean surfaces, restrained ornamentation, and focus on materials over decoration. Polestar uses recycled plastics, bio-based composites, and vegan interior options, tying into sustainability claims. Second, there is growing consumer scepticism about Chinese-made products in the premium automotive segment, which creates a reputational risk for a brand whose Swedish design identity is a core selling point but whose cars are manufactured predominantly in Chengdu.

### 3.4 Technological

The EV industry is in a phase of rapid technological convergence. Battery costs are falling, range anxiety is diminishing, and the software-defined vehicle is becoming a competitive battleground. Polestar's technological differentiation rests partly on its performance-oriented engineering heritage and its integration with Android Automotive OS. However, Polestar has utilised Volvo Cars' and Geely's established R&D capabilities to accelerate technological advancements in automotive technology, meaning it does not own the full technology stack. The asset-light model that reduces capital requirements also reduces technological control.

### 3.5 Environmental

Environmental regulation is a structural tailwind for Polestar's core mission. Since 2020, Polestar has published full Life Cycle Assessments (LCAs) for all its models, with publicly available assessments now covering every vehicle in its lineup, including the four-door GT Polestar 5. Polestar's "Polestar 0" project — an ambition to produce a truly climate-neutral car by 2030 — is referenced in its regulatory filings. Since the Polestar 2 was launched, Polestar has reduced its cradle-to-gate carbon footprint by three tonnes. These quantified improvements support the sustainability narrative and position Polestar to benefit from tightening environmental disclosure requirements.

### 3.6 Legal

Beyond tariffs, Polestar faces legal complexity arising from its dual identity as a Swedish-designed, Chinese-manufactured, UK-incorporated, Nasdaq-listed company. Polestar also announced plans to restate financial results for 2022, 2023, and parts of 2024 due to balance sheet errors related to tooling assets — a governance failure that damaged investor confidence. In the US, by manufacturing vehicles locally, Polestar can potentially avoid tariffs on Chinese-made vehicles and may qualify for EV tax credits under US legislation, which could make Polestar's vehicles more competitive in terms of pricing. This creates a legal incentive to shift production to the US — which Polestar is actively pursuing.

**PESTEL Summary and Connection to Strategic Situation:** The PESTEL analysis reveals that the dominant external pressures are political (tariffs) and economic (margin compression), both of which feed directly into Polestar's financial fragility. The environmental and social dimensions are opportunities that Polestar is partially capturing through its sustainability positioning. The legal dimension reinforces the urgency of manufacturing diversification. These external forces do not create Polestar's strategic problem, but they amplify it: a company with stronger margins and greater brand equity could absorb tariff impacts; Polestar currently cannot.

## 4. PORTER'S FIVE FORCES ANALYSIS

### 4.1 Competitive Rivalry (HIGH)

The premium EV segment has moved from nascent to intensely competitive. Polestar delivered approximately 44,851 vehicles in all of 2024 — a number that established players like BMW, Mercedes-Benz, and even Hyundai's Ioniq sub-brand surpass in a single quarter of EV sales alone. This volume gap matters strategically because scale drives down unit costs, and Polestar's inability to achieve scale is both a symptom and a cause of its financial difficulties. Polestar competes differently: it targets buyers who want premium EV design, restrained styling, and traditional build quality, while Tesla focuses on scale efficiency. This is a coherent positioning, but it must be defended against both Tesla's volume advantages and legacy premium brands' heritage advantages.

**Connection:** High rivalry means that Polestar cannot rely on market growth alone to improve its position; it must take share from better-resourced incumbents. This makes brand differentiation — the subject of the RBV analysis below — the critical competitive lever.

### 4.2 Threat of New Entrants (MODERATE)

The capital intensity of automotive manufacturing creates natural barriers to entry. However, the EV transition has lowered some traditional barriers (no need for ICE powertrain expertise) while raising others (battery supply chains, software capability). For Polestar specifically, the threat is less from entirely new entrants and more from established luxury brands deepening their EV commitment. The Polestar 5 is positioned as a direct competitor to established luxury EVs like the Tesla Model S and Porsche Taycan, both of which benefit from decades of brand equity and loyal customer bases that Polestar must earn.

### 4.3 Bargaining Power of Suppliers (MODERATE TO HIGH)

Polestar's asset-light model makes it structurally dependent on Volvo Cars and Geely for manufacturing, R&D, and back-office functions. Polestar's relationship with Volvo Cars and Geely has provided it with a unique competitive advantage in its ability to rapidly scale and finance commercialisation activities while maintaining an asset-light balance sheet. Service agreements with Volvo Cars allow it to attain operational efficiencies in aftermarket services and back-office functions such as IT, legal, accounting, finance, and HR. This is a double-edged dependency: it reduces capital requirements but constrains strategic autonomy.

**Connection:** The supplier dependency is structurally linked to the tariff problem. Because Polestar does not own its factories, it cannot unilaterally relocate production; it must negotiate with Volvo Cars to shift manufacturing to the US plant in Charleston, which is now planned but not yet complete for all models.

### 4.4 Bargaining Power of Buyers (MODERATE)

Premium EV buyers are relatively sophisticated and have increasing choice. By late 2024, Polestar operated over 160 retail locations globally and shifted toward a 'non-genuine agency model' in markets like the UK and Sweden to improve price consistency and operational efficiency. The direct-to-consumer model reduces dealer intermediation and gives Polestar more control over pricing. However, the resort to heavy discounting on the Polestar 2 in 2023–2024 indicates that buyer power was sufficient to force price concessions, undermining the premium positioning. Polestar has run sizable discounts and subsidised finance offers on new cars, pulling new transaction prices well below MSRP. That drags used prices down, because no buyer will pay almost-new money for yesterday's software and hardware.

### 4.5 Threat of Substitutes (MODERATE)

Within the EV segment, the proliferation of models means that a buyer considering a Polestar 3 has credible alternatives in the BMW iX3, Mercedes EQC, or Tesla Model Y. The threat of substitution is primarily intra-EV rather than EV-versus-ICE, particularly given the regulatory environment. This reinforces the importance of brand distinctiveness as the mechanism by which Polestar reduces substitutability.

**Five Forces Summary and Connection:** The overall industry structure is unfavourable for a sub-scale premium EV brand. High rivalry, moderate-to-high supplier power, and moderate buyer power combine to compress margins. Polestar's strategic response must therefore focus on strengthening the factors that reduce rivalry's impact — namely, brand differentiation and scale — rather than attempting to compete on cost with larger incumbents.

## 5. RESOURCE-BASED VIEW (RBV) ANALYSIS

The RBV posits that sustainable competitive advantage derives from resources that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (the VRIN framework). Applying this to Polestar reveals a mixed picture: some resources are genuinely VRIN-qualifying, others are valuable but not rare, and the firm's financial fragility threatens to erode even its strongest assets.

### 5.1 Scandinavian Design Identity (VRIN: HIGH)

Polestar CEO Michael Lohscheller has stated that Scandinavian minimalist design and the brand's sustainability framing remain the two differentiators that allow Polestar to coexist with Volvo Cars and Zeekr under the same corporate umbrella. This design philosophy is genuinely distinctive in an EV market where most competitors either emphasise technology (Tesla) or heritage (Porsche, BMW). By eschewing maximalist infotainment and flashy styling, Polestar differentiates on design purity and sustainability, appealing to buyers fatigued by tech-for-tech's-sake approaches. It is valuable because it commands a premium; it is rare because it is embedded in a specific design culture and talent base; it is difficult to imitate because brand aesthetics require years of consistent expression to become credible; and it is non-substitutable as a positioning anchor. **This is Polestar's strongest strategic asset.**

### 5.2 Sustainability Transparency (VRIN: HIGH)

Since 2020, Polestar has published full Life Cycle Assessments for all its models, with publicly available assessments now covering every vehicle in its lineup. This is industry-leading practice. It is valuable because sustainability credentials are an increasingly important purchase criterion in the premium segment; it is rare because few automakers publish LCAs at this level of granularity; it is difficult to imitate because it requires genuine supply-chain transparency and the organisational will to disclose unflattering data; and it is non-substitutable as a trust-building mechanism with environmentally conscious premium buyers. **This asset reinforces the design identity and creates a coherent brand narrative.**

### 5.3 Asset-Light Manufacturing Model (VRIN: MODERATE)

Polestar's relationship with Volvo Cars and Geely has provided a unique competitive advantage in its ability to rapidly scale and finance commercialisation activities while maintaining an asset-light balance sheet. This is valuable because it reduces the capital investment required to launch new models. However, as the PESTEL analysis shows, it creates a strategic vulnerability: Polestar cannot rapidly relocate production without its manufacturing partners' cooperation. **This resource is valuable in benign conditions but a liability in geopolitically turbulent ones.**

### 5.4 Volvo/Geely Ecosystem Access (VRIN: MODERATE)

"The uniqueness of Polestar is that both Geely and Volvo are key shareholders and both are also very important for our business," says Lohscheller. The Volvo relationship, in particular, provides practical infrastructure advantages that would be difficult for a standalone startup to replicate. Access to Volvo's engineering, manufacturing, and service infrastructure, and to Geely's Chinese market presence and supply chain, is a resource that Polestar's standalone competitors (Rivian, Lucid) do not possess. However, it comes with the reputational and geopolitical complications of Geely's majority ownership.

### 5.5 Financial Resources (VRIN: LOW)

Polestar is facing a critical period in which its survival will be dependent on its ability to continually raise new capital and renegotiate terms with its lenders to fund its high cash burn and bridge a significant liquidity gap. While management is actively securing new funds, the explicit statement that this financing is not guaranteed is the core of the "material uncertainty" that has been flagged. To support its growth initiatives, Polestar secured a $950 million club loan facility and aimed to raise an additional $350 million to reach its goal of $1.3 billion in external funding for fiscal year 2024. Financial fragility is not merely a resource deficit; it is a strategic constraint that limits every other strategic option.

**RBV Summary and Connection:** The RBV analysis reveals that Polestar's strongest resources — design identity and sustainability transparency — are genuinely differentiated and difficult to replicate. The strategic problem is that these resources are not yet generating sufficient financial returns to make the firm self-sustaining. This is because brand-based differentiation requires scale to generate the unit economics needed for profitability, and Polestar has not yet reached that scale. The RBV therefore points to a core strategic imperative: protect and invest in the VRIN resources while urgently addressing the financial and manufacturing vulnerabilities that prevent them from being monetised.

## 6. PORTER'S GENERIC STRATEGIES

Porter identifies three generic strategies: cost leadership, differentiation, and focus. A firm that fails to commit clearly to one risks being "stuck in the middle" — neither cost-competitive nor distinctively differentiated — which is precisely Polestar's current predicament.

Polestar's intended strategy is **focused differentiation**: targeting the premium EV segment with a distinctive design and sustainability proposition. CEO Lohscheller has stated the brand is focused on targeting the heart of the EV market where "customer demand and profit pools are high." The Polestar 5, with its 884 horsepower, positions it as a direct competitor to established luxury EVs like the Tesla Model S and Porsche Taycan — the clearest expression of this intent.

However, in practice, Polestar has been drifting toward a middle position. Revenue fell 21% for the first nine months of 2024 as global vehicle sales of the Polestar 2 declined amid higher discounts in a competitive market. A brand that competes on design and sustainability cannot simultaneously compete on price without undermining the credibility of both claims. By eschewing maximalist infotainment and flashy styling, Polestar differentiates on design purity and sustainability, appealing to buyers fatigued by tech-for-tech's-sake approaches — but this differentiation is diluted when the same brand is offering significant discounts to move volume.

**Generic Strategy Conclusion:** Polestar must recommit unambiguously to focused differentiation, which means accepting lower volumes at higher margins rather than chasing volume through discounting. This is a painful discipline for a company under cash pressure, but it is the only strategy consistent with the brand's VRIN resources. A cost leadership strategy is structurally unavailable to a sub-scale manufacturer without proprietary factories; a broad differentiation strategy requires the brand scale of BMW or Mercedes. Focused differentiation — owning the "Scandinavian premium EV" niche with absolute clarity — is the only viable generic strategy.

## 7. COHERENT STRATEGIC NARRATIVE

The four analytical frameworks converge on a single diagnosis. PESTEL reveals that tariffs and margin compression are the dominant external threats, while sustainability regulation is a structural tailwind. Five Forces confirms that the industry structure is unfavourable for sub-scale players and that Polestar must differentiate to survive. The RBV identifies design identity and sustainability transparency as genuine VRIN assets, but notes that financial fragility prevents their monetisation. Porter's Generic Strategies shows that Polestar is drifting from its intended focused differentiation strategy through volume-driven discounting.

The chain of causation is: **tariff exposure → manufacturing cost penalty → margin compression → cash burn → discounting to maintain volume → brand dilution → weakened differentiation → reduced pricing power → further margin compression.** Breaking this cycle requires simultaneous action on manufacturing geography (reducing tariff exposure), financial structure (reducing cash burn), and brand discipline (ending discounting and recommitting to premium positioning).

## 8. RECOMMENDATIONS

### Recommendation 1: Accelerate US Manufacturing Consolidation
*(Addresses: PESTEL – Political/Legal; Five Forces – Supplier Power)*

Volvo Cars and Polestar plan to consolidate the production of Polestar 3 cars at Volvo Cars' US-based plant in Ridgeville outside Charleston in South Carolina. The Polestar 3 is currently also produced in Chengdu, China. This recommendation is to treat this not as a logistical adjustment but as a strategic priority deserving accelerated timelines. **The warrant is direct:** by manufacturing vehicles locally, Polestar can potentially avoid tariffs on Chinese-made vehicles and may qualify for EV tax credits under US legislation, which could make Polestar's vehicles more competitive in terms of pricing. The same logic applies to European production: Polestar should negotiate with Volvo Cars to expand European manufacturing capacity for the Polestar 2 replacement and Polestar 4, reducing EU tariff exposure. Manufacturing localisation does not eliminate the asset-light model's advantages — Polestar still avoids owning factories — but it shifts the manufacturing geography to align with the political environment.

### Recommendation 2: Eliminate Volume-Driven Discounting and Manage the Model Transition Deliberately
*(Addresses: Generic Strategy – Focused Differentiation; RBV – Design Identity)*

Polestar should cease using price discounts as a volume lever for the Polestar 2. Polestar has run sizable discounts and subsidised finance offers on new cars, pulling new transaction prices well below MSRP. That drags used prices down, because no buyer will pay almost-new money for yesterday's software and hardware. **The warrant is that a premium differentiation strategy is self-defeating if the price signal contradicts the brand signal.** Instead, Polestar should manage the Polestar 2's decline gracefully — accepting lower volumes as it is superseded by the Polestar 3 and 4 — rather than sacrificing margin to sustain artificial volume. This is consistent with the evidence: the Q1 2025 adjusted EBITDA loss improved by USD 97 million, driven by gross margin improvement, cost savings from headcount reduction in 2024, optimised marketing spend, and positive FX impacts — and a growing share of higher-margin models is the primary driver of that gross margin improvement.

### Recommendation 3: Invest in Brand Awareness with a Coherent Focused Message
*(Addresses: RBV – Design Identity, Sustainability; Five Forces – Buyer Power)*

Brand awareness remains a key challenge for Polestar in major markets. Polestar's Super Bowl commercial brought the brand from being known by enthusiasts and those who follow the EV segment into everybody's living room, and it was described as the most recalled commercial of the Super Bowl. This demonstrates that Polestar can achieve awareness when it invests purposefully. However, cost savings from headcount reduction and optimised marketing spend suggest that awareness investment has been curtailed under financial pressure. **The warrant is that for a brand competing on differentiation rather than price, marketing investment is not discretionary: it is the mechanism by which VRIN resources (design identity, sustainability transparency) are converted into consumer preference and pricing power.** The recommendation is to concentrate marketing investment on a single, coherent message — "Scandinavian design, radical sustainability transparency" — delivered consistently across markets. The LCA publication programme, covering every vehicle in the lineup since 2020, is a unique asset that no competitor currently matches; it should be the centrepiece of brand communications, not a footnote in sustainability reports.

### Recommendation 4: Pursue Positive Adjusted EBITDA as a Non-Negotiable Near-Term Target
*(Addresses: RBV – Financial Resources; PESTEL – Economic)*

The updated business plan envisions a compound annual retail sales volume growth of 30-35% for 2025 to 2027 and a positive adjusted EBITDA in 2025. From 2026, the company expects to see improvements in its margin, fixed costs, and working capital. This recommendation endorses this target as the single most important strategic milestone, because financial viability is a precondition for every other strategic action. **The warrant is that going-concern uncertainty is not merely a financial problem — it is a strategic one, because it undermines customer confidence, supplier terms, and talent retention.** Polestar is facing a critical period in which its survival will be dependent on its ability to continually raise new capital and renegotiate terms with its lenders. The path to positive EBITDA runs through the model mix shift (more Polestar 3 and 4, less discounted Polestar 2), the headcount reductions already implemented in 2024, and the manufacturing localisation that reduces tariff costs. The Q1 2025 trajectory — gross margin improved by 15 percentage points to positive 6.8% year-on-year, net loss reduced by 31% — demonstrates that the direction is correct.

### Recommendation 5: Reframe the Geely/Volvo Relationship as a Customer-Facing Asset
*(Addresses: RBV – Ecosystem Access; PESTEL – Political)*

Volvo Cars confirmed it would remain a strategic partner in areas across R&D, manufacturing, aftersales, and commercial activities even as its equity stake reduces. Polestar should proactively communicate this relationship in terms of what it delivers for customers — Volvo's service network, engineering rigour, safety standards — rather than allowing it to be framed purely in terms of Chinese ownership. **The warrant is that in the premium segment, provenance matters: buyers of a Porsche Taycan are buying German engineering heritage; buyers of a Polestar should be buying Swedish design and engineering rigour, supported by global manufacturing scale.** The Volvo relationship provides practical infrastructure advantages that would be difficult for a standalone startup to replicate. This narrative is available to Polestar but is currently underdeveloped. It will become more important as geopolitical scrutiny of Chinese automotive investment intensifies.

## 10. CONCLUSION

Polestar is not a failing company with no strategic path forward. It possesses genuine, difficult-to-replicate assets in its design identity and sustainability transparency, a structural manufacturing advantage through its Volvo/Geely ecosystem, and a product pipeline (Polestar 3, 4, 5) better positioned for premium margins than its current volume mix. Despite continued financial and market challenges in 2025, Polestar delivered over 60,000 vehicles to customers, a 34% increase from 2024, demonstrating that the turnaround trajectory is real. But the strategic situation remains precarious because the compound tension between differentiation and financial fragility has not yet been resolved. The five recommendations above — manufacturing localisation, discounting discipline, focused brand investment, EBITDA commitment, and relationship narrative — form a coherent programme because they all address different nodes of the same causal chain identified through the four analytical frameworks. Polestar does not need to become a different company; it needs to become a financially disciplined version of the company it already intends to be.
