# Shell plc: Navigating the Strategic Tension Between Commercial Realism and Transition Legitimacy

## A Strategic Case Study and Analysis

---

## 1. INTRODUCTION AND CASE FRAMING

This case study examines the central strategic dilemma confronting Shell plc between 2023 and 2025: under CEO Wael Sawan, Shell has executed a deliberate pivot away from broad-based renewable energy investment toward a concentrated, LNG-led, high-return hydrocarbon strategy — all while maintaining a nominal commitment to net-zero by 2050. The strategic situation is not simply about energy mix; it is a deeper tension between **exploiting existing competitive advantages** (trading, LNG, integrated operations) and **building the capabilities** required for long-run legitimacy and survival in a decarbonising world. The case is analytically rich because it sits at the intersection of resource-based competitive advantage, institutional legitimacy, and dynamic capability theory. The three-part question it poses is: *Is Shell's current strategy coherent? Is it defensible? And what must it do to remain viable beyond the next decade?*

---

## 2. SITUATIONAL BACKGROUND

### 2.1 The Strategic Pivot Under Wael Sawan

When Sawan took the reins at Shell, he reversed a plan to cut absolute oil and gas production by up to 2% each year this decade that had been implemented under his predecessor, and has led the company to take a "much more selective approach" to renewables investment as part of plans to cut costs and "get leaner." Shell cut 200 jobs in its low-carbon solutions division in October 2023.

This reversal is the strategic pivot around which the entire case turns. It was not merely operational — it represented a fundamental repositioning of where Shell believes its competitive advantage lies and how it intends to create value through the energy transition.

### 2.2 The "More Value, Less Emissions" Strategy

The articulated strategy under Sawan is captured in a single phrase. Shell demonstrated that its strategy to deliver "more value with less emissions" is producing strong results and compelling shareholder returns. By the beginning of 2025, Shell had announced $3 billion or more in buybacks for 13 consecutive quarters. The financial results appear to validate this framing: cash flow from operating activities for the full year 2024 was $54.7 billion, primarily driven by Adjusted EBITDA and working capital inflows of $2.1 billion, partly offset by tax payments of $12.0 billion.

### 2.3 The Emissions Target Retreat

Yet the "less emissions" half of the strategy has been materially weakened. Shell has "chosen to retire its 2035 target of a 45% reduction in net carbon intensity" due to "uncertainty in the pace of change in the energy transition." Both goals were intended as stepping stones on the company's journey towards net-zero emissions by 2050. More specifically, Shell is now targeting a 15–20% reduction by 2030 in the net carbon intensity of the energy products it sells, compared with 2016, against its previous target of 20%.

### 2.4 The Legal and Institutional Context

The strategic situation is further complicated by ongoing litigation. On 12 November 2024, the Court of Appeal in The Hague ruled that Shell has an obligation to combat dangerous climate change. The court found that large companies that have contributed to causing dangerous climate change have a special duty to others on this planet to help combat it, and that large companies cannot hide behind regulations. While Shell won the specific legal battle — the judgment overturned a District Court order that had required Shell to reduce its net CO₂ emissions by 45% by 2030 compared with 2019 levels — the court nonetheless affirmed the principle of corporate climate obligation, leaving Shell exposed to future legal challenge.

## 3. ANALYTICAL FRAMEWORK 1: PORTER'S FIVE FORCES

The first step in understanding Shell's strategic situation is to map the competitive structure of the industry it inhabits. Porter's Five Forces provides the structural lens.

### 3.1 Rivalry Among Existing Competitors (HIGH)

Shell faces intense competition from ExxonMobil and Chevron in US upstream operations, and from BP and TotalEnergies in integrated European markets. National oil companies such as Saudi Aramco and ADNOC wield lower cost structures and political support, pressuring margins and access. The rivalry is not simply price-based; it is increasingly about who can claim the most credible energy-transition narrative while maintaining hydrocarbon cash flows — a form of strategic differentiation that goes beyond cost.

Crucially, Shell's LNG position gives it a structural advantage within this rivalry. Shell's success emphasises its dominance as the largest LNG trader globally, controlling nearly 20% of the market, while TotalEnergies, with 11%, works to enhance its position. This gap in market share — nearly double its nearest European rival — is strategically significant because it is self-reinforcing: scale in trading creates information advantages, pricing power, and customer stickiness that are difficult to replicate.

### 3.2 Threat of New Entrants (LOW-MODERATE)

The upstream and LNG segments of the oil and gas industry are protected by enormous capital requirements, long-term infrastructure commitments, and regulatory barriers. However, the energy transition introduces a new form of entry threat: technology companies, utilities, and renewable-energy pure-plays entering adjacent markets (power, EV charging, hydrogen) where Shell is attempting to build positions. This asymmetric threat — where new entrants do not compete in Shell's core markets but colonise its intended future markets — is a structural feature the Five Forces model must be extended to capture.

### 3.3 Bargaining Power of Suppliers (MODERATE)

Shell's integrated model — from upstream production through trading to retail — partially mitigates supplier power. However, Shell's customer segmentation covers industrials (power, steel, cement, aviation, marine), mobility (retail fuels, EV charging), and B2B energy solutions, with geographic and product breadth supporting diversified revenue streams and risk mitigation. The breadth of this customer base reduces buyer concentration risk but also means Shell must serve increasingly heterogeneous decarbonisation demands.

### 3.4 Threat of Substitutes (INCREASING)

The structural threat to Shell's long-run revenue base is the substitution of fossil fuels by renewables, electrification, and efficiency improvements. This threat is not immediate — gas demand in Asia is projected to reach approximately 1.2 trillion cubic metres by 2030, contributing more than half of global demand growth and peaking around 2040 at approximately 1.38 tcm — but it is directionally certain. The strategic question is whether Shell is building the capabilities to compete in the post-substitution world, or merely harvesting its existing assets.

### 3.5 Five Forces Summary

The Five Forces analysis reveals that Shell's current competitive position is structurally strong in its core markets, particularly LNG, but faces a secular substitution threat that intensifies beyond 2035. This means the strategy of maximising short-run returns from hydrocarbons is rational *if and only if* the proceeds are being reinvested in capabilities that will remain competitive after peak fossil fuel demand. The Five Forces analysis therefore creates the analytical bridge to the resource-based view.

## 4. ANALYTICAL FRAMEWORK 2: RESOURCE-BASED VIEW AND VRIO ANALYSIS

The Resource-Based View (RBV) holds that sustained competitive advantage derives from resources and capabilities that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Organised to be exploited (Barney, 1991). This framework allows us to assess whether Shell's current strategy is genuinely exploiting durable advantages or merely harvesting depreciating assets.

### 4.1 LNG Trading Capability — VRIO Assessment

**Valuable:** The most fundamental feature that distinguishes Shell from its competitors (ExxonMobil, Chevron, BP, TotalEnergies) is its unparalleled trading capability. This capability generates direct financial value and enables Shell to arbitrage price differentials across global markets in ways that pure producers or pure traders cannot.

**Rare:** Shell controls roughly 20 percent of the merchant LNG market, making it the global leader in liquefied natural gas. No other integrated oil major holds a comparable share of the global LNG trading market, making this resource genuinely rare.

**Inimitable:** The trading capability is embedded in decades of relationship-building, proprietary market intelligence, physical infrastructure (terminals, shipping, liquefaction plants), and organisational routines. Shell became one of the world's largest traders and suppliers of sustainable aviation fuel in 2024, achieving this because of its long-term agreements with producers, the strength of its customer relationships, and strategic investments in logistics around key terminals and airports. This demonstrates that the trading capability is not narrowly confined to LNG — it is a generalisable organisational competence that can be applied to new energy products. This generalisability makes it more durable and harder to imitate.

**Organised:** In 2024, Shell spent around $500 million on projects that contributed to decarbonisation, and its Integrated Gas segment continued to be organised around combining LNG, GTL, and trading capabilities. The organisational architecture is aligned with exploiting the trading capability.

**VRIO Verdict:** Shell's LNG trading capability meets all four VRIO criteria and constitutes a genuine source of sustained competitive advantage. This is the analytical foundation for the strategic pivot — Sawan is concentrating resources on the firm's most defensible competitive position.

### 4.2 Retail Network and Customer Relationships — VRIO Assessment

**Valuable and Rare:** Over 47,000 service stations provide a platform to expand EV charging and convenience services, shifting retail sites into energy hubs. This physical network represents a distribution asset that cannot be quickly replicated.

**Inimitability and Organisation:** The challenge here is that the retail network is valuable in transition (as an EV charging platform) but only if Shell invests in the organisational capabilities to convert it. The strategic risk is that Shell's current capex allocation — which has reduced Renewables and Energy Solutions spending from $1,655 million in the first three quarters of 2023 to $1,272 million in the equivalent period of 2024 — with Renewables and Energy Solutions cash capital expenditure at $1,272 million in the first three quarters of 2024, compared with $1,655 million in the equivalent period of 2023 — suggests the retail network is being maintained rather than transformed.

**VRIO Verdict:** Conditionally valuable. The retail network is a potentially inimitable asset for the energy transition, but its strategic value depends on investment decisions that are currently being deferred.

### 4.3 Upstream Deep-Water Assets — VRIO Assessment

Shell focuses on deep-water production assets that offer favourable breakeven costs and produce lower CO₂ emission barrels. These assets are valuable, relatively rare, and difficult to imitate due to the technical complexity of deep-water operations. However, they are subject to stranded-asset risk if the energy transition accelerates. Estimates suggest that as a group, the major oil and gas companies need to reduce production by 35% to 2040 to stay within their below-2-degree scenario budgets, with Shell requiring a relatively modest -10% reduction. This suggests Shell's upstream portfolio is among the more transition-resilient among the majors, which partially justifies the continued investment.

### 4.4 RBV Summary and Strategic Implication

The RBV analysis reveals a firm with one genuinely world-class, durable competitive advantage (LNG trading) and several conditionally valuable assets (retail network, deep-water upstream) whose future value depends on transition-related investment decisions being made today. The strategic logic of the Sawan pivot — concentrate on the durable advantage, divest the underperforming — is internally coherent from an RBV perspective. The critical question the RBV raises, however, is whether the *dynamic* capabilities exist to build new advantages for the post-2035 environment. This is where the analysis must extend beyond static RBV.

## 5. ANALYTICAL FRAMEWORK 3: DYNAMIC CAPABILITIES AND STRATEGIC AMBIDEXTERITY

Dynamic capabilities theory (Teece, Pisano & Shuen, 1997) argues that in rapidly changing environments, sustainable advantage requires not just possessing valuable resources but the ability to *sense, seize, and reconfigure* them. Organisational ambidexterity — the simultaneous pursuit of exploitation (current advantages) and exploration (future capabilities) — is the operationalisation of this theory in a transition context.

### 5.1 Shell's Exploitation-Exploration Imbalance

The current strategy is heavily weighted toward exploitation. The structural cost reduction programme — $3.1 billion of pre-tax structural cost reductions were delivered through 2024 compared with 2022 levels, with $2.1 billion in the full year 2024 — is an exploitation activity: it extracts more value from existing operations. Similarly, the LNG investment programme (e.g., in July 2024, Shell signed an agreement to invest in ADNOC's Ruwais LNG project in Abu Dhabi through a 10% participating interest, with a total capacity of 9.6 million tonnes per annum) deepens existing capabilities rather than building new ones.

The exploration side of the equation has been materially reduced. The retirement of the 2035 emissions target, the reduction in Renewables and Energy Solutions capex, and the job cuts in the low-carbon solutions division all signal a shift away from exploratory investment. This creates an ambidexterity deficit: Shell is optimising for today at the potential cost of tomorrow.

### 5.2 The Sensing Capability: LNG Demand Forecasting

One area where Shell does demonstrate genuine dynamic capability is in its market intelligence and scenario analysis. Global demand for LNG is projected to rise by approximately 60% by 2040, primarily fuelled by economic expansion in Asia, according to Shell's LNG Outlook 2025 report. The report highlights the increasing demand for natural gas as the world transitions to cleaner energy sources, and industry forecasts predict LNG demand will reach between 630 million tonnes per annum and 718 mtpa by 2040, surpassing last year's forecast. Shell's ability to generate and act on such forecasts — and to have those forecasts validated by independent analysts — reflects a genuine sensing capability. The strategic question is whether this sensing capability is being applied equally to emerging energy markets (hydrogen, carbon capture, power) or only to markets where Shell already has a dominant position.

### 5.3 The Reconfiguration Gap

The most significant dynamic capability gap is in reconfiguration — the ability to redeploy assets and capabilities toward new strategic positions. The SAF (sustainable aviation fuel) example is instructive: Shell paused construction of a major SAF facility, originally planned for commissioning in 2024, and the project has so far not progressed, representing a potential billion-dollar hit from the decision. This suggests that when the economics of exploratory investments become uncertain, Shell's current strategic logic defaults to exploitation. While individually rational, this pattern of behaviour — if sustained — represents a failure of the reconfiguration capability that dynamic capabilities theory identifies as essential for long-run survival in turbulent environments.

## 6. ANALYTICAL FRAMEWORK 4: STAKEHOLDER AND LEGITIMACY ANALYSIS

Strategy does not occur in a vacuum. Shell operates under intense scrutiny from a heterogeneous stakeholder environment whose demands are in direct tension with each other.

### 6.1 Shareholder Primacy vs. Societal Legitimacy

The shareholder return programme is unambiguously successful on its own terms. Shell demonstrated that its strategy to deliver more value with less emissions is producing strong results and compelling shareholder returns, with $3 billion or more in buybacks announced for 13 consecutive quarters by the beginning of 2025. This satisfies the financial stakeholder constituency.

However, institutional legitimacy — the degree to which Shell's strategy is perceived as acceptable by the broader society in which it operates — is under threat. The weakening of climate targets has drawn sharp criticism. Shell has scrapped its 2035 target just before it moved into its ten-year planning period, raising the question of what is the value of the 2050 net-zero target when Shell can scrap it before it becomes pertinent. The retired and watered-down targets will take Shell even further away from Paris alignment.

### 6.2 The Legal Legitimacy Dimension

The Milieudefensie ruling, even though Shell won the immediate case, establishes an important principle. The Appeal Court found that "companies like Shell have an obligation to limit CO₂ emissions to counter dangerous climate change, even if this obligation is not explicitly laid down in public law regulations of the countries in which the company operates." The judgment contains important takeaways regarding shared responsibility of companies and the EU due diligence directive as the decisive legal framework for the obligations and responsibilities of companies like Shell.

This is strategically significant because it means the legal risk has not been extinguished — it has been reframed. Future litigation grounded in the EU Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD) may be more difficult for Shell to defeat. The legal environment is therefore a source of long-run strategic risk that the current strategy does not adequately address.

### 6.3 The Shareholder Legitimacy Paradox

There is an internal tension even within the shareholder constituency. In 2021, 89% of Shell's shareholders voted in support of its Energy Transition Strategy. The 2024 Energy Transition Strategy revision — which weakened targets — was approved by 78% of shareholders, a meaningful decline. This suggests that even within the investor base, the retreat from climate commitments is not universally welcomed, and that a segment of Shell's own shareholders views the weakening of targets as a long-run risk rather than a short-run benefit.

## 7. SYNTHESIS: THE CORE STRATEGIC TENSION

The four analytical frameworks converge on a single, coherent diagnosis:

**Shell is executing a strategically rational short-run exploitation strategy that is creating a long-run legitimacy and capability deficit.**

- The **Five Forces** analysis shows that Shell's current competitive position is strong, but the substitution threat grows materially beyond 2035.
- The **VRIO analysis** shows that the LNG trading capability is genuinely durable, but the retail network and other transition assets are losing strategic value through underinvestment.
- The **Dynamic Capabilities** analysis shows that Shell is strong on sensing and seizing within its existing domain but weak on the reconfiguration capability needed to build new advantages.
- The **Legitimacy analysis** shows that the strategy is satisfying financial stakeholders today but eroding the institutional legitimacy that is a prerequisite for the social licence to operate in the long run.

The chain of reasoning is: *Strong short-run returns* (evidenced by $54.7bn operating cash flow and 13 consecutive buyback quarters) are being generated by *concentrating on durable competitive advantages* (LNG trading, deep-water upstream) while *reducing investment in exploratory capabilities* (renewables, SAF, hydrogen). This is coherent *if* the energy transition is slow and LNG demand holds to the 60% growth forecast. It becomes *incoherent* if the transition accelerates, regulatory pressure intensifies (via the CSDDD), or if the retreat from climate commitments triggers investor-driven capital reallocation away from Shell's equity. The strategy is therefore a high-conviction, scenario-dependent bet — and the current disclosure does not adequately acknowledge the scenario in which it fails.

## 8. RECOMMENDATIONS AND ACTION PLAN

The following recommendations are structured around three strategic imperatives, each grounded in the preceding analysis.

---

### Recommendation 1: Institutionalise Ambidexterity Through Structural Separation of Exploitation and Exploration Budgets

**The problem:** The current capital allocation process treats LNG/upstream investments and low-carbon investments as competing for the same budget, with the result that in any period of cost pressure, the exploratory investments are cut first. This is the classic exploitation trap identified in dynamic capabilities theory.

**The recommendation:** Shell should create a ring-fenced "Transition Capability Fund" — a dedicated capital allocation of at minimum $3–4 billion per annum (approximately 15% of total capex) — that is structurally insulated from short-run return-on-capital pressure. This fund should target the three areas where Shell has demonstrated a genuine capability-to-advantage pathway: (a) SAF and biofuels, where the trading capability is directly transferable; (b) industrial decarbonisation services (carbon capture, green hydrogen for hard-to-abate sectors), where Shell's B2B customer relationships provide a customer base; and (c) LNG-to-hydrogen conversion infrastructure, which bridges the existing competitive advantage into the next energy system.

**The warrant:** The RBV analysis shows that Shell's trading capability is generalisable — the SAF example demonstrates that it can be applied to new energy products. The dynamic capabilities analysis shows that the reconfiguration gap is the primary strategic vulnerability. Structural separation of budgets is the organisational mechanism that prevents exploitation from crowding out exploration (O'Reilly & Tushman, 2004). The $3–4bn figure is calibrated against the $5.6 billion Shell invested in low-carbon solutions in 2023, scaled back to a level that is financially sustainable while remaining strategically meaningful.

---

### Recommendation 2: Reframe the Climate Target Architecture Around Absolute Scope 1 and 2 Emissions, Supplemented by Customer Transition Metrics

**The problem:** Shell's current net carbon intensity (NCI) metric is both scientifically contested and strategically counterproductive. Shell scrapped its 2035 target just before it moved into its ten-year planning period, raising the question of what is the value of the 2050 net-zero target when Shell can scrap it before it becomes pertinent. The NCI metric measures the carbon intensity per unit of energy sold, which means it can improve even as absolute emissions rise (if Shell sells more gas relative to oil). This creates a credibility gap that undermines stakeholder legitimacy.

**The recommendation:** Shell should replace the NCI framework with a two-tier target architecture: (a) **Absolute Scope 1 and 2 targets** with five-year milestones that are locked into the planning cycle and cannot be retired without shareholder vote; and (b) **Customer Transition Enablement Metrics** — measuring the volume of low-carbon energy sold to customers as a percentage of total energy sold — which are forward-looking and commercially grounded rather than backward-looking and intensity-based. The Scope 3 question should be addressed through the customer enablement metric rather than an absolute target, which Shell cannot unilaterally control.

**The warrant:** The legitimacy analysis shows that the current target architecture is failing on two dimensions: it is not credible to climate-focused stakeholders (because it can be weakened), and it is not commercially meaningful to investors (because NCI is not directly linked to financial performance). The legal risk analysis shows that the CSDDD framework will increasingly require demonstrable progress on emissions reduction. A target architecture that is both locked-in and commercially grounded addresses both vulnerabilities simultaneously. The EU due diligence directive is now the decisive legal framework for the obligations and responsibilities of companies like Shell, making a robust, legally defensible target architecture a risk management imperative as much as a reputational one.

---

### Recommendation 3: Convert the Retail Network Into a Transition Infrastructure Platform Through Partnerships Rather Than Organic Investment

**The problem:** Shell's 47,000+ service stations provide a platform to expand EV charging and convenience services, shifting retail sites into energy hubs. However, the current capex reduction in the Marketing segment (from $4,406 million in the first three quarters of 2023 to $1,634 million in the equivalent period of 2024) suggests this transformation is not being funded organically at the pace required. Shell cannot afford to both maximise hydrocarbon returns and fully fund the retail transformation.

**The recommendation:** Shell should pursue a **partnership and joint venture model** for retail network transformation, bringing in specialist EV charging operators, convenience retail partners, and renewable energy suppliers as co-investors. This allows Shell to retain the asset (the land and customer relationship) while sharing the capital burden and accessing specialist capabilities it does not currently possess. Concretely, Shell should target 20,000 high-throughput stations in urban and peri-urban markets in Europe and Asia for transformation by 2030, funded through a 50/50 JV structure with established EV infrastructure partners, while divesting the remaining lower-throughput rural stations where the transition economics are unfavourable.

**The warrant:** The VRIO analysis shows that the retail network is a potentially inimitable asset, but only if the organisational capabilities to operate it as an energy hub are developed. The partnership model is the mechanism that acquires those capabilities without requiring Shell to divert capital from its core LNG advantage. This is consistent with the ambidexterity imperative (Recommendation 1): Shell should exploit its balance sheet strength and asset base while partnering to access the exploration capabilities it lacks. The geographic focus on Europe and Asia is warranted by the Five Forces analysis, which identifies these as the markets where the substitution threat is most advanced and where first-mover advantage in EV infrastructure is most valuable.

---

### Recommendation 4: Develop a Formal Scenario-Contingent Strategic Review Mechanism

**The problem:** The current strategy is implicitly a single-scenario bet on a slow energy transition and sustained LNG demand growth. The LNG demand forecast of 60% growth by 2040 is Shell's own projection — it reflects the scenario most favourable to the current strategy. There is no publicly disclosed mechanism for what Shell would do if the transition accelerated, if LNG demand peaked earlier than forecast, or if regulatory pressure (via CSDDD or carbon pricing) materially changed the economics of the hydrocarbon portfolio.

**The recommendation:** Shell should publish an annual **Scenario Stress Test Report** — separate from the Energy Transition Strategy — that models the financial and strategic implications of at least three scenarios: (a) a slow-transition scenario (current base case); (b) an accelerated-transition scenario consistent with IEA Net Zero 2050 pathways; and (c) a fragmented-transition scenario in which geopolitical fragmentation produces divergent regional energy systems. For each scenario, the report should specify the trigger indicators that would prompt a strategic reallocation, and the pre-positioned capabilities Shell would activate.

**The warrant:** Shell has a long and distinguished history of scenario planning — the Shell Scenarios methodology is widely regarded as a best-practice tool for managing strategic uncertainty. Shell has previously concluded that it foresees "low risk" of stranded assets in its fossil fuel portfolio, expecting to produce around 80% of its proved oil and gas reserves by 2030. This is a claim that needs to be stress-tested against the accelerated-transition scenario, not simply asserted. The dynamic capabilities analysis shows that sensing capability is strong within Shell's existing domain; extending it to cover scenarios in which the current strategy fails would represent a genuine enhancement of that capability. Transparency about scenario-contingent responses would also partially address the legitimacy deficit by demonstrating to stakeholders that Shell is managing long-run risk rather than ignoring it.

## 9. CONCLUSION

Shell's current strategy is analytically coherent on its own terms: it concentrates resources on a genuinely world-class, VRIO-compliant competitive advantage in LNG trading, generates exceptional short-run returns, and is supported by credible demand forecasts. The Five Forces analysis confirms the structural strength of Shell's current competitive position. The financial evidence — $3.1 billion in pre-tax structural cost reductions delivered through 2024 compared with 2022 levels and $54.7 billion in operating cash flow for full year 2024 — confirms that the strategy is being executed effectively.

However, the analysis also reveals three structural vulnerabilities that the current strategy does not adequately address: an ambidexterity deficit that is widening the gap between Shell's exploitation capabilities and its exploration capabilities; a legitimacy deficit that is eroding the institutional and legal licence to operate; and a scenario-dependency that makes the strategy brittle in the face of an accelerated energy transition.

The four recommendations — structural ambidexterity through ring-fenced transition investment, a credible and legally robust target architecture, a partnership-based retail transformation, and a formal scenario stress-test mechanism — are designed to address each of these vulnerabilities without abandoning the LNG-led commercial logic that is Shell's genuine competitive foundation. The overarching principle is that Shell does not need to choose between commercial realism and transition credibility; it needs an organisational architecture that pursues both simultaneously. That is the definition of strategic ambidexterity — and it is the capability Shell most urgently needs to build.
