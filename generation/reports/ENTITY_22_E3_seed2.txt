# **STRATEGIC CASE STUDY: PFIZER INC.**
## *Navigating the Post-COVID Transition, the Patent Cliff, and the Oncology Pivot*

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This case study analyses the strategic situation facing Pfizer Inc. (NYSE: PFE), one of the world's largest biopharmaceutical companies, as it manages a simultaneous triple challenge: the collapse of COVID-19 product revenues, an imminent multi-billion-dollar patent cliff, and the need to establish a durable growth engine in oncology. Using Porter's Five Forces, PESTEL analysis, the VRIO framework, Ansoff's Growth Matrix, and Eroom's Law as analytical lenses, the analysis demonstrates that Pfizer's $43 billion acquisition of Seagen is strategically coherent but insufficient on its own. A set of integrated recommendations follows.

## SECTION 1: COMPANY AND INDUSTRY BACKGROUND

Pfizer is a global biopharmaceutical company with a diversified portfolio spanning oncology, vaccines, internal medicine, and hospital products. Full-year 2024 revenues reached $63.6 billion, reflecting 7% year-over-year operational growth. However, this headline figure conceals structural fragility: excluding contributions from Paxlovid and Comirnaty, revenues grew 12% operationally, signalling that the non-COVID business is growing but that COVID product revenues remain a declining and unreliable base.

The broader industry context is one of structural pressure. The global biopharmaceuticals market was valued at $666.41 billion in 2025 and is projected to grow from $720.72 billion in 2026 to $1,411.48 billion by 2034, exhibiting a CAGR of 8.60%. This growth trajectory creates both opportunity and intensified rivalry, as every major incumbent and well-funded biotech is competing for the same high-value therapeutic niches.

## SECTION 2: IDENTIFICATION OF THE STRATEGIC SITUATION

Pfizer faces three interlocking problems that together constitute a strategic inflection point.

**Problem 1 — Revenue Concentration and COVID Cliff.** Pfizer's extraordinary COVID-era revenues from Comirnaty and Paxlovid masked underlying portfolio vulnerability. As those revenues normalise, the company must demonstrate that its base business can sustain scale.

**Problem 2 — The Patent Cliff.** By 2028, the company will lose exclusivity on Eliquis, Ibrance, Xtandi, and Prevnar 13. This is not a minor disruption: Eliquis and Ibrance together represented 20% of total revenue generation for the company in its full-year 2023 results. The broader industry faces the same structural pressure: the US market alone is projected to lose more than $230 billion in revenue between 2025 and 2030, with Merck and Pfizer among the most exposed.

**Problem 3 — R&D Productivity Crisis.** The industry faces a structural productivity challenge that Pfizer cannot escape. Pipeline attrition rates continue to grow, with the success rate for Phase 1 drugs plummeting to just 6.7% in 2024, compared to 10% a decade ago. This is an expression of what researchers call Eroom's Law: despite extraordinary advances in biology, chemistry, genomics, AI, and computing, the productivity of pharmaceutical R&D has been declining for decades — we are spending more, discovering less. The average return on investment for new drugs developed by large biopharma firms was around 10% in 2010 but dropped to less than 2% by 2019.

These three problems are causally linked: declining R&D productivity makes organic pipeline replenishment slow and expensive; the patent cliff erodes the revenue base needed to fund that R&D; and the post-COVID normalisation removes the financial buffer that had temporarily masked both.

## SECTION 3: EXTERNAL ENVIRONMENT ANALYSIS

### 3.1 PESTEL Analysis

**Political.** The Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) of 2022 has fundamentally altered the US pricing environment. The IRA requires Medicare to negotiate maximum fair prices for 10 drugs taking effect in 2026, 15 additional drugs for 2027 and 2028, and 20 additional drugs for 2029 and each following year. The first-round outcomes were aggressive: price reductions averaging 40 to 79 percent off list price for drugs including Eliquis, Jardiance, and Januvia established a precedent that CMS will negotiate aggressively. Pfizer's Eliquis — its top-selling product — is directly implicated. This is strategically significant because the IRA does not merely reduce prices on selected drugs; it structurally changes the expected net present value of future small-molecule drug development. Small-molecule drugs become eligible for negotiation 9 years after approval, while biologics become eligible 13 years after approval, creating a so-called "pill penalty" that distorts R&D investment incentives away from small molecules — precisely the modality that has historically dominated Pfizer's portfolio.

**Economic.** Rising clinical trial costs compound the pricing pressure. Half (49%) of all drug developers identified rising costs as the top challenge in 2024. The commercial performance of the average new launch on the market is shrinking, and this diminishing performance is coupled with rising costs per new drug approval, which encompass the expense of conducting clinical trials, prolonged development timelines, and decreasing success rates across the pipeline.

**Social.** An ageing global population is expanding the addressable market for chronic disease therapies. The global population of people aged 60 and older is expected to rise from 1.1 billion in 2023 to 1.4 billion by 2030, with this trend especially rapid in developing regions. This demographic tailwind supports long-run demand for oncology, cardiovascular, and metabolic therapies — areas where Pfizer is building presence.

**Technological.** Artificial intelligence offers a potential partial solution to Eroom's Law. AI has the potential to transform drug development by enhancing productivity across the entire development pipeline, boosting biopharmaceutical innovation, accelerating the delivery of new therapies, and fostering competition to help improve public health outcomes. The clinical evidence is striking: Phase 1 trials for AI-discovered drugs have shown success rates between 80–90%, significantly higher than the historical industry averages of 40–65%. Furthermore, one company estimated AI-accelerated drug discovery could save between $300 million and $400 million per drug, stemming from improved R&D productivity. These figures, if they hold at scale, represent a structural opportunity for companies that embed AI early.

**Environmental and Legal.** Biosimilar competition is intensifying as biologics lose exclusivity. While biosimilars have captured more than 80% of the market in two therapeutic areas, their average market share is only 40%, suggesting uneven but growing competitive pressure. A 2024 IQVIA report identified a looming "biosimilar void": 90% of biologics losing patent protection between 2025 and 2034 currently have no biosimilars in development, which paradoxically protects some of Pfizer's biologic revenue streams in the near term while signalling eventual erosion.

### 3.2 Porter's Five Forces

**Threat of New Entrants — Low to Moderate.** The barriers to entry in biopharmaceuticals are formidable: capital requirements for clinical development run into billions, regulatory approval timelines span over a decade, and established brands command physician loyalty. However, well-funded biotechs backed by venture capital represent a meaningful threat in specific niches. Biopharma venture funding was strong in 2024, with the second quarter of that year marking a notable milestone, with funding levels reaching heights not seen since prior peaks. This capital availability lowers the effective barrier for focused biotech entrants in high-value areas like oncology and rare diseases.

**Bargaining Power of Buyers — High and Rising.** The IRA has institutionalised buyer power at the government level, with Medicare now able to mandate price reductions of 40–79% on selected drugs. The first-round negotiation results established a precedent that CMS will negotiate aggressively. Commercial payers and pharmacy benefit managers also exert formulary leverage. This force is the single most important structural change in the US biopharma environment in the past decade, and it directly compresses the revenue potential of Pfizer's existing portfolio.

**Bargaining Power of Suppliers — Moderate.** Suppliers of active pharmaceutical ingredients (APIs), contract research organisations (CROs), and specialised manufacturing inputs hold some leverage, particularly for complex biologics and ADCs. However, large incumbents like Pfizer can internalise manufacturing capabilities and diversify suppliers, moderating this force.

**Threat of Substitutes — High.** Generic and biosimilar entry following patent expiry constitutes the most direct substitution threat. For small-molecule drugs, the transition to generic competition typically precipitates a revenue collapse of 90% or more within months of multi-source generic entry. The simultaneous loss of exclusivity of Keytruda, Opdivo, and Eliquis represents the largest concentration of revenue risk in pharma history, exceeding $236 billion, necessitating aggressive lifecycle management and pipeline replenishment.

**Competitive Rivalry — Intense.** The oncology and specialty pharma segments where Pfizer competes are fiercely contested. Top competitors in oncology and specialty care include Roche, Merck, Pfizer, and Bristol Myers Squibb in various indications. The resurgence of biopharma M&A in 2025 marked one of the most decisive strategic shifts the life sciences sector has witnessed in a decade, with pharmaceutical majors returning to the acquisition table with renewed urgency, driven by pharmaceutical patent strategy — the structured acquisition of assets backed by deep intellectual property. Every major competitor is pursuing the same solution (M&A-driven pipeline replenishment), which means acquisition targets are expensive and the strategic benefits of any individual deal are quickly competed away.

**Five Forces Summary.** The overall industry structure is unfavourable for incumbents: buyer power has been institutionally strengthened by the IRA; substitutes (generics and biosimilars) are structurally inevitable; rivalry is intensifying as all majors chase the same scarce innovative assets. This analysis warrants the conclusion that Pfizer cannot rely on its existing portfolio to sustain revenue at scale — it must continuously regenerate its pipeline faster than the patent cliff erodes it.

## SECTION 4: INTERNAL ANALYSIS — VRIO FRAMEWORK

The VRIO framework (Barney, 1991) evaluates whether a firm's resources and capabilities are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Organised to capture value, to determine whether they confer sustained competitive advantage, temporary advantage, or merely competitive parity.

| Resource / Capability | Valuable? | Rare? | Inimitable? | Organised? | Competitive Implication |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Global commercial infrastructure | ✓ | ✗ | ✗ | ✓ | Competitive parity |
| mRNA vaccine platform (COVID-era) | ✓ | ✓ | Partially | ✓ | Temporary advantage |
| ADC platform (Seagen) | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | Developing | **Potential sustained advantage** |
| Financial scale / balance sheet | ✓ | ✗ | ✗ | ✓ | Competitive parity |
| Regulatory expertise & FDA relationships | ✓ | Partially | Partially | ✓ | Temporary advantage |
| Oncology clinical development capability | ✓ | ✓ | Partially | ✓ | Temporary advantage |

**ADC Platform — the Critical Resource.** The Seagen acquisition was explicitly justified on the basis of this resource. Pfizer's acquisition of Seagen for $43 billion was designed to enhance Pfizer's position as a leading company in oncology, with Seagen's medicines, late-stage development programs, and pioneering expertise in antibody-drug conjugates strongly complementing Pfizer's oncology portfolio. Pfizer projected that Seagen was expected to contribute more than $10 billion in risk-adjusted revenues in 2030. The early evidence supports this thesis: the acquired Seagen products contributed meaningfully to Pfizer's revenues in 2024 and in the first quarter of 2025, with Pfizer's oncology revenues growing 7% on an operational basis in the first quarter of 2025, driven by drugs like Xtandi, Lorbrena, the Braftovi-Mektovi combination, and Padcev.

The VRIO logic here is explicit: ADC manufacturing is technically complex (inimitability), Seagen's platform is not widely replicated at the same scale (rarity), and oncology is a high-value therapeutic area (value). The remaining question — whether Pfizer is organised to capture the value — is addressed by the integration of Seagen's talent and manufacturing into Pfizer's commercial infrastructure. Full-year 2024 revenues included $3.4 billion in revenue from the legacy Seagen portfolio.

**Weakness — Small-Molecule Portfolio Vulnerability.** Pfizer's traditional strength in small molecules is being structurally undermined by the IRA's "pill penalty." The IRA's 9-year negotiation window for small molecules has caused a 70% drop in funding for the sector, forcing R&D teams to pivot toward large-molecule therapies. This means that Pfizer's small-molecule assets — including Eliquis and Ibrance — are neither rare nor inimitable once they lose exclusivity, and the IRA accelerates the erosion of their value. The VRIO conclusion is that these assets will transition from temporary advantage to competitive disadvantage as the patent cliff materialises.

## SECTION 5: GROWTH STRATEGY ANALYSIS — ANSOFF MATRIX

Ansoff's (1957) Product/Market Growth Matrix provides a framework for categorising Pfizer's current and potential strategic moves across four quadrants.

**Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets).** Pfizer is pursuing this through commercial execution discipline and the cost realignment programme. Pfizer achieved its goal of $4.0 billion in net cost savings through 2024 and anticipates an additional $500 million in savings in 2025 from the ongoing cost realignment programme. The manufacturing optimisation programme is on track to deliver initial net cost savings in the latter part of 2025, toward the goal of improving gross margin performance. These cost savings are necessary but insufficient: they protect margins on existing products but do not address the revenue erosion from patent expiries.

**Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets).** Geographic expansion into emerging markets and label expansion for existing products (e.g., new indications for Padcev or Adcetris) represents this quadrant. The demographic tailwind in emerging markets — particularly the growing aged population — supports this strategy, but regulatory complexity and pricing pressure in those markets limit near-term revenue upside.

**Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets).** This is Pfizer's primary strategic bet. The Seagen ADC pipeline and the obesity drug danuglipron both represent product development plays. On obesity: Pfizer plans to conduct dose optimisation studies in the second half of 2024 evaluating multiple doses of the preferred modified-release formulation to inform the registration-enabling studies for danuglipron, an oral GLP-1 receptor agonist. The market rationale is compelling: the global obesity treatment market is projected to reach $173.5 billion by 2031, and Pfizer's strategy is to differentiate via oral administration in a market dominated by injectable therapies from Eli Lilly and Novo Nordisk.

**Diversification (New Products, New Markets).** Pfizer's entry into obesity via danuglipron also has elements of diversification, as it enters a therapeutic area where Pfizer has no established market position. This is the highest-risk quadrant of the Ansoff matrix, and the clinical and competitive risks are real: Pfizer previously discontinued an earlier twice-daily formulation of danuglipron due to tolerability concerns, and the injectable GLP-1 market leaders have enormous first-mover advantages.

**Ansoff Summary.** Pfizer's portfolio of strategic moves spans all four quadrants, which reflects appropriate hedging given the scale of the patent cliff. However, the moves are not equally weighted by risk-adjusted value: the ADC product development strategy has the most immediate and defensible revenue potential, while the obesity diversification play is high-risk, high-reward.

**Connection to analysis.** This recommendation addresses the Eroom's Law problem identified in Section 2, improves the VRIO position (adding a rare, inimitable AI capability), and supports the product development quadrant of the Ansoff matrix by improving the probability that new products actually reach market.

### Recommendation 4: Manage the IRA Impact Through Portfolio Rebalancing Toward Biologics and Rare Diseases (Medium-Term Priority)

**Rationale.** The IRA's "pill penalty" structurally disadvantages Pfizer's small-molecule portfolio. Small-molecule drugs become eligible for Medicare price negotiation after just 9 years, compared to 13 years for biologics, meaning that new small-molecule launches will have a shorter period of full pricing power. The ITIF has found that these price controls reduce the incentives for biopharmaceutical innovation and thus reduce US biopharmaceutical production, making the implementation of the IRA particularly problematic since small-molecule drugs are at the forefront of new drug development.

**Action.** Pfizer should explicitly rebalance its R&D investment portfolio toward biologics (monoclonal antibodies, ADCs, bispecifics) and rare disease therapies (which benefit from orphan drug pricing power and smaller, more manageable clinical trial requirements). The FDA data supports this: the FDA approved 50 new drugs in 2024, with 48% as first-in-class and 52% targeting rare diseases, confirming that rare disease is a viable and active approval pathway. Pfizer should also pursue lifecycle management strategies for its remaining small-molecule assets — such as combination therapies, new formulations, and paediatric extensions — to extend exclusivity periods beyond the IRA's 9-year clock.

**Connection to analysis.** This recommendation responds directly to the PESTEL finding that the IRA is the most significant political/regulatory force reshaping the industry's economics, and to the VRIO finding that Pfizer's small-molecule assets are transitioning from temporary advantage to competitive disadvantage.

### Recommendation 5: Pursue the Obesity/Metabolic Market as a Long-Term Diversification Bet, but with Disciplined Stage-Gating (Long-Term Priority)

**Rationale.** The obesity market represents a genuine diversification opportunity. The global obesity treatment market is projected to reach $173.5 billion by 2031, and Pfizer's oral GLP-1 strategy (danuglipron) offers a potential differentiation advantage against injectable competitors. However, this is the highest-risk quadrant of the Ansoff matrix. The opportunity cost of pursuing a losing obesity strategy — in terms of capital and management attention — is too high given the patent cliff pressures. The obesity bet should be funded from cost savings, not from the oncology investment budget.

**Connection to analysis.** This recommendation applies the Ansoff diversification logic (high risk, high reward, requires explicit risk management), and is constrained by the VRIO finding that Pfizer has no established competitive advantage in metabolic diseases, and by the Five Forces finding that the obesity market has powerful incumbents with entrenched positions.
