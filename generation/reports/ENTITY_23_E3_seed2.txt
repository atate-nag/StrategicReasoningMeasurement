# Strategic Case Study: Manchester United FC — The Debt-Performance Trap and the Path to Sustainable Competitiveness

---

## 1. Introduction and Case Overview

Manchester United Football Club occupies a paradoxical position in world sport: it is simultaneously one of the most commercially powerful brands on the planet and a club in acute strategic crisis. Through its 141-year heritage, the club has won 66 trophies, including a record 20 English league titles, and has developed what it describes as "one of the world's leading sports brands" with a global community of 1.1 billion fans and followers. Yet this brand equity sits atop a crumbling sporting and financial foundation. The club's 2024/25 accounts showed record revenues of £666.5 million, yet the club still made an overall loss of £33 million. Under Ruben Amorim, appointed in November 2024, the club oversaw its lowest Premier League finish in 51 years, ending the 2024–25 season in 15th place with just 42 points.

This case study analyses the **strategic situation facing Manchester United**, framed as a **debt-performance trap**: a self-reinforcing cycle in which legacy debt obligations crowd out investment in football operations, producing sporting underperformance, which in turn depresses revenues, which further constrains investment. The analysis uses the **Resource-Based View (RBV)**, **Porter's Five Forces**, a **PESTLE** scan, and an **Ansoff Matrix** to diagnose the situation, before presenting a structured set of strategic recommendations.

## 2. Background: The Structural Origins of the Crisis

### 2.1 The Glazer Leveraged Buyout

The roots of United's current predicament lie in the 2005 takeover. The debt taken on by the Glazers to finance the takeover was split between the club and the family; between £265 million and £275 million was secured against Manchester United's assets, putting the club into debt for the first time since James Gibson saved them in 1931. This is a textbook leveraged buyout (LBO): acquisition debt placed on the acquired entity rather than the acquirer. While debt-funded acquisition facilitated rapid revenue expansion and commercial success, the attendant interest burdens — totalling £834 million to date — have diverted resources away from crucial football operations and infrastructure, with commercial priorities designed to service debt.

The financial extraction did not stop with debt service. The Glazer family will have pocketed more than £1.3 billion from share sales and dividend payments at Manchester United, while around £1 billion has been sucked out of the club in debt repayment, interest, and management fees to service the leveraged buyout. In essence, the club has been paying interest of £30 million-plus every season not to build a new stadium, ramp up the wage bill, or make new signings, but for the luxury of having the Glazers as owners. United's total debt has meanwhile risen to £1.3 billion.

The governance structure entrenched this dynamic. Manchester United's ownership became even more complex in December 2023 when Sir Jim Ratcliffe and his company INEOS purchased a 27.7% stake — a deal officially completed in January 2024. This partial takeover created one of the most unusual arrangements in world football: the Glazers retain majority control, while INEOS now runs all football operations. The dual-class share structure means that despite INEOS's operational control, the Glazers' equity position gives them structural veto power over capital allocation decisions, including the stadium redevelopment — the single largest investment the club needs to make.

### 2.2 The Consequence: Chronic Underinvestment and Sporting Decline

The financial drain produced a vicious cycle. Sir Jim Ratcliffe revealed that "the club has been spending more money than it has been earning for the last seven years," and that "at the end of 2025, Manchester United would have run out of cash." This is the clearest possible statement that the club's operating model was structurally unsustainable. The sporting consequences are severe: the 2023–24 campaign under Erik ten Hag saw United finish the season with just 60 points, their second-worst-ever total in the Premier League era; in 2024–25, things deteriorated further, with United amassing just 42 points — the worst season in 51 years.

## 3. External Environment Analysis: PESTLE

**Political/Regulatory:** The Premier League's Profitability and Sustainability Rules (PSR) will be scrapped in favour of Squad-Cost Rules (SCR), which limit clubs to spending no more than 85% of their football revenue and net profit or loss from transfers. This regulatory shift is material for United: it caps the wage and transfer spend of loss-making clubs, directly constraining the investment needed to break the sporting decline cycle. Clubs that cannot generate Champions League revenue face a tightening regulatory ceiling on squad investment.

**Economic:** The club's debt structure creates an acute interest-rate and currency exposure. United's senior secured notes are denominated in USD, and the club recorded a foreign exchange gain of £26 million in Q4 2024/25 driven by the weakening of the USD. This gain is the mirror image of an FX risk: dollar-denominated debt becomes more expensive when sterling weakens, directly increasing the club's financial obligations independent of sporting performance.

**Social/Cultural:** Fan sentiment is a strategic asset under pressure. The Glazers plunged the club into debts of around £600 million, and four years after the takeover had racked up £90 million in dividends — leaving fans feeling betrayed. Fan alienation is not merely reputational; it threatens matchday revenue, merchandise sales, and the social licence to command premium sponsorship rates. The formation of FC United of Manchester in 2005 as a protest club illustrates the depth of this fracture.

**Technological:** The digitisation of media rights and direct-to-consumer streaming represents both a threat and an opportunity. The Premier League's broadcasting rights for the 2024/25 season alone were estimated at approximately 3.4 billion euros. United's in-house media channel, MUTV, remains underdeveloped relative to this potential: MUTV revenue fell by £0.4 million (7.1%) to £5.8 million in 2024/25. This is a negligible contribution for a club with 1.1 billion followers.

**Legal:** The dual-class share structure and the complex Glazer/INEOS governance arrangement create legal and fiduciary ambiguities that slow strategic decision-making — particularly on the stadium project.

**Environmental:** The proposed new 100,000-seat stadium carries significant planning and environmental considerations. The future of a rail freight terminal on the land adjacent to Old Trafford is reportedly a key sticking point for members of the Man Utd board. This is not merely a logistical issue; it represents a material constraint on the club's most important long-term revenue lever.

## 4. Industry Analysis: Porter's Five Forces

**Threat of New Entrants (Low–Medium):** The barriers to entry in elite football are extremely high — stadium infrastructure, broadcast rights, established fan bases, and UEFA coefficient rankings all create durable moats. However, state-backed ownership models (Manchester City, Newcastle United, PSG) represent a form of "entrant" that can circumvent normal capital constraints, effectively lowering the competitive barrier for well-resourced new actors.

**Bargaining Power of Suppliers (High):** Elite players and their agents hold exceptional bargaining power in a market with a fixed supply of world-class talent. Manchester United's wage bill stood at £313 million in 2024/25, down from £365 million the previous year, partly driven by lower performance bonuses. Even after cost-cutting, this represents the club's single largest operating cost. Agent influence over transfer decisions has been identified as a structural problem: an internal audit identified excessive over-spending, indecision that led to inflated transfer fees, and a flawed policy of extending player contracts simply to avoid players becoming free agents.

**Bargaining Power of Buyers (Low–Medium):** Individual fans have limited direct bargaining power, but their collective behaviour — attendance, merchandise spend, streaming subscriptions — determines matchday and commercial revenue. The emergence of fan ownership models and regulatory pressure on ticket pricing suggests this force is strengthening over time.

**Threat of Substitutes (Medium):** Alternative entertainment forms (streaming, gaming, other sports) compete for fan attention and leisure spend. More specifically within football, the Europa League is a direct substitute for Champions League participation — but a financially inferior one. United's participation in the Europa League rather than the Champions League in 2024/25 reduced UEFA-linked revenue by £22.7 million (42.2%) to £31.1 million.

**Competitive Rivalry (Very High):** The Premier League's financial model concentrates the most intense rivalry at the top. The key insight here is that United's competitive position has deteriorated relative to its direct peers. Commercial revenue rose to £333 million in 2024/25, a 10% year-on-year increase — estimated to be the third highest in the Premier League. However, commercial revenue was just 23% higher than in 2018/19. In contrast, rivals have accelerated sharply over the same period — Manchester City up 60%, Liverpool similarly. This relative stagnation means United is losing commercial ground even as its absolute revenue grows.

## 5. Internal Analysis: Resource-Based View (RBV)

Barney's (1991) RBV framework holds that sustainable competitive advantage derives from resources that are **Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN)**. Applying this to United reveals a stark divergence between the club's brand resources (which remain VRIN) and its operational resources (which have been systematically eroded).

### 5.1 Valuable and Rare Resources (Brand and Fan Base)

United's global brand is its most powerful strategic asset. Its influence in the global sport marketplace can be seen through the size of the international audience: 1.1 billion fans and followers worldwide, and a customer relationship management database of more than 36.1 million records. This fan base is the foundation of United's commercial model. Total sponsorship revenue for 2024/25 was £188.4 million, underpinned by a segmentation sponsorship strategy that allows corporations to associate with the Manchester United brand and a global marketing platform. The Adidas partnership exemplifies the brand's commercial leverage: at a reported £90 million per year, it is comfortably the biggest kit deal in the Premier League, eclipsing Manchester City's deal with Puma at £65 million.

**RBV Verdict:** The brand is Valuable (it generates premium commercial revenues), Rare (no other English club commands comparable global reach), Inimitable (built over 141 years of history and success), and Non-substitutable (no alternative asset replicates it). It is a genuine source of sustainable competitive advantage — but one that is being progressively eroded by sporting underperformance.

### 5.2 The Critical Vulnerability: Brand Erosion Through Sporting Decline

The RBV analysis reveals a dynamic threat: United's brand advantage is conditional on maintaining minimum sporting standards. This conditionality is now contractually embedded. United's own SEC filing states that the club's failure to qualify for the 2025–26 Champions League triggered a £10 million deduction from the minimum annual guarantee in the Adidas contract. The brand is not merely weakened by poor results in reputational terms — it is directly monetarily penalised. This creates a feedback loop: underperformance reduces revenue, which constrains squad investment, which perpetuates underperformance.

### 5.3 Eroded Resources: Football Operations

The club's football operations capabilities have been degraded by years of poor governance. The club left it late to pursue Benjamin Sesko, who joined Leipzig from Red Bull Salzburg in July 2023; even live interest in Cody Gakpo smacked of negligence, as it could have been resolved early in the window. A further question arises: why was the manager leading the transfer approach? This points to a structural failure in the separation of sporting and managerial decision-making — a failure that produced a decade of expensive, poorly targeted signings. United paid a then-world record £89 million fee to re-sign Paul Pogba in 2016; he won only the Europa League and League Cup in his six-year stay, with persistent injuries and inconsistency defining his tenure.

### 5.4 Conditional Resource: The Academy

The academy represents a potentially VRIN resource that has been under-utilised. Marcus Rashford, Alejandro Garnacho, Kobbie Mainoo, and Scott McTominay have all graduated from Carrington in recent years; since 1937, over 250 players from the academy have made appearances in more than 4,000 matches. The academy is Valuable (it produces first-team players), Rare (few academies have this historical output), Inimitable (built on decades of youth development culture), and Non-substitutable (no transfer market alternative replicates the cost-efficiency and cultural alignment of homegrown players). However, its strategic potential has been systematically underutilised by a transfer strategy that prioritised expensive external signings over internal development.

## 6. Strategic Diagnosis: The Debt-Performance Trap

The analyses above converge on a single diagnostic framework: Manchester United is caught in a **debt-performance trap** — a self-reinforcing negative cycle with three interlocking components.

**Component 1 — Financial Drain:** The Glazer LBO imposed structural debt servicing costs that have consumed over £834 million in interest payments since 2005. The club has been paying interest of over £30 million per season simply to maintain the Glazers as owners, while total debt has risen to £1.3 billion. This drain crowded out investment in the squad, stadium, and infrastructure.

**Component 2 — Sporting Decline:** Underinvestment in football operations, combined with dysfunctional transfer governance, produced a decade of sporting deterioration. United broke the record for fewest wins (10), fewest goals (42), fewest points (39), and most defeats (17) in a single Premier League season in 2024–25. This is not cyclical underperformance; it is structural decline.

**Component 3 — Revenue Compression:** Sporting underperformance directly compresses revenue, which tightens the financial constraint on investment. The Champions League absence cost £22.7 million in UEFA distributions alone, plus a £10 million Adidas penalty — a combined £32.7 million revenue reduction directly attributable to sporting failure. The winner of the Europa League final was guaranteed a $100 million payday through Champions League qualification. United lost that final to Tottenham, compounding the cycle.

The trap is self-sealing: debt reduces investment → investment shortfall produces poor results → poor results reduce revenue → reduced revenue tightens investment capacity → repeat.

## 7. Strategic Options: Ansoff Matrix

The Ansoff Matrix provides a framework for evaluating United's growth options across two dimensions: existing vs. new markets, and existing vs. new products/services.

**Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets):** Deepening revenue extraction from the existing fan base — through improved matchday experience, premium ticketing, and digital engagement — is the lowest-risk option. The constraint is Old Trafford itself: the stadium's ageing infrastructure limits matchday revenue per head relative to newer stadia. A survey of Manchester United season ticket holders and members revealed that 52% would prefer a new stadium over a renovated Old Trafford. Market penetration is therefore constrained by a physical asset bottleneck.

**Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets):** United's 1.1 billion global followers represent a massive undermonetised audience in Asia, North America, and Africa. The club's commercial revenue growth of just 23% since 2018/19 — compared to Manchester City's 60% — suggests significant headroom. This is the highest-return, medium-risk option: the brand asset is already in place; the execution capability is what requires investment.

**Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets):** Developing new revenue streams for the existing fan base — enhanced digital content, direct-to-consumer media, gaming partnerships, and fan tokens — is a medium-risk option. The current MUTV revenue of £5.8 million is negligible relative to the potential of a 1.1 billion-strong audience. The Snapdragon shirt sponsorship with Qualcomm, which drove commercial revenue growth of 10% in 2024/25, is an example of successful product development in the sponsorship space.

**Diversification (New Products, New Markets):** This is the highest-risk quadrant and is not strategically appropriate for United's current situation. The club's priority must be to stabilise and strengthen its core football business before diversifying.

**Strategic conclusion from Ansoff:** The optimal path combines **market penetration** (unlocked by stadium redevelopment) and **market development** (monetising the global fan base more effectively), supported by targeted **product development** in digital media.

## 8. Recommendations and Action Plan

The following recommendations are sequenced to address the debt-performance trap systematically, from the most urgent financial stabilisation measures to longer-term structural reforms.

### Recommendation 1: Resolve the Governance Deadlock on Stadium Redevelopment (Urgent, 0–18 months)

**The evidence:** Old Trafford is the single most important revenue bottleneck. A new 100,000-seat stadium has been proposed, with first images published in September 2024. It was reported that the new stadium could bring in £7.3 billion each year to the UK economy, and the Mayor of Greater Manchester, Andy Burnham, said no public money would fund the stadium. The rail freight terminal dispute is the primary sticking point. **The warrant:** Resolving this deadlock is prerequisite to unlocking matchday revenue growth, which is the most direct mechanism for improving the club's financial position without increasing debt. The Glazer governance structure — which gives the majority owners effective veto power over capital decisions — must be addressed. INEOS should negotiate a formal capital allocation agreement that ring-fences stadium investment from Glazer dividend extraction. Without this, all other recommendations are constrained.

### Recommendation 2: Accelerate the Transition to Executive-Led, Data-Driven Recruitment (Urgent, 0–12 months)

**The evidence:** The internal audit identified excessive overspending, inflated fees, and manager-led transfer decisions as structural failures. The audit identified excessive over-spending, indecision that led to inflated transfer fees, and a flawed policy of extending player contracts simply to avoid players becoming free agents. **The warrant:** The RBV analysis shows that football operations capability is United's most critically eroded resource. Rebuilding it requires separating transfer authority from managerial influence, investing in analytical scouting infrastructure, and implementing a coherent squad-building philosophy aligned to a multi-year plan. The appointment of a Director of Football with genuine authority — not merely advisory status — is the institutional mechanism for this change.

### Recommendation 3: Prioritise Academy-to-First-Team Pipeline as a Cost-Efficient Talent Strategy (Medium-term, 12–36 months)

**The evidence:** Since 1937, over 250 players from the Carrington academy have made appearances in more than 4,000 matches, with recent graduates including Rashford, Garnacho, Mainoo, and McTominay. **The warrant:** Under the new Squad Cost Ratio rules, clubs are limited to spending no more than 85% of their football revenue and net profit or loss from transfers. Homegrown players do not count against this ratio in the same way as external signings, and their amortisation cost is zero. Prioritising academy graduates is therefore both a sporting and a financial strategy: it reduces amortisation costs (currently £193 million per year and rising), improves squad cost ratios, and produces players culturally aligned with the club. The academy is a VRIN resource currently being underutilised; its strategic elevation is both necessary and achievable.

### Recommendation 4: Monetise the Global Fan Base Through Digital Investment (Medium-term, 12–36 months)

**The evidence:** Commercial revenue was just 23% higher in 2024/25 than in 2018/19, while Manchester City's grew 60% over the same period. Yet United has a larger global fan base than any rival. MUTV generated just £5.8 million in revenue in 2024/25. **The warrant:** The gap between United's fan base size and its digital revenue is a strategic failure of execution, not of asset quality. The club should invest in a direct-to-consumer digital platform — a subscription-based service offering exclusive content, behind-the-scenes access, interactive experiences, and localised content in key markets (Asia, North America). The commercial revenue differential with Manchester City demonstrates the scale of the opportunity: closing even half the gap would add tens of millions of pounds annually. This recommendation is consistent with the Ansoff market development strategy and requires no additional debt.

### Recommendation 5: Manage the Debt Structure Actively to Reduce Annual Interest Burden (Ongoing)

**The evidence:** The club pays interest of over £30 million per season on debt that traces back to the Glazer LBO, with total debt now at £1.3 billion. The club's senior secured notes are denominated in USD, creating currency risk: a £26 million FX gain in Q4 2024/25 reflected USD weakness, but this is equally a source of potential loss. **The warrant:** Active debt management — refinancing USD-denominated notes into sterling to eliminate currency risk, and using Champions League revenue (now secured for 2025–26) to reduce principal — would directly lower the annual interest charge. Every £10 million reduction in annual interest payments is equivalent to a mid-tier transfer signing in terms of resource freed for football investment. This is a necessary but not sufficient condition for breaking the debt-performance trap.

### Recommendation 6: Establish Champions League Qualification as the Non-Negotiable Sporting KPI (Ongoing)

**The evidence:** The financial consequences of Champions League absence are now contractually embedded across multiple revenue streams: the Adidas contract includes a £10 million deduction for each season without Champions League participation. UEFA distributions fall by over £22 million when competing in the Europa League rather than the Champions League. The Europa League winner receives a $100 million financial windfall through Champions League qualification. **The warrant:** Champions League qualification is not merely a sporting aspiration; it is a financial covenant. Every season outside the Champions League costs the club approximately £30–35 million in direct revenue losses, before accounting for the indirect effects on commercial partner negotiations, player recruitment attractiveness, and brand valuation. The sporting and financial strategies must therefore be explicitly aligned around this single KPI. All squad investment decisions, managerial appointments, and loan/sale decisions should be evaluated against their contribution to Champions League qualification probability.

## 9. Conclusion

Manchester United's strategic situation is defined by a debt-performance trap with structural roots in the 2005 leveraged buyout. The PESTLE analysis reveals a tightening regulatory environment (SCR rules), embedded financial risks (USD debt, currency exposure), and eroding fan sentiment. Porter's Five Forces analysis shows that competitive rivalry has intensified precisely as United's relative position has weakened. The RBV analysis identifies the global brand as a genuine VRIN asset — but one whose value is conditionally dependent on sporting performance, and whose commercial monetisation has fallen dramatically behind rivals. The Ansoff Matrix points to market development (digital monetisation of the global fan base) and market penetration (stadium redevelopment) as the priority growth vectors.

The recommendations form a coherent chain: resolve the governance deadlock on the stadium (unlocking matchday revenue); rebuild football operations capability through executive-led recruitment (stopping the talent investment waste); elevate the academy (reducing squad costs under SCR); invest in digital monetisation (closing the commercial gap with rivals); actively manage the debt structure (freeing cash for investment); and align all decisions around Champions League qualification (the revenue covenant that holds the entire model together). Each step is warranted by the evidence, and each connects to the others: none is sufficient alone, but together they constitute a credible path out of the debt-performance trap.
