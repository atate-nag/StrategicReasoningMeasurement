# Mercedes-Benz at the Crossroads: A Strategic Case Study

## The Electrification Gamble, the China Crisis, and the Path to Profitable Luxury

---

## 1. Introduction and Case Overview

Mercedes-Benz Group AG stands as one of the most iconic luxury automotive brands in history, yet the period 2022–2025 represents one of the most strategically turbulent chapters in its modern existence. The company simultaneously faces three interlocking strategic crises: a failed all-electric pivot that has damaged short-term financial performance; a structural deterioration of its most critical single market — China — driven by technologically aggressive local competitors; and a cost structure that is increasingly misaligned with a rapidly shifting competitive landscape.

Battery-electric car sales fell 23% in 2024, with the company selling only 185,100 electric vehicles last year, reflecting the profound challenges it faces in the electric vehicle market. Simultaneously, for 2024, the Mercedes-Benz Group reported revenue of EUR 145.6 billion (down 4.5% year-on-year), EBIT of EUR 13.6 billion (down 30.8% year-on-year), and net profit of EUR 10.4 billion (down 28.4% year-on-year).

This case study constructs a coherent analytical chain — from macro-environmental scanning (PESTEL), through industry-level forces (Porter's Five Forces), to internal capability assessment (SWOT and Value Chain Analysis), and finally to growth strategy evaluation (Ansoff Matrix) — to diagnose the root causes of Mercedes-Benz's strategic predicament and generate a prioritised, evidence-based set of recommendations.

## 2. Situational Background: The Strategic Inflection Point

### 2.1 The All-Electric Promise and Its Retreat

In 2021, Mercedes-Benz made one of the boldest strategic declarations in automotive history. In July 2021, the Mercedes-Benz Group announced that "by the end of the 2020s, all new cars sold by the Mercedes-Benz Group (in markets where possible) will be EV-only." CEO Ola Källenius reinforced this in 2022, declaring the company was "going all-in on electric," with all new vehicle architectures from 2025 to be electric-only.

By 2024, that commitment had dramatically unravelled. In February 2024, the company revised that plan. The pace of electrification and the transition to all-electric BEVs will be determined by customer and market conditions, so the company will be tactically flexible and will continue to meet the needs for internal combustion engine vehicles well into the 2030s. CEO Källenius publicly admitted to a "course correction," retaining internal combustion engines longer than initially planned. After planning to become fully electric by the end of the decade, Källenius now says the maker of the S-Class sedan will continue to make combustion-engine and hybrid vehicles "well into the 2030s," if demand is there.

The scale of the miscalculation is illustrated by the abandonment of key infrastructure: the suspected development stop of the MB.EA-Large platform is said to be part of a larger strategic reorientation, the background of which was a misjudgement by Group CEO Ola Källenius, who assumed sales of e-cars should increase more quickly.

### 2.2 The China Deterioration

China, which accounts for over 30% of global sales, has become a battleground rather than a profit engine. Germany's automakers are struggling in the world's biggest auto market, where local manufacturers led by BYD Co. are taking over. They are also contending with weak demand for EVs in Europe after several countries reduced subsidies. Sales of top-end cars like the S-Class and Maybach sedans and sport utility vehicles fell 14% in 2024.

The competitive threat from Chinese brands has moved decisively upmarket. Brands such as BYD, NIO, and Li Auto have successfully captured market share by offering "tech-laden" premium vehicles at aggressive price points. The threat is now reaching into ultra-luxury: during this period, several Chinese car companies have successively launched models with a starting price of over 700,000 yuan, directly targeting traditional ultra-luxury products. From January to August, the sales of the Mercedes-Benz S-Class sedan were only 78 units, a year-on-year decline of 92.4%.

### 2.3 Financial Deterioration

Revenue fell to €145.6 billion (down 5.0% from FY 2023). Net income fell to €10.2 billion (down 28% from FY 2023). Profit margin dropped to 7.0%, down from 9.3% in FY 2023. The automotive division's adjusted return on sales fell sharply, and the company forecasts its automotive margin to fall to around 6% to 8% in 2025, down from 12.6% in 2023.

## 3. PESTEL Analysis: The Macro-Environmental Forces

A PESTEL analysis surfaces the external pressures that define the boundaries within which Mercedes-Benz must manoeuvre.

### Political
Tariffs and trade regulations are a critical factor for Mercedes-Benz, which as a global brand relies on smooth international trade. Any new tariffs or trade barriers imposed can increase the cost of importing or exporting cars and parts, affecting pricing strategy and profitability. The EU–China trade tensions are particularly acute, with Chinese EV tariffs and retaliatory measures creating a bifurcated competitive environment. Europe is the main battleground because tensions between China and the US all but shut out expansion there, and Chinese carmakers are desperate for sales amid economic struggles and intense competition at home.

### Economic
The macroeconomic environment has compressed luxury demand. Rising interest rates increased the cost of vehicle financing, dampening consumer appetite. The adjusted EBIT from financial services was impacted mainly due to a reduced interest margin resulting from interest rate developments and increased competition in the financial services sector, especially in China. In China specifically, the luxury slowdown has been structural rather than cyclical, as local brands have eroded the status premium that European marques once commanded.

### Sociocultural
Consumer preferences are shifting in two directions simultaneously: towards electrification among younger, environmentally conscious buyers, and towards technology-rich, software-defined vehicles. Sales to customers under 45 grew by 12% year-over-year in 2024, with this younger demographic now accounting for nearly 28% of total sales volume — consumers who are digitally native and place a high value on sustainability. This generational shift creates both opportunity and threat: opportunity if Mercedes can deliver compelling digital luxury, and threat if Chinese rivals — who are perceived as more technologically agile — capture this cohort first.

### Technological
The technological frontier is moving rapidly on two axes: electrification and software-defined vehicles. Mercedes-Benz spent over €10 billion on R&D in 2024, focusing on autonomous driving, electrification, and digital ecosystems. The connected car market was valued at $171.7 billion in 2024, demonstrating the scale of the software opportunity. The proprietary MB.OS operating system is Mercedes-Benz's central technological bet: with the transformation to "Digital First," Mercedes-Benz will introduce its own data-supported and flexibly updateable operating system from 2025 — MB.OS — which connects all vehicle domains including infotainment and automated driving.

### Environmental
Governments worldwide are becoming stricter about vehicle emissions due to increasing concerns about climate change. For Mercedes-Benz, this means investing in cleaner technologies, including hybrid and electric vehicles. Mercedes-Benz Group AG must continually adapt to evolving product liability and safety regulations globally, such as the EU's General Safety Regulation (GSR2) mandating advanced driver-assistance systems in new vehicles as of 2024. Mercedes-Benz has committed to carbon neutrality across its entire fleet by 2039 under its "Ambition 2039" strategy.

### Legal
Regulatory compliance costs are escalating. Meeting stringent safety and emissions standards, which are constantly evolving, demands considerable expertise and investment. In 2024, new emissions regulations in key markets continued to increase the cost of compliance. The EU's CO₂ fleet targets impose significant financial penalties for non-compliance, effectively mandating a minimum pace of electrification regardless of market demand — creating a structural tension with the company's pragmatic ICE extension strategy.

**PESTEL Summary:** The macro-environment presents Mercedes-Benz with a compressed window for action. Regulatory pressure mandates electrification; geopolitical fragmentation threatens the China profit engine; and sociocultural shifts demand digital competence. The company cannot simply retreat to ICE comfort; it must execute a credible dual-track strategy.

## 4. Porter's Five Forces: Industry-Level Competitive Dynamics

### Force 1: Competitive Rivalry (HIGH)
The luxury and premium automobile industry is very competitive. The major competitors of Mercedes-Benz include BMW, Audi, Volvo, and Jaguar. The highly competitive nature of the industry, as well as growing customer expectations from car brands, are the reasons why companies invest so heavily in product development and marketing. The rivalry has intensified qualitatively as well as quantitatively: the competitive landscape for Mercedes-Benz is intensifying, particularly in the luxury EV market. In 2024, the company faces pressure from established rivals like BMW and Audi, as well as new entrants like Tesla and various Chinese EV manufacturers. The performance gap with BMW is measurable and widening: in H1 2025, BMW sold 1,070,814 vehicles globally, down just 2.3% year-over-year, compared to Mercedes's 900,000 units, reflecting a 6% decline.

### Force 2: Threat of New Entrants (MODERATE, RISING)
Historically, the capital intensity of automotive manufacturing created near-impenetrable barriers to entry. However, the EV transition has disrupted this calculus. The threat of new entrants for Daimler, particularly for its Mercedes-Benz luxury brand, remains relatively low due to substantial barriers. The sheer capital required to establish a presence, from R&D to manufacturing and global distribution, is immense. Yet Chinese OEMs — BYD, NIO, Li Auto, and Xiaomi — have demonstrated that state-backed capital can overcome these barriers with remarkable speed, particularly in software-defined vehicle capabilities where traditional automotive expertise is less decisive.

### Force 3: Threat of Substitutes (MODERATE)
Urban transit, congestion pricing, and 56% urbanisation in 2024 reduce private-car need, yet luxury demand persists as status and convenience resist full substitution. In the premium segment, the status dimension of ownership creates a psychological barrier to substitution. However, high-quality used Mercedes vehicles deliver similar utility at lower cost, making them a credible substitute for new sales; certified pre-owned programs add warranty and manufacturer financing that further bridge the gap. The certified pre-owned channel, while cannibalising new vehicle sales at the margin, also serves as a customer retention mechanism.

### Force 4: Bargaining Power of Buyers (HIGH)
Customers in the premium and luxury automotive segment wield considerable bargaining power. This is largely due to the wide array of high-quality alternatives available from rivals such as BMW, Audi, and Lexus. This intense competition compels Mercedes-Benz to remain competitive on pricing and consistently deliver enhanced value to its clientele. The EV transition has amplified buyer power further: the luxury electric vehicle market, a key growth area, has experienced heightened price competition, further amplifying customer leverage. In early 2024, adjustments in EV pricing by major manufacturers demonstrated how this dynamic amplifies customer leverage.

### Force 5: Bargaining Power of Suppliers (MODERATE, RISING)
The EV transition has created new supplier dependencies, particularly in battery cells and semiconductors. Mercedes-Benz relies on a complex web of suppliers to ensure timely production, and disruptions in any part of this chain — due to geopolitical tensions, natural disasters, or other unforeseen events — can significantly impact the company's ability to deliver vehicles on time. As the shift towards electric vehicles necessitates specialised parts and technologies, any shortages can hinder Mercedes-Benz's ability to compete effectively in this segment. The company's response has been to pursue localisation: a 10% reduction in material costs is targeted through deeper localisation of parts in the China supply chain.

**Porter's Five Forces Summary:** The industry structure has become materially less favourable for incumbents. Rivalry is intensifying from both traditional peers and technologically capable new entrants; buyer power is elevated by abundant alternatives; and supplier power is growing in strategically critical EV components. The structural profit pool is under compression.

## 7. Ansoff Matrix: Strategic Growth Options

The Ansoff Matrix provides a structured framework for evaluating Mercedes-Benz's four strategic growth options and their associated risk profiles.

| | **Existing Products** | **New Products** |
|---|---|---|
| **Existing Markets** | **Market Penetration** | **Product Development** |
| **New Markets** | **Market Development** | **Diversification** |

### Quadrant 1: Market Penetration (Lowest Risk)
Mercedes-Benz's most immediate lever is deepening penetration of existing luxury segments with its proven ICE and hybrid portfolio. The "Economics of Desire" strategy — concentrating on Maybach, AMG, and G-Class — is a market penetration play within the ultra-luxury segment. Increasing the sales share of its top-end luxury segment by 60% compared to 2019 levels by 2026, focusing on high-margin vehicles is the clearest near-term revenue defence. This is rational because ultra-luxury buyers are least price-sensitive and least susceptible to Chinese competition in the short term.

### Quadrant 2: Product Development (Moderate Risk)
The second-generation EV portfolio — anchored by the new CLA on the MMA platform and MB.OS — represents product development for existing luxury customers. Mercedes-Benz is introducing its proprietary MB.OS, a comprehensive chip-to-cloud platform that will debut in the new CLA, integrating various vehicle domains from infotainment to automated driving capabilities. The new Mythos ultra-luxury series, with the AMG PureSpeed as its first model, also falls here — a new product for the existing ultra-wealthy customer base. The strategy is based on profitable growth through the introduction of luxury EVs, and as part of this strategy, it will launch the Mercedes-AMG PureSpeed, the first in the ultra-luxury "Mythos" series, in 2025.

### Quadrant 3: Market Development (Moderate Risk)
Emerging markets, particularly in Asia, offer substantial growth potential due to rising disposable incomes and a strong appetite for premium vehicles. Mercedes-Benz is well-positioned to leverage these trends by tailoring its product offerings. India, Southeast Asia, and the Middle East represent geographies where the Mercedes-Benz brand carries aspirational status but penetration remains low. These markets are less exposed to Chinese competitor pressure and provide geographic diversification away from China dependency.

### Quadrant 4: Diversification (Highest Risk)
Software-as-a-service revenue through MB.OS represents a form of related diversification — applying automotive assets to generate recurring digital revenues. The company expects total software-enabled revenues to evolve to a high single-digit billion EUR figure by the end of the decade. This is the highest-risk quadrant but also the highest potential value creator, as it fundamentally changes the company's revenue model from transactional to recurring.

## 8. Synthesis: The Core Strategic Tension

The analytical chain reveals a single, central strategic tension: **Mercedes-Benz must simultaneously defend its profitable present (ICE luxury) and invest in its necessary future (software-defined electric luxury), while its cost base, China exposure, and EV credibility are all under acute stress at the same time.**

The company's current posture — maintaining separate ICE and EV platforms, extending combustion engine life, and investing in MB.OS — is directionally correct but operationally complex and financially demanding. The risk is that half-commitments deliver neither the cost savings of full standardisation nor the credibility of a clear electric identity.

The China situation is the most urgent strategic threat. Mercedes-Benz CFO Harald Wilhelm has outlined a mid-term strategy to reach 600,000 annual units in China as the brand faces intense competition and margin pressure. However, volume targets without a credible software-defined vehicle proposition risk being achieved through price discounting, which would further erode the brand equity that is Mercedes-Benz's ultimate competitive moat.

## 9. Recommendations and Action Plan

The following recommendations are sequenced by urgency and connected to the analytical findings above.

---

### Recommendation 1: Accelerate and Ringfence MB.OS as the Core Competitive Differentiator (Immediate, 0–18 months)

**Rationale:** The Porter's Five Forces analysis shows that buyer power is high and rivalry is intensifying. The only sustainable source of differentiation that competitors cannot rapidly replicate is a proprietary, deeply integrated software ecosystem. The value chain analysis shows that post-sale service revenue through MB.OS is the highest-margin activity in the future business model.

**Action:** Treat MB.OS as a standalone strategic business unit with dedicated P&L accountability. Accelerate the rollout beyond the CLA to the full model range by 2027. Establish a Mercedes Me digital marketplace with a target of 10 million active subscribers by 2028. Partner with luxury content and experience providers (high-end hospitality, curated travel, concierge services) to create an "ecosystem of desire" that no Chinese competitor can replicate through hardware alone.

**Expected Outcome:** Already in 2022, Mercedes-Benz generated more than €1 billion in software-enabled revenues. Accelerating this trajectory towards the targeted high single-digit billion EUR by end of decade would provide a margin buffer that partially offsets ICE volume declines.

---

### Recommendation 2: Execute the "Economics of Desire" Strategy with Ruthless Discipline (Immediate, 0–24 months)

**Rationale:** The SWOT analysis shows that ultra-luxury is the segment most resilient to Chinese competition and most aligned with Mercedes-Benz's brand equity. Luxury vehicles command some of the highest profit margins in the industry, and customers tend to be more loyal and make repeat purchases. The Ansoff Matrix identifies this as a market penetration strategy — the lowest-risk growth option.

**Action:** Prioritise capital allocation to Maybach, AMG, and G-Class. Launch the Mythos series as planned in 2025. Ensure every ultra-luxury model has a credible electric variant (e.g., the Maybach EQS SUV) to future-proof the sub-brand against regulatory pressure. Limit discounting across all top-end models to protect residual values and brand exclusivity.

**Expected Outcome:** Achieving the 60% increase in top-end sales share versus 2019 by 2026 would materially improve the revenue-per-unit metric and partially offset volume declines in mid-range segments.

### Recommendation 3: Implement a China-Specific "Tech Luxury" Strategy (Short-term, 6–24 months)

**Rationale:** The PESTEL and Porter's analyses both identify China as the most structurally challenged market. Brands such as BYD, NIO, and Li Auto have successfully captured market share by offering "tech-laden" premium vehicles at aggressive price points. A generic global product strategy will not arrest the decline; China requires a tailored response.

**Action:** Establish a China-specific R&D centre with full authority to develop China-first features for MB.OS — integrating with WeChat, Alipay, Baidu Maps, and Chinese-specific voice assistants. Localise the supply chain aggressively: a 10% reduction in material costs is targeted through deeper localisation of parts in the China supply chain. Partner with a Chinese technology company (not an automotive competitor) for AI and digital services integration, replicating the successful model of Chinese OEMs who have integrated Huawei or Baidu technology stacks. Target the 600,000 unit mid-term goal through technology differentiation, not price competition.

**Expected Outcome:** Arresting the S-Class and Maybach decline in China — where the S-Class saw a 92.4% sales collapse — requires demonstrating that Mercedes-Benz can deliver Chinese-grade digital integration within a European luxury shell. This is a product-market fit problem, not a brand problem.

### Recommendation 4: Stabilise the EV Transition with a Credible Dual-Track Architecture (Medium-term, 12–36 months)

**Rationale:** The strategic inconsistency identified in the SWOT analysis — repeated revision of EV targets — is destroying investor confidence and creating brand confusion. The Ansoff Matrix's product development quadrant requires a clear, committed product roadmap to be credible.

**Action:** Commit publicly to the MMA platform as the foundation for all new EV models from 2026, with the CLA as the flagship proof point. Mercedes-Benz is targeting an electrified vehicle (xEV) share of more than 30% by 2027. This is a realistic and credible target that should be held to publicly. Simultaneously, invest in "high-tech combustion" for the ICE portfolio to ensure compliance with tightening EU CO₂ standards, thereby avoiding regulatory penalties that would erode the financial headroom needed for EV investment. Standardise the design language across ICE and EV variants to reduce development costs and signal a unified brand identity.

**Expected Outcome:** A credible 30% xEV share by 2027, rising to 50%+ by 2030, would satisfy regulatory requirements, rebuild investor confidence, and position Mercedes-Benz to benefit from the next wave of EV demand growth as charging infrastructure matures.

---

### Recommendation 5: Structurally Reset the Cost Base to Sustain Investment Capacity (Medium-term, 12–48 months)

**Rationale:** The financial analysis shows that a 7% profit margin (2024) versus 12.6% (2023) is unsustainable for a company that needs to simultaneously invest in MB.OS, new EV platforms, and China localisation. The value chain analysis identifies cost reduction as a support activity that enables all primary activities.

**Action:** By 2027, Mercedes-Benz aims to slash production costs by 10%, with a goal to double this reduction by 2030. Accelerate the outsourcing of non-core functions — finance, HR, procurement — as outlined by CFO Harald Wilhelm. Consolidate the model range: the proliferation of EQ sub-brand models that have failed commercially should be rationalised. Invest the savings into MB.OS and the ultra-luxury portfolio. By 2025, the company anticipates €2.5 billion in savings from workforce adjustment measures. These savings must be explicitly ring-fenced for strategic reinvestment, not distributed as dividends.

**Expected Outcome:** Restoring automotive margins to 8–10% by 2027 would provide the financial foundation for sustained strategic investment without requiring external capital, preserving the financial independence that underpins Mercedes-Benz's premium positioning.

---

### Recommendation 6: Diversify Geographic Revenue through Emerging Market Development (Long-term, 24–60 months)

**Rationale:** The Ansoff Matrix's market development quadrant identifies emerging markets as a lower-risk growth option than China. The PESTEL analysis highlights rising disposable incomes in Asia and the Middle East as structural tailwinds.

**Action:** Establish dedicated market entry programmes for India (targeting 50,000 annual units by 2030), Southeast Asia, and the Gulf Cooperation Council. Tailor the product mix to local preferences — right-hand-drive variants, heat-resilient battery management, and culturally resonant design elements. Use MB.OS as a platform differentiator, offering localised digital services from launch. This reduces the dangerous concentration of revenue and profit in China, which currently creates existential exposure to a single geopolitical risk.

**Expected Outcome:** Reducing China's share of global sales from 30%+ to below 25% by 2030, while growing absolute China revenues, would materially improve the company's geopolitical risk profile without sacrificing the world's largest luxury market.

## 10. Conclusion

Mercedes-Benz is not in strategic crisis — it is at a strategic inflection point. Its brand equity, engineering heritage, and financial resources remain formidable. But the analytical chain constructed in this case study reveals three structural vulnerabilities that, if unaddressed, could compound into a genuine crisis: an EV transition that has been both over-promised and under-delivered; a China market where technological credibility is eroding faster than brand loyalty can compensate; and a cost structure calibrated for a more forgiving competitive environment.

The recommended action plan is built on a coherent strategic logic: **defend and monetise the ultra-luxury moat in the short term; build the software-defined vehicle capability that will define luxury in the medium term; and diversify geographic exposure to reduce systemic risk in the long term.** Each recommendation flows directly from the analytical frameworks applied, and each is connected to the others through the central insight that brand equity alone is no longer sufficient — it must be expressed through technological excellence and digital integration to remain compelling to the next generation of luxury buyers.

As the company's own strategy documents state, the goal is to be "the undisputed icon of adventurous luxury" through G-Class, "the epitome of sophisticated luxury" through Maybach, and "the performance luxury brand" through AMG. The frameworks applied here suggest that achieving this aspiration requires not just product excellence, but a fundamental reconception of what luxury means in the software-defined vehicle era — one where the drive experience extends seamlessly into a digital ecosystem that is as desirable as the three-pointed star itself.
