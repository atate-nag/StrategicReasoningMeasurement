# **STRATEGIC CASE STUDY**
# **MTN Group: From Connectivity Provider to Digital Platform Ecosystem — Navigating the "Dumb-Pipe" Threat in Pan-African Telecoms**

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

MTN Group, Africa's largest mobile operator by subscriber count, faces a structural strategic dilemma that is simultaneously an industry-wide inflection point: the commoditisation of core voice and SMS revenues by Over-The-Top (OTT) digital platforms, combined with mounting macroeconomic and regulatory turbulence across its 16-country African footprint. This case study diagnoses the competitive situation using Porter's Five Forces, the Resource-Based View (RBV) and Teece's Dynamic Capabilities framework, and a PESTLE scan. The analysis reveals that MTN's survival and growth depend on its ability to reconfigure itself from a network utility into a multi-sided digital platform — principally through its fintech division (MoMo), infrastructure ownership, and enterprise services. A five-point action plan is derived directly from the analytical findings.

---

## 1. CASE BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### 1.1 The Pan-African Telecoms Landscape

Africa's mobile internet sector, with a penetration rate of only 27%, is the stage for aggressive competition among five telecom giants: Orange, MTN, Maroc Telecom, Vodacom, and Airtel. The market is structurally fragmented: the telecom sector spans over 150 operators across 54 countries, and the investments needed for the industry's future are equally significant. This fragmentation matters strategically because it suppresses economies of scale while simultaneously increasing capital intensity — a combination that squeezes margins and forces operators to seek differentiation beyond basic connectivity.

Mobile money is the critical growth vector across the continent, processing over 108 billion transactions worth more than $1.68 trillion in 2024. This ecosystem is a direct revenue stream and a powerful tool for customer acquisition and retention. This establishes why the strategic battleground has shifted from spectrum and towers to financial services platforms.

In 2024, mobile technologies and services generated 7.7% of Africa's GDP, amounting to $220 billion in economic value. The scale of this contribution means that telecom strategy is inseparable from national development policy — a fact that shapes the regulatory environment operators must navigate.

### 1.2 MTN Group: The Focal Firm

MTN Group is the largest pan-African mobile operator. MTN Group has a footprint in sixteen countries in Africa and the Middle East. MTN is a Pan-African mobile operator with the strategic ambition to lead Africa's digital transformation. Its 'Ambition 2025' strategy identifies five growth pillars: connectivity, fintech, digital infrastructure, Ayoba (its super-app), and enterprise services.

Financially, MTN's performance in 2024 illustrates the dual pressures of opportunity and vulnerability. Fintech revenue increased by 11% on a reported basis, but by almost 29% in constant currency. As part of its expense efficiency programme (EEP), MTN realised sustainable savings of R3.8 billion in 2024. These figures are significant: the divergence between reported and constant-currency growth exposes the degree to which macroeconomic volatility — not operational failure — suppresses headline numbers. Mobile Money (MoMo) monthly active users were up 0.9% to 63.1 million, and fintech transaction volumes rose 15.3% to 20.3 billion.

## 2. STRATEGIC SITUATION: THE CORE PROBLEM

The central strategic problem facing MTN can be stated as follows: **voice and SMS revenues — which historically funded network investment — are being eroded by OTT platforms, while the replacement revenue streams (data, fintech) are subject to currency risk, regulatory caps, and intensifying competition from both telco peers and pure-play fintechs.** This creates a "burning platform" dynamic: standing still accelerates decline, yet the investments required to pivot are large, slow to yield returns, and exposed to macro volatility.

### 2.1 The OTT / "Dumb-Pipe" Threat

OTT services provide a direct threat to telecom operators due to substitution in service, leading to a marked decrease in revenue generation from voice and messaging services. The structural logic is stark: someone in Nairobi opens Netflix and watches a film for two hours — Safaricom earns a few shillings from the data that stream uses, while Netflix earns advertising and subscription revenue that adds up to billions of dollars every quarter. Safaricom built the road; Netflix opened a cinema on it. The same logic applies to WhatsApp displacing SMS and voice calls across MTN's markets.

The financial evidence is concrete. Q3 2024 financial reports from MTN Nigeria and Airtel Nigeria revealed significant declines in ARPU, with MTN dropping to $2.09 (from $3.24) and Airtel to $1.60 (from $3). This 35–47% ARPU compression is not merely cyclical; it reflects a structural shift in how subscribers use their connections. The inference is direct: if MTN remains primarily a connectivity provider, declining ARPU will compress margins faster than subscriber growth can compensate, because as of 2023, mobile penetration in sub-Saharan Africa was 44% and mobile internet penetration was 27% — meaning the remaining unconnected population is the hardest and most expensive to reach.

### 2.2 Macroeconomic and Currency Exposure

MTN's largest single market, Nigeria, has inflicted severe balance-sheet damage. MTN Nigeria recorded a significant ₦514.9 billion after-tax loss in 2024, primarily due to currency volatility. More specifically, MTN Nigeria experienced significant financial strain due to a continued depreciation of the naira, reporting a net foreign exchange loss of N656.3 billion in Q1 2024, contributing to a record high forex loss of N1.396 trillion since 2023. This matters for the strategic analysis because it demonstrates that even strong operational performance can be negated by macro forces — which in turn shapes what kinds of assets and revenue streams MTN should prioritise.

However, the situation is not static. MTN Nigeria crossed the N1 trillion revenue mark in the first quarter of 2025, recovering strongly from a loss position last year, buoyed largely by the phased implementation of a 50% tariff hike approved by the Nigerian Communications Commission (NCC) and early signs of exchange rate stability. This recovery illustrates an important strategic insight: regulatory pricing power, when obtained, can partially offset currency erosion — but it is fragile and politically contingent.

## 3. ANALYTICAL FRAMEWORKS

### 3.1 Porter's Five Forces Analysis

**Force 1 — Rivalry Among Existing Competitors (HIGH)**

After years of mergers and acquisitions — especially with Millicom's withdrawal from Africa — the African telecom market seems to have reached a certain level of stability, with an average of three operators per country. Three-player national markets typically produce oligopolistic rivalry characterised by price competition and network investment races rather than outright price wars. For governments, having at least three operators guarantees innovation and price competition, which benefits consumers. The implication is that rivalry is structurally sustained by regulatory design — regulators actively prevent the consolidation that would reduce competitive intensity. This means MTN cannot rely on market concentration to ease competitive pressure; it must differentiate on capabilities.

**Force 2 — Threat of New Entrants (MODERATE-LOW for connectivity; HIGH for fintech)**

For traditional network operations, barriers remain high. The telecommunications industry has high barriers to entry due to significant capital investment required for infrastructure and spectrum licenses. However, the fintech and digital services layer — which is where MTN is seeking growth — faces a very different entry dynamic. Pure-play fintechs such as Wave operate with radically lower cost structures: Wave differentiates itself by focusing on extremely low fees and a seamless user experience tailored to local needs. This bifurcation is strategically critical: MTN's network moat protects its connectivity business but does not automatically protect its fintech ambitions.

**Force 3 — Threat of Substitutes (HIGH)**

The OTT substitution dynamic is already materialising in ARPU data (see §2.1). OTT services, including WhatsApp, Skype, Netflix, and TikTok, enable users to communicate, stream content, and access a plethora of services directly over the internet, bypassing traditional telecommunications networks. The warrant for treating this as a high-severity force is that substitution does not merely reduce revenue per user — it fundamentally changes what customers value. If customers value the application layer (WhatsApp) rather than the network layer (MTN's pipes), then MTN's brand and distribution become less defensible.

**Force 4 — Bargaining Power of Buyers (INCREASING)**

Buyer power is rising for two reasons. First, multi-SIM ownership is widespread in Africa, enabling consumers to switch between operators for price arbitrage. Second, the tariff approval process — illustrated by MTN Nigeria's dependence on NCC approval for its 50% tariff hike — means that the state acts as a proxy buyer, capping pricing power. MTN Nigeria's Q1 2025 revenue rose by 40.5% compared to the same period in 2024, even as the impact of the tariff adjustment had yet to be fully realised — demonstrating that regulatory pricing relief, when granted, is transformative, but that dependence on such relief is a structural vulnerability.

**Force 5 — Bargaining Power of Suppliers (MODERATE, but shifting)**

Tower companies have historically been powerful suppliers. As of Q3 2025, about 62% of IHS Towers' revenue came from MTN operating companies, particularly MTN Nigeria. Every year, MTN pays tower lease fees that ultimately become revenue and profit inside IHS. This relationship represents a significant value leakage. MTN's proposed acquisition of IHS Towers is a direct strategic response: MTN says revenue earned from MTN's operating companies will be eliminated, removing a significant portion of tower rental expenses and generating roughly $1.1 billion in savings. By internalising its largest supplier, MTN transforms a cost centre into a strategic asset — and the Five Forces logic makes clear why: reducing supplier power directly improves industry profitability for the acquirer.

**Five Forces Summary and Strategic Implication**

The combined force analysis yields a structurally unattractive industry for pure-play connectivity: high rivalry, rising substitute threats, and moderate-to-high supplier and buyer power. The strategic implication — which flows directly from this diagnosis — is that MTN must migrate value creation to layers where these forces are less hostile: specifically, fintech platforms (where network effects create switching costs that dampen rivalry) and digital infrastructure (where ownership converts supplier power into internal margin).

### 3.2 Resource-Based View (RBV) and VRIN Analysis

The RBV (Barney, 1991) holds that sustained competitive advantage derives from resources that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN). Applying this to MTN:

| Resource | Valuable? | Rare? | Inimitable? | Non-substitutable? | Strategic Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Pan-African network coverage (16 markets) | ✓ | ✓ | Partially | No (other operators) | Temporary advantage |
| MoMo customer base (63.1m MAU) | ✓ | ✓ | Partially | No (M-Pesa, Airtel Money) | Moderate advantage |
| Distribution/agent network | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | Partially | Strong advantage |
| Brand and regulatory relationships | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | Partially | Strong advantage |
| Tower infrastructure (post-IHS) | ✓ | Partially | ✓ | Partially | Moderate-Strong |

The agent network and regulatory relationships are the most VRIN-compliant resources. Companies like Mastercard have pursued numerous partnerships since 2019, including taking stakes in the fintech divisions of Airtel Africa and MTN Group — a strategy that allows them to integrate into a distribution infrastructure that would take decades and billions to replicate organically. Mastercard's willingness to pay for access to MTN's agent network confirms its rarity and inimitability. The strategic inference is that MTN should double down on assets that meet VRIN criteria — the agent network, regulatory relationships, and multi-country operational experience — rather than competing on price in connectivity markets where its resources are not differentiated.

**RBV Limitation**: Static RBV analysis is insufficient in a rapidly changing environment. The agent network that is VRIN today could be rendered obsolete by smartphone-native digital wallets tomorrow. This is why the Dynamic Capabilities framework is needed as a complement.

### 3.3 Teece's Dynamic Capabilities Framework

Teece (2007) defines dynamic capabilities in terms of three clusters: **sensing** (scanning for opportunities and threats), **seizing** (mobilising resources to capture opportunities), and **reconfiguring** (transforming the asset base as markets evolve). According to Teece (2007), dynamic capabilities can be defined in terms of sensing, seizing and reconfiguring. Sensing comprises analytical systems to learn and to sense, filter, shape, and calibrate opportunities.

**Sensing Capability — MTN's Track Record**

MTN has demonstrated strong sensing: it identified the mobile money opportunity early, launching MoMo across its markets before most Western operators recognised the potential of mobile finance in low-banked populations. Orange Money revenue increased by 20.4% in 2024 — confirming that the mobile money opportunity MTN and its peers sensed is now materialising at scale. MTN also sensed the OTT threat: its Ambition 2025 strategy explicitly acknowledges the need to move beyond connectivity. However, sensing alone does not create advantage.

**Seizing Capability — Mixed Execution**

MTN's seizing has been uneven. On the positive side, MTN finalised spin-offs of its Nigerian and Ugandan fintech operations, with Mastercard's involvement bringing global infrastructure, regulatory relationships, and merchant acceptance capabilities that MoMo would struggle to build on its own timeline. This is a textbook seizing move: MTN mobilised an external partner's resources to accelerate capability development.

Conversely, MTN's Ayoba super-app — a key seizing vehicle in Ambition 2025 — has been abandoned. MTN has begun shutting down Ayoba, shifting to a unified digital platform. The CEO's focus is now on three core platforms: connectivity, fintech, and digital infrastructure. The Ayoba failure matters analytically because it reveals a seizing limitation: MTN attempted to compete directly with global OTT platforms (WhatsApp, TikTok) on content and social features — a domain where it lacks the scale, data, and engineering depth to match Silicon Valley competitors. The strategic warrant for abandoning Ayoba is therefore sound: resources should be concentrated in areas of genuine VRIN advantage.

**Reconfiguring Capability — The IHS Acquisition as Pivot**

The most significant reconfiguring move is MTN's proposed $2.2 billion acquisition of IHS Towers. MTN Group announced that the board of IHS Towers has accepted an offer of US$8.50 a share in a transaction that would see MTN increasing its shareholding to 100%. By reintegrating the tower assets, MTN will be able to internalise the margin currently paid to IHS, benefit from current and future incremental third-party revenues, improve cost predictability and unlock significant long-term value. This reconfiguration converts a supplier relationship into an owned asset — directly addressing the supplier bargaining power identified in the Five Forces analysis. The dynamic capability logic is explicit: as the environment shifts (rising capital costs, currency pressure), MTN reconfigures its asset base to reduce external dependencies.

### 3.4 PESTLE Analysis

**Political**: In Africa, telecom operators comply with internet shutdown directives, often not out of agreement but under threat of licence revocation or other legal consequences. In 2024, shutdowns cost sub-Saharan Africa economic losses of over US$1.6 billion. This political risk is not merely reputational; it directly disrupts revenue in affected markets. MTN's multi-country footprint partially diversifies this risk, but Nigeria — its largest market — remains highly exposed.

**Economic**: Currency volatility is the dominant economic risk. MTN Nigeria reported a net loss of N137 billion in 2023 due to rising inflation, currency devaluation, and foreign exchange shortages. The naira's devaluation transformed what was operationally a profitable business into a reported loss-maker — illustrating how macro forces can overwhelm operational performance.

**Social**: As of 2023, only 56% of adults in rural areas were mobile internet subscribers, compared to 77% in urban areas — meaning rural populations are 28% less likely to access mobile internet than their urban counterparts. This urban-rural divide is both a social challenge and a strategic opportunity: the unconnected population represents MTN's largest addressable growth market, but reaching it requires different economics.

**Technological**: By June 2025, fewer than 30% of African countries — just 16 nations — had allocated spectrum for 5G services. The slow 5G rollout means that 4G densification, rather than 5G deployment, is the near-term network investment priority for most of MTN's markets. This has capital allocation implications: resources should go to 4G coverage extension in rural areas, not premature 5G urban deployments.

**Legal/Regulatory**: South Africa's ICASA, Nigeria's NCC, and Ghana's NCA each operate distinct licensing, pricing, and spectrum policy regimes. This regulatory fragmentation across 16 markets creates compliance costs and strategic uncertainty. The NCC's approval of MTN Nigeria's 50% tariff hike demonstrates that regulatory relationships can be turned into competitive advantage — but this requires sustained investment in regulatory engagement.

**Environmental**: MTN Nigeria introduced biodegradable SIM cards as part of its Project Zero initiative, demonstrating a commitment to environmental responsibility. While environmental factors are not currently a primary competitive driver in African telecoms, they are increasingly relevant to ESG-focused international investors whose capital MTN needs for its infrastructure programme.

## 4. SYNTHESIS: THE COHERENT STRATEGIC NARRATIVE

The analytical frameworks converge on a single coherent diagnosis. Porter's Five Forces shows that the connectivity layer is structurally unattractive (high rivalry, high substitution, regulated pricing). The RBV shows that MTN's most defensible resources are its agent network, regulatory relationships, and multi-country operational experience — not its pipes. Teece's Dynamic Capabilities framework shows that MTN has strong sensing and partial seizing capability, but must accelerate reconfiguration of its asset base. PESTLE confirms that macroeconomic and regulatory volatility will continue to suppress headline numbers, making operational efficiency and cost internalisation (e.g., IHS acquisition) strategically urgent.

The logical chain is therefore:

1. **Voice/SMS revenues are structurally declining** (OTT substitution + ARPU compression evidence) →
2. **Replacement revenues must come from fintech and enterprise services** (where MTN's VRIN resources are most applicable) →
3. **Fintech growth requires reducing infrastructure cost drag** (so margin can fund platform investment) →
4. **Infrastructure cost drag is reduced by internalising tower ownership** (IHS acquisition) →
5. **Platform growth requires interoperability and scale beyond single markets** (cross-border mobile money) →
6. **Scale requires regulatory relationships and device affordability solutions** (GSMA coalition, tariff advocacy) →
7. **Therefore, MTN's strategic priority is platform-layer value creation, funded by infrastructure efficiency gains, enabled by regulatory engagement.**

## 5. RECOMMENDATIONS AND ACTION PLAN

The following five recommendations flow directly from the analytical synthesis. Each is warranted by specific evidence cited above.

---

### Recommendation 1: Complete the IHS Towers Acquisition and Leverage the Cost Savings for Platform Investment

**Warrant**: The Five Forces analysis identifies tower companies as a high-power supplier capturing significant value from MTN. The IHS acquisition converts this external cost into internal margin. Buying the company effectively turns lease payments into internal transfers — instead of paying rent, MTN captures the margin. At the end of 2024, lease liability across MTN Group stood at $4.65 billion. The $1.1 billion in projected annual savings should be ring-fenced for fintech and digital infrastructure investment, not absorbed into general operating expenditure.

**Caution**: if competitors continue leasing towers from IHS, the neutral tower model could survive in modified form. But if operators begin building or acquiring their own towers, the industry could fragment into competing infrastructure networks. MTN must therefore ensure IHS remains open to third-party tenants post-acquisition, preserving the multi-tenancy economics that justify the purchase price.

---

### Recommendation 2: Accelerate MoMo's Cross-Border Interoperability as the Primary Growth Vector

**Warrant**: The RBV analysis identifies MoMo's agent network as MTN's most VRIN-compliant resource. The cross-border payments opportunity is large and growing: Africa now dominates mobile money, processing 74% of global transactions — $1.1 trillion in 2024 alone. By early 2025, PAPSS had enabled real-time cross-border payments across 17 countries, connecting 14 national switches and over 150 commercial banks, aligning with the African Continental Free Trade Area (AfCFTA) goals. MTN's 16-country footprint is uniquely positioned to leverage this infrastructure — no pure-play fintech has comparable physical distribution across this many markets. The Dynamic Capabilities framework supports this as a seizing move: MTN should mobilise its existing agent network to capture the cross-border payment corridor opportunity before pure-play fintechs (Wave, PalmPay) build comparable reach.

**Specific Action**: Integrate MoMo into PAPSS in all 16 operating markets within 24 months, and negotiate corridor-specific partnerships with diaspora remittance flows (West Africa–Europe, East Africa–Gulf) as anchor use cases.

---

### Recommendation 3: Pursue Device Affordability as a Strategic Demand-Creation Initiative

**Warrant**: The PESTLE analysis identifies device affordability as the binding constraint on data revenue growth. Smartphone adoption remains a key barrier — only about 24% of the population in Africa owned smartphones in 2024, the lowest rate globally, largely due to affordability challenges. In many parts of sub-Saharan Africa, the cost of a basic smartphone can account for as much as 26% of monthly income. This matters strategically because MTN's fintech and data revenues both depend on smartphone penetration: a feature-phone user cannot access MoMo's full digital wallet functionality or consume data-intensive services. MTN is already part of the solution: the GSMA, in collaboration with Airtel, Axian Telecom, Ethio Telecom, MTN, Orange, and Vodacom, proposed a groundbreaking baseline set of minimum requirements for an affordable entry-level 4G smartphone.

**Specific Action**: MTN should go beyond the GSMA coalition and introduce operator-subsidised device financing (e.g., 12-month instalment plans bundled with data and MoMo activation), targeting the bottom two income quintiles in its top five markets. The cost of the subsidy is justified by the lifetime value of a converted MoMo user.

---

### Recommendation 4: Institutionalise Regulatory Engagement as a Core Competence

**Warrant**: The Nigeria tariff case demonstrates that regulatory pricing power is transformative when obtained. MTN Nigeria's total revenue reached N5.20 trillion in 2025, a significant increase from N3.36 trillion in 2024, with operating profit more than doubling. Profit after tax marked a major recovery — driven substantially by the NCC-approved tariff hike. The Dynamic Capabilities framework frames this as a seizing capability: MTN must build the organisational routines to consistently translate regulatory goodwill into pricing relief across all 16 markets, not just Nigeria. The PESTLE analysis confirms that South Africa's ICASA, Nigeria's NCC, and Ghana's NCA each operate distinct licensing, pricing, and spectrum policy regimes — meaning this capability must be market-specific, not generic.

**Specific Action**: Establish dedicated regulatory affairs units in each of MTN's top eight markets (by revenue contribution), staffed with locally credentialed professionals who maintain sustained relationships with national regulators, rather than engaging only at crisis points.

---

### Recommendation 5: Refocus the Digital Services Portfolio on Fintech and Enterprise; Avoid Super-App Competition with Global OTTs

**Warrant**: The Ayoba failure provides direct evidence that MTN cannot win in direct competition with global OTT platforms on social and content features. Although Ayoba was central to MTN's Digital Services segment, its financial performance was never disclosed separately. MTN's fintech business grew 24.9% and processed $500 billion in transaction value, highlighting where the company is now placing its strategic focus. The RBV analysis confirms this: MTN's VRIN resources (agent network, regulatory relationships, multi-country reach) are applicable to fintech and enterprise services, not to content curation or social networking. The Dynamic Capabilities reconfiguring logic supports a clean portfolio pivot: resources freed by Ayoba's shutdown should be redirected to MoMo product development (micro-insurance, micro-credit, merchant payments) and enterprise cloud services.

**Specific Action**: Within 18 months, launch MoMo-integrated micro-lending products in at least five markets, using the transaction history data MTN already holds to build credit-scoring models — a capability that pure-play fintechs without telco distribution cannot easily replicate.

## 6. CONCLUSION

MTN Group stands at a strategic inflection point that is common to all pan-African telecoms operators, but acute for MTN given its scale and Nigeria exposure. The analytical frameworks applied here converge on a coherent prescription: the company must migrate from a network utility model — where it is exposed to OTT substitution, currency volatility, and regulated pricing — to a multi-sided digital platform model, where its unique VRIN resources (agent network, regulatory relationships, multi-country operational experience) generate defensible, high-margin revenue streams. The five recommendations above are sequenced to address the most urgent structural vulnerabilities first (infrastructure cost via IHS, regulatory pricing via engagement) before investing in growth initiatives (cross-border MoMo, device affordability, enterprise fintech). The logic is not merely additive; each recommendation reinforces the others, because reducing infrastructure costs funds platform investment, platform investment requires device penetration, and device penetration requires regulatory pricing headroom.
