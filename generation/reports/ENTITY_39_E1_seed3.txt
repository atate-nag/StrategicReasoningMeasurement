# Strategic Case Study: Trustpilot and the Platform Integrity Dilemma

---

## 1. Executive Summary

Trustpilot Group plc is the world's largest independent consumer review platform. Its freemium-to-SaaS business model generates revenue almost exclusively from businesses that pay subscription fees to manage and promote their reputations — a structural arrangement that places the company at the centre of a profound strategic tension: the very customers who fund the platform have a direct financial incentive to manipulate the content that gives the platform its value to consumers. This case study argues that Trustpilot's strategic situation is best understood not merely as a "fake review problem" but as a **platform integrity dilemma** rooted in misaligned incentives between its two user groups. Drawing on Porter's Five Forces, SWOT analysis, two-sided platform theory, and the institutional moral hazard literature, the analysis traces how this dilemma has been crystallised by a short-seller attack in late 2024, a €4 million regulatory fine in Italy in 2026, and intensifying competition from Google Reviews. The case concludes with a five-point action plan centred on structural transparency, tiered verification reform, regulatory co-creation, enterprise deepening, and a redefined brand narrative.

## 2. Case Background

### 2.1 Origins and Financial Trajectory

Trustpilot was founded in Denmark in 2007 by Peter Holten Mühlmann while he was studying at Aarhus University. After raising $3 million in early venture funding, the company received an initial capital injection from SEED Capital Denmark and Northzone in November 2011, followed by a $13 million Series B from Index Ventures in 2012. Trustpilot was listed on the London Stock Exchange in March 2021. In March 2023, Mühlmann announced his resignation as CEO and was succeeded by Adrian Blair in July of that year.

As of June 2025, Trustpilot hosts 330 million reviews and has 60 million monthly active users. Financially, the company has reached an inflection point: revenue grew 18% in constant currency to $210.7 million (from $176.4 million in 2023), adjusted EBITDA reached a record $24.1 million (from $15.5 million), and profit before tax was $5.2 million, reversing a $1.9 million pre-tax loss in 2023. Bookings grew 21% in constant currency, setting the group up for a good 2025.

### 2.2 The Business Model

Trustpilot operates a freemium model, earning most of its revenue from subscription-based customers. Businesses may claim a free profile with limited functionality — capped at 50 review invitations per month — or upgrade to paid SaaS subscription tiers that unlock advanced analytics, unlimited review invitations, widget integrations, and service-recovery tools. As of mid-2024, the platform had approximately 26,031 paying customers (net of churn), up 2% year-on-year, with an average contract value of $8,221, up 18% year-on-year, driven by a deliberate pivot toward higher-value enterprise accounts.

A critical commercial advantage is Trustpilot's status as an official Google Review Partner: verified Trustpilot reviews count toward Google Seller Ratings, resulting in stars being displayed on Google Ads and driving higher click-through rates. The platform also integrates natively with Shopify, BigCommerce, Magento, WooCommerce, Salesforce, HubSpot, and Google Seller Ratings to shorten time-to-value for merchants.

### 2.3 The Strategic Situation

Despite strong financials, Trustpilot entered 2025 facing a convergence of threats that exposed a structural vulnerability. In December 2024, short-seller Grizzly Research published a report alleging "mafia-style extortion campaigns against non-paying businesses" and claiming that paid-for profiles "see their review score magically lift from under 2 of 5 stars to over 4 of 5 stars." Trustpilot's shares fell approximately 30% after the allegations were published. Then, in March 2026, Italy's antitrust regulator fined Trustpilot €4 million (approximately $4.61 million) for failing to sufficiently verify review authenticity and misleading consumers about how its services work. These events are the symptomatic expression of a business model in which the party that funds the platform is also the party whose reputation the platform is supposed to objectively assess.

## 3. Framing the Strategic Problem

Before deploying analytical frameworks, it is necessary to state the central strategic problem with precision. Trustpilot's value proposition rests on a **credibility asset**: the perception, held by consumers, that its reviews are independent and authentic. This credibility asset is what makes businesses willing to pay for subscription services — because a high Trustpilot score is only commercially valuable if consumers believe it is earned rather than purchased.

This dynamic is formalised in the academic literature on institutional moral hazard. Institutions that broadcast reputations in a marketplace confront two intertwined moral hazards. The first is the classic hazard between buyers and sellers: buyers cannot directly observe seller types, and sellers face incentives to defect. The second arises at the institutional level: the platform itself may compromise accuracy of the reputation system, exaggerating positive signals to encourage transactions that boost its commission revenue. The platform's choices about signal accuracy shape the composition of sellers, the willingness of buyers to engage, and ultimately the alignment — or misalignment — between institutional profit and social welfare.

The Grizzly Research allegations, whether or not fully accurate, articulate exactly this second-order moral hazard. Grizzly argued that Trustpilot "always faced a fundamental conflict in its business model as a third-party review site that generates its revenues from selling subscriptions to companies," and concluded that the company had "failed to balance this conflict." What is novel about Trustpilot's situation in 2024–2026 is that this theoretical tension has been operationalised into a specific set of observable crises that demand a strategic response.

## 4. Analysis — Part I: Industry Environment (Porter's Five Forces)

Porter's Five Forces framework (1979) analyses the structural determinants of competitive intensity and industry profitability. Applied to the online review platform industry, it reveals a mixed picture for Trustpilot.

### 4.1 Threat of New Entrants — **Moderate**

The review platform industry benefits from significant network effects. Trustpilot's corpus of 330 million reviews and 60 million monthly active users constitutes a meaningful barrier to replication. However, entry barriers are not prohibitive. Reviews.io, Feefo, Sitejabber, and ResellerRatings offer direct alternatives focused on price, agility, and customer service, appealing to SMBs and cost-sensitive merchants. The real constraint on new entrants is less technological than reputational: building consumer trust in a new review platform takes years. This moderates the threat but does not eliminate it.

### 4.2 Bargaining Power of Buyers (Businesses) — **Moderate to High**

Trustpilot's paying customers have several credible alternatives. Bazaarvoice, PowerReviews, and Yotpo embed product reviews and syndication into e-commerce stacks, competing on integrations, retailer networks, and merchandising ROI, sometimes substituting for Trustpilot. The platform's deliberate pivot toward enterprise customers is partly a response to this: gross churn fell to 13%, described by analysts as "a reflection of growing enterprise" customers, and net dollar retention came in at approximately 102%. This means existing customers are on balance spending more over time — a positive signal, but one that masks continued SMB churn.

### 4.3 Bargaining Power of Suppliers — **Low**

Trustpilot's primary "suppliers" are consumers who write reviews voluntarily and without payment, giving Trustpilot a structurally advantaged cost position. There is no meaningful supplier concentration or switching-cost dynamic. This force is weak and does not constrain strategic freedom.

### 4.4 Threat of Substitutes — **High**

This is the most potent structural force facing Trustpilot. If the objective is to be more discoverable, Trustpilot will almost never fit the bill except in the very specific case of Google Ads Star ratings. Google Reviews will make a local business more discoverable, but Trustpilot's free listings do not provide those search-ranking benefits. Yelp remains dominant in hospitality and local services in North America, challenging Trustpilot mainly at the city and service-provider level. Emerging channels — Reddit communities, TikTok creator reviews, AI-generated summary overlays in search — further fragment the consumer trust ecosystem. The substitution threat is high and structural, not cyclical.

### 4.5 Competitive Rivalry — **High**

Trustpilot reported approximately 60% of revenue from subscription products in 2024, highlighting dependence on SaaS monetisation in a market where well-resourced rivals — Google, Yelp, Bazaarvoice — compete across overlapping segments. The competitive landscape now combines regulatory resilience, AI capability, and social integration as differentiators versus peers like Google Reviews, Yelp, and emerging niche sites.

**Summary:** The industry structure is moderately attractive for an incumbent with Trustpilot's network scale, but the high substitution threat and intense rivalry mean competitive advantage must be actively maintained. The structural analysis points toward differentiation on **integrity and integration depth** as the only sustainable basis for pricing power.

## 5. Analysis — Part II: Internal Position (SWOT Analysis)

### 5.1 Strengths

**Scale and AI-powered integrity.** Trustpilot removed 4.5 million fake reviews in 2024, with 90% taken down automatically by AI tools, as the customer feedback platform stepped up efforts to protect its integrity amid rising regulatory pressure and record volumes of user content. Implementation of this technology led to 53% more reviews being removed automatically than in 2023, demonstrating its importance in maintaining integrity as the platform grows. Crucially, the scale of the review corpus itself strengthens detection: larger datasets improve machine learning models, creating a virtuous cycle.

**Google Seller Ratings integration.** One customer that implemented Trustpilot and became eligible for Google Store ratings saw an average 30% increase in click-through rate on those ads — a measurable commercial return that competitors without official Google Review Partner status cannot replicate.

**Financial momentum.** Trustpilot saw a 21% increase in bookings and record net dollar retention, and reached $250 million in annual recurring revenue, boosting profitability. The company also returned $43 million to shareholders via share buybacks in 2024 — signalling confidence in cash generation.

**Regulatory engagement.** Trustpilot became a founding member of the Coalition for Trusted Reviews in 2024, a cross-industry body that has helped promote best practices in the reviews industry, and has engaged with lawmakers to shape and define policy on reviews, including the FTC, the Digital Markets, Competition and Consumers Act in the UK, and the Fitness Check of EU consumer law in Europe.

### 5.2 Weaknesses

**Structural conflict of interest.** Trustpilot "always faced a fundamental conflict in its business model as a third-party review site that generates its revenues from selling subscriptions to companies" and "must balance the integrity of its reviews with the value they can provide to paying businesses." This is not merely a reputational risk; it is a structural incentive misalignment that no amount of AI moderation fully resolves.

**Dark pattern practices and selective invitation.** The Italian Competition Authority found that the platform's review collection services allowed businesses to hand-pick which consumers received review invitations, undermining the representativeness of published ratings even when reviews were labelled as "verified." The regulator also determined Trustpilot deployed "dark pattern" interface techniques to obscure which businesses paid for services, in breach of Italy's consumer code.

**SMB churn and subscriber volume stagnation.** The number of paying customers, net of churn, increased only 2% year-on-year to 26,031 — meaning revenue growth is driven almost entirely by ACV expansion rather than subscriber volume growth. This dependence on upselling a slowly growing subscriber base creates vulnerability if enterprise customers face budget pressure.

**Open platform vulnerability.** Unlike verified-purchase-only platforms such as Feefo, Trustpilot allows any consumer to review any business, whether or not they have transacted with it. Grizzly Research cited a "flourishing ecosystem of third-party entities that sell fake reviews on Trustpilot and openly advertise the success of specific profiles."

### 5.3 Opportunities

**AI-driven business intelligence.** Trustpilot launched an AI insights tool to surface operational pain points from aggregated review data — a capability that transforms it from a reputation management tool into a business intelligence platform commanding higher ACV. The corpus of 330 million independent reviews is a dataset no competitor possesses.

**Regulatory tailwind.** Article 7 of the amended Unfair Commercial Practices Directive prohibits misleading practices like fake or manipulated reviews, and platforms face obligations to implement systems for review moderation and user education on authenticity. The Digital Services Act, which entered into force in 2024, reinforces these requirements by mandating illegal content removal and active cooperation from major platforms. Trustpilot's compliance infrastructure, if sufficiently credible, becomes a competitive differentiator relative to captive on-site review systems.

**North American growth.** North America bookings are expected to rise 21% to $62 million from $52 million — from a lower base than UK/Europe — representing the largest single untapped geographic opportunity.

### 5.4 Threats

**Regulatory escalation.** Italy's competition regulator, the AGCM, fined Trustpilot €4 million for breaking consumer protection laws in the way it operates its business. The fine comes months after short-seller Grizzly Research accused Trustpilot of creating fake profiles that gave negative reviews and then pressuring companies to pay for subscriptions. A finding in one EU member state can inform proceedings in others, and the documented evidentiary record can be cited by the FTC and CMA.

**Reputational contagion.** Trustpilot's share price fell by over 30% following the Grizzly Research report, wiping around £240 million from its value. The long-term damage to Trustpilot's reputation is harder to quantify.

**Google's structural advantage.** For an e-commerce brand or SaaS company without a strong local search component, Trustpilot offers clear value; but for businesses with local presence, Google Reviews directly improves Google Maps ranking, local SEO visibility, and click-through rate in ways Trustpilot's free listings cannot match.

## 6. Analysis — Part III: The Core Strategic Tension (Two-Sided Platform Theory)

### 6.1 Two-Sided Platform Dynamics

Rochet and Tirole (2003) establish that two-sided platforms create value by enabling interactions between two distinct user groups, with cross-side network effects: the value of the platform to each side increases with the size of the other side. Two-sided platforms, by playing an intermediary role, produce certain value for both users that are interconnected through it, and the presence of positive cross-side effects, potentially negative same-side effects, and asymmetric pricing are characteristic features.

Trustpilot is a canonical two-sided platform: consumers (who read and write reviews) and businesses (who claim profiles and pay for management tools) are the two sides, connected through review content. The critical insight from platform theory is that the platform must manage the interests of both sides simultaneously. Platforms that over-serve one side at the expense of the other destroy the cross-side network effects that make them valuable.

Trustpilot's competitive edge derives from an open, high-authority platform with a continuously refreshed review corpus, strong integrity systems that detect and remove fraudulent reviews, and network effects that increase search ranking and merchant retention. The network effect logic is clear: more reviews → more consumer trust → more business subscribers → more review invitations → more reviews. But the integrity of this flywheel depends entirely on consumer confidence that reviews are authentic.

### 6.2 The Institutional Moral Hazard Problem

Institutions that broadcast reputations in a marketplace confront two intertwined moral hazards. The first is the classic hazard of trust between buyers and sellers. The second arises at the institutional level: the platform itself may compromise accuracy of the reputation system, exaggerating positive signals to encourage transactions that boost its commission revenue. This is precisely the structural dynamic that critics allege Trustpilot has failed to manage.

Trustpilot's response to the Grizzly allegations — publishing data showing that 14% of reviews were negative for free business profiles and 14% were also negative for paid business profiles — addresses the first-order claim (are paid profiles treated differently?) but does not fully resolve the second-order concern, which is about whether the *threshold* of enforcement and the *design* of invitation systems subtly advantages paying subscribers in ways that aggregate statistics do not capture.

### 6.3 The Freemium Trap

Trustpilot's freemium model creates a specific variant of this problem. The free tier allows businesses to claim profiles and receive unsolicited reviews — maximising consumer-side utility and creating a large funnel of potential paying customers. Businesses find themselves caught in a dilemma: do they pay to protect their reputations, or do they risk the potential fallout from negative reviews that they believe are illegitimate?

The Italian AGCM's finding that businesses could hand-pick review invitation recipients reveals the structural consequence of this model: the paid tier offers a partial solution (more invitations to satisfied customers), but the solution itself introduces a selection bias. The platform's review collection services allowed businesses to hand-pick which consumers received review invitations, undermining the representativeness of published ratings even when reviews were labelled as "verified." This is the freemium trap: the free tier creates the problem; the paid tier offers a solution that, by its design, introduces a bias that regulators interpret as manipulation.

## 7. Analysis — Part IV: Escalating Threats (2024–2026)

### 7.1 The Grizzly Research Attack (December 2024)

Grizzly Research said its research uncovered "mafia-style extortion campaigns against non-paying businesses" and claimed that paid-for profiles "see their review score magically lift from under 2 of 5 stars to over 4 of 5 stars." Grizzly further cited a "flourishing ecosystem of third-party entities that sell fake reviews on Trustpilot and openly advertise the success of specific profiles," and concluded that "while extortion tactics might boost revenues in the short term, they lead to a rapid degradation in the value proposition for website visitors who want to get objective reviews."

Trustpilot's response was categorical: "It is false to claim that we have traded the integrity of reviews for revenue. We have c.200 people focused on ensuring trust and integrity across our platform and have removed 6.7 million reviews from the platform so far this year. We have successfully brought lawsuits against bad actor businesses and review sellers."

The strategic significance of this event is not whether the allegations are true but what they reveal about Trustpilot's **vulnerability to narrative attacks on its integrity**. London-listed shares of Trustpilot fell about 30% after the short seller alleged that the review platform was running a "mafia-style extortion" campaign. A platform whose entire value proposition is trust is uniquely exposed to credibility challenges.

### 7.2 The Italian AGCM Fine (March 2026)

Italy's competition regulator, the AGCM, fined Trustpilot €4 million for breaking consumer protection laws in the way it operates its business. The fine, which Trustpilot said it would appeal, comes as the company has been seeing a surge in revenues due to the use of its content by AI search providers. The AGCM found that Trustpilot allowed businesses to hand-pick which consumers received review invitations — undermining the representativeness of published ratings even when labelled "verified" — and deployed "dark pattern" interface techniques to obscure which businesses paid for services, in breach of Italy's consumer code.

The fine's significance transcends its financial value. It represents the first time a national regulator has formally found that Trustpilot's operating practices breach consumer protection law. The fine comes months after short-seller Grizzly Research's accusations, which the company rejected. The sequencing matters: the short-seller attack preceded the regulatory finding, suggesting that the allegations, even if contested, directed regulatory attention toward genuine operational vulnerabilities.

### 7.3 The Regulatory Environment

Both events occur within a rapidly hardening regulatory context. The Digital Services Act became fully operational in February 2024, making digital companies across the EU accountable for the content posted on their platforms. Article 7 of the amended Unfair Commercial Practices Directive prohibits misleading practices like fake or manipulated reviews, and platforms face obligations to implement systems for review moderation and user education on authenticity. The Omnibus Directive imposes transparency obligations concerning the verification of online reviews, aligned with ISO 20488 standards.

## 8. Strategic Options

The analysis generates three broad options, each with a distinct logic and trade-offs.

**Option A — Integrity-First Repositioning:** Radically strengthen verification standards, moving toward purchase-verified-only for the "verified" badge and eliminating selective invitation. This prioritises the long-term credibility asset over short-term commercial flexibility but risks reducing the commercial value of the paid tier and accelerating SMB churn.

**Option B — Enterprise Deepening and Vertical Integration:** Double down on enterprise segments, deepening integrations and developing AI-powered business intelligence, accepting the structural integrity tension as a manageable operational risk. This maximises near-term revenue growth but does not address the underlying credibility problem and leaves the platform exposed to further regulatory shocks.

**Option C — Structural Separation:** Legally or operationally separate the consumer-facing review platform from the business-facing SaaS product, creating a "Chinese wall" between content integrity decisions and commercial relationships. This addresses the institutional moral hazard at its root but introduces operational complexity and risks being perceived as cosmetic.

## 9. Recommendations and Action Plan

The analysis supports a **hybrid strategy** combining Options A and B, with structural transparency measures drawn from Option C. The five recommendations are sequenced to address root causes before symptoms.

### Recommendation 1: Structurally Separate Content Integrity from Commercial Operations

**Rationale.** The credibility gap cannot be resolved by communication alone. The platform's choices about signal accuracy shape the alignment — or misalignment — between institutional profit and social welfare. Structural independence of content enforcement from commercial relationships is the only credible resolution.

**Action.** Establish a formally independent Content Integrity Board with a majority of external members — consumer advocates, independent technologists, and a former regulator. This board holds sole authority over enforcement policy and algorithmic parameters. Publish quarterly enforcement reports disaggregated by subscription tier, sector, and geography, audited by a named third party (e.g., a Big Four firm). This operationalises the transparency that Trustpilot currently claims but cannot fully demonstrate.

**Timeline.** 12–18 months (governance restructuring and auditor procurement).

### Recommendation 2: Reform the "Verified" Label — End Selective Invitation

**Rationale.** The platform's review collection services allowed businesses to hand-pick which consumers received review invitations, undermining the representativeness of published ratings even when reviews were labelled as "verified." This is the most operationally specific and legally documented weakness in the current model.

**Action.** Redesign the invitation system so that "verified" reviews are triggered automatically by transaction data (via e-commerce integrations with Shopify, BigCommerce, etc.) rather than manually selected by businesses. Reviews generated from manually filtered lists should be labelled "invited" rather than "verified." This aligns with the Omnibus Directive's transparency obligations concerning the verification of online reviews, aligned with ISO 20488 standards.

**Timeline.** 6–12 months. The technical infrastructure already exists; the change requires commercial renegotiation with subscribers who currently rely on filtered lists.

### Recommendation 3: Proactive Regulatory Co-Creation

**Rationale.** Trustpilot is currently in a reactive posture with regulators. Given the hardening environment, a reactive posture is strategically inferior to a proactive one. Trustpilot has engaged with lawmakers to shape and define policy on reviews, including the FTC, the Digital Markets, Competition and Consumers Act in the UK, and EU consumer law — but engagement in consultation processes is different from leading standard-setting.

**Action.** Expand the Coalition for Trusted Reviews into a formal standard-setting body with regulatory observer status. Propose an industry-wide "Review Platform Integrity Standard" (building on ISO 20488) that Trustpilot is first to certify against. Engage the AGCM in a structured dialogue, offering to implement the verification reforms in Recommendation 2 as part of an agreed remediation plan rather than a contested appeal. This converts a regulatory liability into a competitive differentiator: certification under a Trustpilot-designed standard becomes a barrier to entry for smaller competitors.

**Timeline.** Ongoing; first standard published within 18 months.

### Recommendation 4: Accelerate AI-Powered Business Intelligence Upsell

**Rationale.** Trustpilot launched an AI insights tool to surface operational pain points from aggregated review data — a capability that transforms the platform from a reputation management tool into a business intelligence platform. This is where the highest ACV expansion opportunity lies, and where Trustpilot's competitive moat is deepest: no competitor has an equivalent corpus of independent, cross-sector review data. Net dollar retention came in at approximately 102%, meaning the existing base is modestly expanding — targeted investment in AI analytics could push this materially higher.

**Action.** Invest in a dedicated "Trustpilot Intelligence" product line offering: (a) real-time sentiment dashboards segmented by product, location, and customer cohort; (b) competitive benchmarking against anonymised industry peers; (c) predictive churn signals derived from review sentiment trends. Price this as a premium add-on to the Scale tier, targeting enterprise customers with $50k+ ACV. Beta with 50 enterprise customers within 12 months; full launch within 24 months.

### Recommendation 5: Reframe the Brand Narrative — from "Trust" to "Transparency"

**Rationale.** Trustpilot's stated vision is to become the "Universal Symbol of Trust." By fostering a layer of mutually beneficial trust, Trustpilot creates socio-economic value in the communities it serves. But "trust us" is an unverifiable claim. Any allegation of inauthenticity is an attack on the brand's core proposition. The narrative must evolve from an assertion to a demonstration.

**Action.** Relaunch the corporate brand around transparency as the *mechanism* of trust: "Transparency you can verify." Anchor this to the structural reforms in Recommendations 1 and 2 — the independent integrity board, the reformed verification label, the quarterly enforcement audit. Convert the annual Trust Report from a defensive document into an offensive thought-leadership asset: commission and publish an independent "State of Online Reviews" report that positions Trustpilot as the authoritative voice on review integrity globally. Trustpilot's existing engagement with regulators and industry bodies provides the credibility foundation for this repositioning.

**Timeline.** Brand relaunch within 6 months, timed to coincide with announcement of the independent integrity board.

## 10. Conclusion

Trustpilot's strategic situation in 2024–2026 is a textbook illustration of the platform integrity dilemma: a two-sided platform whose revenue model creates structural incentives that, if unchecked, erode the very credibility asset on which the model depends. The company's financial performance — 18% revenue growth, first statutory profitability, 21% bookings growth — demonstrates that the current model is commercially viable in the short term. The Grizzly Research attack, the Italian regulatory fine, and the hardening global regulatory environment demonstrate that it is strategically fragile in the medium term.

The analytical chain is coherent. Porter's Five Forces reveals that Trustpilot's competitive advantage is concentrated in its integrity-backed network effects and Google integration, and that substitution from Google Reviews is the most potent structural threat — meaning integrity is not merely an ethical obligation but a commercial necessity. SWOT analysis identifies the structural conflict of interest and dark pattern practices as the most material weaknesses, and the regulatory tailwind as the most underexploited opportunity. Two-sided platform theory explains why the integrity dilemma cannot be resolved without changing the incentive architecture, not just the messaging. The institutional moral hazard framework explains why the Grizzly allegations — whether or not accurate in detail — are strategically credible: they describe a pattern of behaviour that the platform's incentive structure makes rational.

The recommended action plan — structural separation of content integrity, verification reform, regulatory co-creation, AI product expansion, and brand narrative reframing — is designed to resolve the dilemma at its structural root. The sequencing matters: Recommendations 1 and 2 must precede Recommendation 5, because a transparency brand narrative built on unreformed practices will accelerate rather than arrest the credibility erosion. If Trustpilot executes this plan, it has a realistic path to becoming what it claims to be: the universal symbol of trust in digital commerce. If it does not, the Italian fine is a preview of a longer regulatory and reputational reckoning.
