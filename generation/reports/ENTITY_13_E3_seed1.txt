# DIAGEO PLC: STRATEGIC CASE STUDY

## *Navigating a Multi-Front Crisis: Portfolio Concentration, Demand Softness, and the Limits of Premiumisation*

---

## 1. INTRODUCTION AND CASE CONTEXT

Diageo plc is the world's largest premium spirits company by volume, recognised as the largest spirits marketer globally, managing around 230 million cases, with operations spanning over 180 countries. Its portfolio includes iconic billion-dollar brands such as Johnnie Walker, Guinness, Smirnoff, Don Julio, and Baileys. Yet despite these structural advantages, Diageo has entered a period of acute strategic stress. Reported operating profit declined 27.8% in FY2025, and reported operating profit margin declined 819 basis points, primarily due to exceptional impairment and restructuring costs, unfavourable foreign exchange, and a decline in organic operating margin. This case study examines the strategic situation confronting Diageo, analyses it through established frameworks—PESTEL, Porter's Five Forces, the Resource-Based View (RBV), and Ansoff's Growth Matrix—and derives a set of actionable recommendations.

The **central strategic problem** is this: Diageo built its growth model around premiumisation in concentrated geographies (North America and China), but both have deteriorated simultaneously. The company must now decide whether to defend that model, pivot to new growth engines, or fundamentally restructure its portfolio. Each choice has different implications for competitive position, capital allocation, and long-term value.

## 2. SITUATIONAL ANALYSIS

### 2.1 The Strategic Situation in Numbers

Diageo's FY2025 reported net sales of $20.2 billion declined 0.1%, due to unfavourable foreign exchange of (0.6)% and acquisition and disposal adjustments of (1.1)%, partially offset by hyperinflation adjustments and organic net sales growth of 1.7%. While organic growth returned, the headline numbers mask a bifurcated business: strong performance in Europe, Africa, and Latin America & Caribbean (LAC) is being structurally offset by weakness in the two most strategically important markets.

Solid organic net sales growth in Europe, LAC, and Africa was offset by weakness in Chinese White Spirits (CWS) impacting Asia Pacific results and softer performance in North America, as US Spirits declined reflecting weak consumer confidence. Weakness in CWS in China negatively impacted group net sales by approximately 2.5% in the quarter.

In North America specifically, US Spirits organic net sales were down 4.1%, with overall net sales impacted by lapping tough comparatives in tequila last year in Don Julio given restocking and additional size extensions.

### 2.2 The Deeper Structural Tension

These are not merely cyclical headwinds. They reflect a structural tension at the heart of Diageo's strategy. Diageo set out its ambition in 2021 to grow its share of the Total Beverage Alcohol (TBA) market from 4% to 6% by 2030. Achieving that ambition requires volume and value growth precisely in the markets now deteriorating. TBA has grown at a 4.4% CAGR over the past decade, and international spirits has grown at 5.1% over the same period, as measured by IWSR. But the conditions that drove that decade of growth—post-pandemic restocking, premiumisation tailwinds, and buoyant consumer confidence—have reversed in Diageo's core markets.

## 3. PESTEL ANALYSIS: THE MACRO ENVIRONMENT

The PESTEL framework surfaces the external forces shaping Diageo's strategic context. Each factor is assessed for its direction and magnitude of impact.

### 3.1 Political

Geopolitical risk has become a first-order strategic variable. Diageo is taking a number of actions to mitigate the impact and disruption to its business that tariffs may cause, while also continuing to engage with the US administration on the broader impact that tariffs will have on everyone supporting the US hospitality industry, including consumers, employees, distributors, restaurants, bars, and other retail outlets. The mechanism linking tariffs to strategy is direct: Diageo's Scotch whisky is produced in Scotland and exported to the US, its largest single market. Tariffs raise landed costs, either compressing margins if Diageo absorbs them or reducing volume if prices are passed to consumers. Tariff mitigation tactics include price hikes, inventory preloading, and portfolio swaps—for example, substituting Cîroc for Lobos 1707 tequila—to offset an estimated $150 million in annual tariff costs. This confirms that the tariff threat is not notional: it is already shaping portfolio and pricing decisions.

### 3.2 Economic

The consumer demand environment has weakened materially across Diageo's key markets. After a difficult year for beverage alcohol in 2024, when total beverage alcohol volumes declined by 1% and revenue gains were elusive, with value up by only 1%, brand owners are facing an ever more complex and changeable marketplace. This macro deterioration is particularly acute in the US. US Spirits declined, reflecting weak consumer confidence. In China, the situation is structural rather than cyclical: China experienced volume declines across a number of categories, amid concerns over slowing economic growth and rising unemployment among younger legal drinking age consumers.

The economic stress is compounded by the leverage position Diageo has accumulated. The leverage ratio stood at 3.1x net debt to EBITDA as at 31 December 2024, which is above Diageo's own stated target range of 2.5–3.0x, constraining financial flexibility precisely when the company needs to invest in recovery.

### 3.3 Social

Two intersecting social trends are structurally reshaping demand. First, the moderation movement: the moderation movement and health-conscious trends significantly restrain the premium alcohol market, as Gen Z alcohol consumption leans toward low- or no-alcohol alternatives, with consumers increasingly prioritising wellness. Second, the "sober-curious" phenomenon is accelerating: no/low-alcohol volumes grew 9% in 2024, outpacing full-strength categories in most regions. These trends are not peripheral. They represent a generational shift in the consumer base that underpins Diageo's long-term volume assumptions.

However, the social landscape also contains opportunity. Diageo's strategy document projects that 600 million new legal-purchase-age consumers will enter the market by 2030, with India expected to account for a quarter of that growth, and the expanding global middle class further contributing to industry growth. This demographic dividend is real, but it is geographically concentrated in markets where Diageo currently under-indexes.

### 3.4 Technological

Technology is reshaping both the demand and supply sides of the industry. Diageo reduced development costs (non-working) to 14% of A&P spend in fiscal 2025, down from 21% in fiscal 2024, leveraging AI-driven content production and agile ways of working across its marketing function, including Agile Brand Communities. This is strategically significant: it means Diageo is extracting efficiency from its marketing spend, improving the productivity of investment in brand equity. On the supply side, AI-driven supply-chain optimisation reduced stockouts and improved working-capital rotation in 2024–25.

### 3.5 Environmental

Sustainability obligations are becoming a source of both cost and competitive differentiation. Diageo has committed to carbon-neutral production targets. While these do not represent an immediate financial crisis, they do impose capital expenditure obligations that compete with brand investment and debt reduction. ESG credentials are increasingly relevant to institutional investors and, in premium segments, to consumers.

### 3.6 Legal

Regulatory pressure on alcohol is intensifying globally. Regulatory pressures in a number of global markets reinforce moderation trends, as does the influence of guidance on alcohol consumption from governments and world agencies. In specific markets, advertising restrictions, health warning requirements, and minimum unit pricing policies are tightening. This is a slow-burn threat, but one that Diageo's compliance and government affairs teams must treat as a structural constraint on marketing strategy.

**PESTEL Summary Warrant:** The macro environment is structurally adverse in Diageo's two largest markets (North America and China) and is subject to emergent long-run headwinds (moderation, regulation). However, demographic growth in emerging markets and the productivity gains from technology represent genuine offsets. The net implication is that Diageo cannot rely on its existing geographic and product mix to restore growth; it must actively rebalance.

## 4. PORTER'S FIVE FORCES: INDUSTRY STRUCTURE

Porter's Five Forces analysis identifies where competitive pressure is most acute and where Diageo's structural position is strongest.

### 4.1 Competitive Rivalry (HIGH)

Diageo's most prominent direct competitors in the spirits sector include Pernod Ricard, Bacardi, Beam Suntory, Brown-Forman, Constellation Brands, LVMH's Moët Hennessy Division, Campari Group, William Grant & Sons, and Rémy Cointreau. Rivalry is intensifying as the category growth rate slows, turning the competitive dynamic from expansion to share-taking. The attempted merger between Pernod Ricard and Brown-Forman—though ultimately unsuccessful—illustrates the industry's consolidation logic: the global spirits industry is sliding into a phase of defensive consolidation, with merger talks between Pernod Ricard and Brown-Forman reflecting common industry struggles of slowing growth and bloated inventories. This consolidation pressure means Diageo faces the prospect of a more formidable second-tier competitor, which would intensify rivalry further.

### 4.2 Threat of New Entrants (MODERATE-LOW)

Barriers to entry into this industry remain high and stable, even for the production of white spirits—besides having to deal with the difficult access to limited resources, distillers also have to cope with the complications imposed by the industry's capital intensity, legal requirements, and distribution challenges. The time required to age premium Scotch whisky or tequila creates an inherent barrier that cannot be replicated quickly. However, craft spirits and celebrity-backed brands (e.g., the Casamigos model) can achieve rapid scale with lower capital, representing a more targeted form of new entry at the premium end.

### 4.3 Threat of Substitutes (HIGH AND RISING)

The substitution threat is the most structurally significant force acting on Diageo. Substitutes operate on two levels. First, within alcohol: beer and wine saw decreases in both sales and volume in 2024, but spirits experienced slight overall growth, with growth within spirits almost entirely attributable to tequila and ready-to-drink products. This means that within-alcohol substitution is already compressing Diageo's Scotch and vodka categories. Second, outside alcohol: the emergence of GLP-1 drugs remains a potential headwind, leading some fund managers to sell their stakes in drinks companies because of concerns about the entire drinks sector. GLP-1 drugs suppress appetite and cravings and are being adopted at scale in the US—Diageo's largest market. While the direct causal link to spirits consumption is not yet established empirically, the directional risk is credible and is already affecting investor sentiment.

### 4.4 Bargaining Power of Buyers (MODERATE AND INCREASING)

Buyers operate at two levels: trade (distributors, retailers, on-trade) and consumers. At the trade level, large distributors and retailers have significant leverage in shelf-space and listing negotiations. At the consumer level, health concerns and economic considerations are leading consumers to drink less, but to choose higher-quality when they do. This "drink less but better" dynamic is double-edged: it supports premiumisation for strong brands but increases the penalty for brands perceived as offering insufficient value. The data shows consumers are exercising this power: even beyond tequila, weak consumer demand for high-end premium and super-premium priced liquors was cited by the Distilled Spirits Council of the US as the key factor behind slow US spirits sales, with total spirit sales in 2023 largely flat.

### 4.5 Bargaining Power of Suppliers (LOW)

Diageo's scale gives it significant leverage over agricultural commodity suppliers (grain, agave, barley). Its investment in owned production assets—distilleries, cooperages, agave fields—further insulates it from upstream price shocks. This remains a structural advantage.

**Five Forces Summary Warrant:** The industry structure is deteriorating from Diageo's perspective: rivalry is intensifying, substitution threats are rising, and buyer power is increasing. The two forces that remain favourable—barriers to entry and supplier power—are precisely the forces that Diageo's scale and assets already exploit. The implication is that Diageo cannot simply defend its current position; the industry structure is moving against it.

## 5. RESOURCE-BASED VIEW: DIAGEO'S COMPETITIVE ADVANTAGES

The RBV (Barney, 1991) asks which of Diageo's resources are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN), and therefore a source of sustainable competitive advantage.

### 5.1 Brand Portfolio (VRIN: Strong)

Diageo's market position is fortified by significant competitive advantages, primarily its vast brand equity and an extensive portfolio of over 200 brands. Iconic names like Johnnie Walker, Guinness, Smirnoff, and Baileys foster deep consumer loyalty and broad market appeal across various price points and occasions. This brand equity is rare (decades of investment), inimitable (consumer trust cannot be purchased or replicated quickly), and valuable (it supports pricing power and shelf presence). The current crisis does not destroy this asset; it reveals which brands within the portfolio are truly VRIN and which are merely large.

The divergence within the portfolio is instructive. Don Julio delivered double-digit growth in all regions and gained share in measured markets representing over 90% of net sales; Guinness delivered double-digit growth for an eighth consecutive half. These brands are demonstrably VRIN. By contrast, Cîroc saw an organic sales decline of 26% in FY2024, with Jefferies estimating the disposal could be accretive by more than 50 basis points to group growth. Cîroc fails the VRIN test: its brand equity is neither rare nor inimitable in a crowded vodka market.

### 5.2 Global Distribution Network (VRIN: Strong)

All of Diageo's assets are backed by a massive distribution network built on top of privileged connections—both direct and indirect—with bars, clubs, wholesalers, casinos, restaurants, and other retail outlets. This network is a genuine VRIN resource: it took decades to build, cannot be replicated by a new entrant quickly, and provides a structural advantage in route-to-consumer that amplifies the value of every brand in the portfolio.

### 5.3 Maturing Inventory (VRIN: Strong, Scotch-specific)

Diageo's competitive edge rests on deep brand equity, unmatched Scotch maturing inventory, and scale advantages that underpin production, distribution, and M&A capacity. The maturing inventory of aged Scotch whisky is a textbook VRIN asset: it is valuable (premium pricing), rare (finite supply), inimitable (cannot be accelerated), and non-substitutable (aged Scotch is categorically distinct from younger whisky). This asset underpins Diageo's long-term pricing power in Scotch even through demand cycles.

### 5.4 Non-Alcoholic Portfolio (Emerging VRIN)

Diageo's non-alc portfolio organic net sales grew approximately 40% in fiscal 2025. The company is more than four times the size of its nearest competitor in non-alcoholic spirits. This early-mover advantage in non-alcoholic spirits is a potentially VRIN resource if Diageo can extend its lead before the category matures. The mechanism is straightforward: as the moderation trend accelerates, a credible non-alcoholic portfolio allows Diageo to retain consumers who would otherwise exit the category entirely.

**RBV Summary Warrant:** Diageo's most durable advantages—brand equity in select brands, distribution, and maturing inventory—remain intact. The crisis does not undermine these assets; it exposes the fact that a significant portion of Diageo's portfolio does not share these VRIN characteristics. The RBV therefore supports a portfolio rationalisation strategy: concentrate resources on VRIN assets and divest those that are not.

## 6. ANSOFF MATRIX: GROWTH OPTIONS

The Ansoff Matrix maps Diageo's growth options across existing/new products and existing/new markets, allowing systematic evaluation of strategic alternatives.

### 6.1 Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets)

In North America, Diageo's largest market, the strategy of pushing existing premium spirits harder is constrained by the demand environment. However, Scotch growth was strong, with double-digit growth on Johnnie Walker, and strong performance on ready-to-serve and ready-to-drink products was led by the Bulleit and Ketel One Cocktail Collections and the newly launched Casamigos RTD. This suggests that within North America, the opportunity lies in shifting the *format* of existing brands (into RTDs) rather than simply pushing the neat spirit harder. Market penetration through format innovation is therefore viable.

### 6.2 Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets)

This is the most compelling near-term growth quadrant. Diageo aims to rebalance growth beyond a soft North America in FY2024–FY2025 toward Europe, Africa, India, and Travel Retail, targeting mid- to high-single-digit organic net sales growth over the cycle. The demographic rationale is strong: India is expected to account for a quarter of legal-purchase-age consumer growth by 2030. Diageo is already acting on this: Diageo is a global leader in whisky and is driving the category forward in India with the introduction of Godawan, a locally crafted Indian single malt. Market development in India and Africa offers the highest risk-adjusted return because it deploys VRIN assets (global brands, distribution capability) into markets with structural growth tailwinds.

### 6.3 Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets)

The no/low alcohol segment represents the most strategically urgent product development opportunity. Non-alcoholic and RTD categories expanded, with Guinness 0.0 and Tanqueray 0.0 seeing 15% year-on-year demand growth for premium alcohol-free alternatives. Diageo is extending this: Diageo continues to strategically acquire brands that tap into emerging consumer trends, such as the September 2024 acquisition of a non-alcoholic spirits brand. Product development in no/low is not merely defensive; it is a mechanism for retaining consumers who are moderating, thereby protecting the long-run customer base.

### 6.4 Diversification (New Products, New Markets)

Diversification carries the highest risk and is least supported by Diageo's current VRIN resources. The company should avoid diversification beyond adjacent beverage alcohol categories.

**Ansoff Summary Warrant:** The matrix reveals that Diagoff's most viable growth paths are market development (emerging markets) and product development (no/low, RTDs), both of which leverage existing VRIN assets. Market penetration in core markets remains relevant but requires format innovation rather than price/volume pushes. Diversification is not warranted.

## 7. THE ACCELERATE PROGRAMME: CURRENT STRATEGIC RESPONSE

Diageo's management has responded to the crisis with the "Accelerate" programme. Launched in May 2025, this initiative is not merely a cost-cutting exercise but a comprehensive overhaul of the company's operating model, aimed at driving long-term value creation through operational excellence, strategic reinvestment, and market share gains. The cost savings target has been upgraded: cost savings guidance of approximately $625 million over the next three years is fully on track, up from an initial $500 million goal.

Portfolio rationalisation is underway. Savings from the Accelerate programme are being directed toward high-growth areas like Don Julio and Guinness, which saw double-digit sales growth in 2025. The company is also exiting underperforming assets, such as Cîroc, to sharpen its focus on brands with strong equity. The disposal of East African Breweries Limited (EABL) to Asahi also signals a willingness to exit businesses that, while profitable, are not core to Diageo's premium spirits identity. Diageo remains committed to returning the Group to well within its target leverage ratio range of 2.5–3.0x through disposals of non-strategic, non-core assets, alongside delivering positive operating leverage.

**Assessment of Accelerate:** The programme is directionally correct—it aligns with the RBV conclusion (concentrate on VRIN assets) and the Ansoff conclusion (invest in market development and product development). However, it faces three execution risks. First, the pace of disposals is constrained by buyer appetite and valuation uncertainty. Second, the cost savings are back-loaded over three years, providing limited near-term relief. Third, the programme does not fully address the structural demand headwinds identified in the PESTEL analysis—cost efficiency cannot substitute for volume growth.

## 8. STRATEGIC RECOMMENDATIONS

The following recommendations flow directly from the analytical chain above. Each is grounded in the evidence and frameworks deployed.

### Recommendation 1: Accelerate Portfolio Rationalisation Around VRIN Brands

**Rationale:** The RBV analysis identifies that Diageo's portfolio contains a small number of genuinely VRIN brands (Johnnie Walker, Guinness, Don Julio, Casamigos) and a larger number of brands that lack inimitability or rarity in their categories. The Five Forces analysis shows that competitive rivalry and buyer power are rising, making it increasingly costly to maintain scale across a sprawling portfolio. The Accelerate programme has begun this work but should be accelerated further.

**Action:** Diageo should establish an explicit "Core 20" brand portfolio that receives the majority of marketing and capital investment, and set a three-year timeline for disposing of all brands outside this set. Diageo is already looking to sell Cîroc vodka and could look to dispose of other subscale or underperforming labels, with a potential spinoff or sale of Guinness valued north of $10 billion also under consideration. While a Guinness disposal would be controversial, the principle is correct: capital should follow VRIN assets, not legacy scale.

**Warrant:** Concentration of marketing spend on fewer, stronger brands is empirically supported by Diageo's own data. Don Julio and Guinness—both beneficiaries of sustained investment—delivered double-digit growth even in a deteriorating market. The causal mechanism is clear: focused investment in brands with genuine brand equity generates returns that diversified investment across a large portfolio cannot.

### Recommendation 2: Pivot the Geographic Mix Toward Emerging Markets

**Rationale:** The PESTEL analysis shows that North America and China—currently approximately 39% and 10% of net sales respectively—face structural demand headwinds. The Ansoff analysis identifies market development in India, Africa, and LAC as the highest-return growth quadrant. The demographic data is compelling: India is expected to account for a quarter of legal-purchase-age consumer growth by 2030.

**Action:** Diageo should increase its capital allocation to India and Sub-Saharan Africa by redirecting the proceeds from portfolio disposals. Specifically, it should: (a) scale up the Godawan Indian single malt as a locally produced premium brand that complements imported Scotch; (b) invest in distribution infrastructure in Nigeria, Kenya, and South Africa to deepen route-to-consumer; and (c) leverage the Guinness brand's existing strength in Africa—where it is a culturally embedded brand—to drive volume in the beer and RTD segments.

**Warrant:** Diageo already has proof of concept. Africa, India, and Travel Retail are identified as rebalancing targets, with the aim of mid- to high-single-digit organic net sales growth over the cycle. The company's existing distribution infrastructure and brand recognition in these markets mean this is a market development play, not a diversification play—it uses VRIN resources in new geographies, which is the lowest-risk Ansoff quadrant after market penetration.

### Recommendation 3: Build a Structurally Distinct No/Low Alcohol Business Unit

**Rationale:** The PESTEL analysis establishes that the moderation trend is structural, not cyclical. No/low-alcohol volumes grew 9% in 2024, outpacing full-strength categories in most regions. The Five Forces analysis shows that this trend is a substitution threat. However, the RBV analysis reveals that Diageo has an early-mover advantage: Diageo is more than four times the size of its nearest competitor in non-alcoholic spirits. This advantage will erode if not reinforced by dedicated investment and organisational focus.

**Action:** Diageo should establish a dedicated No/Low Alcohol Business Unit with its own P&L, leadership team, and innovation budget. This unit should have explicit targets to grow no/low to 10% of net sales by FY2030 (from a current low base), using Guinness 0.0 and Tanqueray 0.0 as anchor brands and pursuing selective acquisitions of credible no/low brands to fill category gaps.

**Warrant:** The mechanism is clear: a dedicated business unit with its own P&L creates accountability and prevents no/low from being deprioritised in resource allocation decisions that favour the established full-strength portfolio. The competitive advantage in no/low is currently four times the nearest competitor, but this lead will compress as FMCG giants and craft producers enter the category. Structural separation is the mechanism for protecting the lead.

### Recommendation 4: Manage the Balance Sheet to Restore Strategic Flexibility

**Rationale:** The financial analysis shows that Diageo's leverage ratio of 3.1x exceeds its target range of 2.5–3.0x. The company aims to reduce its leverage to within the 2.5–3.0x target range by fiscal 2028 through a combination of organic growth, positive operating leverage, tighter capital discipline, and selective disposals. A leverage ratio above target is not merely a financial metric; it is a strategic constraint. It limits Diageo's ability to make acquisitions, invest in brand building, or weather further demand shocks without triggering covenant concerns.

**Action:** Diageo should prioritise disposal proceeds for debt reduction before brand investment, accepting short-term underinvestment in marketing to restore balance sheet flexibility by FY2027 rather than FY2028. The target of $3 billion free cash flow in FY2026 should be treated as a floor, not a ceiling. Diageo remains committed to delivering approximately $3 billion free cash flow in fiscal 2026 and returning to well within its target leverage ratio range of 2.5–3.0x no later than fiscal 2028, supported by appropriate and selective disposals.

**Warrant:** The causal logic is straightforward: a balance sheet within target range restores the optionality to make acquisitions at the bottom of the cycle (when valuations are depressed) and to increase brand investment when demand recovers. Conversely, remaining above target leverage during a period of demand uncertainty increases the risk of a credit rating downgrade, which would raise the cost of debt and further constrain strategic options.

### Recommendation 5: Develop a Tariff-Resilient Supply Chain Architecture

**Rationale:** The PESTEL political analysis identifies US tariffs as a near-term cost shock with an estimated $150 million annual impact. The Five Forces analysis shows that Diageo cannot easily pass this cost to consumers in a market where buyer power is already elevated by weak consumer confidence. The current mitigation tactics (inventory preloading, portfolio swaps) are tactical, not structural.

**Action:** Diageo should invest in US-based production capacity for its highest-volume US brands. This includes: (a) expanding the capacity of its US-based tequila bottling and blending operations; (b) exploring a US-based malt whisky distillery (a "Scotch-style" American whisky) that can be positioned as a premium American product while leveraging Diageo's blending expertise; and (c) accelerating the growth of US-produced brands (Crown Royal is Canadian; Don Julio is Mexican) to reduce the proportion of net sales exposed to import tariffs.

**Warrant:** The mechanism is geographic diversification of the production base, which directly reduces tariff exposure. This is not a substitution for Scotch whisky—the VRIN maturing inventory asset remains—but it reduces the proportion of US revenue that depends on cross-border trade, thereby lowering geopolitical risk concentration.

## 9. INTEGRATED STRATEGIC LOGIC

The five recommendations form a coherent chain rather than a list of independent actions:

1. **Portfolio rationalisation** (Rec. 1) frees capital and management attention.
2. **Disposal proceeds** reduce leverage (Rec. 4), restoring strategic flexibility.
3. **Strategic flexibility** enables investment in emerging market infrastructure (Rec. 2) and no/low innovation (Rec. 3).
4. **Emerging market growth** and no/low growth together rebalance the geographic and product mix away from the deteriorating North America/China premium spirits concentration.
5. **Supply chain restructuring** (Rec. 5) reduces the political risk premium on the North American business that remains.

The underlying logic is a managed transition from a model built on premiumisation in concentrated markets to a model built on VRIN brand equity deployed across a more diversified geographic and product base. This is not a rejection of Diageo's strategy; it is its evolution in response to a changed macro and competitive environment.
