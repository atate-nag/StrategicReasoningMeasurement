# CASE STUDY: De Beers Group — Strategic Disruption and the Fight for Relevance

## Anglo American's Diamond Subsidiary at the Crossroads, 2023–2025

---

## 1. Introduction and Case Context

De Beers Group is the world's most iconic diamond company and, until recently, one of Anglo American's most prized assets. Founded in 1888, it spent over a century as the undisputed architect of the global diamond market — controlling supply, shaping desire, and minting the phrase "A Diamond is Forever." By 2023–2025, however, the company faced the most structurally threatening crisis in its modern history: a convergence of demand collapse, the rise of laboratory-grown diamonds (LGDs), geopolitical complexity, and a parent company in radical restructuring.

This case study examines the strategic situation confronting De Beers between 2023 and 2025, applies Porter's Five Forces, a SWOT analysis, the Ansoff Matrix, and a value-chain lens to diagnose the problem, and concludes with a structured set of strategic recommendations. The analysis is built on a chain of sourced, connected inferences.

---

## 2. Background: Anglo American and De Beers

Anglo American plc, the London-listed diversified miner, holds an 85% stake in De Beers Group; the Government of Botswana holds the remaining 15%. De Beers is the world's largest diamond producer by value, accounting for an estimated 26% of global rough-production volume.

The strategic situation changed decisively in early 2024. In May 2024, Anglo American announced plans to separate De Beers and potentially exit steelmaking coal and certain nickel operations as part of a simplification strategy. This announcement was itself triggered by external pressure: BHP made a takeover bid valuing Anglo American at roughly $49 billion, which Anglo American's board rejected, arguing it undervalued the company and would introduce execution risks, particularly in relation to regulatory and political approvals in South Africa.

Anglo American's strategic response was to pivot sharply. Anglo American's transformation strategy focuses on creating a streamlined organisation prioritising high-margin commodities and value over volume, simplifying the portfolio to focus on copper, premium iron ore, and crop nutrients. De Beers — once a crown jewel — had become a liability on that balance sheet, and the scale of the problem demands careful analysis.

## 3. The Strategic Situation: Diagnosis

### 3.1 Financial Deterioration

The financial evidence of structural distress at De Beers is stark and consistent across multiple years. De Beers reported a 21% decline in total revenue from $2.8 billion in H1 2023 to $2.2 billion in H1 2024, and experienced a 22% year-on-year decrease in total rough diamond sales. This was not a one-quarter anomaly. Revenue declined 23% year-over-year to $3.29 billion in full-year 2024, with rough diamond sales falling 25% to $2.7 billion, and underlying EBITDA swung from $72 million positive in 2023 to negative $25 million in 2024.

The cumulative damage is even more revealing. Anglo American wrote down De Beers by a combined $6.8 billion across three years: $1.6 billion in 2023, $2.9 billion in 2024, and $2.3 billion in 2025, cutting its carrying value from over $4 billion to $2.3 billion. These impairments are not accounting technicalities — they signal that management no longer believes the business will generate the cash flows previously assumed, which is a direct warrant for concluding that the strategic model underlying De Beers is broken, not merely cyclically depressed.

A further operational indicator reinforces this conclusion. De Beers' rough diamond production slumped 22% to 24.7 million carats in 2024, with the company predicting a further reduction to between 20 million and 23 million carats in 2025 in line with demand expectations. And the inventory position — "It's been a bad year for rough diamond sales," CEO Al Cook told the Financial Times, with De Beers sitting on a diamond stockpile worth approximately $2 billion, the largest since the 2008 financial crisis. — confirms that the production cuts are demand-driven, not supply-driven. This matters analytically because it rules out the explanation that operational problems are the cause; the cause is market-structural.

### 3.2 Root Cause: The Laboratory-Grown Diamond Disruption

The primary structural driver of De Beers' crisis is the explosive growth of laboratory-grown diamonds (LGDs) as substitutes. According to The Knot, 52% of engagement ring centre stones were lab-grown in 2024, up from 12% in 2019. This is a fivefold increase in market share in five years — a pace of substitution that is categorically different from prior competitive threats. De Beers CEO Al Cook himself acknowledged the scale: his estimate was that the lab-grown craze cost the US natural diamond business 14% of the total market, or $7 billion, in 2023.

The economics of LGD production are the mechanism driving this substitution. Wholesale prices for a two-carat lab-grown diamond fell 34% from 2020 to 2024, while retail prices dropped 25%, squeezing margins. More dramatically, LGD prices have fallen by more than 90% in just two years in some segments, making them accessible to consumers who previously could not afford natural diamonds. The forward trajectory of the LGD market compounds the threat: the lab-grown diamond market is projected to grow from $26.05 billion in 2024 to $97.85 billion by 2034, representing a 14.15% CAGR.

This substitution dynamic is not merely a pricing problem — it is a demand-architecture problem. Natural and lab-grown diamonds are physically identical to the naked eye; therefore, the only defensible difference is one of narrative (origin, rarity, emotional meaning) rather than physical product. The strategic implication, which will be developed in the frameworks below, is that De Beers must compete on meaning, not matter.

### 3.3 Compounding Demand Headwinds

The LGD threat is amplified by macroeconomic and demographic headwinds. Economic challenges in China led to low consumer confidence in the diamond market, and the US is experiencing similar economic uncertainty that is preventing diamond purchases. China is the world's second-largest diamond market, and its property-sector-led slowdown has had a disproportionate impact on luxury discretionary spending. De Beers Group CEO Al Cook noted that "consumers in China are buying substantially fewer luxury products." These macroeconomic factors are exogenous to De Beers' strategy, but they amplify the structural LGD threat by reducing consumer willingness to pay a premium for natural stones.

## 4. Strategic Analysis

### 4.1 Porter's Five Forces

Porter's Five Forces framework (1980) analyses the structural attractiveness of an industry by examining five competitive forces. Applied to the natural diamond industry as experienced by De Beers, the framework reveals a significantly deteriorated competitive environment.

**Force 1 — Threat of Substitutes (HIGH and Rising)**

This is the dominant force reshaping the industry. The LGD substitution threat is high because the substitute is physically indistinguishable from the original, is produced at rapidly declining cost, and is gaining mainstream consumer acceptance. A recent McKinsey report raised the possibility that lab-grown diamonds could take over, relegating the rest of the market to niche segments. McKinsey identified two scenarios: one in which LGDs dominate outside luxury niches, and another in which LGD prices fall so far that they become fashion accessories and cease to compete with natural diamonds. In 2023, nearly half of all engagement ring centre stones in the US were lab-grown. The rate of substitution — from 12% in 2019 to 52% in 2024 — is the clearest quantitative evidence that the substitute force is not merely high but accelerating. This force directly explains De Beers' revenue and EBITDA collapse and warrants the conclusion that defending volume in the mass market is strategically futile; the only viable response is to retreat to the premium-luxury segment where narrative differentiation can sustain price.

**Force 2 — Bargaining Power of Buyers (INCREASING)**

Buyers (both midstream sightholders and end consumers) have gained power. Midstream sightholders — the rough diamond traders who purchase at De Beers' periodic "sights" — can now compare natural rough against LGDs and, with midstream inventory elevated, they have leverage to resist pricing. De Beers cut prices by 10% in its first sale of 2024 in an attempt to stimulate demand. End consumers have gained power because the existence of a credible, cheaper substitute means they can credibly threaten to switch. This is a textbook increase in buyer power arising from the availability of substitutes. The implication is that De Beers cannot sustain volume-based pricing power; it must shift to a differentiated, brand-premium model where buyers are willing to pay for something LGDs cannot replicate.

**Force 3 — Threat of New Entrants (MODERATE)**

Natural diamond mining has high barriers to entry (capital intensity, geological scarcity, regulatory requirements, long lead times). However, the threat of new entrants into the *LGD* segment is low-to-moderate because LGD production technology (CVD and HPHT) is increasingly commoditised. De Beers noted that "the wholesale prices of lab-grown diamonds are falling sharply, leading to financial challenges at some leading lab-grown diamond producers." This means that even within LGDs, competition is intensifying, which further compresses the pricing floor for natural diamonds. The net effect on De Beers is that the threat of new entrants into LGDs reinforces the substitution pressure.

**Force 4 — Bargaining Power of Suppliers (LOW for De Beers)**

De Beers is itself a vertically integrated miner-to-retailer; its primary "suppliers" are the mines it operates. The key supplier relationship is with the Government of Botswana through the Debswana joint venture. This relationship is a strategic asset but also a source of constraint. De Beers and the Government of Botswana signed formal new agreements for a 10-year sales agreement (extendable by five years) and a 25-year extension of Debswana mining licences through to 2054; Debswana operates the Jwaneng and Orapa diamond mines as a 50:50 joint venture between De Beers and the Government of Botswana. This long-term agreement secures supply but does not resolve the demand problem — it locks in costs at a time when revenues are declining. The warrant here is that supplier security is necessary but not sufficient for strategic recovery.

**Force 5 — Competitive Rivalry (HIGH)**

De Beers faces rivalry from Alrosa (Russia), Rio Tinto, and other natural diamond producers, as well as from LGD producers. De Beers accounts for an estimated 26% of global rough-production volume, second only to Russia's Alrosa, while it ranks as the largest supplier by value. The rivalry with Alrosa is particularly acute because Russian sanctions have created market uncertainty, but Alrosa continues to supply through alternative channels, maintaining competitive pressure on rough prices. Combined with the LGD substitution dynamic, competitive rivalry is high.

**Five Forces Summary:** The framework reveals that the natural diamond industry's structural attractiveness has deteriorated materially. The threat of substitutes and increased buyer power are the two dominant forces, both pointing to the same strategic imperative: De Beers must shift from volume-based commodity mining to a premium, differentiated, brand-driven model. The Five Forces analysis therefore provides the diagnostic foundation for the strategic recommendations below.

### 4.2 SWOT Analysis

**Strengths:**

- *Brand heritage and emotional equity.* De Beers possesses over 130 years of brand-building. Its "A Diamond is Forever" campaign, ranked among the top 100 advertising campaigns of all time, has embedded natural diamonds as the cultural symbol of enduring love. This is a non-replicable asset that LGD producers cannot appropriate.
- *Vertical integration and traceability.* De Beers' integration from mine to retail, combined with the Tracr blockchain platform, constitutes a distinctive capability. Tracr uses blockchain technology to enable an unalterable record of a diamond's provenance; the platform now has over three million diamonds registered at source. This is a source of differentiation precisely because it is impossible for LGDs to claim the same kind of natural provenance.
- *Element Six's industrial technology.* Element Six, as one of the world's largest suppliers of synthetic diamond and advanced material solutions, is trusted to develop novel solutions for the toughest technical challenges, thanks to its unique capabilities, know-how and patented technology. This positions De Beers in high-growth technology markets (semiconductors, quantum computing) independent of jewellery demand.
- *Long-term supply security.* The 25-year Debswana licence extension to 2054 secures access to some of the world's highest-value diamond deposits, including Jwaneng, which De Beers describes as "the richest diamond mine in the world by value."

**Weaknesses:**

- *Dependence on a single commodity category.* Unlike diversified miners, De Beers' core revenue is almost entirely derived from rough diamond sales, making it acutely vulnerable to demand shocks in that category.
- *Elevated midstream inventory.* Rough diamond production was reduced to 13.3 million carats in H1 2024 (compared to 16.5 million carats in H1 2023), reflecting the decision to intentionally lower production in response to higher than average levels of inventory in the midstream and cautious retailer restocking. This inventory overhang suppresses pricing power and signals that the midstream channel lacks confidence in near-term demand recovery.
- *Failed LGD experiment.* De Beers' Lightbox brand, launched in 2018 to commoditise LGDs and thereby protect natural diamond premiums, ultimately failed. De Beers CEO Al Cook noted that lab-grown diamonds had shifted to "low-margin fashion jewelry," undermining their appeal for high-value purchases; De Beers later confirmed its decision to halt production of synthetic diamonds for Lightbox. The strategic misalignment of Lightbox — attempting to compete in a market it was simultaneously trying to devalue — is a significant strategic misstep that consumed resources and management attention.

**Opportunities:**

- *Supply-side scarcity as a long-term moat.* Global supply of natural diamonds is declining with no new mines discovered in the past decade; McKinsey's analysis shows natural diamond production is expected to stay below pre-COVID-19 levels of 130 million carats per annum going forward due to mine depletion. If demand stabilises or recovers, constrained supply creates structural pricing power.
- *India's growing affluent consumer class.* In India, strong economic growth has underpinned positive natural diamond jewellery growth, providing a market where rising incomes and cultural affinity for natural diamonds can offset weakness in China.
- *Technology differentiation via provenance and authentication.* De Beers' DiamondProof detection device and Tracr platform can be deployed to make provenance verification a consumer-facing differentiator, converting a back-office capability into a front-end marketing tool.
- *Element Six's growth markets.* Element Six has entered a collaboration with Japanese manufacturer Orbray to develop the world's highest-quality wafer-scale single-crystal diamond, addressing the growing demand for energy-efficient solutions in sectors like artificial intelligence and telecommunications. This represents a strategic pivot toward technology markets with structural tailwinds.

**Threats:**

- *Accelerating LGD market share capture.* The LGD market's projected growth to $97.85 billion by 2034 at a 14.15% CAGR represents a permanent and growing pricing ceiling on natural diamonds in the mass market.
- *China macro-structural slowdown.* China's real estate-driven economic deceleration is not a cyclical blip; it represents a structural shift in luxury demand that may persist for years.
- *Anglo American divestment execution risk.* The planned sale or spin-off of De Beers introduces uncertainty about ownership, strategy continuity, and capital access. The De Beers divestment faces complex dynamics as Botswana, Angola, and Namibia governments compete alongside private bidders for control of the $3–5 billion diamond giant. Political complexity in the sale process could delay resolution and prolong strategic uncertainty.
- *Geopolitical and sanctions-related market distortion.* Alrosa's continued supply through alternative channels despite Western sanctions creates pricing competition that undermines De Beers' ability to manage rough diamond market conditions.

### 4.3 Ansoff Matrix: Strategic Growth Options

The Ansoff Matrix (1957) maps growth strategies along two axes — existing vs. new products, and existing vs. new markets — generating four strategic options. Applied to De Beers, this framework clarifies which growth paths are viable given the competitive context established by the Five Forces and SWOT analyses.

**Quadrant 1 — Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets):** Selling more natural rough diamonds to existing midstream and consumer markets is the lowest-risk Ansoff option in theory, but the Five Forces analysis has already demonstrated that this path is structurally blocked. Natural diamond prices declined by 6% in 2024 according to Zimnisky's global rough diamond index, and midstream inventory remains elevated. Attempting to penetrate existing markets with existing products would require price cuts that further erode margins — a self-defeating strategy. This quadrant is therefore ruled out as a primary strategy.

**Quadrant 2 — Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets):** De Beers is already pursuing this path. De Beers Jewellers will expand into 16 new markets by 2025, emphasising high-end storytelling and traceability via its Tracr platform. The India opportunity is particularly significant given its demonstrated growth trajectory. This is a viable and necessary strategy, but it is insufficient on its own because it does not address the structural LGD substitution threat in existing markets.

**Quadrant 3 — Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets):** This is the most strategically promising quadrant. De Beers' Origins strategy explicitly pursues product development through: (a) provenance-authenticated natural diamonds as a premium product category distinct from LGDs; (b) the DiamondProof authentication device as a retail-facing product; and (c) Element Six's expansion into semiconductors, quantum technologies, and industrial applications. De Beers is doubling down on natural diamonds, positioning them as premium, story-driven assets, with the company forecasting 3%–5% annual growth in natural diamond prices through 2032, driven by constrained supply and demand from emerging markets like India. The warrant for this strategy is that it converts De Beers' non-replicable asset — natural origin — into a product category defined by that attribute, rather than competing on price with LGDs.

**Quadrant 4 — Diversification (New Products, New Markets):** Element Six's expansion into quantum technologies and semiconductor applications represents a diversification strategy. By centralising CVD synthetic diamond production at its state-of-the-art facility in Oregon, Element Six will work with its growing global network of partners to accelerate cutting-edge technologies for high-growth industries, such as semiconductors and quantum technologies. This is the highest-risk Ansoff quadrant but offers the highest potential upside because it insulates De Beers from jewellery market volatility and taps into structural technology demand.

**Ansoff Summary:** The matrix analysis recommends a dual-track strategy: Market Development (India and other emerging affluent markets) combined with Product Development (provenance-authenticated luxury diamonds and technology products). Market Penetration in existing commodity markets is structurally blocked, and full Diversification carries execution risk but should be pursued incrementally through Element Six.

### 4.4 Value Chain Analysis

Porter's value chain framework (1985) distinguishes primary activities (inbound logistics, operations, outbound logistics, marketing and sales, service) from support activities (infrastructure, HRM, technology development, procurement). Applying this to De Beers identifies where value is being eroded and where it can be rebuilt.

**Primary Activities — Where Value is Leaking:**

- *Operations:* Mining costs remain largely fixed even as production is deliberately curtailed. De Beers is well on the way to delivering over $100 million in sustainable annual cost savings through a reorganisation and by focusing its capital spend. However, $100 million in savings against a $6.8 billion cumulative impairment signals that cost reduction alone cannot restore the business model.
- *Marketing and Sales:* The sightholder system — De Beers' controlled rough diamond sales mechanism — is under pressure because sightholders themselves face margin compression from LGD competition. The midstream's reluctance to restock is a symptom of this pressure.
- *Outbound Logistics (Downstream Retail):* De Beers' downstream retail presence (De Beers Jewellers) is underdeveloped relative to its brand potential. Expanding this channel, as planned, would capture more of the retail margin currently captured by third-party jewellers.

**Support Activities — Where Value Can Be Built:**

- *Technology Development:* Tracr and DiamondProof are genuine competitive advantages. The focus on provenance is part of De Beers' broader sustainability strategy, Building Forever, which centres on climate, livelihoods, nature, and transparency in the diamond supply chain. These technologies convert supply-chain transparency into a consumer-facing value proposition — the critical link in the value chain between mining origin and retail purchase.
- *Procurement and Supplier Relations:* The Debswana agreement secures the upstream supply chain for 25 years, providing a stable cost base. However, the value chain analysis reveals that the bottleneck is not upstream (supply is secured) but downstream (demand and differentiation).

**Value Chain Summary:** De Beers' value chain is strongest at the upstream (mining, provenance) and weakest at the downstream (consumer marketing, retail presence, brand activation). The strategic implication — which connects directly to the Ansoff and Five Forces analyses — is that De Beers must invest in downstream value chain activities to convert upstream advantages (natural origin, traceability) into consumer willingness to pay.

## 5. Synthesis: The Coherent Strategic Problem

The three frameworks converge on a single diagnosis. The Five Forces analysis establishes that the natural diamond industry's structural attractiveness has deteriorated due to LGD substitution and increased buyer power. The SWOT analysis identifies that De Beers' only non-replicable assets are its brand heritage, provenance capability, and geological supply security — none of which are being fully monetised. The Ansoff Matrix indicates that growth through existing-product, existing-market strategies is blocked, and that the viable paths are premium market development and product/technology development. The value chain analysis pinpoints the downstream as the critical gap between De Beers' upstream strengths and its revenue recovery.

The coherent strategic problem is therefore: *De Beers possesses unique, non-replicable assets (natural origin, heritage brand, provenance technology, geological scarcity) that are currently undermonetised because the company's business model is still oriented toward volume-based rough diamond sales rather than premium, differentiated, narrative-driven consumer value.*

## 6. Recommendations and Action Plan

The following recommendations are derived directly from the analytical chain above. Each recommendation is connected to a specific analytical finding and includes the warrant explaining why the evidence supports the prescribed action.

### Recommendation 1: Execute a Full Luxury Repositioning of Natural Diamonds

**What:** Reposition natural diamonds as a luxury category defined by provenance, rarity, and emotional narrative — explicitly distinct from LGDs, which are repositioned as fashion accessories.

**Why (Warrant):** The Five Forces analysis establishes that De Beers cannot compete on price against LGDs (the substitute force is structurally insurmountable at the mass-market level). The SWOT analysis identifies brand heritage as the primary non-replicable strength. The Ansoff Matrix confirms that product development (premium, differentiated natural diamonds) is the viable growth path. De Beers CEO Al Cook noted that "consumers in key regions are becoming more affluent and are increasingly differentiating between natural diamonds and lab-grown diamonds," which provides the demand-side evidence that a luxury repositioning can find a receptive audience. The warrant is: because physical differentiation is impossible, narrative and origin differentiation is the only viable basis for premium pricing, and De Beers' heritage and provenance capabilities are the only assets that can deliver it.

**How:** Deploy the Tracr blockchain platform as a consumer-facing authentication and storytelling tool at point of sale; scale the DiamondProof detection device across retail partners; invest in campaigns that make provenance verification a purchase ritual rather than a back-office process. Partner with luxury brands (as already initiated with Signet and Chow Tai Fook) to embed natural diamond provenance into luxury gifting occasions.

### Recommendation 2: Accelerate Downstream Retail Expansion, Especially in India

**What:** Accelerate De Beers Jewellers' expansion into India and other high-growth emerging markets, capturing retail margin currently lost to third-party retailers and building direct consumer relationships.

**Why (Warrant):** The value chain analysis identifies downstream retail as the critical gap between De Beers' upstream strengths and consumer willingness to pay. India's strong economic growth has underpinned positive natural diamond jewellery growth, making it the most strategically important market where luxury repositioning can generate near-term revenue. The Ansoff Matrix's Market Development quadrant supports geographic expansion as a necessary complement to product development. The warrant is: De Beers captures only a fraction of the retail margin in markets dominated by third-party jewellers; direct retail expansion converts upstream competitive advantage into downstream pricing power, and India's growth trajectory provides the demand base to make this expansion profitable.

**How:** Establish De Beers Jewellers flagship stores in Mumbai, Delhi, and Bangalore; leverage the Debswana co-investment in a diamond-jewellery manufacturing facility in Botswana (already committed under the 2025 agreement) to create a vertically integrated, Africa-to-India supply narrative that resonates with Indian consumers' appreciation for craftsmanship and origin stories.

### Recommendation 3: Ringfence and Accelerate Element Six as a Strategic Growth Engine

**What:** Formally separate Element Six as a distinct strategic business unit with its own investment thesis, focused on industrial and technology applications of synthetic diamonds (semiconductors, quantum computing, PFAS water purification).

**Why (Warrant):** The Ansoff Matrix's Diversification quadrant identifies Element Six as De Beers' best path to structural revenue diversification beyond jewellery. Element Six has entered strategic partnerships including a collaboration with Orbray to develop wafer-scale single-crystal diamond for AI and telecommunications, and a partnership with Lummus Technology for PFAS removal from water. These are high-growth markets with structural demand tailwinds unrelated to jewellery consumer sentiment. The SWOT analysis identifies Element Six's patented technology and industrial expertise as a distinct strength. The warrant is: because jewellery demand is structurally impaired by LGD substitution, De Beers requires a revenue stream not correlated with jewellery cycles, and Element Six's technology portfolio provides exactly that, with the additional benefit of being built on De Beers' existing synthetic diamond manufacturing capability.

**How:** Establish Element Six as a separately reported segment within De Beers' accounts to make its financial performance visible to investors and potential partners; pursue strategic partnerships with semiconductor manufacturers and quantum computing companies; consider a partial IPO of Element Six to unlock capital and establish an independent market valuation.

### Recommendation 4: Manage the Anglo American Divestment to Preserve Strategic Continuity

**What:** Ensure that the sale or spin-off of De Beers results in an ownership structure that can execute the luxury repositioning and technology diversification strategies outlined above, rather than a financial buyer focused on short-term cost extraction.

**Why (Warrant):** Anglo American's "dual-track" approach — simultaneously exploring direct sale and potential IPO — reflects sophisticated process management given market uncertainty, with the engagement of Morgan Stanley, Goldman Sachs, and Centerview as financial advisors demonstrating commitment to maximising competitive tension. However, the risk is that a financially distressed sale results in an owner who prioritises cost cuts over the brand and technology investments required for recovery. The SWOT analysis identifies strategic uncertainty as a threat; the Five Forces analysis establishes that competitive recovery requires sustained investment in differentiation, not cost extraction. The warrant is: the recommendations above (luxury repositioning, retail expansion, Element Six growth) all require multi-year investment horizons that are incompatible with a short-term financial buyer's return expectations; therefore, the divestment process must be managed to select an owner with patient capital and strategic alignment.

**How:** Prioritise a consortium structure that includes the Government of Botswana (which has both the motivation and the political legitimacy to support long-term mining licence security) alongside a strategic luxury or mining partner; use the Debswana agreement's 25-year mining licence extension as a negotiating asset that demonstrates long-term value to patient buyers.

### Recommendation 5: Institutionalise Demand Stimulation as a Core Competency

**What:** Restore De Beers' historic role as the industry's demand stimulator through co-funded, industry-wide marketing campaigns that rebuild the cultural primacy of natural diamonds — particularly among millennials and Gen Z.

**Why (Warrant):** De Beers and the Government of Botswana will co-invest in marketing campaigns to boost demand, a project that would span the life of the current sales agreement. This co-investment structure is significant because it aligns the interests of the largest producer and the most important mining-state government behind demand creation. Gen Z's preference for less expensive lab-grown diamonds has hit demand for natural stones, confirming that the generational challenge is real. The warrant is: De Beers' historical competitive advantage was not just in mining but in manufacturing desire; the "A Diamond is Forever" campaign created a market that did not previously exist, and the same capability — applied to provenance, sustainability, and emotional authenticity — can rebuild the premium natural diamond market with the next generation of consumers.

**How:** Invest in digital-native campaigns that tell the geological and human story of natural diamonds (formation over billions of years, communities supported in Botswana and Namibia, individual diamond journeys tracked on Tracr); partner with the Natural Diamond Council to share the cost of category marketing; use DiamondProof and Tracr as consumer-facing proof points in campaigns, converting technical provenance verification into emotionally resonant storytelling.

De Beers Group faces a strategic crisis that is structural, not cyclical.
