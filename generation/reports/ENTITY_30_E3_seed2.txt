# Polestar: A Strategic Case Study

## Navigating the "Stuck in the Middle" Trap — From Existential Crisis to Profitable Differentiation

---

## 1. Introduction and Strategic Situation

Polestar Automotive Holding UK PLC (Nasdaq: PSNY) is a Swedish premium electric vehicle (EV) manufacturer, established as a joint venture between Volvo Cars and Zhejiang Geely Holding (Geely), the Chinese multinational automotive group that acquired Volvo in 2010. The turning point came in 2017 when Volvo Cars announced the spin-off of Polestar into a standalone brand focused exclusively on electric performance vehicles, as part of Volvo's broader electrification strategy.

The strategic situation examined in this case study is the following: **between 2023 and 2025, Polestar faced a simultaneous crisis of financial sustainability, competitive identity, and strategic dependency** — threatening its long-term viability as an independent brand. Despite operating in the fastest-growing automotive segment, Polestar was losing money at scale, saw its core model's sales decline, recorded a going-concern doubt in its own financial statements, and struggled to articulate a position clearly distinct from both its parent (Volvo) and its principal rival (Tesla). The case analyses this predicament using Porter's Five Forces, the Resource-Based View (RBV), Porter's Generic Strategies, and the Ansoff Matrix, and concludes with a set of strategic recommendations.

## 2. Background and Context

### 2.1 Financial Position

The financial severity of Polestar's situation is not in dispute. For the full year 2024, Polestar delivered 44,851 vehicles, down 15% versus 2023. Revenue in the first half of 2024 was $909.2 million, a decrease of $327.2 million, or 26%, compared to $1,236.4 million for the same period in 2023. In Q3 2024, revenue was $551 million, down 10% versus Q3 2023, on lower volume and competitive market conditions, with a net loss of $323 million.

More gravely, Polestar's own auditors and management flagged existential risk. There is "substantial doubt about Polestar's ability to continue as a going concern," as timely realisation of financing endeavours is necessary to cover forecasted operating and investing cash outflows. This going-concern qualification is not a technicality; it signals that without continued external capital injections, the business model was not self-sustaining — a critical strategic fact that frames every decision the company must make.

### 2.2 The Rescue and Ownership Restructuring

To address the liquidity crisis, Polestar secured USD 950 million in external funding in February 2024, provided by 12 leading international banks including BNP Paribas, Natixis, Standard Chartered, BBVA, HSBC and SPDB, in the form of a three-year loan facility. Simultaneously, ownership was restructured: Volvo transferred a portion of its 48% stake to parent company Geely, which became a "significant" new shareholder in Polestar, promising to provide "full operational and financial support" going forward.

### 2.3 Leadership Change

In August 2024, a leadership transition underscored the urgency of the situation. Michael Lohscheller, a seasoned automotive leader who previously served as CEO of Opel, VinFast and Nikola, was appointed CEO, bringing expertise in navigating competitive markets and scaling businesses. The appointment of a turnaround-oriented CEO signals that the board recognised the existing strategy required fundamental revision.

## 3. Industry Analysis: Porter's Five Forces

Porter's Five Forces framework (Porter, 1980) allows us to assess the structural attractiveness of the premium EV industry and, critically, to understand *why* Polestar's position within it has been so precarious. The argument is that industry structure shapes the distribution of value, and a firm's strategic position must be calibrated to that structure.

### 3.1 Competitive Rivalry (HIGH)

The premium EV segment is characterised by intense rivalry, which directly erodes Polestar's margins and forces costly discounting. Polestar's primary direct competitor is Tesla, particularly with models like the Model 3 and Model Y, whose strong brand presence and extensive Supercharger network provide a significant advantage. In 2024, Tesla delivered approximately 1.8 million vehicles globally. Legacy premium brands are also electrifying rapidly: BMW, Mercedes-Benz, and Audi are leveraging their brand loyalty and extensive dealer networks, with BMW's EV sales increasing by 100% year-over-year in 2024.

The consequence for Polestar is direct and measurable. Revenue in the first nine months of 2024 fell primarily due to "lower global vehicle sales of Polestar 2, higher discounts in a competitive market and a delay in sales ramp up of new carlines." The discounting required to maintain volume in this rivalry-intensive environment is a direct cause of Polestar's negative gross margins — the mechanism connecting industry structure to firm-level financial distress.

### 3.2 Threat of New Entrants (MODERATE)

The EV sector presents a paradox: capital requirements are enormous, yet new entrants continue to emerge. The threat of new entrants for Polestar is notably high due to the need for advanced technology and expertise, with the cost to develop a new EV platform potentially exceeding $2 billion. However, Chinese manufacturers — backed by state support and vertically integrated battery supply chains — represent a structurally different threat that bypasses traditional entry barriers. This matters for Polestar because it relies on Chinese manufacturing for much of its production, meaning it cannot easily erect cost-based barriers against Chinese competitors.

### 3.3 Supplier Power (HIGH)

Polestar's dependency on its parent companies constitutes an unusual and strategically limiting form of supplier power. Polestar achieves its operations primarily through contract manufacturing, supply, and financing agreements with Volvo Cars and Geely. This means Polestar does not own its own factories, and its production continuity is contingent on the goodwill and financial health of related parties — a structural vulnerability that constrains strategic independence. When Volvo's own EV plans accelerated, the rationale for subsidising Polestar was weakened, directly precipitating the 2024 funding crisis.

### 3.4 Buyer Power (HIGH)

Premium EV buyers are well-informed, have numerous alternatives, and are increasingly price-sensitive as the market matures. The evidence of buyer power is visible in Polestar's forced discounting: gross margin decreased by 3.4 percentage points to a gross loss of 2.4%, with increased discounts for Polestar 2 and negative impact from IP-related costs. When buyers can switch easily to Tesla, BMW iX, or Mercedes EQE, Polestar must compete on price — which destroys the margin premium that justifies its differentiation strategy.

### 3.5 Threat of Substitutes (MODERATE)

The broader substitute threat — from hybrid vehicles or, in some markets, continued ICE vehicles — is declining as government mandates and infrastructure investment accelerate EV adoption. The global electric vehicle market is projected to reach $957.4 billion by 2028, with a CAGR of 18.2%. This growth dynamic is a structural tailwind, but it benefits all EV makers, not Polestar specifically. The key implication is that the "rising tide" argument does not resolve Polestar's competitive positioning problem — it merely confirms the importance of winning within the segment rather than waiting for segment growth to compensate for share losses.

**Five Forces Summary:** The industry analysis reveals a structurally difficult environment: high rivalry, high buyer power, and high supplier dependency all compress margins simultaneously. This structural reality means that Polestar cannot survive on volume alone; it must achieve a defensible position that reduces its exposure to competitive pricing pressure. This conclusion motivates the next analytical layer.

## 4. Internal Analysis: Resource-Based View

The Resource-Based View (Barney, 1991) holds that sustainable competitive advantage derives from resources and capabilities that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN). This framework is used here to identify what Polestar genuinely possesses that competitors cannot easily replicate — and, critically, to identify where its resource base is weaker than its strategic ambitions require.

### 4.1 Strengths (VRIN Resources)

**Design and Brand Identity.** Polestar has cultivated a distinctive Scandinavian minimalist aesthetic that is genuinely differentiated in the premium EV market. Polestar has established itself as a premium brand within the electric vehicle market, promoting a unique identity that emphasises performance, environmental sustainability, and advanced technology. This identity is rare in the sense that no other manufacturer simultaneously combines Swedish design heritage, pure-EV focus, and Geely's manufacturing scale. The brand is valuable because Polestar outsold Porsche in Europe in 2025 in electric vehicles, demonstrating that the brand commands genuine consumer pull in its target segment.

**Sustainability Credentials.** Polestar's "Polestar 0" initiative is a genuinely rare strategic asset. The Polestar 0 Project aims to eliminate all greenhouse gas emissions from every aspect of the supply chain and production, from conception all the way to customer delivery, without resorting to offsetting. This commitment — if credibly executed — is difficult for legacy automakers to replicate because it requires supply chain transformation that conflicts with their existing ICE infrastructure investments. It is, therefore, a source of potential inimitability.

**Shared Platform Access.** Polestar benefits from Volvo's engineering, safety expertise and design influence, while still operating as its own standalone electric car brand. Access to Volvo's SPA and SEA platforms, and future SPA3 architecture, reduces Polestar's R&D cost per vehicle — a capability that smaller pure-play EV startups cannot replicate.

### 4.2 Weaknesses (Resource Gaps)

**Financial Resources.** Polestar's most critical resource gap is capital. Net losses for the six months ended June 30, 2024 and 2023 amounted to $539 million and $341 million respectively. This sustained cash consumption means the firm cannot self-fund the investments in manufacturing, retail expansion, and product development that its strategy requires. Unlike Tesla, which achieved manufacturing scale and positive cash flow before expanding its model range, Polestar is attempting to do both simultaneously — a resource-stretching approach that explains the going-concern risk.

**Manufacturing Independence.** Because Polestar achieves its operations primarily through contract manufacturing, supply, and financing agreements with Volvo Cars and Geely, it lacks the manufacturing capability that Barney (1991) would classify as an organisational resource. This dependency means Polestar's cost structure is partly outside its own control, limiting its ability to achieve the cost reductions needed for profitability.

**Brand Awareness at Scale.** Despite strong brand identity among enthusiasts, Polestar's absolute scale remains limited. Total retail sales for the full year 2024 were 44,851 vehicles, compared to Tesla's 1.8 million. This scale gap means Polestar cannot amortise fixed costs (R&D, marketing, platform development) across a large enough volume base — a resource disadvantage that compounds the margin problem identified in the Five Forces analysis.

**RBV Summary:** The RBV analysis reveals a firm with genuine but fragile differentiation assets (brand, design, sustainability), undermined by critical resource gaps in capital and manufacturing independence. The strategic implication is that Polestar must protect and invest in its genuine VRIN resources while urgently addressing its resource gaps — a conclusion that connects directly to the generic strategy analysis below.

## 5. Strategic Position Analysis: Porter's Generic Strategies

Porter (1980) argues that firms must choose between cost leadership, differentiation, or focus — and that firms which fail to commit to one position become "stuck in the middle," achieving neither the cost advantages of high-volume producers nor the premium pricing of genuine differentiators. This is the central diagnosis for Polestar's crisis.

### 5.1 The "Stuck in the Middle" Diagnosis

Polestar's 2023–2024 crisis exhibits the classic symptoms of being stuck in the middle. On one hand, it positioned itself as a premium differentiator, competing on design, sustainability, and performance. On the other hand, its financial pressure forced it to discount aggressively — revenue fell due to "higher discounts in a competitive market" — which undermined the premium positioning and failed to generate cost-leadership margins because its volumes were too low to achieve scale economies. The result was negative gross margins: a firm paying more to produce vehicles than it received for selling them.

The Polestar 2's ageing lifecycle illustrates this trap precisely. The Polestar 2 recorded its best annual sales of 2,463 vehicles in 2023 in Australia, but dropped by 40% in 2024, despite recent price cuts of $5,000 across the range. Price cuts failed to arrest volume decline because the model was neither cheap enough to attract value-conscious buyers nor distinctive enough to retain premium buyers who could access newer alternatives. This is the "stuck in the middle" outcome in its purest form.

### 5.2 The Required Strategic Commitment: Focused Differentiation

Porter's framework implies that Polestar's viable path is **focused differentiation** — competing in the premium EV segment on the basis of design, sustainability, and Scandinavian identity, at a price point that commands margin, targeting a specific buyer segment (design-conscious, environmentally motivated, affluent urban consumers) rather than attempting mass-market volume. This is consistent with the RBV finding that Polestar's VRIN resources are in brand and sustainability, not in cost.

The evidence that Polestar is beginning to execute this shift is visible in its 2025 results. Revenue for full-year 2025 was USD 3,058 million, up 50.3% from USD 2,034 million the prior year, driven predominantly by higher volumes, a growing share of higher-priced models (Polestar 3 and Polestar 4) in the sales mix, and carbon credits sales. The mix shift toward higher-priced models (Polestar 3 at ~$85,000 and Polestar 4) is precisely the move that focused differentiation requires: trading volume for margin.

Furthermore, gross margin improved to positive 1.9% (adjusted), an improvement of 40.9 percentage points from the prior year, mainly due to favourable product and margin mix, especially Polestar 4 sales, higher carbon credits sales, and lower residual value guarantee adjustments. This improvement, while still fragile, confirms that the strategic logic of focused differentiation is beginning to translate into financial results.

## 6. Growth Strategy Analysis: Ansoff Matrix

The Ansoff Matrix (Ansoff, 1957) maps growth options against the dimensions of existing/new products and existing/new markets, with risk increasing from market penetration (lowest) to diversification (highest). This framework is used here to evaluate Polestar's product and market expansion decisions and to assess whether its growth trajectory is coherent with its resource base.

### 6.1 Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets)

Polestar's most immediate lever has been to deepen penetration in established markets — Europe and North America — through retail network expansion. The company's sales network grew by over 50% in 2025, enabling the 34% volume growth that year. This is the lowest-risk growth quadrant and is appropriate given Polestar's financial constraints, as it leverages existing models and brand recognition without the capital requirements of new product development or market entry.

### 6.2 Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets)

Polestar has also pursued market development, notably expanding US sales. US sales grew 76% in Q1 2025, supported by the strategic decision to manufacture the Polestar 3 in South Carolina. Polestar shifted manufacturing to the US to circumvent EU and US tariffs on China-made new energy vehicles, with the South Carolina factory dedicated to producing cars for the European and US markets. This move is strategically sound: it simultaneously reduces tariff exposure (a threat), reduces supply chain dependency on China (addressing the RBV resource gap), and opens the world's largest premium car market more effectively.

### 6.3 Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets)

Polestar's most ambitious growth vector is product development: launching new models (Polestar 3, 4, 5, 6, 7) to expand its addressable market within the premium EV segment. Polestar's largest model offensive in its history plans four new cars in three years: Polestar 5 in 2026, a new Polestar 4 variant in 2026, next-generation Polestar 2 in 2027, and Polestar 7 in 2028. This is a high-risk quadrant in the Ansoff matrix — it requires significant capital and execution capability — and it is precisely here that Polestar's resource gaps create the greatest risk.

The Polestar 3 impairment illustrates this risk concretely. As a consequence of less favourable macroeconomic and market conditions than originally anticipated, the recoverable amount of the Polestar 3 CGU was estimated to be USD 25 million, resulting in an impairment loss of USD 739 million. This write-down occurred because the product development investment was made on demand forecasts that did not materialise — a direct consequence of operating in a high-rivalry environment (Five Forces) without the financial resilience (RBV) to absorb forecast errors.

### 6.4 Ansoff Summary

The Ansoff analysis reveals that Polestar is simultaneously pursuing all three non-diversification quadrants, which stretches its limited resource base. The strategic implication is that Polestar must sequence its growth moves: prioritise market penetration (lowest risk, immediate cash generation) and selective market development (US manufacturing) before committing to the full product development pipeline. Attempting all simultaneously risks repeating the Polestar 3 impairment scenario at scale.

## 7. Synthesis: The Coherent Strategic Logic

The three frameworks converge on a coherent diagnosis:

1. **Five Forces** shows that the premium EV industry distributes value away from mid-positioned players and toward those with either cost leadership (Tesla's scale, BYD's vertical integration) or genuine differentiation premium (Porsche Taycan's brand, BMW's heritage). Polestar sits in between.

2. **RBV** confirms that Polestar has genuine differentiating assets (brand, design, sustainability) but lacks the financial and manufacturing resources to convert them into sustainable advantage without external support.

3. **Generic Strategies** diagnoses the "stuck in the middle" trap and prescribes focused differentiation — which Polestar's 2025 mix shift toward higher-margin models suggests it is now pursuing, but incompletely.

4. **Ansoff** shows that Polestar is pursuing too many growth vectors simultaneously for its resource base, with the product development pipeline carrying the highest risk of further impairment write-downs.

The chain of reasoning is: *structural industry pressure forces margin compression → Polestar's resource gaps prevent it from absorbing this pressure → its ambiguous strategic position amplifies the damage → the prescription is to commit more fully to focused differentiation while sequencing growth investments in line with available resources.*

## 8. Recommendations

The following action plan is derived directly from the analytical findings above. Each recommendation addresses a specific gap identified in the analysis and is supported by evidence.

### Recommendation 1: Commit Fully to Focused Differentiation — Eliminate Discounting on Core Models

**Rationale:** The Five Forces analysis establishes that Polestar cannot win a price war against Tesla or Chinese manufacturers. The Generic Strategies analysis identifies discounting as the mechanism by which Polestar falls into the "stuck in the middle" trap. The 2025 financial results confirm that mix shift toward premium models (Polestar 3 and 4) is already improving gross margins — the strategy is working when applied. Polestar should therefore formally discontinue volume-based discounting on its current model range and instead manage residual values through certified used vehicle programmes, preserving the brand's premium positioning. The evidence that this is viable is Polestar's 2025 European performance: Polestar sold more electric vehicles in Europe in 2025 than Porsche, demonstrating that a premium-positioned EV brand can achieve meaningful volume without discounting to commodity levels.

### Recommendation 2: Sequence the Product Pipeline — Prioritise Polestar 4 and 5, Delay Lower-Margin Entries

**Rationale:** The Ansoff analysis establishes that simultaneous pursuit of multiple product development projects stretches Polestar's resource base dangerously. The Polestar 3 impairment of USD 739 million demonstrates the financial consequence of over-committing to product development without matching demand certainty. Polestar's plan for four new cars in three years is ambitious relative to its capital position. The recommendation is to concentrate investment on the Polestar 4 (already the best-selling model) and Polestar 5 (the GT flagship that anchors brand aspiration), while extending the timeline for the Polestar 7 (a lower-priced entry that risks diluting the premium position). The Polestar 5's role as a halo product is strategically critical: it signals technological capability and design ambition to the entire range, justifying premium pricing downstream — a logic consistent with the focused differentiation prescription.

### Recommendation 3: Accelerate Manufacturing Independence — Expand South Carolina, Pursue South Korea Polestar 4 Production

**Rationale:** The RBV analysis identifies manufacturing dependency on Volvo and Geely as a critical resource gap that limits strategic independence and cost control. The tariff environment provides an external forcing function: US automotive manufacturers face 100% tariffs on Chinese-made EVs. Polestar has already begun addressing this by moving Polestar 3 production to South Carolina and planning Polestar 4 production in South Korea. These moves should be accelerated and formalised as a strategic priority, not merely a tariff-avoidance tactic. Each unit of manufacturing capacity that Polestar controls independently reduces its exposure to the supplier-power dynamic identified in the Five Forces analysis and reduces the risk that Geely or Volvo's own strategic priorities override Polestar's.

### Recommendation 4: Monetise the Sustainability Differentiator — Build "Polestar 0" into Commercial Strategy

**Rationale:** The RBV analysis identifies the Polestar 0 sustainability commitment as a potentially inimitable resource, but one that is currently undermonetised. The Polestar 0 Project aims to eliminate all greenhouse gas emissions from every aspect of the supply chain and production, without resorting to offsetting. This commitment is credible precisely because it forswears offsets — making it harder for legacy automakers to replicate by purchasing carbon credits. Polestar should translate this into a commercial differentiator by: (a) publishing independently verified lifecycle carbon data for every model; (b) partnering with B-Corp certified organisations and fleet buyers who have science-based targets; and (c) pricing a "verified carbon-neutral" trim or certification into its commercial offering. This directly addresses the buyer-power problem (Five Forces) by creating switching costs for environmentally committed buyers who cannot find equivalent credibility elsewhere.

### Recommendation 5: Resolve the Identity Ambiguity Relative to Volvo

**Rationale:** The line between Polestar and Volvo has become confusing, with Volvo's own EV plans taking off successfully and Polestar struggling. This ambiguity is a brand resource problem (RBV) that undermines the focused differentiation strategy (Generic Strategies). Polestar must articulate a clear, non-overlapping position relative to Volvo: Volvo = safe, family-oriented, Nordic; Polestar = performance-oriented, design-forward, sustainability-leading. Polestar intends to differentiate its offerings from sibling brand Volvo, even as it moves into more mainstream segments with new crossovers and estate vehicles. The recommendation is to make this differentiation explicit and consistent across all customer touchpoints — pricing, design language, marketing, and retail experience — so that buyers choosing between a Volvo EX90 and a Polestar 3 understand they are making a meaningful lifestyle choice, not selecting between two versions of the same car.

## 10. Conclusion

Polestar's strategic crisis between 2023 and 2025 is best understood not as a failure of execution alone, but as the predictable consequence of occupying an ambiguous strategic position in a structurally difficult industry, with insufficient resources to sustain the investment required to escape that ambiguity. The Five Forces analysis explains why the industry distributes value away from mid-positioned players. The RBV explains why Polestar lacks the resources to absorb that pressure. The Generic Strategies framework names the trap — "stuck in the middle" — and prescribes the exit: focused differentiation. The Ansoff Matrix shows that the exit must be pursued sequentially, not simultaneously across all growth vectors.

The early evidence from 2025 — revenue of USD 3,058 million, up 50.3%, record retail sales of 60,119 vehicles, a growth of 34%, and improving gross margins — suggests that Polestar's current leadership has begun executing the right strategic logic. The recommendations above are designed to accelerate and deepen that commitment, before the next product development cycle creates another round of impairment risk.
