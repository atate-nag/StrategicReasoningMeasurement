# Manchester United: From Commercial Giant to Strategic Paradox — A Case Study in Structural Misalignment

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Manchester United is one of the world's most recognisable sports brands, yet it has spent the past decade in accelerating decline on the pitch while preserving much of its commercial power off it. This case study diagnoses that paradox as the product of a single, compounding strategic problem: **structural misalignment between the club's financial architecture, governance model, and sporting operations**. Using Porter's Five Forces, the Resource-Based View (VRIO), and McKinsey's 7-S framework, the analysis shows that debt-servicing obligations inherited from the Glazer leveraged buyout have systematically crowded out investment in football operations; that a dysfunctional dual-class ownership structure has prevented coherent long-term decision-making; and that, consequently, the club's most valuable resources — its global brand and matchday pull — are slowly being eroded by sporting failure. The analysis concludes with a prioritised action plan targeting governance reform, financial deleveraging, and a sporting model built on structural continuity rather than managerial churn.

## 1. SITUATIONAL CONTEXT

### 1.1 The Scale of the Crisis

Manchester United finished 15th in the Premier League in 2024–25 with 42 points — their lowest league position since 1989–90 and their fewest points in a top-flight season since they were last relegated in 1973–74. This is not a short-term dip. By mid-May 2025, United had suffered nine home league defeats (a joint club record) and their seventeenth defeat in the league overall. The on-pitch collapse matters strategically, not merely in sporting terms, because broadcasting revenue for the year ending June 30, 2025 decreased by £48.9m to £172.9m after the men's team played in the Europa League rather than the Champions League. Sporting failure therefore directly translates into revenue loss, which in turn constrains the resources available to correct sporting failure — a reinforcing negative loop.

### 1.2 The Ownership Transition and Its Limits

INEOS Group, led by Sir Jim Ratcliffe, became minority owners in February 2024 after acquiring a 27.7% stake in the club in a deal costing £1.3 billion. INEOS was given operational control over football matters. Yet the structural problem persists: Manchester United's dual-class share structure continues to favour the Glazer family, which retains control through its ownership of the majority of Class B shares, each carrying ten voting rights compared to one vote for Class A shares. This means that a 28.94 percent minority share limits formal decision-making authority for INEOS, while the Glazers retain board control, potentially generating strategic misalignment in aligning sporting and financial objectives.

### 1.3 The Financial Paradox

Despite sporting failure, Manchester United achieved record revenues of £666.5m in 2024–25 — and yet still made an overall loss of £33m. Commercial revenue rose to £333.3m while matchday revenues reached a record £160.3m. This paradox — record revenues co-existing with persistent losses — is the analytical centrepiece of this case. It signals that the problem is not insufficient revenue generation but structural cost extraction from the club, chiefly through debt service.

## 2. ANALYTICAL FRAMEWORK 1: PORTER'S FIVE FORCES — INDUSTRY CONTEXT

Porter's Five Forces (1979) establishes the competitive structure of the industry within which United operates. This matters because industry structure sets the ceiling on profitability regardless of firm-level strategy.

### 2.1 Competitive Rivalry (HIGH)

The Premier League is characterised by intense rivalry among a small number of well-resourced clubs. Manchester City's wage bill in 2024–25 was £223.7m, Arsenal's £172.7m, and Manchester United's £171.4m — yet United finished 15th with the second-highest wage bill in the league, making them one of the Premier League's biggest underachievers when comparing performance to wage outlay. The implication is that financial inputs no longer reliably convert into sporting outputs at United, whereas rivals such as Liverpool (fifth in the wage table yet title contenders) demonstrate that efficient resource allocation produces superior competitive returns. Rivalry is therefore not merely about spending power but about the quality of strategic decision-making — an area where United has been structurally deficient.

### 2.2 Threat of New Entrants (LOW–MODERATE)

The Premier League's promotion/relegation system means entrants from lower leagues are a theoretical threat, but the financial gap between established top-six clubs and the rest is large. More relevant is the threat from clubs backed by sovereign wealth or state capital (Manchester City, Newcastle United, Chelsea). These clubs can absorb losses that market-rate clubs cannot, distorting competitive conditions. For United, this matters because it cannot compete by simply outspending rivals — it must compete smarter.

### 2.3 Bargaining Power of Suppliers (HIGH)

In football, the primary "suppliers" are elite players and their agents. One audit identified excessive over-spending, indecision that led to inflated transfer fees, and a flawed policy of extending player contracts simply to avoid players becoming free agents. This indicates that United's historically weak transfer governance allowed supplier power — agents and selling clubs — to extract rents far above fair value. The consequence is a bloated wage structure: United's wage-to-revenue ratio decreased from 55.1% to 47.0% in 2024–25 following restructuring, yet this still exceeded the pre-COVID norm of 63.4%–73.0% on a total-wage-adjusted basis, reflecting years of accumulated poor contracting decisions.

### 2.4 Bargaining Power of Buyers (LOW)

Football fans exhibit high brand loyalty and low price sensitivity — Old Trafford maintained an impressive 97% capacity across 2024–25 despite mixed results on the pitch, with United attracting 2.2 million spectators across all competitions, more than any other European club. This loyalty is a structural advantage, but it is not unconditional. Fan group The 1958 stated: "The club is slowly dying before our eyes, on and off the pitch and the blame lies squarely at the current ownership model." Sustained sporting failure risks converting passive dissatisfaction into active disengagement, eroding the matchday revenue base.

### 2.5 Threat of Substitutes (LOW–MODERATE)

Alternative entertainment options compete for fan time and attention, but the emotional attachment to Manchester United is not easily substitutable. However, the threat of *commercial* substitution is real: sponsors and broadcasters allocate budgets based on performance and visibility. Broadcasting revenue sank 22% to $235m in 2024–25 due to the 15th-place finish and competing in the Europa League rather than the Champions League. This demonstrates that United's commercial ecosystem is partially substitutable — sponsors and broadcasters will redirect resources toward clubs with greater visibility in elite competition.

**Five Forces Summary**: The industry structure is moderately attractive for well-run clubs but punishing for poorly governed ones. United's core problem is not industry-level — the Premier League remains highly lucrative — but firm-level: it is failing to convert industry revenues into competitive sporting outcomes because of structural internal constraints.

## 3. ANALYTICAL FRAMEWORK 2: RESOURCE-BASED VIEW (VRIO) — COMPETITIVE ADVANTAGE AUDIT

Barney's (1991) VRIO framework asks whether a firm's resources are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Organised to capture value. The VRIO Framework, developed by Jay Barney in 1991, is a cornerstone of the Resource-Based View of strategy, evaluating resources and capabilities through four questions to determine whether they provide sustained competitive advantage. Applying this to United reveals a fundamental disconnect: the club possesses rare, inimitable resources but is failing to organise them effectively.

### 3.1 Global Brand (Valuable ✓, Rare ✓, Inimitable ✓, Organised? ✗)

Forbes valued Manchester United at approximately $6.55 billion, reflecting the brand's extraordinary global reach. The club has converted 146 years of history into a global marketing powerhouse, with 1.1 billion global followers cited in Kantar studies, sustaining unmatched unaided awareness and premium sponsor appeal. This brand is genuinely rare and inimitable — no competitor can replicate the history of 20 English league titles and decades of European football. The commercial evidence confirms its value: United's Snapdragon shirt sponsorship deal is worth a tick above the current record of $75m a year that Emirates pays Real Madrid, making it the most valuable shirt deal in world football. However, the brand is not being fully *organised* to generate sporting advantage: commercial revenues are decoupled from the football operation, with the brand sustaining revenues that are then extracted through debt service rather than reinvested in squad quality.

### 3.2 Matchday Infrastructure (Valuable ✓, Rare ✓, Inimitable ✓, Organised? ✗)

Old Trafford's history and atmosphere generate revenues other clubs cannot replicate. Yet the stadium is ageing and structurally constrained. Manchester United has announced its intention to build a new 100,000-seater stadium as the centrepiece of the regeneration of the Old Trafford area, with the club stating that the project has the potential to deliver an additional £7.3bn per year to the UK economy and create 92,000 new jobs. The new stadium, costing £2 billion to build, represents the single largest strategic investment in United's future. However, the Mayor of Greater Manchester stated that no public money would fund the stadium, placing the financing burden on a club already carrying net debt of approximately £749m ($1.002bn) as of late 2024. The resource is not currently organised to generate value because the infrastructure is deteriorating and the financing plan for its replacement is unresolved.

### 3.3 Football Operations Capability (Valuable? ✗)

This is where the VRIO analysis reveals the deepest failure. A club's ability to recruit, develop, and deploy talent coherently is a potentially inimitable capability — but only if it exists. At United, it has been systematically absent. One audit identified excessive over-spending, indecision that led to inflated transfer fees, and a flawed policy of extending player contracts simply to avoid players becoming free agents. Individual transfer decisions exemplify the broader failure: Casemiro (£60m) and Raphaël Varane (£34m) were signed on huge contracts from Real Madrid despite both players having their best years behind them, while Mason Mount was signed from Chelsea for £55m with no obvious role in the team. The cumulative cost is staggering: since 2014, the cumulative cost of sackings, settlements, and misaligned transfers has been enormous, with the club cycling through multiple managers without establishing the structural continuity that rivals like Arsenal and Liverpool have built. Football operations capability therefore fails the VRIO test at the first hurdle — it is not currently *valuable* in the sense of enabling competitive advantage.

### 3.4 VRIO Summary

The VRIO analysis reveals a paradox: United possesses globally rare, inimitable brand and matchday resources that should confer sustained competitive advantage, but these resources are not organised to generate sporting returns because (a) revenues are extracted by debt service rather than reinvested, (b) football operations have been chronically mismanaged, and (c) governance prevents the alignment needed to connect commercial strength to sporting strategy. The brand is sustaining the club commercially while the sporting operation erodes the brand's long-term foundations.

## 4. ANALYTICAL FRAMEWORK 3: McKINSEY 7-S — INTERNAL ALIGNMENT ANALYSIS

The McKinsey 7-S framework analyses whether a firm's Strategy, Structure, Systems, Shared Values, Style, Staff, and Skills are mutually aligned. Misalignment between any elements undermines strategic execution. At Manchester United, multiple elements are misaligned, which explains why each individual intervention — new manager, new signings, new ownership — has failed to produce sustained improvement.

### 4.1 Strategy (Unclear)

United has lacked a coherent, stable sporting strategy for over a decade. Manchester United is stuck in a cycle of poor business decisions off the field. The arrival of INEOS to assist majority owners the Glazer family has not broken it. The club has oscillated between short-term firefighting (sacking managers, making reactive signings) and long-term ambitions (new stadium, restructuring) without integrating the two into a coherent plan. The club continues to display the same damaging tendencies that have defined the era after Sir Alex Ferguson's management period, including reacting to performances on the pitch rather than prioritising the long-term vision, and allowing managerial changes to disrupt wider restructuring projects.

### 4.2 Structure (Dysfunctional)

The governance structure is the root cause of misalignment. The dual-class share structure gives the Glazer family ten voting rights per Class B share versus one for Class A shares, meaning that despite INEOS's operational control, the Glazers retain ultimate strategic authority. This creates a hybrid governance model that introduces novel challenges in aligning sporting and financial objectives. The result is what The Conversation describes as a situation where Manchester United's biggest problem is not who stands on the touchline, but how the club makes decisions when the cameras are off.

### 4.3 Systems (Broken)

The club's transfer and recruitment systems have been consistently deficient. An internal audit identified excessive over-spending and a flawed policy of extending player contracts simply to avoid players becoming free agents. Financial control systems have also been inadequate: United avoided breaching Premier League Profit and Sustainability Rules (PSR) only through a combination of allowances, including approximately £35m related to the INEOS share sale and an exceptional £40m COVID allowance — the latter being double the amount claimed by all other 19 Premier League clubs combined, which was subsequently rejected by UEFA, resulting in a €300,000 fine for breaching Financial Fair Play rules. Systems that require exceptional accounting manoeuvres to remain compliant are not systems built for sustainable performance.

### 4.4 Shared Values (Contested)

The club's stated values — tradition, excellence, community — are undermined by ownership behaviour that fans perceive as extractive. Manchester United have paid a total of more than £1bn on interest fees generated from the debt piled onto the club by the Glazer family when they completed their takeover in 2005. This extraction has been visible to supporters, generating sustained protest. Fans stated: "The club is slowly dying before our eyes, on and off the pitch and the blame lies squarely at the current ownership model." When the ownership's financial behaviour contradicts the club's stated values of sporting ambition and community, shared values fragment — and with them, the social licence that sustains commercial revenues.

### 4.5 Style, Staff, and Skills (Inconsistent)

INEOS's arrival brought a "marginal gains" philosophy drawn from elite sport. Ratcliffe is applying the philosophy of 'marginal gains' — a concept pioneered by Sir Dave Brailsford in cycling — involving fine-tuning operations from financial restructuring to infrastructure investment. However, this philosophy has been applied inconsistently: cost-cutting measures have included two waves of redundancies totalling approximately 450 staff, with the first wave affecting academy coaches, which reportedly left children in tears. Cutting from the academy — the primary pipeline for sustainable talent development — contradicts any long-term sporting strategy and reveals that short-term cost reduction is overriding strategic logic.

### 4.6 7-S Summary

All seven elements are misaligned. Strategy is reactive; Structure is split between competing ownership interests; Systems are broken in recruitment and financial control; Shared Values are contested; Style is inconsistent; Staff have been cut in strategically damaging areas; and Skills in football operations remain deficient. The 7-S analysis confirms that the problem is systemic, not symptomatic — no single intervention will resolve it without addressing the underlying misalignments.

## 5. THE CORE STRATEGIC PROBLEM: THE LEVERAGED BUYOUT PARADOX

The three frameworks converge on a single structural diagnosis. Manchester United's experience under the Glazers exemplifies the "leveraged buyout paradox" in sport: while debt-funded acquisition facilitated rapid revenue expansion and commercial success, the attendant interest burdens — totalling £834 million to date — have diverted resources away from football operations. The causal chain is as follows:

1. **Debt extraction constrains investment**: Over £1bn in interest payments since 2005 represents capital that was unavailable for squad investment, infrastructure maintenance, or talent development — directly weakening the football operation.

2. **Weakened football operation produces poor results**: The 15th-place finish in 2024–25 was United's lowest league position in 35 years.

3. **Poor results reduce broadcasting revenue**: Broadcasting revenue fell £48.9m in 2024–25 due to Europa League rather than Champions League participation.

4. **Reduced revenue tightens financial constraints further**: United's total net debt broke $1bn, while the club still posted a £33m loss despite record commercial revenues — meaning the commercial operation is running to stand still against the debt burden.

5. **Financial constraints force further cost-cutting**: Manchester United expects to make cost savings of between £40m and £45m a year due to its restructuring, but cuts to staff and the academy risk undermining the long-term sporting infrastructure needed to break the cycle.

This is a classic strategic trap: the very actions taken to address financial pressure (cost-cutting, player sales) weaken the sporting operation, which worsens financial performance, which demands further cost-cutting. Breaking this cycle requires structural, not incremental, intervention.

## 7. RECOMMENDATIONS AND ACTION PLAN

The analysis generates three strategic priorities, ordered by urgency and causal primacy.

### Priority 1: Resolve the Governance Structure (Immediate — 0–18 months)

**Recommendation**: INEOS should negotiate to acquire a controlling stake, or the Glazers should convert to a single-class share structure with independent board oversight.

**Warrant**: The VRIO, 7-S, and Five Forces analyses all identify governance as the root cause of misalignment. The club continues to allow managerial changes to disrupt wider restructuring projects because no single decision-maker has both the authority and the long-term interest to enforce structural continuity. The hybrid governance model introduces novel challenges in aligning sporting and financial objectives, and comparisons with other ownership transitions (Liverpool, Arsenal, Chelsea) reveal that partial changes usually yield incremental, rather than radical, performance improvements. A clear, unified governance structure is the prerequisite for every other reform — without it, strategic decisions will continue to be contested, delayed, and reversed.

**Implementation**: Engage independent advisers to structure a buyout of the Glazers' Class B shares, funded through a combination of INEOS equity and external debt refinancing at lower rates than the existing leveraged debt. The Chancellor of the Exchequer has already expressed support for the stadium regeneration project, with Rachel Reeves stating in January 2025 that she supported the Old Trafford regeneration — government appetite for partnership could be leveraged to attract co-investment that reduces the Glazers' financial leverage over the club.

### Priority 2: Deleverage the Balance Sheet (0–36 months)

**Recommendation**: Reduce net debt from £749m to below £300m over three years through a combination of strategic asset sales, equity issuance, and revenue-linked bond refinancing.

**Warrant**: The leveraged buyout paradox identified above shows that debt service is the primary mechanism through which commercial revenues are prevented from reaching football operations. Over £1bn in interest payments since 2005 represents a structural drain that no level of commercial revenue growth can overcome if debt continues to compound. The current effective interest rate on debt is approximately 13.9% — a punishing rate that reflects the risk premium attached to the club's financial structure. Refinancing at lower rates, combined with partial debt retirement through asset monetisation (including the stadium project's associated real estate development), would materially reduce the annual interest burden and free capital for sporting investment.

**Implementation**: The new stadium project offers a vehicle for this: the wider regeneration project has the potential to create 17,000 new homes and 92,000 new jobs, generating real estate revenues that could service stadium construction debt independently of football revenues. Structuring the stadium as a separate special purpose vehicle, with government infrastructure co-investment for surrounding public realm, would isolate the construction risk from the football club's balance sheet.

### Priority 3: Build Structural Sporting Continuity (12–60 months)

**Recommendation**: Commit to a minimum five-year sporting project with a defined playing philosophy, a director of football with genuine authority, and a recruitment model aligned to that philosophy.

**Warrant**: The 7-S analysis shows that the club's football operations capability fails the VRIO test because it is neither valuable nor organised. Manchester United's biggest problem is not who stands on the touchline, but how the club makes decisions when the cameras are off. The evidence from rivals is instructive: Liverpool's success under Jürgen Klopp was built on a consistent 4–3–3 pressing system that informed every recruitment decision over eight years; Arsenal's resurgence under Arteta reflects a similarly coherent long-term project. United, by contrast, has hired and fired managers at vast cost — the cumulative cost of sackings, settlements, and misaligned transfers since 2014 has been enormous — without establishing the structural continuity that converts individual talent into collective performance.

**Implementation**:

- **Appoint a Director of Football with genuine authority** over recruitment, loan management, and academy integration — not merely advisory influence. This role must be insulated from managerial changes so that the recruitment model survives coaching transitions.

- **Protect and reinvest in the academy**: The first wave of INEOS cost-cutting saw academy coaches made redundant, undermining the long-term talent pipeline. United's academy has historically produced elite players (Scholes, Giggs, Beckham, Rashford) — this is a genuinely inimitable capability that cost-cutting is destroying. Academy investment should be ring-fenced from operational cost reduction.

- **Align transfer strategy to playing model**: Recent signings have been smarter, with Ruben Amorim's 3-4-2-1 system guiding purchases around a distinct philosophy, suggesting the club is beginning to learn this lesson. This alignment must be institutionalised so it survives any future managerial change.

- **Pursue Champions League qualification as the non-negotiable sporting KPI**: Broadcasting revenue fell 42% in the quarter following Champions League absence. Champions League participation is not merely a sporting goal — it is a financial floor below which the club's revenue model becomes unviable at current cost levels.
