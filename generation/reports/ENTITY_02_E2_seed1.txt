# AbbVie Inc.: Navigating the Post-Humira Strategic Inflection Point

## A Strategic Case Study

---

## 1. Introduction and Case Framing

AbbVie Inc. presents one of the most instructive strategic situations in contemporary pharmaceutical industry history: a firm that built a $50+ billion revenue base on a single blockbuster drug, watched that drug's exclusivity collapse, and must now execute a full-scale portfolio transformation while simultaneously managing regulatory headwinds, a leveraged balance sheet, and clinical-stage setbacks. The central strategic question is not whether AbbVie can survive the loss of Humira — the data suggest it already has — but whether it can build *durable*, *diversified* competitive advantage that does not simply recreate the dangerous single-product dependency it is escaping.

This case study analyses AbbVie's strategic situation using Porter's Five Forces, the Resource-Based View (VRIO), the Ansoff Growth Matrix, and a SWOT synthesis. Each framework is applied sequentially and their outputs are connected into a coherent chain of reasoning, culminating in a set of concrete strategic recommendations.

---

## 2. Company Background and the Humira Era

AbbVie was spun off from Abbott Laboratories in 2013. Its rise to the top tier of global pharma was almost entirely powered by Humira (adalimumab), a monoclonal antibody treating rheumatoid arthritis, psoriasis, Crohn's disease, and multiple other inflammatory conditions. AbbVie delivered full-year net revenues of $54.318 billion in 2023, a decrease of 6.4 percent on a reported basis, with full-year global net revenues from the immunology portfolio at $26.136 billion — a decrease of 9.6 percent — due to Humira biosimilar competition. This decline was the direct consequence of a structural event that had been forecast for years: the loss of Humira's U.S. patent exclusivity.

Humira remained high-priced despite its core patent expiring in 2016 due to AbbVie's creation of a "patent thicket" — over 130 additional patents covering manufacturing methods, formulations, and future variants, most filed after the drug was already on the market. This extensive late-stage patenting made it nearly impossible for biosimilars to enter the U.S. market without risking infringement. Rather than pursue costly litigation, most biosimilar makers settled with AbbVie, agreeing to delayed U.S. market entry in 2023 with ongoing royalty payments, while some gained earlier access to European markets starting in 2018.

The European experience proved to be a harbinger. In Europe, where biosimilars entered in 2018, Humira's list price dropped up to 90% in some countries. When the U.S. cliff arrived, Humira faced competition from 10 adalimumab biosimilars, likely contributing to a $6 billion drop in revenue in 2023. By 2024, the erosion was accelerating: in the United States, Humira sales decreased by 40% for the three months ended March 31, 2024, primarily driven by direct biosimilar competition following the loss of exclusivity on January 31, 2023.

This context establishes the *strategic situation*: AbbVie faces a structural revenue displacement of approximately $9–10 billion per annum (comparing peak Humira U.S. revenues to their post-cliff trajectory), and must replace it with a portfolio of next-generation products. The analysis below examines whether its current strategy is sufficient, and where the critical vulnerabilities remain.

## 3. External Analysis: Porter's Five Forces

Porter's Five Forces framework (Porter, 1980) examines the structural attractiveness of an industry and the competitive pressures a firm must navigate. Applied to AbbVie's specific situation, it reveals *why* the patent cliff is so damaging and *where* the firm's strategic choices have genuine leverage.

### 3.1 Threat of New Entrants — **Moderate to High for Biosimilars; Low for Novel Biologics**

The pharmaceutical industry has historically been protected by high barriers to entry: the cost of clinical development, regulatory approval requirements, and intellectual property. However, the biosimilar pathway created by the Biologics Price Competition and Innovation Act (BPCIA) has structurally lowered barriers for *imitation* of existing biologics. The threat is not from de novo innovators, but from manufacturers of approved copies. According to a congressional inquiry, AbbVie abused the U.S. patent system to block biosimilar competition for its top-selling Humira, including its settlements with competitors to delay their entry into the market until 2023. The fact that AbbVie needed 130+ patents and a decade of litigation to delay entry illustrates the underlying structural vulnerability: once a biologic's core patent expires, the threat of imitation is real and significant.

This matters strategically because it means that AbbVie's *next-generation* products — Skyrizi and Rinvoq — face the same eventual fate. The firm has already begun responding: AbbVie has settled with each of the drugmakers that have filed for FDA approval of Rinvoq generics, preventing copycat versions of the drug from entering the U.S. market before April 2037. This is the same patent-defence playbook applied to Humira, now deployed proactively. The strategic logic is sound — buying time for the next pipeline generation — but it does not eliminate the structural threat; it defers it.

### 3.2 Bargaining Power of Buyers — **High and Rising**

In the U.S. pharmaceutical market, "buyers" operate at multiple levels: payers (insurance companies, pharmacy benefit managers), hospital systems, and government programmes. Their power has increased materially. The Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) of 2022 represents the most significant structural shift. In August 2024, HHS published the negotiated prices that will take effect in 2026 for ten drugs, and in January 2025, HHS published the list of 15 additional drugs that will be subject to price negotiations. This programme directly caps what Medicare will pay for selected high-cost drugs, reducing manufacturers' pricing power on their most commercially mature products.

For AbbVie, this is a two-edged threat. Humira's decline has already stripped one revenue pillar; IRA negotiations could constrain the pricing upside of Skyrizi and Rinvoq as they mature. The strategic implication is that AbbVie cannot rely on price increases to sustain revenues from existing products in the way it historically did with Humira, making volume growth and indication expansion *essential* rather than *supplementary* levers.

### 3.3 Bargaining Power of Suppliers — **Low to Moderate**

As of 2024, AbbVie relies on a limited number of specialized suppliers for critical pharmaceutical ingredients. In 2023, the company reported approximately 12–15 key global suppliers for its most critical raw materials. Biologic manufacturing is highly specialised, and switching costs are high, which gives suppliers some leverage. However, AbbVie's scale and long-term supply agreements mitigate this. This force is not the primary strategic concern.

### 3.4 Threat of Substitutes — **High in Oncology; Moderate in Immunology**

In immunology, Skyrizi (risankizumab) and Rinvoq (upadacitinib) compete in markets where multiple mechanism-of-action classes exist — IL-17 inhibitors, IL-23 inhibitors, JAK inhibitors — meaning physicians have genuine therapeutic alternatives. However, AbbVie's products have demonstrated differentiated clinical profiles, particularly in inflammatory bowel disease. In oncology, the threat is more acute: Calquence was taking 64% of new patient market share in the U.S. among BTK inhibitors. This directly erodes Imbruvica (ibrutinib), AbbVie's oncology anchor, illustrating how rapidly a therapeutic class leader can be displaced by a next-generation molecule.

### 3.5 Competitive Rivalry — **Intense**

The biopharmaceutical industry is characterised by intense rivalry among a small number of large incumbents (Pfizer, Johnson & Johnson, Eli Lilly, Roche, Novartis) each investing heavily in overlapping therapeutic areas. As AbbVie and Johnson & Johnson's Imbruvica retreated, AstraZeneca's Calquence has become the first BTK inhibitor to claim a unique win in previously untreated mantle cell lymphoma. This is not merely competitive pressure — it is a demonstration of how quickly a product can be rendered obsolete by a rival's clinical programme. The strategic implication is that competitive advantage in pharmaceuticals is *time-bounded* and must be continuously renewed.

**Five Forces Summary:** The external environment is structurally challenging. Biosimilar entry threatens all mature biologics, buyer power is increasing through regulatory intervention, and competitive rivalry is intense. This means AbbVie cannot defend existing positions indefinitely; it must continuously generate new sources of advantage. The question is whether its internal resources and capabilities are sufficient to do so.

## 4. Internal Analysis: The Resource-Based View and VRIO Framework

The Resource-Based View (Barney, 1991) argues that sustained competitive advantage derives from resources and capabilities that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Organised (VRIO). This framework directs attention away from the external environment and toward what AbbVie *owns* that competitors cannot easily replicate.

### 4.1 Commercial and Regulatory Infrastructure — **VRIO: Sustained Advantage**

AbbVie operates in over 170 countries with deep relationships with payers, prescribers, and regulatory agencies. AbbVie intends to execute its strategy by leveraging its commercial strength and international infrastructure across therapeutic areas and ensuring strong commercial execution of new product launches. This infrastructure is *valuable* (it accelerates time-to-revenue for new products), *rare* (few firms have equivalent global reach), *inimitable* (built over decades of relationship investment), and *organised* (the Allergan acquisition in 2019 extended it into aesthetics). This is a genuine source of sustained competitive advantage: it means that when AbbVie launches a new product, it does so with a commercial machine that smaller rivals cannot match.

### 4.2 Immunology Franchise (Skyrizi + Rinvoq) — **VRIO: Temporary to Sustained Advantage**

Both Skyrizi and Rinvoq have performed exceptionally well, generating more than $17.6 billion in combined sales in 2024. More precisely, full-year global net revenues from the immunology portfolio were $26.682 billion, with global Skyrizi net revenues of $11.718 billion and global Rinvoq net revenues of $5.971 billion. These figures represent a remarkable replacement of Humira revenue — Skyrizi alone has grown from $3.969 billion in 2023 to $11.718 billion in 2024 (full-year), driven by new indications including Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis.

The VRIO assessment is nuanced. The *molecules* are valuable and currently rare, but their inimitability is time-bounded by patent expiry. However, the *indication breadth* — the accumulated clinical evidence across multiple disease states — is harder to replicate quickly. AbbVie has settled with each of the drugmakers that have filed for FDA approval of Rinvoq generics, preventing copycat versions from entering the U.S. market before April 2037. This legal protection, combined with AbbVie's reaffirmed expectations for a high single-digit compound annual revenue growth rate through 2029, suggests the franchise has a meaningful window of advantage.

### 4.3 R&D Capability and Pipeline Depth — **VRIO: Conditional Advantage**

AbbVie raised its adjusted R&D investment to $10.8 billion in 2024, achieving important regulatory approvals and expanding its pipeline. This investment spans five therapeutic areas: immunology, oncology, neuroscience, aesthetics, and eye care. The pipeline is valuable and the investment scale is rare among mid-tier pharma. However, the *inimitability* of R&D output is conditional on clinical success — a condition that was starkly tested in 2024.

The failure of Cerevel's emraclidine in schizophrenia will cost AbbVie an impairment charge of about $3.5 billion. Specifically, emraclidine did not demonstrate a significant difference from a placebo in the positive and negative syndrome scale total score, a key measure used in assessing schizophrenia symptoms. AbbVie had acquired Cerevel for $8.7 billion, making emraclidine the primary strategic rationale. Emraclidine was the main reason AbbVie acquired Cerevel for $8.7 billion. This failure illustrates the fundamental inimitability challenge of pharmaceutical R&D: even well-resourced firms cannot guarantee clinical outcomes, and the VRIO condition of "organised to exploit" breaks down when the underlying asset fails.

### 4.4 Acquisitive Growth Capability — **VRIO: Valuable but Contested**

AbbVie has demonstrated a consistent ability to identify, acquire, and integrate external assets. AbbVie has significantly bolstered its portfolio through strategic acquisitions in 2024, including ImmunoGen for $10.1 billion, Cerevel Therapeutics for $8.7 billion, and Aliada Therapeutics for $1.4 billion. The ImmunoGen acquisition is particularly strategic: the acquisition adds flagship antibody-drug conjugate (ADC) Elahere (mirvetuximab soravtansine-gynx) for folate receptor-alpha positive platinum-resistant ovarian cancer to AbbVie's portfolio. ADCs represent one of oncology's most productive current platforms, and AbbVie's entry via acquisition is rational given the time required to build internal ADC expertise.

However, the emraclidine write-down demonstrates that acquisition capability is a necessary but not sufficient condition for value creation. The ability to perform due diligence on complex clinical-stage assets — particularly in neuroscience, where mechanism-to-clinical translation is notoriously difficult — is a genuine capability gap.

**VRIO Summary:** AbbVie's most durable advantages are its commercial infrastructure and its immunology franchise (protected to 2037). Its R&D capability is valuable but has demonstrated a conditional failure mode in neuroscience. Its acquisition capability is real but carries integration and clinical risk.

## 5. Strategic Position: Ansoff Matrix Analysis

The Ansoff Matrix (Ansoff, 1957) maps growth strategies across two dimensions: existing vs. new markets, and existing vs. new products. Applied to AbbVie's current portfolio and pipeline, it reveals a firm executing strategies across all four quadrants simultaneously — a sign of strategic ambition, but also of execution risk.

### 5.1 Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets)

AbbVie is aggressively deepening the penetration of Skyrizi and Rinvoq in markets where they are already approved. AbbVie continues to pursue strategies to maintain broad formulary access of Humira and manage the impact of biosimilar erosion — but the more important penetration play is growing Skyrizi and Rinvoq's share of existing immunology patient populations. The evidence that this is working: net revenues for Skyrizi increased 48% for the three months ended March 31, 2024, primarily driven by continued strong performance across all indications.

### 5.2 Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets)

This is the most critical quadrant for AbbVie. The firm is developing new molecules — including ADCs from the ImmunoGen pipeline and neuroscience assets from Cerevel — for markets it already serves or understands. ImmunoGen's pipeline complements AbbVie's existing solid tumor pipeline of novel targeted therapies and next-generation immuno-oncology assets, which have the potential to create new treatment possibilities across multiple solid tumors and hematologic malignancies. The strategic logic is sound: leverage existing oncology commercial infrastructure to commercialise new products.

### 5.3 Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets)

AbbVie is expanding geographic reach for its existing portfolio. Botox Cosmetic is the first neurotoxin approved in China for MMP (masseter muscle prominence), the largest global MMP market, and Allergan Aesthetics intends to develop Botox Cosmetic treatment for MMP in additional global markets. This represents a classic market development strategy: taking a proven product into geographies where it has not previously been indicated or reimbursed.

### 5.4 Diversification (New Products, New Markets)

The acquisition of Cerevel (neuroscience) and ImmunoGen (ADC oncology) represent genuine diversification — entering new product categories in therapeutic areas where AbbVie's historical commercial presence is limited. These acquisitions are strategically aimed at enhancing expertise in high-growth therapeutic areas such as oncology and neuroscience. Diversification carries the highest risk in the Ansoff framework, and the emraclidine failure confirms this: the further AbbVie moves from its immunology core competency, the higher the probability of clinical and commercial misjudgement.

**Ansoff Summary:** AbbVie is simultaneously executing all four growth strategies. This is rational given the scale of the Humira gap, but it creates execution risk and capital allocation challenges. The firm must prioritise quadrants where its VRIO-identified advantages are strongest — principally immunology market penetration and product development.

## 6. SWOT Synthesis

The SWOT framework integrates the external (Five Forces) and internal (VRIO/Ansoff) analyses into a single strategic picture.

| | **Helpful** | **Harmful** |
|---|---|---|
| **Internal** | **Strengths:** Skyrizi/Rinvoq franchise ($17.6B combined, 2024); commercial infrastructure (170+ countries); $10.8B R&D investment; ADC platform via ImmunoGen; Rinvoq exclusivity to 2037 | **Weaknesses:** Imbruvica declining under BTK competition; Cerevel emraclidine failure ($3.5B write-down); heavy acquisition debt load; historical over-reliance on single products |
| **External** | **Opportunities:** Skyrizi/Rinvoq indication expansion; ADC oncology growth (Elahere + pipeline); neuroscience pipeline (post-emraclidine assets); aesthetics geographic expansion; high single-digit CAGR to 2029 | **Threats:** IRA drug price negotiation; biosimilar threat to Skyrizi/Rinvoq post-2033/2037; Imbruvica displacement by Calquence; regulatory risk; pipeline clinical failure rate |

The SWOT reveals a firm in a *transitional* strategic position: strong enough to survive the Humira cliff (as evidenced by full-year net revenues of $56.334 billion in 2024, an increase of 3.7 percent), but not yet securely positioned for the *next* transition. The core strategic risk is that AbbVie recreates a two-product dependency (Skyrizi + Rinvoq) analogous to its former Humira dependency, with the cliff now set for the mid-2030s.

## 7. The Central Strategic Problem

Connecting the frameworks produces a coherent diagnosis:

**Porter's Five Forces** establishes that the external environment structurally punishes single-product dependency: biosimilar entry is inevitable, buyer power is rising through IRA, and competitive rivalry continuously displaces therapeutic class leaders.

**VRIO** shows that AbbVie's most durable advantages — commercial infrastructure and the immunology franchise — are real but time-bounded. The emraclidine failure reveals a specific capability gap in neuroscience clinical development that weakens the firm's ability to diversify into that area.

**Ansoff** demonstrates that AbbVie is pursuing all four growth strategies simultaneously, which is strategically necessary but operationally risky. The highest-probability, highest-return quadrant (product development in immunology) is already being exploited; the highest-risk quadrant (diversification into neuroscience) has already produced a costly failure.

**The strategic problem, precisely stated:** AbbVie has successfully navigated the first phase of the post-Humira transition by growing Skyrizi and Rinvoq to replace lost revenues. However, this success has created a *structural analogue* to the original problem: two products now constitute the majority of immunology revenue, with patent protection extending only to 2033–2037. The firm's diversification strategy — intended to prevent this recurrence — has suffered a significant setback (emraclidine) and has not yet produced a third pillar of comparable scale. AbbVie must use the current window of financial strength to build that third pillar before the next cliff arrives.

## 8. Recommendations and Action Plan

The following recommendations are derived directly from the analytical chain above. Each is grounded in the evidence, connected to the identified strategic problem, and calibrated to AbbVie's specific capabilities and constraints.

---

### Recommendation 1: Accelerate Indication Expansion for Skyrizi and Rinvoq — *Defend the Core*

**Rationale:** The VRIO analysis identifies the immunology franchise as AbbVie's strongest current advantage. The Ansoff framework identifies market penetration and product development in immunology as the highest-probability growth strategies. The Five Forces analysis shows that buyer power (IRA) will compress pricing, making *volume* through indication breadth the primary revenue driver.

**Action:** AbbVie should prioritise clinical programmes that expand Skyrizi and Rinvoq into additional indications — particularly in inflammatory bowel disease, where both drugs have demonstrated strong Phase 3 results. AbbVie is raising its long-term outlook for Skyrizi and Rinvoq revenues, and has raised its 2027 combined sales outlook for the pair to more than $31 billion. Realising this requires sustained indication expansion investment. Concretely, AbbVie should file for at least two additional indications per drug by 2027, targeting conditions with high unmet need and limited existing competition — atopic dermatitis sub-populations and additional IBD indications being the most proximate.

**Why the evidence warrants this conclusion:** Skyrizi's 48% quarterly revenue growth in Q1 2024 was primarily driven by continued strong performance across all indications — demonstrating that indication breadth, not price, is the primary growth driver. This pattern should be extended deliberately.

---

### Recommendation 2: Restructure the Neuroscience Portfolio — *Triage and Refocus*

**Rationale:** The Cerevel acquisition and emraclidine failure reveal a specific capability gap: AbbVie lacks the clinical development expertise to evaluate and advance CNS mechanism-of-action assets with the same confidence it applies to immunology. The $3.5 billion impairment charge is not merely a financial loss; it is a signal that AbbVie's due diligence and clinical development processes are not calibrated for the complexity of psychiatric drug development.

**Action:** AbbVie should conduct a rigorous portfolio review of remaining Cerevel assets, retaining only those with (a) validated mechanisms of action, (b) clear Phase 2 proof-of-concept data, and (c) addressable patient populations large enough to justify continued investment. Assets that do not meet all three criteria should be out-licensed or abandoned. Neuroscience is a key area of focus for AbbVie. In addition to emraclidine, through the Cerevel acquisition AbbVie gained a neuroscience pipeline of multiple clinical-stage and preclinical candidates that are complementary to the company's existing neuroscience portfolio with leading on-market brands in psychiatry, migraine, and Parkinson's disease. The on-market neuroscience portfolio (Botox Therapeutic, Vraylar, Ubrelvy, Qulipta) is already generating full-year global net revenues from the neuroscience portfolio of $7.717 billion, an increase of 18.2 percent — this *commercial* neuroscience capability is real, even if the *clinical development* capability in CNS is not. AbbVie should consider co-development partnerships with specialist CNS biotechs rather than attempting to build this capability entirely in-house.

**Why the evidence warrants this conclusion:** The emraclidine failure occurred despite strong Phase 1b data and the success of a competitor (BMS's Cobenfy) in the same mechanism class, suggesting the failure was not a market-level signal but an asset-specific one. However, the pattern of costly CNS failures across the industry (Alzheimer's, schizophrenia) suggests the capability gap is structural, not idiosyncratic.

---

### Recommendation 3: Build the ADC Platform into a Genuine Third Pillar — *Invest in Oncology*

**Rationale:** The Ansoff analysis identifies product development in oncology as the most strategically important diversification move AbbVie can make. The ImmunoGen acquisition has provided a beachhead. The Five Forces analysis shows that oncology rivalry is intense, but that ADCs represent a differentiated modality with high barriers to imitation (complex manufacturing, proprietary linker-payload chemistry).

**Action:** AbbVie should increase investment in Elahere's label expansion (from platinum-resistant to platinum-sensitive ovarian cancer) and accelerate next-generation ADC clinical programmes. Results from a confirmatory trial currently under review by the FDA show that Elahere is the first targeted agent to offer a survival benefit in platinum-resistant ovarian cancer patients, with label expansion opportunities across larger segments of the ovarian cancer market. Label expansion into platinum-sensitive ovarian cancer would approximately triple the addressable patient population. AbbVie should target regulatory submission for this expanded indication within 18 months, and simultaneously advance at least two additional ADC programmes into Phase 2 by 2026.

**Why the evidence warrants this conclusion:** Full-year global net revenues from the oncology portfolio were $6.555 billion in 2024, representing approximately 11.6% of total revenues. For oncology to constitute a genuine third pillar capable of partially offsetting a future immunology cliff, it needs to reach $12–15 billion by 2030. This requires both Elahere's expansion and successful pipeline progression — both of which are achievable given the current clinical data.

---

### Recommendation 4: Proactively Manage the IRA Exposure — *Engage the Policy Environment*

**Rationale:** The Five Forces analysis identifies rising buyer power through the IRA as a structural threat. In January 2025, HHS published the list of 15 additional drugs that will be subject to price negotiations that will take effect in 2026. As Skyrizi and Rinvoq mature and their Medicare patient volumes grow, they become candidates for future negotiation rounds. AbbVie cannot prevent this; it can only manage the timing and magnitude of impact.

**Action:** AbbVie should (a) engage actively in the IRA negotiation process to maximise the negotiated price, (b) accelerate indication approvals before products enter the negotiation window (drugs with fewer than 9 years of market exclusivity for small molecules, 13 for biologics, are eligible), and (c) structure its pipeline to include assets in therapeutic areas with high unmet need, where negotiators have historically been more accommodating. Additionally, AbbVie should invest in health economics and outcomes research (HEOR) to build the evidentiary base for value-based pricing arguments in negotiations.

**Why the evidence warrants this conclusion:** AbbVie's historical strategy of using patent thickets and litigation to delay pricing pressure has been effective but is increasingly politically contested. According to a congressional inquiry, AbbVie abused the U.S. patent system to block biosimilar competition for its top-selling Humira. A purely defensive IP strategy will attract regulatory scrutiny and reputational damage; a proactive engagement strategy that demonstrates value is more sustainable.

---

### Recommendation 5: Establish a Disciplined Capital Allocation Framework — *Manage the Leverage*

**Rationale:** AbbVie spent approximately $20 billion on acquisitions in 2024 (ImmunoGen: $10.1B; Cerevel: $8.7B; Aliada: $1.4B), financed partly through debt. In connection with the acquisition agreements, AbbVie obtained a commitment for up to $9.0 billion of senior unsecured bridge term loans. The emraclidine write-down demonstrates the clinical risk embedded in this capital deployment. A high-leverage balance sheet reduces AbbVie's flexibility to respond to unexpected setbacks or to seize additional acquisition opportunities.

**Action:** AbbVie should establish explicit capital allocation criteria for future acquisitions: (1) a minimum Phase 2 proof-of-concept requirement for any asset constituting more than 30% of an acquisition's value; (2) a maximum acquisition premium of 40% above the 30-day average share price; (3) a leverage ceiling of 3.0x net debt/EBITDA before any new major acquisition is pursued. These criteria would not have prevented the ImmunoGen acquisition (which had confirmed Phase 3 data) but would have required more rigorous structuring of the Cerevel deal.

**Why the evidence warrants this conclusion:** AbbVie raised its adjusted R&D investment to $10.8 billion in 2024, and reaffirmed expectations for a high single-digit compound annual revenue growth rate through 2029. This growth trajectory, combined with robust operating cash flows, gives AbbVie the financial capacity to be selective. Selectivity — not acquisition volume — is the appropriate response to the emraclidine failure.

## 9. Conclusion: The Coherent Strategic Chain

The analysis produces a clear and connected strategic logic:

1. **External environment** (Porter's Five Forces) shows that single-product dependency is structurally punished by biosimilar entry and rising buyer power. AbbVie must diversify.

2. **Internal capabilities** (VRIO) show that AbbVie's strongest advantages are its immunology franchise and commercial infrastructure, but that its neuroscience clinical development capability is a proven weakness. Diversification must be calibrated to these capabilities.

3. **Growth strategy** (Ansoff) shows that AbbVie is pursuing all four quadrants simultaneously, but that the highest-probability returns lie in immunology product development and oncology diversification — not CNS diversification.

4. **The strategic problem** is a *temporal* one: AbbVie has successfully navigated the first cliff but is at risk of building a second cliff (Skyrizi/Rinvoq expiry, mid-2030s) without a sufficient third pillar.

5. **The recommendations** address this problem by: defending the immunology core through indication expansion; restructuring the neuroscience portfolio to focus on proven assets; building ADC oncology into a $12–15B third pillar; managing IRA exposure proactively; and imposing capital allocation discipline to prevent future Cerevel-type write-downs.

AbbVie's full-year net revenues of $56.334 billion in 2024, an increase of 3.7 percent, demonstrate that the first phase of the post-Humira transition has been executed successfully. The strategic challenge now is to ensure that this recovery is the beginning of durable diversification, not the prelude to a second concentrated dependency. The frameworks applied here converge on a single conclusion: AbbVie's greatest strategic risk is not what it faces today, but what it will face in the mid-2030s if it does not use its current window of financial strength to build a genuinely diversified portfolio.
