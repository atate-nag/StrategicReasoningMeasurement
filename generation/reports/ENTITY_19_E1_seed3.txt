# Diageo plc: Navigating the Post-Premiumisation Plateau — A Strategic Case Study

---

## 1. Introduction and Case Overview

Diageo plc is the world's largest premium spirits company by revenue, headquartered in London and listed on both the London Stock Exchange and the New York Stock Exchange. Diageo is diversified by category, price point and region, and positions itself as an industry leader in total beverage alcohol (TBA). Its portfolio spans more than 200 brands — from Johnnie Walker Scotch whisky and Don Julio tequila to Guinness stout and Ketel One vodka — sold across 180 countries.

For much of the 2010s, Diageo was a textbook case of a company riding the "premiumisation" wave: consumers in developed markets were drinking less but spending more per drink, while an expanding middle class in emerging markets was trading up to international brands. That strategic tailwind has now stalled. In fiscal 2024, organic net sales were down 0.6%, with positive price/mix performance mostly mitigating a decline in volume. The Latin America and Caribbean (LAC) region was a particular drag, with organic net sales declining 23%, driven by softening consumer demand and elevated channel inventory. In fiscal 2025, recovery was only partial: reported net sales of $20.2 billion declined 0.1% due to unfavourable foreign exchange, partially offset by organic net sales growth of 1.7%.

The strategic situation is further complicated by a CEO departure. Debra Crew stepped down as Chief Executive Officer with immediate effect in July 2025 by mutual agreement, with CFO Nik Jhangiani assuming the interim role. Sir Dave Lewis, former Group CEO of Tesco plc, was subsequently appointed CEO effective 1 January 2026, tasked with turning around recent performance at the global drinks giant.

This case study identifies Diageo's central strategic challenge as a **dual disruption**: a cyclical demand correction in its core developed markets, compounded by structural shifts in consumer behaviour — most notably the rise of the "sober-curious" generation and intensifying geopolitical headwinds. The analysis employs PESTEL, Porter's Five Forces, the Resource-Based View (VRIO), the BCG Growth-Share Matrix, and Ansoff's Growth Matrix to diagnose the situation, before developing a set of strategic recommendations.

## 2. The Strategic Situation: Diagnosis

### 2.1 The Premiumisation Model Under Stress

Diageo's core growth strategy for the past decade has been premiumisation — the deliberate migration of consumers up the value ladder. This tiering allows Diageo to move consumers up the value ladder over time. When the macro environment tightens, mainstream labels defend volume; when times are flush, reserve brands drive margin expansion. At the apex sit brands such as Don Julio 1942, Johnnie Walker Blue Label, and Casamigos. This model worked brilliantly during the post-pandemic "revenge spending" boom of 2021–2022, but the reversal has been sharp.

Global on-trade spirits volumes declined by 7.6% in the year to January 2024 as consumers cut their alcohol consumption, with on-trade spirits sales hit by inflation and premiumisation reaching a ceiling. Analysts noted that moribund economic growth and the hangover from inflation may see consumers continue the trend of buying more mainstream products, with premium price points having hit a maximum for now.

### 2.2 Geographic Fault Lines

Diageo's performance is increasingly bifurcated across geographies, creating a complex portfolio management challenge:

- **North America** (c.35% of net sales): The company's most profitable market has been a consistent drag. Organic net sales declined 7.5% driven by Greater China, partially offset by double-digit growth in India and growth across other markets in the region. In North America specifically, weakness reflects both a cautious consumer environment and post-pandemic inventory normalisation.

- **Emerging Markets**: Diageo's fiscal 2025 results underscored the diverging fortunes of its portfolio, with overall organic sales rising 1.7%, as growth was driven by emerging market outperformance. Diageo's plans to deepen its presence in emerging regions remain central to its growth strategy, with targeted investments, localised marketing activations and portfolio innovation expected to sustain momentum.

- **China**: A structural problem has emerged beyond the cyclical. Price/mix declined 12.8% in Asia Pacific, driven largely by weakness in Chinese white spirits (CWS) in China. Diageo estimates that weakness in CWS in China negatively impacted group net sales by approximately 2.5% in the quarter.

### 2.3 Leadership Instability

The CEO departure mid-turnaround is a significant compounding factor. Medium-term guidance has been removed due to current macroeconomic and geopolitical uncertainty in many key markets impacting the pace of recovery. The withdrawal of guidance signals that the Board itself cannot confidently project the trajectory of the business — a rare and serious admission for a company of Diageo's standing.

## 3. PESTEL Analysis

### Political
Diageo is taking a number of actions to mitigate the impact and disruption that tariffs may cause, while continuing to engage with the US administration on the broader impact on the US hospitality industry, including consumers, employees, distributors, restaurants, bars and other retail outlets. The US–UK trade dispute over Scotch whisky tariffs represents a material political risk, given that North America is Diageo's single largest market. The Scotch Whisky Association expressed disappointment that Scotch Whisky could be impacted by these tariffs, while praising the UK government's efforts to find resolution.

### Economic
The Total Beverage Alcohol industry has grown at a 4.4% compound annual growth rate (CAGR) over the past decade, with international spirits growing at 5.1%, indicating attractive long-run sector economics despite near-term volatility. However, the near-term consumer environment is challenged. Inflation has eroded real disposable incomes in Diageo's key Western markets, while government excise receipts topped $200 billion in 2024, with UK spirits duty rising 13.4% in 2023, amplifying volume risk given price elasticity of approximately -0.7.

### Sociocultural
This is arguably the most structurally significant force. The percentage of Millennials and Gen Z who consume alcohol is down 10% and 9% respectively since 2023, with their preference for moderation, wellness, and value fuelling a multi-year decline in beer, wine, and spirits consumption. The growth of "sober-curious" trends — including Dry January, sober bars, and alcohol-free spirits — has given social permission for young people to skip booze without stigma. This is not merely a fad; it represents a generational reorientation of social norms around drinking.

Conversely, on the demand side, the expanding middle class in Southeast Asia and Africa — forecast to add over one billion people by 2030 — boosts disposable income, directly improving demand for branded spirits.

### Technological
Diageo has begun leveraging AI in marketing: in fiscal 2025, the company reduced development costs (non-working) to 14% of A&P spend, down from 21% in fiscal 2024, leveraging AI-driven content production and agile ways of working across its marketing function. Technology also enables direct-to-consumer engagement and personalisation, which are critical for premium brand building.

### Environmental
Diageo's ESG targets include a 40% improvement in water use efficiency in water-stressed areas and becoming net zero carbon across scopes 1, 2 and 3 by 2050. Climate change poses a supply-side risk to key agricultural inputs (barley, agave, grapes), while ESG credentials are increasingly a factor in institutional investor decisions and consumer brand perception.

### Legal
Diageo currently manages 37 active legal cases related to alcohol consumption impacts, with potential financial exposure estimated at £82.4 million. Tightening advertising regulations in several markets and increasing minimum unit pricing legislation in the UK and Ireland add further compliance complexity.

## 4. Porter's Five Forces Analysis

| Force | Assessment | Intensity |
|---|---|---|
| Threat of New Entrants | Low–Moderate | ↓ |
| Bargaining Power of Suppliers | Low | ↓ |
| Bargaining Power of Buyers | Moderate | → |
| Threat of Substitutes | Moderate–High | ↑ |
| Competitive Rivalry | High | ↑ |

**Threat of New Entrants (Low–Moderate):** Alcohol industry regulations create significant market entry barriers, with compliance costs potentially reaching £50–100 million annually for new entrants. Huge capital requirements, fixed costs, specialist equipment expenses, and plant construction costs act as barriers, and companies have entered exclusive retail chain distribution arrangements, limiting new entrants' access to the market. However, craft distillers and celebrity-backed brands (e.g., Ryan Reynolds' Aviation Gin, acquired by Diageo itself) have demonstrated that niche entry is possible, raising the threat modestly.

**Bargaining Power of Suppliers (Low):** There exist substantial switching costs associated with changing ingredient suppliers, particularly for specialised ingredients such as enzymes used in fermentation. Diageo has invested approximately £1 million in developing proprietary yeast strains for its whiskey production, creating an inherent cost barrier should it decide to switch suppliers. Diageo's global scale gives it significant purchasing leverage over most commodity inputs.

**Bargaining Power of Buyers (Moderate):** At the retail level, major supermarkets and off-licence chains wield consolidation power. However, Diageo's iconic brand portfolio reduces retailer substitutability — a consumer requesting Johnnie Walker cannot easily be switched to a private label.

**Threat of Substitutes (Moderate–High, rising):** This is the most dynamic force. The market for non-alcoholic beer, wine, and spirits is expected to increase at a 7% compound annual growth rate (CAGR) between 2024 and 2028, presenting both a threat and an opportunity for alcohol companies. Cannabis-infused beverages, functional drinks, and premium soft drinks are all competing for the same social-occasion consumption moment.

**Competitive Rivalry (High):** Diageo operates in a highly competitive global alcoholic beverages market. Pernod Ricard is Diageo's most direct competitor and the second-largest spirits company globally. Both companies compete across overlapping categories (Scotch, gin, vodka, tequila), and the post-pandemic inventory correction has intensified price competition at the distributor level.

## 5. Resource-Based View: VRIO Analysis

The Resource-Based View (RBV), developed by Barney (1991), holds that sustained competitive advantage derives from resources that are **V**aluable, **R**are, **I**nimitable, and **O**rganised to capture value.

| Resource / Capability | Valuable? | Rare? | Inimitable? | Organised? | Competitive Implication |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Brand Portfolio (Johnnie Walker, Guinness, Don Julio) | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ | **Sustained Advantage** |
| Aged Whisky Inventory | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ | **Sustained Advantage** |
| Global Distribution Network | ✅ | ✅ | ⚠️ Partially | ✅ | **Temporary Advantage** |
| Marketing & Brand-Building Capability | ✅ | ⚠️ Partially | ⚠️ Partially | ✅ | **Competitive Parity** |
| Emerging Market Infrastructure | ✅ | ✅ | ⚠️ Partially | ⚠️ Partially | **Temporary Advantage** |

**Brand Portfolio:** Protected trademarks across the reserve portfolio and multiple industry awards in 2023–2025 for Johnnie Walker special releases, Don Julio and Guinness strengthen pricing power and trade support. Johnnie Walker remains the world's number one Scotch by value; premium tiers drive mix and Scotch provides durable cash flow due to scale and aged-stock advantages. These brands represent decades of investment in heritage, storytelling, and consumer trust that cannot be rapidly replicated.

**Aged Whisky Inventory:** Scotch whisky legally requires a minimum of three years' maturation; premium expressions require 12, 18, or 25 years. This creates a structural inventory moat — a competitor deciding today to enter the premium aged Scotch segment cannot compete on product quality for over a decade.

**Distribution Network:** Diageo's reach across 180 countries is a significant asset, though competitors like Pernod Ricard and LVMH's Moët Hennessy are investing heavily to close the gap in key emerging markets.

**Emerging Market Infrastructure:** Africa delivered strong results, reflecting Diageo's strategic intent to expand its footprint in high-growth, middle-class-driven markets. In Africa, Diageo's strategy is to grow its beer portfolio fast and its spirits brands faster, producing a range of mainstream spirits at the mid-level price range tailored to local tastes and flavours. This localisation capability — combining global brand power with local product development — is genuinely difficult to replicate quickly.

**Critical VRIO Insight:** Diageo's most durable competitive advantages are concentrated in its brand equity and aged inventory. Its current strategic crisis is not a failure of these core resources; it is a failure to *organise* them effectively in response to shifting demand conditions. The VRIO lens thus points toward strategic execution and portfolio reallocation as the priority, not resource creation.

**Stars — Tequila and Guinness:** Don Julio and Casamigos outgrew the US super-premium segment; tequila is among the highest-margin categories and rising as a share of group profit — US tequila value overtook US whiskey in 2023. **Cash Cows — Johnnie Walker and Smirnoff:** These brands generate the cash flows that fund the Stars. The risk is over-milking these cows while underinvesting in the next generation of Stars.

**Question Marks — NoLo and RTD:** Global scaling of Guinness, RTD expansion (Crown Royal RTD, Ketel One Botanical) and experiential taps aim to capture RTD growth projected to outpace total beverage alcohol. Diageo's Seedlip brand (acquired 2019) is its primary NoLo play, but it remains small relative to the scale of the market opportunity. These require strategic commitment to move from Question Mark to Star.

**Dogs — Underperforming Regional Brands:** Certain mainstream vodka brands and declining LAC portfolio brands are consuming management attention and capital without strategic upside. Portfolio rationalisation is warranted.

## 7. Ansoff Growth Matrix

Mapping Diageo's strategic options onto Ansoff's matrix reveals where the company is currently placing its bets — and where it should be placing them:

| | **Existing Markets** | **New Markets** |
|---|---|---|
| **Existing Products** | **Market Penetration** — Regain US spirits share; drive Guinness in existing markets | **Market Development** — Accelerate Africa/India expansion of existing brands |
| **New Products** | **Product Development** — NoLo extensions; RTD innovation; Johnnie Walker non-age-statement expressions | **Diversification** — Alcohol-free spirits category; functional beverage adjacencies |

**Market Penetration (Highest Priority, Short-Term):** Recovering lost ground in North America is existential. Organic net sales returned to growth and increased $101 million or 1.0% in fiscal 2025, driven by positive price/mix of 1.2%, partially offset by a 0.2% volume decline. This recovery is fragile. Diageo must rebuild distributor relationships, reduce channel inventory overhang, and reinvest behind its core US brands.

**Market Development (Highest Priority, Medium-Term):** The structural growth story is in emerging markets. The expanding middle class in Southeast Asia and Africa — forecast to add over one billion people by 2030 — boosts disposable income, directly improving demand for branded spirits. India, in particular, delivered double-digit growth in fiscal 2025 and represents a multi-decade opportunity.

**Product Development (Strategic Imperative):** Post-integration of Don Papa and Casamigos, Diageo targets niche, high-growth categories such as non-alcoholic spirits and ready-to-drink (RTD) cocktails. This is essential to capture the sober-curious consumer without abandoning the brand relationships built over decades.

**Diversification (Exploratory):** This is the highest-risk quadrant and should be pursued cautiously. Functional beverages and wellness drinks represent an adjacency, but Diageo's core competence is in alcohol — diversification into unrelated categories risks diluting management focus during a critical turnaround period.

## 8. The Coherent Strategic Narrative: Connecting the Analysis

The frameworks, taken together, tell a coherent story:

1. **PESTEL** establishes that Diageo faces a convergence of structural headwinds (Gen Z sobriety, tariffs, regulatory pressure) and structural tailwinds (emerging market growth, premiumisation in developing economies). The net macro position is *uncertain but not terminal*.

2. **Porter's Five Forces** shows that the industry structure remains attractive — high barriers to entry, low supplier power — but the threat of substitutes is rising sharply, and competitive rivalry is intensifying in a slower-growth environment. The key strategic implication is that *defending category relevance* is as important as defending market share.

3. **VRIO** reveals that Diageo's brand equity and aged inventory are genuine, durable competitive advantages. The current performance crisis is therefore not a resource problem — it is an *execution and allocation* problem. The company is not deploying its resources optimally across its portfolio and geographies.

4. **BCG** identifies where to invest (Stars: tequila, Guinness), where to harvest (Cash Cows: Scotch, Smirnoff), where to decide (Question Marks: NoLo, RTD), and where to divest (Dogs: underperforming regional brands).

5. **Ansoff** clarifies the sequencing: stabilise existing markets first (penetration), then accelerate emerging market development, while simultaneously building the next-generation product portfolio.

The chain of reasoning is clear: **Diageo's VRIO strengths remain intact, but they are being deployed into a market environment that has shifted. The strategic response must rebalance the portfolio toward growth geographies and growth occasions, while protecting the cash-generative core.**

## 9. Recommendations and Action Plan

### Recommendation 1: Stabilise North America Through Operational Discipline (0–12 months)

The immediate priority is halting the share erosion in Diageo's most profitable market. This requires:

- **Resolve the distributor inventory overhang** through coordinated shipment discipline — prioritising depletions over shipments until channel inventory normalises. The LAC experience, where inventory levels in the channel remained elevated at the end of the first half of fiscal 2024 despite depletions running ahead of shipments, is a cautionary tale that must not be repeated in North America.
- **Reinvest the Accelerate savings behind US brand marketing.** The Accelerate programme is increasing its cost savings target by approximately $125 million, to approximately $625 million over the next three years. A material portion of these savings should be ring-fenced for demand-generating A&P investment in Don Julio, Casamigos, and Crown Royal — the US portfolio's Stars.
- **Prioritise on-premise recovery.** The on-premise channel (bars, restaurants) is disproportionately important for premium brand building and trial. Diageo should deploy its sales force and trade marketing resources to rebuild presence in key US on-premise accounts.

### Recommendation 2: Accelerate the Emerging Market Pivot (12–36 months)

The TBA industry has a long runway for growth, and Diageo has significant headroom, with 4.5% value share of TBA currently against a 2030 ambition of 6%. Closing this gap requires a decisive emerging market push:

- **India as the strategic priority:** India delivered double-digit growth in fiscal 2025 and has a young, aspirational demographic, a growing middle class, and a cultural affinity for Scotch whisky. Diageo should consider deepening local manufacturing partnerships to reduce import duties and improve price competitiveness in the value and standard segments.
- **Africa: beer fast, spirits faster.** Diageo's Africa strategy is explicitly to grow its beer portfolio fast and its spirits brands faster, producing a range of mainstream spirits at the mid-level price range tailored to local tastes and flavours. This localisation model — which differs from the one-size-fits-all premium approach used in developed markets — should be resourced and scaled.
- **Reduce China exposure to Chinese White Spirits.** Weakness in CWS in China negatively impacted group net sales by approximately 2.5% in a single quarter. The CWS category is dominated by domestic competitors with deep cultural embeddedness. Diageo should reorient its China strategy toward international spirits (Scotch, gin) targeting the aspirational consumer, rather than competing directly in CWS.

### Recommendation 3: Build a Credible NoLo Strategy (12–36 months)

The sober-curious trend is structural, not cyclical. Growth projections from the IWSR suggest that the sober movement is likely to continue, with the market for non-alcoholic beer, wine, and spirits expected to increase at a 7% CAGR between 2024 and 2028. Diageo cannot afford to cede this occasion to competitors.

- **Scale Seedlip aggressively.** Diageo's Seedlip brand is a first-mover in premium non-alcoholic spirits, but it remains a niche player. Investment in distribution, marketing, and NPD is needed to convert this Question Mark into a Star.
- **Develop NoLo extensions of hero brands.** Ketel One Botanical already demonstrates the model — a brand extension that captures health-conscious consumers without cannibalising the core. Johnnie Walker 0.0 and Guinness 0.0 (already in market) should be supported with dedicated media investment.
- **Target Gen Z through occasions, not just products.** Although Gen Z drinks less overall, legal-age Gen Z consumers still spend on alcohol when the product matches their lifestyle, taste preferences, and social context. Diageo should invest in occasion-based marketing (music festivals, sports, social dining) that positions its brands — alcoholic and non-alcoholic — as part of a broader lifestyle portfolio.

### Recommendation 4: Portfolio Rationalisation — Divest the Dogs (6–18 months)

The BCG analysis suggests Diageo is carrying brands that dilute management focus and capital allocation. The company should:

- **Conduct a formal portfolio review** to identify brands with sub-threshold growth prospects and below-hurdle returns on invested capital.
- **Divest or license underperforming regional brands** in categories where Diageo lacks scale advantage. The proceeds should be redeployed into the Stars (tequila, Guinness) and the emerging market build.
- **Resist the temptation to acquire.** In a period of balance sheet repair — Diageo is committed to delivering approximately $3 billion free cash flow in fiscal 2026 to strengthen its balance sheet — large M&A would be strategically premature. Organic investment and selective bolt-on acquisitions in RTD and NoLo are preferable.

### Recommendation 5: Stabilise Leadership and Restore Strategic Clarity (Immediate)

The withdrawal of medium-term guidance and CEO departure have damaged investor confidence. Sir Dave Lewis, a proven CEO with extensive marketing and brand-building experience and an outstanding track record leading global consumer businesses, has been appointed to lead the turnaround. His Tesco experience — stabilising a large, complex consumer business under pressure — is directly relevant. The new CEO should:

- **Publish a clear strategic framework within 90 days** of taking office, reinstating medium-term targets once the macro environment permits.
- **Communicate a simplified strategic narrative** to investors, employees, and trade partners: *protect the core, invest in growth geographies, build for the next generation of consumers.*
- **Anchor the Accelerate programme to strategic outcomes**, not just cost targets. Cost savings are a means to an end — the end being reinvestment in growth, not just margin expansion.

## 10. Conclusion

Diageo's current strategic predicament is best understood not as a crisis of competitive position, but as a crisis of strategic adaptation. Its core resources — an unrivalled brand portfolio, aged inventory, and global distribution — remain formidable and defensible. What has changed is the environment in which those resources must operate: demand in developed markets has plateaued, the sober-curious movement is reshaping consumer occasions, geopolitical disruption is complicating trade flows, and leadership continuity has been broken.

The analytical chain developed in this case study leads to a clear conclusion: Diageo must execute a **three-horizon strategy** — stabilise the core (Horizon 1), accelerate the emerging market pivot (Horizon 2), and build the next-generation portfolio for health-conscious consumers (Horizon 3) — all while exercising the financial discipline required to restore balance sheet strength and investor confidence.

The TBA industry's long-run fundamentals remain attractive. International spirits have grown at a 5.1% CAGR over the past decade. Diageo's VRIO advantages mean it is better positioned than any competitor to capture that growth — but only if its new leadership team executes the strategic reorientation with the urgency the situation demands.
