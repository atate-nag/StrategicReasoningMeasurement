# AbbVie Inc.: Navigating the Post-Humira Era — A Strategic Case Study

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This case study examines AbbVie's central strategic challenge: how to manage the structural revenue collapse caused by the loss of exclusivity (LOE) of Humira — once the world's best-selling drug — while simultaneously building a durable, diversified enterprise. The analysis employs Porter's Five Forces, the Resource-Based View (RBV)/VRIO framework, the Ansoff Growth Matrix, and a SWOT analysis to diagnose AbbVie's position and evaluate its strategic responses. The case concludes with a prioritised action plan grounded in the evidence.

---

## 1. SITUATIONAL BACKGROUND: THE STRATEGIC PROBLEM

AbbVie was spun off from Abbott Laboratories in 2013 with Humira (adalimumab) as its principal asset. For nearly a decade, Humira was the world's best-selling drug, generating peak annual revenues of approximately $21 billion. This concentration was both AbbVie's greatest strength and its most acute vulnerability. When U.S. biosimilar competition began in earnest in January 2023, the company faced what the academic literature on pharmaceutical strategy describes as an existential "patent cliff" — a predictable but potentially catastrophic revenue event arising from the expiry of market exclusivity on a blockbuster asset.

The global Humira market, estimated at $10.34 billion in 2024, is projected to shrink to $4.11 billion by 2030, declining at a compound annual rate of approximately 11.3%, as the FDA approved numerous biosimilars — including Amjevita, Hyrimoz, and Yuflyma — which introduced significant pricing competition.

AbbVie's Humira market share dropped to 82% of the anti-inflammatory adalimumab market by May 2024, according to Samsung Bioepis's biosimilar report.

The strategic situation is therefore this: AbbVie must replace a revenue stream that was generating over $20 billion annually at its peak, in an environment of intensifying biosimilar competition, growing regulatory pricing pressure, and a pipeline that suffered a high-profile late-stage failure. The question is whether AbbVie's strategic responses — organic pipeline investment, targeted M&A, and portfolio diversification — are sufficient to sustain competitive advantage.

## 2. INDUSTRY ANALYSIS: PORTER'S FIVE FORCES

Porter's (1980) Five Forces framework provides a structural account of the competitive pressures shaping AbbVie's industry environment. The framework matters here because it explains *why* the Humira cliff was so damaging and *what structural conditions* AbbVie's new strategy must navigate.

### 2.1 Threat of New Entrants — **Moderate but Rising**

The pharmaceutical industry erects formidable entry barriers through patent protection, regulatory approval requirements, and the capital intensity of R&D. However, the biosimilar pathway created by the Biologics Price Competition and Innovation Act (BPCIA) in the United States has lowered the entry barrier for follow-on biologics. Biosimilar versions of Humira entered the U.S. market in 2023 after AbbVie reached a settlement with Amgen in 2016, with Amgen arguing that most of AbbVie's patents on Humira were invalid. This is a critical structural point: AbbVie's "patent thicket" strategy — building a wall of secondary patents to delay biosimilar entry — ultimately failed to provide permanent protection, confirming that entry barriers in biologics are finite rather than permanent. The implication for strategy is that any competitive advantage built solely on patent exclusivity is inherently time-limited, and sustainable advantage must be rooted in capabilities that are harder to replicate.

### 2.2 Bargaining Power of Buyers — **High and Intensifying**

Buyers in the pharmaceutical industry — primarily pharmacy benefit managers (PBMs), hospital systems, and government payers — wield significant power, especially when therapeutic alternatives exist. The Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) authorises the Secretary of HHS to negotiate prices directly with manufacturers for drugs with the highest Medicare spending, with the Congressional Budget Office forecasting that the programme will reduce net prices for selected drugs by approximately 50% on average. This is structurally significant for AbbVie: Skyrizi and Rinvoq — the drugs replacing Humira — are precisely the kind of high-spending immunology biologics that attract Medicare negotiation. While the IRA targets Medicare-specific cost reductions through direct negotiation, an Executive Order introduces a broader international pricing benchmark that could influence both public and private markets. Buyer power therefore represents an ongoing structural threat to AbbVie's pricing architecture, not merely a one-time event.

### 2.3 Bargaining Power of Suppliers — **Low**

AbbVie's suppliers — contract manufacturers, raw material providers — hold limited power relative to a company of AbbVie's scale and vertical integration. This force is not a primary driver of AbbVie's strategic situation and is not elaborated further.

### 2.4 Threat of Substitutes — **High**

The threat of substitutes operates at two levels. First, biosimilars directly substitute for Humira, as documented above. Second, within the immunology space, competing branded biologics from Johnson & Johnson (Stelara, Tremfya), Eli Lilly (Taltz), and UCB (Bimzelx) offer therapeutic alternatives to Skyrizi and Rinvoq, constraining AbbVie's pricing power on its growth drivers. AbbVie competes with other research-based pharmaceutical and biotechnology companies that research, develop, manufacture, market and sell proprietary pharmaceutical products and biologics, all of which may introduce new products or develop technological advances in therapeutic areas such as immunology, oncology, aesthetics, neuroscience and eye care.

### 2.5 Competitive Rivalry — **Intense**

AbbVie contends with intense rivalry from major biopharmaceutical companies like Roche, Novartis, and Pfizer, all possessing vast financial resources. The oncology segment illustrates this vividly: Imbruvica earned just over $1.1 billion in Q2 2024, a decline of 17% from the same period the prior year, as AbbVie executives acknowledged that AstraZeneca's Calquence had taken away market share in leukaemia, particularly for patients who had not previously received other drugs. As Imbruvica stumbled, BeiGene's Brukinsa and AstraZeneca's Calquence became locked in rivalry for the BTK inhibitor market, with Imbruvica losing doctor favour in part because of its safety profile.

**Five Forces Summary:** The overall structural attractiveness of AbbVie's industry is moderate-to-low. Buyer power is intensifying via regulatory intervention; the threat of substitutes is high across all segments; and rivalry is fierce. This structural assessment warrants the conclusion that AbbVie cannot rely on market structure for protection — it must build resource-based advantages that are difficult to imitate.

## 3. INTERNAL ANALYSIS: RESOURCE-BASED VIEW AND VRIO FRAMEWORK

Barney's (1991) Resource-Based View (RBV) argues that sustainable competitive advantage derives from resources that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Organised to be exploited (VRIO). Applied to AbbVie, this framework identifies which of its capabilities can generate durable advantage beyond the Humira era.

### 3.1 Immunology R&D and Commercial Capabilities — **Sustained Competitive Advantage**

AbbVie's core competence in immunology — built over decades of Humira commercialisation — represents a genuinely rare and inimitable capability. The company's understanding of cytokine biology, its relationships with rheumatologists and gastroenterologists, its clinical trial infrastructure, and its regulatory expertise in biologics are all embedded in organisational routines that competitors cannot rapidly replicate. The evidence that this capability is being successfully deployed on next-generation assets is compelling:

Full-year 2024 global Skyrizi net revenues were $11.718 billion and Rinvoq net revenues were $5.971 billion, contributing to a combined $17.6 billion in immunology sales; AbbVie now expects combined Skyrizi and Rinvoq 2027 revenues of more than $31 billion, an increase of approximately $4 billion compared to previous guidance.

AbbVie raised its 2027 combined Skyrizi and Rinvoq guidance to more than $31 billion, with Skyrizi expected to account for more than $20 billion annually — roughly what Humira earned at its peak — and Rinvoq $11 billion.

This evidence supports the inference that AbbVie's immunology capability is not merely valuable (it generates revenue) but rare and inimitable (competitors have not replicated it at scale). The VRIO test is passed: this is a source of sustained competitive advantage.

### 3.2 Commercial Scale and Global Infrastructure — **Competitive Parity / Advantage**

AbbVie's global commercial infrastructure — a worldwide salesforce, market access teams, and payer relationships across 175 countries — represents a valuable and somewhat rare resource. However, rivals such as Johnson & Johnson and Pfizer possess comparable infrastructure, limiting its uniqueness. Its primary strategic value is as an *organisational* resource that amplifies the impact of new product launches. AbbVie intends to maximise the benefits of a diversified revenue base with multiple long-term growth drivers, leveraging its commercial strength and international infrastructure across therapeutic areas and ensuring strong commercial execution of new product launches.

### 3.3 M&A Capability and Financial Resources — **Temporary Advantage**

AbbVie has demonstrated a pattern of large, strategically targeted acquisitions: Pharmacyclics (2015, $21 billion, acquiring Imbruvica), Allergan (2020, $63 billion, acquiring Botox and neuroscience assets), ImmunoGen (2024, $10.1 billion, acquiring the ADC Elahere), and Cerevel Therapeutics (2024, $8.7 billion, acquiring a neuroscience pipeline). AbbVie has executed more than 30 M&A transactions and invested $10.8 billion in R&D in 2024, with R&D expenses for the twelve months ending March 2025 reaching $12.919 billion, a 76.44% increase year-over-year. This financial firepower is valuable but not rare — large pharma rivals can also deploy capital. Its strategic value depends on *how well* acquisitions are integrated and whether they yield pipeline assets that pass clinical validation. The emraclidine failure (discussed below) illustrates the limits of M&A as a reliable capability.

### 3.4 Neuroscience Portfolio — **Emerging Capability, Not Yet Sustained Advantage**

Full-year 2024 global neuroscience net revenues were $7.717 billion, an increase of 18.2% on a reported basis, driven by Botox Therapeutic ($2.991 billion), Vraylar, Ubrelvy, and Qulipta. The neuroscience portfolio is growing rapidly, but it remains anchored in assets acquired from Allergan rather than generated organically. The Cerevel acquisition was intended to deepen this pipeline, but the failure of emraclidine — the flagship Cerevel asset — is a material setback:

AbbVie announced that its two Phase 2 EMPOWER trials investigating emraclidine as a once-daily oral monotherapy for adults with schizophrenia experiencing acute psychotic symptoms did not meet their primary endpoint of a statistically significant reduction in the Positive and Negative Syndrome Scale (PANSS) total score compared with placebo at week 6.

Emraclidine, once projected to compete with Bristol Myers Squibb's Cobenfy, failed to beat a placebo in two Phase 2 clinical trials; it was the lead asset from AbbVie's $8.7 billion acquisition of Cerevel Therapeutics.

This failure demonstrates the inherent uncertainty of pharmaceutical R&D and signals that the neuroscience capability is not yet sufficiently proven to constitute a sustained competitive advantage. It passes the "valuable" test (neuroscience is a large market with unmet need) but fails the "inimitable" test at present (AbbVie has not yet demonstrated a repeatable capability to generate neuroscience approvals).

## 5. GROWTH STRATEGY ANALYSIS: ANSOFF MATRIX

Ansoff's (1957) Product/Market Growth Matrix provides a framework for categorising AbbVie's strategic moves by their risk profile and growth logic.

### 5.1 Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets) — **Current Primary Strategy**

AbbVie's most immediate growth lever is deepening the penetration of Skyrizi and Rinvoq in their approved indications. In 2025, Skyrizi and Rinvoq propelled AbbVie to a record-breaking $61.1 billion in total sales, an 8.6% increase over 2024 that eclipsed the company's previous peak revenue of $58 billion in 2022. This demonstrates that market penetration — converting Humira patients and gaining new prescriptions in psoriasis, psoriatic arthritis, and rheumatoid arthritis — is working. The risk is low because the markets are known and the products are approved; the constraint is competitive pressure from J&J and Eli Lilly.

### 5.2 Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets) — **Critical Medium-Term Strategy**

AbbVie is investing heavily in expanding the label indications of Skyrizi and Rinvoq into new disease areas within its existing immunology customer base. Rinvoq has received approvals in atopic dermatitis and ulcerative colitis; Skyrizi has been approved for Crohn's disease. These represent product development moves — new clinical applications to the same rheumatology/gastroenterology prescriber base. This strategy carries moderate risk (clinical trial uncertainty) but leverages existing commercial infrastructure, making it the highest-expected-value growth option in the near-to-medium term.

### 5.3 Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets) — **Selective International Expansion**

AbbVie's international expansion of Skyrizi and Rinvoq — particularly in Europe and Japan — represents market development. Full-year 2024 global immunology net revenues were $26.682 billion, an increase of 2.1% on a reported basis, or 2.9% on an operational basis, with global Skyrizi net revenues of $11.718 billion and Rinvoq net revenues of $5.971 billion. International markets represent a significant opportunity, particularly as biosimilar erosion of Humira is more advanced internationally (where biosimilars entered earlier), accelerating the transition to next-generation agents.

### 5.4 Diversification (New Products, New Markets) — **Long-Term Hedge, High Risk**

The Allergan acquisition (aesthetics, neuroscience), the ImmunoGen acquisition (ADC oncology), and the Cerevel acquisition (psychiatric/neurological drugs) all represent diversification moves — entering new therapeutic areas and customer segments. The Allergan acquisition significantly expanded and diversified AbbVie's revenue base, complementing its immunology leadership with new growth opportunities in neuroscience (Botox Therapeutics, Vraylar, Ubrelvy) and a global aesthetics business.

However, diversification is the riskiest quadrant of the Ansoff matrix, and the emraclidine failure illustrates this: AbbVie's fourth-quarter 2024 results included a partial intangible asset impairment charge related to emraclidine, contributing to a diluted loss per share of $0.02 on a GAAP basis. The $8.7 billion paid for Cerevel yielded a Phase 2 failure in its lead asset, demonstrating that diversification through M&A carries substantial execution risk.

**Ansoff Summary:** AbbVie's portfolio of strategic moves spans all four quadrants, which is strategically coherent — it reduces dependence on any single growth path — but it also spreads risk management attention thinly. The evidence warrants the conclusion that market penetration and product development in immunology should remain the primary resource allocation priority, with diversification pursued selectively and with greater clinical de-risking before large capital commitments.

## 6. DYNAMIC CAPABILITIES AND THE TRANSITION CHALLENGE

Teece, Pisano, and Shuen (1997) argue that in rapidly changing environments, competitive advantage derives not just from static resources but from *dynamic capabilities* — the ability to sense new opportunities, seize them through investment decisions, and reconfigure the asset base. AbbVie's situation is precisely the kind of environmental shift — technological (biosimilars), regulatory (IRA), and competitive — that demands dynamic capability.

The evidence suggests AbbVie has demonstrated dynamic capability in two respects. First, it sensed the Humira cliff well in advance and began investing in Skyrizi and Rinvoq before the cliff arrived, enabling a smoother transition. AbbVie's CEO stated: "We are entering 2025 with significant momentum and expect net revenues to exceed their previous peak in just the second full year following the U.S. Humira loss of exclusivity." Second, it reconfigured its asset base through a series of acquisitions totalling over $80 billion since 2015, building positions in aesthetics, oncology, and neuroscience.

However, dynamic capability also requires the ability to *reconfigure* when acquisitions underperform. The emraclidine failure demands a rapid strategic response — not defensive rationalisation. After the Phase 2 failure of its lead Cerevel asset, AbbVie reset expectations and narrowed the clinical programme to an adjunct approach, demonstrating at least a partial reconfiguration response. Whether this reconfiguration is sufficient — or whether the neuroscience thesis requires more fundamental reassessment — is a central strategic question addressed in the recommendations.

## 7. CRITICAL STRATEGIC TENSIONS

Before turning to recommendations, it is important to identify the three core tensions in AbbVie's current position, as these define the strategic choices the company faces.

**Tension 1: Concentration vs. Diversification.** AbbVie successfully escaped Humira dependence by concentrating resources on Skyrizi and Rinvoq — but this creates a new form of concentration. Combined Skyrizi and Rinvoq revenues of $25.9 billion in 2025 already surpassed AbbVie's own 2027 long-term guidance target by $500 million, two full years early. While impressive, this means that AbbVie's growth narrative is again substantially dependent on two assets. If either faces unexpected clinical setbacks, regulatory action, or earlier-than-expected biosimilar competition (Skyrizi's composition-of-matter patent expires around 2031), the revenue risk is concentrated.

**Tension 2: M&A Firepower vs. Organic R&D.** AbbVie's acquisition-led growth model has generated both successes (Allergan's Botox, ImmunoGen's Elahere) and failures (emraclidine). AbbVie invested $10.8 billion in R&D in 2024, with expenses rising 76.44% year-over-year. The question is whether this investment is sufficiently balanced between internal pipeline development and external acquisition, or whether AbbVie is systematically overpaying for late-stage assets and under-investing in earlier-stage internal discovery.

**Tension 3: Short-Term Revenue Defence vs. Long-Term Innovation.** The IRA creates a structural incentive problem: drugs that achieve commercial success become targets for Medicare price negotiation, which reduces the return on innovation. The Congressional Budget Office forecasts that the IRA negotiation programme will reduce net prices for selected drugs by about 50% on average, and in January 2025 CMS released a list of the next 15 drugs selected for price setting. This pressure may distort AbbVie's R&D allocation toward shorter-cycle, lower-risk programmes that generate revenue quickly but are more vulnerable to negotiation, at the expense of longer-cycle, higher-risk innovations with more durable exclusivity.

The following recommendations are derived directly from the analytical chain above: the Five Forces analysis identifies structural threats; the RBV/VRIO analysis identifies where durable advantage can be built; the Ansoff analysis identifies the risk-return profile of each growth path; and the dynamic capabilities lens identifies where reconfiguration is needed. ### Recommendation 1: Accelerate Skyrizi and Rinvoq Label Expansion as the Primary Growth Engine
**Priority: Immediate | Risk: Moderate | Rationale: Builds on proven VRIO advantage**

The VRIO analysis establishes that AbbVie's immunology R&D and commercial capability is the firm's only resource that currently passes all four tests for sustained competitive advantage. The Ansoff analysis shows that product development in existing markets (new indications for Skyrizi/Rinvoq) offers the best risk-adjusted return. AbbVie reaffirmed expectations for a high single-digit compound annual revenue growth rate through 2029, with Skyrizi revenues expected to exceed $20 billion and Rinvoq revenues to exceed $11 billion by 2027. AbbVie should prioritise regulatory submissions for additional indications — including atopic dermatitis for Skyrizi and additional IBD indications for Rinvoq — and allocate disproportionate commercial investment to converting Humira patients and winning new prescriptions in gastroenterology, where the competitive moat is strongest. The inference from the Five Forces analysis is that competitive rivalry from J&J's Tremfya and Stelara is the primary threat to this strategy; AbbVie should invest in head-to-head clinical trials demonstrating superiority to maintain prescriber preference.

### Recommendation 2: Restructure the Neuroscience Strategy Around Proven Assets and Disciplined Pipeline Investment
**Priority: Urgent | Risk: High | Rationale: Addresses VRIO weakness; limits M&A overpayment risk**

The emraclidine Phase 2 failure is not merely a clinical disappointment — it is a strategic signal. AbbVie paid $8.7 billion for Cerevel on the basis of Phase 1b data in a notoriously difficult therapeutic area (CNS drug development has the highest clinical failure rate of any therapeutic area). This represents a failure of the "seize" element of dynamic capability — committing too much capital too early in the clinical de-risking process. The recommendation is twofold:

First, AbbVie should continue to pursue the remaining Cerevel assets — particularly tavapadon for Parkinson's disease, which is in Phase 3 and represents a more advanced, better-de-risked opportunity. Tavapadon is a first-in-class dopamine D1/D5 selective partial agonist for managing Parkinson's disease, currently in Phase 3 clinical trials with proven efficacy and safety.

Second, AbbVie should revise its neuroscience M&A criteria to require Phase 2 proof-of-concept data before committing more than $3 billion to any single CNS asset. This is a direct response to the emraclidine lesson: the premium paid for early-stage CNS assets does not reflect the probability of Phase 2 success, which is structurally lower than in immunology or oncology.

### Recommendation 4: Develop a Proactive IRA Response Strategy
**Priority: Immediate | Risk: High if unaddressed | Rationale: Addresses buyer power threat from Five Forces**

The Five Forces analysis identifies intensifying buyer power through the IRA as a structural threat that will not diminish. The IRA creates a perverse incentive: the more successful a drug becomes, the more likely it is to be selected for Medicare price negotiation, with projected price reductions of ~50%. AbbVie's response should be threefold:

First, **portfolio sequencing**: structure the launch timing of new indications for Skyrizi and Rinvoq to maximise the period of unrestricted commercial pricing before IRA negotiation eligibility (currently triggered after 9 years for small molecules and 13 years for biologics under the IRA). Second, **indication breadth**: pursue rare disease and paediatric indications where IRA negotiation exemptions may apply, generating both clinical and commercial value. Third, **pricing architecture**: work with PBMs and payers to establish value-based contracts that demonstrate cost-effectiveness relative to alternatives, providing a defensible pricing narrative when negotiation occurs. While the IRA targets Medicare-specific cost reductions through direct negotiation, the Executive Order introduces a broader international pricing benchmark that could influence both public and private markets, making a proactive pricing strategy essential.

## 9. CONCLUSION: THE COHERENT STRATEGIC CHAIN

The analysis generates a coherent strategic logic. AbbVie's core problem — catastrophic revenue concentration in a single patent-protected asset — was structurally predictable from the pharmaceutical industry's patent cliff dynamics. The Five Forces analysis explains why the industry environment is structurally unfavourable: rising buyer power (IRA), high threat of substitutes (biosimilars), and intense rivalry. The RBV/VRIO analysis identifies that AbbVie's only source of sustained competitive advantage is its immunology R&D and commercial capability — and the evidence from Skyrizi and Rinvoq's performance confirms this. If AbbVie executes the recommendations above — concentrating on immunology excellence, restructuring neuroscience around proven assets, building the ADC oncology platform, proactively managing IRA exposure, and revitalising aesthetics internationally — it has the resource base and strategic position to achieve this target. The risk is not execution failure in immunology, where the track record is strong, but rather the concentration risk of a portfolio that has successfully replaced one dominant asset (Humira) with another pair of dominant assets (Skyrizi and Rinvoq), and the ongoing challenge of building genuinely durable advantage in therapeutic areas — neuroscience and oncology — where its capabilities are still being proven.
