# ExxonMobil at the Crossroads: Doubling Down on Hydrocarbons While Building a Low-Carbon Hedge

## A Strategic Case Study

---

## 1. Introduction and Case Overview

ExxonMobil Corporation (NYSE: XOM) is the world's largest publicly traded international oil and gas company by market capitalisation, operating across the full energy value chain — from upstream exploration and production, through downstream refining, to chemicals and, increasingly, low-carbon solutions. The strategic situation examined in this case study centres on a fundamental tension: ExxonMobil is simultaneously executing the most aggressive hydrocarbon growth programme in its recent history while positioning a nascent low-carbon business as a hedge against long-run demand destruction. This dual-track strategy — what the company calls being "positioned for growth irrespective of the pace and direction of the energy transition" — raises critical questions about internal coherence, resource allocation, and long-term competitive positioning.

ExxonMobil describes its strategy as "a bold blueprint to win, irrespective of pace and direction of the energy transition," using projections from its Global Outlook as the basis for business planning, including lower-demand assumptions in extreme scenarios. The strategic tension is sharpened by two concurrent acquisitions — Pioneer Natural Resources (completed May 2024, ~$60 billion) and Denbury Inc. (completed November 2023, ~$4.9 billion) — which together represent the largest capital commitment in ExxonMobil's modern history, betting heavily on the continued primacy of fossil fuels even as the energy transition accelerates globally.

This case study analyses that strategic situation using five interconnected frameworks: PESTEL analysis (macro-environment), Porter's Five Forces (industry structure), the Resource-Based View / VRIN framework (internal capabilities), the Ansoff Growth Matrix (growth strategy options), and Porter's Generic Strategies (competitive positioning). The analysis culminates in a set of actionable strategic recommendations.

## 2. The Strategic Situation: Diagnosis

### 2.1 Financial Context

ExxonMobil delivered $33.7 billion in earnings and $55.0 billion in cash flow from operations in 2024 — its third-best year in a decade — achieving record production in the Permian Basin and Guyana, and record sales volumes of high-value products. The company distributed $36.0 billion to shareholders in 2024 — more than all but five companies in the S&P 500 — and achieved $12.1 billion in cumulative structural cost savings since 2019, more than offsetting inflation and growth costs.

This financial strength is not incidental to the strategy — it is the foundation of it. The ability to fund a $27–$29 billion annual capital programme while simultaneously returning $36 billion to shareholders is only possible because of the cost-leadership discipline built into ExxonMobil's operating model. ExxonMobil's capital allocation approach prioritises "competitively advantaged, high-return, low-cost-of-supply, value-accretive investments," with the company expecting capital investments to generate average returns of approximately 30%, with payback periods of less than 10 years for more than 90% of capex.

### 2.2 The Dual-Track Strategy

ExxonMobil is pursuing two simultaneous strategic thrusts that exist in productive tension:

**Track 1 — Hydrocarbon Growth:** ExxonMobil now expects Permian production to reach about 2.5 million barrels of oil equivalent per day (MMboed) by 2030, roughly double 2024 levels, reflecting technology gains, improved capital efficiency, and integration benefits from the Pioneer acquisition. Total upstream output is projected to reach 5.5 MMboed by 2030, with advantaged assets contributing approximately 65% of total volumes.

**Track 2 — Low-Carbon Hedge:** ExxonMobil is pursuing up to $30 billion in lower-emissions investment opportunities from 2025 to 2030, investing $27–$29 billion of cash capex in 2025 and $28–$33 billion annually from 2026 to 2030. ExxonMobil, having already captured over 120 million metric tons of CO₂, is actively expanding its portfolio to include carbon capture and storage (CCS), hydrogen, and lithium, spearheaded by its Low Carbon Solutions business.

The central strategic question is whether these two tracks are genuinely complementary or whether they represent an unstable compromise that leaves ExxonMobil neither fully committed to transition nor fully insulated from it.

## 3. PESTEL Analysis: The Macro-Environment

### 3.1 Political

The political environment for ExxonMobil is bifurcated. In the United States, the Trump administration's pro-fossil-fuel stance has reduced regulatory pressure on domestic drilling. However, globally, the political trajectory is towards tighter carbon regulation. Geopolitical risks can disrupt supply chains and cause price fluctuations — the 2024–2025 period saw oil prices react strongly to Middle East tensions — while stricter emission controls and renewable energy focus continue to influence demand.

Critically, ExxonMobil has taken an unusual political stance for an oil major. In the company's 2024 10-K, ExxonMobil discusses the risks of climate change, efforts to reach net zero by 2050, and states that "the company's objective is to play a leading role in the energy transition." The company also publicly supports a price on carbon, a position that distinguishes it from many peers and which, strategically, would advantage ExxonMobil's CCS business relative to competitors with higher-cost abatement.

### 3.2 Economic

Oil price volatility remains the dominant economic risk. ExxonMobil's 2030 plan is modelled on $65/barrel Brent in real terms — a conservative but not catastrophic assumption. The structural cost savings programme is designed precisely to lower the break-even price, so that ExxonMobil remains profitable even in a weaker price environment. ExxonMobil's CEO Darren Woods stated that "oil consumption goes up every year…2024 is going to be the highest, and we expect 2025 is going to be higher," with the company's philosophy being to "get the lowest cost barrels." This economic logic — that the last barrels standing in a declining market will be the cheapest ones — is the core rationale for the hydrocarbon growth track.

### 3.3 Social

Social licence to operate is under increasing pressure. ExxonMobil filed a lawsuit on January 22, 2024, to exclude a shareholder proposal from activist investors Arjuna Capital and Follow This from its 2024 proxy statement, arguing that in each of the past two years, shareholders had overwhelmingly rejected proposals with substantially the same subject matter. This legal manoeuvre — unprecedented among major corporations — signals an aggressive posture toward ESG activists, but it also carries reputational risk by appearing to suppress shareholder voice on climate accountability.

### 3.4 Technological

Technological change is both a threat and an opportunity. Electric vehicles are displacing oil demand in transport — the IEA's Global EV Outlook projects that increased EV penetration will displace nearly 5 million barrels per day (mb/d) in oil consumption growth from 2024 to 2030 — but ExxonMobil is also using technology to enter new markets. Oil and gas majors like ExxonMobil are leveraging their subsurface and fluid-handling expertise to enter the critical minerals space through direct lithium extraction (DLE) from brines, driven by surging demand for lithium and policy incentives.

### 3.5 Environmental

The environmental dimension presents ExxonMobil's most acute long-run strategic risk. ExxonMobil has made no net-zero emissions by 2050 commitment and has no GHG reduction targets covering Scope 3 emissions; the company reported at least 650 million tonnes of Scope 3 emissions from petroleum product sales in 2020, compared with 112 million tonnes of Scope 1 and 2 operational emissions. This Scope 3 gap is strategically significant because it represents the emissions from customers burning ExxonMobil's products — the very activity that drives demand-side transition risk.

### 3.6 Legal

Carbon Tracker has identified ExxonMobil's $10 billion Golden Pass LNG project as one of 15 newly approved oil and gas projects at risk of becoming stranded assets as the world pursues decarbonisation efforts. The five supermajors have 9–17 years' worth of oil and gas reserves (proved reserves divided by current production), with ExxonMobil at 17 years, meaning that declining demand growth, even over long time frames, significantly impacts discounted cash flow valuations. Regulatory tightening — carbon taxes, methane regulations, mandatory climate disclosures — represents a material legal risk to the hydrocarbon growth track.

## 4. Porter's Five Forces: Industry Structure

### 4.1 Threat of New Entrants — Low to Moderate

The capital intensity of upstream oil and gas exploration creates formidable barriers to entry. ExxonMobil's integrated business model, technological leadership, and global presence have established it as a dominant force in the energy sector. However, the threat of new entrants is rising in the low-carbon segments ExxonMobil is entering: CCS, hydrogen, and lithium attract well-capitalised technology firms, utilities, and national oil companies with different cost structures and government backing.

### 4.2 Bargaining Power of Suppliers — Moderate to High

Technological advancements significantly influence supplier bargaining power within ExxonMobil's operational landscape. Suppliers offering cutting-edge technologies crucial for efficiency and cost reduction gain leverage, and spending on digital transformation in the oil and gas sector is projected to reach $35.8 billion in 2024. ExxonMobil's need for highly specialised technology and equipment in exploration, drilling, and refining gives suppliers of unique or proprietary solutions considerable leverage; providers of advanced carbon capture technologies can command higher prices due to the substantial investment and lack of readily available alternatives for a company of ExxonMobil's scale.

### 4.3 Bargaining Power of Buyers — Moderate

Buyers of crude oil and refined products are largely price-takers in commodity markets, limiting their individual bargaining power. However, the shift towards industrial decarbonisation services (CCS-as-a-service, low-carbon hydrogen supply) introduces a new buyer dynamic: large industrial customers contracting for long-term emissions reduction services have significant negotiating leverage because they are choosing between competing abatement pathways.

### 4.4 Threat of Substitutes — High and Rising

This is the most strategically significant force for ExxonMobil. The IEA has stated that "global oil demand will peak before 2030, driven by what is happening on clean energy technologies, especially in the transportation sector, and also economic shifts." Renewables, battery storage, and EVs constitute substitutes for oil-derived energy in transportation and power generation. The speed of substitution is contested — OPEC reiterated forecasts for global oil demand growth of 2.25 million b/d for 2024 and 1.85 million b/d for 2025, while the IEA sees a peak before 2030 — but the directional trend is unambiguous.

### 4.5 Competitive Rivalry — High

ExxonMobil competes with Shell, BP, TotalEnergies, Chevron, and national oil companies (Saudi Aramco, ADNOC) across all segments. The competitive dynamic is complicated by divergent transition strategies among peers. BP aims to elevate its renewable energy capacity to 50 GW by 2030, while TotalEnergies aspires for a 40% share of its energy mix to comprise renewables by 2025. ExxonMobil's decision to remain primarily hydrocarbon-focused while peers invest heavily in renewables creates a strategic differentiation — but also a concentration risk if the transition accelerates faster than ExxonMobil's models predict.

**Five Forces Summary:** The overall industry attractiveness is declining for traditional hydrocarbons due to rising substitutes and intensifying rivalry, but ExxonMobil's scale, cost position, and integration provide structural insulation. The key implication is that competitive advantage must increasingly be built on cost leadership (surviving in a commoditising market) and on building defensible positions in low-carbon adjacencies before those markets mature.

## 5. Resource-Based View and VRIN Analysis

The Resource-Based View (RBV), developed by Barney (1991), holds that sustainable competitive advantage derives from resources and capabilities that are **V**aluable, **R**are, **I**mperfectly imitable, and **N**on-substitutable. Barney stated that for resources to hold potential as sources of sustainable competitive advantage, they should be valuable, rare, imperfectly imitable, and not substitutable — now generally known as the VRIN criteria.

Applying VRIN to ExxonMobil's resource portfolio:

### 5.1 Geoscience and Subsurface Expertise (VRIN: Strong)

ExxonMobil exercises unique geoscience capabilities and understanding of the global hydrocarbon endowment to identify and prioritise all quality resources in a cost-effective manner. This capability is valuable (enables low-cost resource identification), rare (few firms have equivalent proprietary datasets accumulated over 140+ years), imperfectly imitable (embedded in tacit knowledge and proprietary data), and non-substitutable in the near term. Crucially, this same capability is being leveraged for lithium brine extraction — ExxonMobil has the expertise needed to pump brine out and reinject it after processing, making direct lithium extraction a natural extension of its subsurface competence. This represents a VRIN-consistent diversification: deploying rare, hard-to-imitate capabilities into an adjacent market.

### 5.2 Integrated Infrastructure and Scale (VRIN: Moderate to Strong)

ExxonMobil's strategy is to leverage core competencies in engineering and infrastructure to build a profitable Low Carbon Solutions business, focusing on CCS, hydrogen, and lithium. The Denbury acquisition is the clearest expression of this: of Denbury's 1,300 miles of CO₂ pipeline, roughly 70% are in the Gulf Coast states of Louisiana, Texas, and Mississippi — one of the largest U.S. markets for CO₂ reduction — and home to some of ExxonMobil's largest integrated refining and chemical sites and nine of their ten strategically-located CO₂ storage sites. Geographic co-location of CO₂ sources, pipelines, and storage sites constitutes a network asset that is rare and imperfectly imitable by new entrants.

### 5.3 Financial Strength and Capital Discipline (VRIN: Moderate)

ExxonMobil's robust financial health enables substantial investments in new projects, technology, and sustainability initiatives. The structural cost savings programme — adding $7 billion more in structural cost savings versus Q3 2024 — continuously lowers the cost base, making ExxonMobil more resilient to price downturns than peers. However, financial strength alone is not VRIN-compliant, as it is potentially substitutable by well-capitalised national oil companies. It is most valuable as an *enabler* of other VRIN resources.

### 5.4 Brand and Social Licence (VRIN: Weakening)

ExxonMobil's brand is valuable in B2B markets but is under reputational pressure due to its climate positioning. The Arjuna Capital lawsuit — a federal judge ruled ExxonMobil can sue to bar a climate change proposal from an activist investor, in a case that raised concerns about its future effect on shareholder rights — signals a company willing to use legal force to suppress ESG accountability mechanisms. This weakens the "non-substitutable" dimension of social licence, as industrial customers in regulated jurisdictions may prefer suppliers with more credible decarbonisation credentials.

**RBV Conclusion:** ExxonMobil's most durable competitive advantages lie in its geoscience capabilities and integrated infrastructure — both of which are being strategically extended into low-carbon adjacencies. The strategic logic is sound: the company is not diversifying into unrelated businesses but is redeploying VRIN resources into new applications. The risk is that this redeployment is too slow relative to the pace of the transition.

## 6. Ansoff Growth Matrix: Strategic Growth Options

The Ansoff Matrix (1957) maps growth strategies across two dimensions — existing/new products and existing/new markets — into four quadrants: Market Penetration, Market Development, Product Development, and Diversification. The Ansoff Product–Market Growth Matrix organises growth into four paths — penetration, market development, product development, and diversification — with risk increasing with capability distance.

ExxonMobil is simultaneously pursuing strategies in all four quadrants, which is unusual and reflects the complexity of its transition challenge:

### 6.1 Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets): Pioneer Acquisition

The Pioneer acquisition is the paradigmatic market penetration move — more oil and gas, sold to existing markets, at lower unit cost. ExxonMobil stated the Pioneer acquisition "will further differentiate our advantaged Upstream portfolio," with synergies expected to "create significant shareholder value and accelerate Pioneer's net zero ambitions by 15 years." The strategic rationale is cost leadership: the Pioneer integration provides improved capital efficiency, with ExxonMobil's Permian resource reaching 16 billion oil-equivalent barrels and 15 to 20 years of remaining inventory, with Pioneer's highly contiguous tier-one acreage enabling economies of scale in drilling and hydraulic fracturing scheduling.

This is the lowest-risk Ansoff strategy but carries the highest transition risk: it deepens ExxonMobil's exposure to an asset class that faces long-run demand headwinds. The Ansoff framework would flag this as appropriate *only if* ExxonMobil's demand forecasts are correct and peers' peak-demand projections are wrong.

### 6.2 Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets): LNG and Developing Economies

ExxonMobil is taking existing hydrocarbon products (LNG, refined products) into new geographic markets, particularly in developing economies. ExxonMobil's Global Outlook shows an approximately 25% increase in energy use in developing countries versus 2024, driven by population growth and rising living standards, and the company argues there is "no credible energy transition scenario that doesn't include a role for oil and natural gas." This market development strategy is coherent: demand in OECD countries may peak, but non-OECD demand growth provides a temporal bridge.

### 6.3 Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets): CCS and Hydrogen

The Denbury acquisition and the Baytown hydrogen project represent product development — new low-carbon products and services offered to existing industrial customers. ExxonMobil plans to install a world-scale low-carbon hydrogen plant at Baytown, Texas, capable of producing up to 1 billion cubic feet per day (Bcf/d) of hydrogen, with over 98% of associated CO₂ captured and up to 10 million tonnes per annum (MTA) of CO₂ handled via CCS. The strategic logic is that existing industrial customers (refineries, chemical plants, steel mills) need to decarbonise and ExxonMobil can sell them that service using its existing infrastructure relationships.

### 6.4 Diversification (New Products, New Markets): Lithium

Diversification is the riskiest Ansoff strategy as it radically shifts the scope of the organisation by entering completely new markets with completely new products. ExxonMobil's lithium programme in Arkansas represents a genuine diversification — selling a battery material to EV manufacturers, a market entirely new to the company. ExxonMobil's lithium development in Arkansas aims for first production in 2027 and supplying approximately 1 million EVs per year by 2030. The risk-mitigation logic is that ExxonMobil is applying its VRIN subsurface capability to this new market, reducing the "capability distance" that makes diversification dangerous. However, the scale ($1 million EVs/year) is modest relative to ExxonMobil's overall portfolio, limiting the hedge value.

**Ansoff Conclusion:** ExxonMobil's portfolio of growth strategies is internally coherent in that each quadrant leverages existing capabilities. However, the weighting is heavily skewed toward market penetration (Pioneer) and market development (LNG), which deepens hydrocarbon exposure, while product development and diversification (CCS, hydrogen, lithium) remain small relative to total capex. The strategic tension is that the company is betting the majority of its capital on the continuation of hydrocarbon demand while hedging only a minority of capital on the transition — a rational bet only if the IEA's peak-demand scenario is wrong.

## 7. Porter's Generic Strategies: Competitive Positioning

Porter (1980) identified three generic strategies for achieving competitive advantage: Cost Leadership, Differentiation, and Focus. ExxonMobil's primary competitive strategy is **cost leadership with selective differentiation** — a hybrid that Porter warned could result in being "stuck in the middle" but which, at ExxonMobil's scale, appears sustainable.

### 7.1 Cost Leadership in Upstream

ExxonMobil's strategy is a cost leadership strategy in the upstream segment, outperforming the competition by creating comparable value at low cost, using core competencies of industry-leading technology and capabilities, disciplined approach to investing and cost management, and operational excellence. The structural cost savings programme — $12.1 billion since 2019 — is the operational expression of this strategy. Cumulative structural cost savings totalled $12.1 billion, including an additional $2.4 billion in 2024, with these savings expected to be sustainable compared to 2019 levels.

The Pioneer acquisition reinforces cost leadership by reducing the per-barrel cost of supply in the Permian: contiguous acreage enables shared infrastructure, optimised drilling schedules, and reduced logistics costs. This is consistent with Porter's logic that cost leadership requires "aggressive construction of efficient-scale facilities, vigorous pursuit of cost reductions from experience, tight cost and overhead control."

### 7.2 Differentiation in Low-Carbon Solutions

In the emerging low-carbon services market, ExxonMobil is pursuing differentiation — positioning its CCS network, CO₂ pipeline infrastructure, and integrated decarbonisation offer as superior to competitors. The combined ExxonMobil-Denbury CCS network supports multiple low-carbon value chains — including CCS, hydrogen, ammonia, and biofuels — and ExxonMobil sees an opportunity to create a CCS business with compelling customer decarbonisation propositions.

This differentiation strategy is credible because of the network effect: following the Denbury acquisition, ExxonMobil now controls more than 1,500 miles of CO₂ pipelines — the largest CO₂ pipeline network in the United States — and says its solutions could help reduce third-party emissions by over 50 million tonnes per annum (MTA) by 2030. A competitor seeking to replicate this network would face prohibitive capital costs and permitting timelines, giving ExxonMobil a durable differentiation advantage in CCS.

### 7.3 The Strategic Risk: Credibility Gap

Porter's generic strategies analysis reveals a coherent positioning in *existing* markets but a credibility problem in *transition* markets. A net-zero commitment "contains enough flexibility to accommodate both genuinely ambitious decarbonisation plans and elaborate exercises in reputational positioning," and the challenge is determining which mechanism will deliver the target — actual emission reductions or carbon removal. ExxonMobil's absence of Scope 3 targets means that industrial customers in jurisdictions with mandatory supply-chain emissions accounting may face regulatory barriers to procuring ExxonMobil's products, undermining the market development strategy.

## 8. Synthesis: The Core Strategic Tension

The five frameworks converge on a single core tension: **ExxonMobil's competitive advantages are real and defensible in the near term, but its strategic posture creates compounding risk over the medium term if the energy transition accelerates faster than its models assume.**

The logic chain is as follows:

1. **PESTEL** establishes that the macro-environment is bifurcated: near-term demand remains robust (especially in non-OECD markets), but the long-run trajectory of regulation, technology, and social expectations is adverse to pure-play hydrocarbon businesses.

2. **Five Forces** shows that ExxonMobil's industry position is structurally strong today — high barriers to entry, moderate buyer power, disciplined rivalry — but the threat of substitutes is the dominant force that will erode this position over time.

3. **RBV/VRIN** confirms that ExxonMobil's core competencies (geoscience, integrated infrastructure) are genuinely rare and hard to imitate, and that the company is making intelligent moves to redeploy them into low-carbon adjacencies. However, the *pace* of redeployment is insufficient relative to the *pace* of substitution risk.

4. **Ansoff** reveals that ExxonMobil's capital allocation is overwhelmingly weighted toward market penetration (Pioneer) and market development (LNG/non-OECD) — the highest-transition-risk quadrants — while product development (CCS, hydrogen) and diversification (lithium) receive proportionally small capital allocations.

5. **Porter's Generic Strategies** shows that ExxonMobil has a coherent cost leadership position in hydrocarbons and a credible differentiation position in CCS, but the Scope 3 credibility gap undermines the differentiation strategy in markets where customers face regulatory pressure.

The conclusion is that ExxonMobil's strategy is *internally consistent* but *externally fragile*: it works well if hydrocarbon demand remains robust through 2030 and beyond, but it is under-hedged against a faster-than-expected transition.

## 9. Recommendations and Action Plan

The following recommendations are designed to resolve the strategic tension identified above — preserving ExxonMobil's near-term competitive advantages while building a more robust hedge against transition risk. Each recommendation is grounded in the analytical frameworks above.

---

### Recommendation 1: Rebalance Capital Allocation Toward Low-Carbon Solutions

**The Problem:** ExxonMobil is pursuing up to $30 billion in lower-emissions investment opportunities from 2025 to 2030, but this represents only approximately 20–25% of total planned capex of $28–$33 billion per year — a ratio that is insufficient to constitute a genuine strategic hedge.

**The Recommendation:** Increase the low-carbon capex share from ~20% to ~35% of total annual capex by 2027, funded by accelerating the divestiture of high-cost, high-carbon upstream assets (non-Permian, non-Guyana conventional assets). This rebalancing does not require abandoning the hydrocarbon growth strategy — the Permian and Guyana assets remain the core — but it reduces the concentration risk in the portfolio.

**The Reasoning:** The Ansoff analysis shows that product development (CCS, hydrogen) and diversification (lithium) are the quadrants where ExxonMobil's VRIN capabilities are most differentiated relative to competitors. Increasing capital allocation to these quadrants improves the risk-adjusted return of the portfolio under transition scenarios without abandoning the cost leadership position in hydrocarbons. The RBV logic supports this: redeploying geoscience and infrastructure capabilities into low-carbon markets creates new value streams that are harder to imitate than commodity hydrocarbon production.

---

### Recommendation 2: Adopt Scope 3 Emissions Targets with a Credible Pathway

**The Problem:** ExxonMobil has made no net-zero emissions by 2050 commitment and has no GHG reduction targets covering Scope 3 emissions, reporting at least 650 million tonnes of Scope 3 emissions from petroleum product sales in 2020. This creates a credibility gap that undermines ExxonMobil's differentiation strategy in the CCS and low-carbon solutions market.

**The Recommendation:** Adopt a Scope 3 intensity target (emissions per unit of energy sold) rather than an absolute Scope 3 reduction target, phased in over three years. An intensity target is consistent with ExxonMobil's demand forecasts (total energy sold may grow) while demonstrating a credible trajectory toward lower-carbon products. This should be paired with a formal commitment to link executive compensation to Scope 3 intensity metrics.

**The Reasoning:** Porter's Generic Strategies analysis shows that ExxonMobil's differentiation in low-carbon services is undermined by the Scope 3 gap. Industrial customers in the EU and UK — subject to mandatory supply-chain emissions reporting under the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) — face regulatory barriers to procuring from suppliers without Scope 3 targets. Adopting a Scope 3 intensity target resolves this barrier without requiring ExxonMobil to commit to an absolute production reduction, which would contradict its hydrocarbon growth strategy. The Five Forces analysis supports this: reducing buyer switching barriers (by becoming CSRD-compliant) strengthens ExxonMobil's competitive position in the growing low-carbon services market.

---

### Recommendation 3: Scale the Lithium Business to a Strategically Significant Size

**The Problem:** ExxonMobil's lithium development in Arkansas aims to supply approximately 1 million EVs per year by 2030 — a figure that is strategically marginal relative to projected global EV demand of 40+ million vehicles per year by 2030.

**The Recommendation:** Scale the lithium programme through partnerships with EV manufacturers (OEM offtake agreements) and battery manufacturers, targeting 5 million EV-equivalents of lithium supply per year by 2032. This requires accelerating DLE technology development and expanding the Arkansas brine acreage position through targeted acquisitions.

**The Reasoning:** The Ansoff diversification analysis shows that the lithium business is ExxonMobil's most important hedge against long-run oil demand destruction in transportation — the sector most directly threatened by EVs. However, diversification is the riskiest Ansoff strategy because it enters completely new markets with new products, and the current scale of the lithium programme is too small to provide meaningful portfolio diversification. The RBV analysis supports scaling: ExxonMobil's subsurface expertise is a genuine VRIN resource in lithium brine extraction, and companies like ExxonMobil are leveraging their subsurface and fluid-handling expertise to enter the critical minerals space, meaning the capability transfer is real. Scaling to strategic significance requires moving from a pilot to a core business.

---

### Recommendation 4: Accelerate the CCS-as-a-Service Commercial Model

**The Problem:** ExxonMobil's CCS infrastructure is strategically valuable but commercially underdeveloped. The company has contracted for up to 16 MTA of capture or transport by 2030, but the broader market potential is far larger.

**The Recommendation:** Establish a standalone CCS commercial unit with dedicated sales and business development capability, targeting long-term (15–20 year) take-or-pay contracts with hard-to-abate industrial sectors (steel, cement, chemicals). Prioritise the Gulf Coast industrial corridor, where ExxonMobil's pipeline network creates a natural monopoly-like position. The cost-efficient transportation and storage system accelerates CCS deployment for both ExxonMobil and third-party customers, supporting multiple low-carbon value chains including CCS, hydrogen, ammonia, and biofuels.

**The Reasoning:** Porter's differentiation analysis shows that ExxonMobil's CO₂ pipeline network is a genuine source of competitive advantage — it is rare (largest in the US), imperfectly imitable (permitting and capital barriers), and valuable (industrial customers need it to meet regulatory obligations). However, a differentiation advantage only generates returns if it is commercially exploited. The Five Forces analysis identifies industrial customers as having moderate-to-high bargaining power in this nascent market; long-term take-or-pay contracts lock in revenue streams and reduce buyer bargaining power by creating switching costs. This recommendation converts a strategic asset into a recurring revenue business.

---

### Recommendation 5: Engage Constructively with Shareholder Climate Governance

**The Problem:** Activist investor Arjuna Capital sought to include a greenhouse gas-related shareholder proposal in ExxonMobil's 2024 proxy statement, and ExxonMobil filed a lawsuit to exclude it. This legal strategy won the immediate battle but may be losing the longer-term war for institutional investor confidence.

**The Recommendation:** Replace the adversarial legal strategy with a proactive climate governance framework: establish an independent Climate Advisory Board comprising credible scientists and transition economists; publish an annual "Climate Scenario Analysis" that stress-tests the corporate plan against IEA Net Zero and Stated Policies scenarios; and commit to a shareholder-approved climate transition plan by 2026.

**The Reasoning:** The PESTEL analysis identifies social licence as a material and weakening asset. Institutional investors — who hold the majority of ExxonMobil's shares — increasingly require credible climate governance as a condition of continued ownership, not because of activism but because of fiduciary duty under evolving regulatory frameworks (SEC climate disclosure rules, TCFD requirements). The current adversarial posture creates a compounding risk: a net-zero commitment that relies on flexibility rather than genuine emission reductions risks being characterised as "elaborate reputational positioning," which could trigger divestment by major institutional shareholders and raise ExxonMobil's cost of capital. Proactive governance converts a liability into a differentiator.

## 10. Conclusion

ExxonMobil's strategic situation in 2024–2025 is one of deliberate, high-conviction asymmetry: the company has made a large bet on the continued primacy of hydrocarbons while building a small but strategically intelligent hedge in low-carbon solutions. The analytical frameworks applied in this case study confirm that this strategy is coherent given ExxonMobil's VRIN capabilities and current industry structure, but they also reveal three compounding vulnerabilities: an under-weighted low-carbon capital allocation, a Scope 3 credibility gap that undermines differentiation in transition markets, and an adversarial governance posture that risks institutional investor confidence.

The recommendations above are designed to resolve these vulnerabilities without abandoning ExxonMobil's core competitive advantages. They are not a call to become a renewable energy company — that would destroy value by abandoning VRIN resources — but a call to move the balance point of the dual-track strategy: heavier investment in low-carbon adjacencies where ExxonMobil's capabilities are genuinely differentiated, more credible climate governance to protect social licence and institutional capital, and a more aggressive commercial model for CCS that converts infrastructure assets into contracted revenue streams.

The strategic logic is ultimately Porterian: sustainable competitive advantage requires either the lowest cost or a differentiation that customers value and cannot easily substitute. In hydrocarbons, ExxonMobil has the lowest cost. In low-carbon solutions, it has the infrastructure. The task now is to build the commercial and governance architecture that makes both advantages durable.
