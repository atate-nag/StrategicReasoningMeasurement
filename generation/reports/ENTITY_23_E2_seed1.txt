# Manchester United: A Strategic Case Study

## *Decoupled Fortunes — Resolving the Structural Misalignment Between Commercial Supremacy and Sporting Decline*

---

## 1. Introduction and Case Framing

Manchester United occupies one of the most paradoxical positions in world sport: it is simultaneously a commercial giant and a sporting underperformer. United's 15th-place finish in the 2024–25 Premier League season was the club's worst in the Premier League era and the lowest in 25 years, yet in the same fiscal year the club achieved a record £666.5m in revenue. This divergence — record commercial income coexisting with historic sporting failure — constitutes the central strategic problem. The situation is not simply a bad run of form; it is the product of deep structural misalignments across governance, resource allocation, and competitive strategy that have accumulated over two decades. This case study diagnoses those misalignments using Porter's Five Forces, a SWOT/VRIO internal analysis, and McKinsey's Three Horizons framework, before deriving a coherent set of strategic recommendations.

## 2. Background and Context

### 2.1 The Glazer Legacy: A Debt-Funded Foundation

The root cause of United's structural weakness traces to 2005. When the Glazers — a family of prominent American entrepreneurs — bought the club in 2005 with a leveraged takeover, they plunged the club into debts of around £600m. This was a critical strategic error in ownership design: the Glazer family's acquisition of Manchester United remains one of the most consequential ownership changes in football history; rather than a traditional cash purchase, the deal was structured as a leveraged buyout, with significant debt secured against the club itself. Unlike competitors such as Manchester City (Abu Dhabi sovereign wealth) or Chelsea (Roman Abramovich's initial equity injection), United's ownership model extracted value rather than injecting it, constraining every subsequent strategic decision.

### 2.2 The INEOS Intervention

The INEOS Group, led by Britain's richest man, Sir Jim Ratcliffe, became minority owners in February 2024 after acquiring a 27.7% stake in the club in a deal costing £1.3 billion. Critically, while the Glazers retained majority ownership, the club's football operations are now overseen by INEOS, but the Glazer family, who have owned United since 2005, remain in overall charge of everything else. This split-governance structure — operational control separated from majority ownership — created its own strategic complications, as explored below.

### 2.3 The Sporting Crisis

Last year, Manchester United finished 15th in the Premier League, their worst league placement in Premier League history. This record broke their previous all-time low in eighth place, set the season before. In European competition, Tottenham Hotspur beat Manchester United 1-0 in the 2025 Europa League final in Bilbao, denying United the silverware and Champions League place the run had promised. The managerial situation was equally turbulent: Ruben Amorim, appointed in November 2024, managed just 27 points from 27 matches in 2024–25, an average of 1.0 points per game.

## 3. External Analysis: Porter's Five Forces

Porter's (1979) Five Forces framework examines the structural attractiveness of the competitive environment. Applied to elite European football, it reveals the forces that shape United's strategic options.

### Force 1: Rivalry Among Existing Competitors — *Very High*

The Premier League's competitive intensity has never been greater. Liverpool's recruitment wins through analytics — signing Mohamed Salah and Sadio Mané — improved tactics and delivered significant titles; Manchester City emphasised possession-based football using pass and position data for domestic dominance. Rivals have institutionalised data-driven competitive advantages. Laurie Shaw moved to Liverpool from Manchester City to strengthen Will Spearman's team and build the first analytics supergroup; Arsenal have recruited Karun Singh, who is building a complete deep learning model of football. United's rivals are not merely spending more — they are building superior decision-making architectures. This matters strategically because competitive advantage in modern football is increasingly a function of *system quality* (recruitment, coaching philosophy, data infrastructure) rather than raw spending power alone.

### Force 2: Threat of New Entrants — *Moderate but Rising*

Traditional barriers to entry (brand, stadium, history) remain high. However, state-backed ownership has lowered effective financial barriers for new entrants. Manchester City, Newcastle United, and Paris Saint-Germain represent a new category of competitor — state-funded clubs with effectively unlimited capital — that did not exist in the same form when United last dominated. This changes the competitive logic: United cannot compete on spending alone against sovereign wealth funds, making differentiation on *system* and *brand* more, not less, important.

### Force 3: Bargaining Power of Suppliers (Players and Agents) — *High*

Elite players and their agents wield significant power, evidenced by United's wage bill. United's wage bill for the 2024–25 season dropped to £313 million from £364.7 million a year earlier, but this reduction was achieved through exits rather than structural reform of the wage model. The broader problem is that United's poor on-pitch performance weakens its ability to attract top talent on favourable terms — a vicious cycle in which sporting underperformance raises recruitment costs.

### Force 4: Bargaining Power of Buyers (Fans, Broadcasters, Sponsors) — *Mixed*

Sponsors retain strong bargaining power in aggregate, but United's global brand partially offsets this. United's commercial revenue was second among its domestic 'Big 6' peer group for both 2022–23 and 2023–24, though that position is now under pressure. Crucially, broadcasting revenue is highly performance-sensitive: broadcasting revenue decreased by £48.9m to £172.9m after the men's team played in the Europa League rather than the Champions League. This £48.9m revenue cliff illustrates the direct financial cost of sporting failure — and provides the causal link between on-pitch results and financial sustainability.

### Force 5: Threat of Substitutes — *Low to Moderate*

The threat of fans switching to alternative entertainment is structurally low for a club of United's heritage. However, the risk of *sponsor substitution* — sponsors redirecting investment to more successful clubs — is real and growing. Manchester United's revenue was second among its domestic 'Big 6' peer group for both 2022–23 and 2023–24, but is expected to be fourth among the cohort for 2024–25 as Manchester City and Liverpool will each exceed £700m. The erosion of commercial ranking signals that sponsors are beginning to reprice United's brand relative to sporting performance.

**Five Forces Summary:** The external environment is highly competitive, with state-backed rivals institutionalising data advantages, performance-linked revenue streams creating financial vulnerability, and United's commercial position beginning to erode. The strategic implication is that United cannot afford a prolonged period of mid-table mediocrity: the longer sporting underperformance continues, the more the commercial moat — United's primary strategic asset — is eroded.

## 4. Internal Analysis: SWOT and VRIO

### 4.1 SWOT Analysis

**Strengths:**
- *Brand and global reach:* 1.1 billion global followers cited in prior Kantar studies, sustaining unmatched unaided awareness and premium sponsor appeal. This brand equity is genuinely rare and difficult to replicate.
- *Commercial infrastructure:* The club's global community is estimated at 1.1 billion fans and followers, which provides a massive worldwide platform; this engagement translated into a record commercial revenue of £333.3 million in the 2025 fiscal year, an increase of 10.0%.
- *Record sponsorship deals:* The Snapdragon shirt sponsorship deal was estimated at above $75 million per year — a record for any shirt deal in world sport. The Adidas kit deal is equally landmark: the new Manchester United × Adidas 2035 kit deal is worth "at least" £900m (£90m/season), valid for ten years from 2025 until 2035.
- *Stadium redevelopment pipeline:* Manchester United announced plans for a new 100,000-seat stadium designed by Foster + Partners, which the club claims will generate an additional £7.3 billion to the UK economy.

**Weaknesses:**
- *Structural debt burden:* Debt has reduced somewhat (by around £39m to £471.9m), but that reduction is because of a change in the exchange rate between the British pound and the US dollar, rather than any of the debt being specifically paid off. The debt is not being repaid; it is merely fluctuating with exchange rates.
- *Governance instability:* Dan Ashworth left his role as Sporting Director of Manchester United after five months; INEOS paid between £2–3m in compensation to Newcastle to secure Ashworth, then backed Erik ten Hag in the summer and sacked him 13 games later. This pattern of expensive, short-cycle leadership decisions signals an absence of coherent strategic planning.
- *PSR vulnerability:* Manchester United revealed that they are at risk of failing to comply with Profit and Sustainability Rules (PSR), admitting that ticket prices could rise in a bid to reduce losses. The club acknowledged losses totalling over £300m in the past three years.
- *Wage-to-revenue inefficiency:* Even after the reduction, a wage bill of £313m against revenue of £666m represents a wage ratio of approximately 47% — above the UEFA-recommended threshold for financial sustainability, and consuming resources that could fund infrastructure or recruitment.

**Opportunities:**
- *Champions League return:* Arsenal, Manchester City, Manchester United and Aston Villa are guaranteed a top-five finish and will all play in the UEFA Champions League next season. Champions League participation is estimated to add approximately £50–80m in broadcasting revenue, directly addressing the PSR gap.
- *New stadium revenue uplift:* A 100,000-seat stadium would materially increase matchday revenue, currently the smallest of United's three revenue streams. Old Trafford's capacity of approximately 74,000 is significantly below the proposed new venue.
- *Data analytics gap:* United's rivals have invested heavily in analytics infrastructure; United has the financial scale to close this gap rapidly if it commits to doing so.
- *Academy pipeline:* Chido Obi, 17, has dominated the academy level and represents the type of homegrown talent that could reduce transfer dependency and improve PSR compliance (homegrown player costs are treated differently under financial regulations).

**Threats:**
- *State-backed competition:* Manchester City, Newcastle, and PSG represent a structural competitive threat that cannot be matched on spending alone.
- *Broadcasting revenue sensitivity:* The £48.9m revenue drop from missing the Champions League demonstrates acute revenue volatility tied to sporting performance.
- *Stadium financing risk:* If the club continues to lose money, it could reach a point where it has to rethink its plans, especially as the current iteration will cost over £1bn. The stadium project is a long-term strategic asset but a short-term financial liability.

### 4.2 VRIO Analysis

The VRIO framework (Barney, 1991) asks whether a resource is Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Organised to capture value.

| Resource | Valuable? | Rare? | Inimitable? | Organised? | Competitive Implication |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Global brand (1.1bn fans) | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ | **Sustained competitive advantage** |
| Commercial deal network (Snapdragon, Adidas) | ✅ | ✅ | Partially | ✅ | **Temporary competitive advantage** |
| Old Trafford / new stadium pipeline | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ | ⚠️ (financing risk) | **Potential advantage, currently constrained** |
| Youth academy | ✅ | ❌ | ❌ | ⚠️ | **Competitive parity** |
| Data/analytics infrastructure | ✅ | ❌ | ❌ | ❌ | **Competitive disadvantage** |
| Managerial/governance stability | ✅ | ❌ | ❌ | ❌ | **Competitive disadvantage** |

**VRIO Conclusion:** United's *only* genuinely sustained competitive advantage is its global brand. All other potential advantages are either temporary (commercial deals that rivals can eventually replicate), constrained (stadium financing), or currently deficient (analytics, governance). This finding is strategically decisive: every recommendation must either protect the brand or build new inimitable capabilities.

## 5. Strategic Diagnosis: The Core Problem

The SWOT and VRIO analyses converge on a single diagnosis: **United's commercial machine has decoupled from its sporting engine.** The brand generates record revenues *despite* sporting failure, but this decoupling is not sustainable. The Five Forces analysis shows why: broadcasting revenue is performance-sensitive (−£48.9m for missing the Champions League), sponsor value is beginning to erode relative to rivals, and the PSR framework imposes a hard financial constraint that links losses to regulatory punishment.

The strategic situation can be characterised as a **negative feedback loop**:

> Sporting underperformance → lower broadcasting revenue → PSR pressure → constrained transfer investment → further sporting underperformance

INEOS's response — cost-cutting, restructuring, and large transfer spending — has addressed symptoms rather than root causes. Manchester United spent up to $47.8m on a Ratcliffe-commissioned review, and expects to make cost savings of between £40m and £45m a year due to its restructuring — but simultaneously committed well over £200m in transfer spending while prioritising younger, resale-value assets. These two impulses — cost discipline and high-volume spending — are in tension and reflect the absence of a coherent strategic framework.

The governance instability compounds the problem. INEOS paid £2–3m to secure Dan Ashworth, then saw him leave after five months — a costly signal of strategic incoherence at the leadership level. Without stable football leadership, no recruitment or coaching strategy can be executed consistently.

## 6. Strategic Framework: McKinsey's Three Horizons

To resolve the negative feedback loop, United requires a phased strategic response across three time horizons (McKinsey, 1999):

- **Horizon 1 (0–2 years):** Stabilise — stop the financial bleeding and restore competitive credibility.
- **Horizon 2 (2–5 years):** Build — construct the institutional capabilities (analytics, governance, academy) that generate sustainable sporting performance.
- **Horizon 3 (5–10 years):** Transform — use the new stadium and Champions League presence to re-establish United as a top-three global club.

This framework is appropriate because United's problems are not all solvable in the same timeframe. Treating a Horizon 3 problem (stadium) as an urgent Horizon 1 priority, or expecting Horizon 2 capability-building (analytics) to deliver immediate results, leads to the kind of strategic confusion that has characterised the club since 2013.

## 7. Recommendations

### Recommendation 1 (Horizon 1): Resolve Governance Instability Through a Defined Football Leadership Model

**The problem:** The rapid turnover of football leadership — two managers in one season, a Sporting Director who lasted five months — has made it impossible to execute any consistent recruitment or playing strategy. The new model was intended to see a head coach focused primarily on the training pitches of Carrington, while recruitment would be led by the incoming sporting director and CEO — but this model was never properly implemented.

**The recommendation:** United should adopt the *sporting director model* successfully used by Liverpool (FSG/Michael Edwards) and Arsenal (Edu Gaspar). Even before acquiring Liverpool FC, FSG was aligned with the Moneyball approach to recruitment, implementing it successfully in their baseball team, the Boston Red Sox; the club was subsequently acquired for £300m and valued at £1.7bn in 2019. The key feature of this model is *continuity*: the sporting director outlasts individual managers and maintains strategic coherence across coaching changes. United must appoint a long-tenure Sporting Director with a clear mandate, insulated from short-term results pressure, and this person must have genuine authority over recruitment — not a shared mandate with the CEO and Technical Director.

**Why this resolves the problem:** Without governance stability, every other recommendation fails. A new analytics system requires a leader to commission and use it. A youth-first recruitment strategy requires a director to enforce it. The causal chain is: stable leadership → consistent strategy → improved sporting outcomes → restored broadcasting revenue → PSR compliance.

---

### Recommendation 2 (Horizon 1/2): Adopt a Data-Driven, Value-Based Recruitment Strategy

**The problem:** United's transfer record since 2013 has been characterised by high-cost, short-tenure signings that have failed to improve the team and damaged PSR compliance. The club has spent hundreds of millions on players who did not fit any coherent system.

**The recommendation:** United should invest in a world-class analytics department to underpin a *value-based recruitment* philosophy targeting players aged 20–26 with high resale potential. Liverpool's recruitment wins through analytics — signing Mohamed Salah and Sadio Mané — improved tactics and won significant titles; Manchester City's tactical edge was built on possession-based football using pass and position data for domestic dominance. Teams like Liverpool and Manchester City have invested heavily in injury prediction models, creating recruitment systems that blend science with strategy. United's scale — a £666m revenue base — gives it the financial capacity to build this infrastructure rapidly if it commits to doing so.

**Why this resolves the problem:** The current approach — spending £250m+ in a single summer on high-profile signings — is both financially unsustainable under PSR and strategically incoherent without a defined playing system. A data-driven approach would reduce transfer fees (buying undervalued players), improve squad fit, and generate resale profits that improve PSR compliance. The VRIO analysis identified analytics as a current competitive disadvantage; closing this gap converts a weakness into a potential source of advantage.

---

### Recommendation 3 (Horizon 1): Achieve and Sustain Champions League Qualification as a Non-Negotiable Financial Constraint

**The problem:** Missing the Champions League cost United approximately £48.9m in broadcasting revenue in 2024–25 alone, directly threatening PSR compliance. Manchester United admitted they are at risk of failing to comply with PSR rules, with losses totalling over £300m in the past three years.

**The recommendation:** Champions League qualification must be treated not merely as a sporting aspiration but as a *financial constraint* — a minimum performance threshold below which the entire strategic plan becomes unviable. This means squad investment decisions should be evaluated against their expected contribution to top-four finishes, not merely transfer market optics. Positively, Manchester United are guaranteed a top-five finish in 2025–26 and will play in the Champions League next season, restoring approximately £50–80m in broadcasting revenue and providing the financial breathing room to execute Horizons 2 and 3.

**Why this resolves the problem:** The negative feedback loop identified in the diagnosis is broken at the first link — sporting performance — which restores broadcasting revenue, relieves PSR pressure, and creates the financial headroom for capability investment. The Champions League return in 2025–26 is the single most important near-term development in United's strategic situation.

---

### Recommendation 4 (Horizon 2): Restructure the Wage Model Around Performance and Incentive Pay

**The problem:** Even after INEOS's cost-cutting, United's wage bill was £313.2m for 2024–25, down by approximately £52m on the season before, but still representing approximately 47% of revenue. High fixed wages reduce financial flexibility and create PSR risk regardless of performance.

**The recommendation:** United should restructure player contracts to increase the proportion of performance-related pay — bonuses tied to Champions League qualification, individual appearances, and clean sheets. This model, used effectively by clubs like Juventus and Borussia Dortmund, aligns player incentives with club outcomes and converts fixed costs into variable costs that automatically adjust with revenue. The Adidas deal provides a useful model: Manchester United will not receive the full annual value of the Adidas deal in seasons where the club does not qualify for the Champions League — performance-contingency is already embedded in United's commercial contracts and should be mirrored in player contracts.

**Why this resolves the problem:** A more variable wage structure reduces the PSR exposure from sustained losses, creates financial flexibility for investment in analytics and infrastructure, and aligns player incentives with the sporting performance that drives broadcasting revenue.

---

### Recommendation 5 (Horizon 2): Accelerate the Academy-to-First-Team Pipeline

**The problem:** United's academy has historically produced elite talent (Beckham, Scholes, Giggs, Rashford) but the pipeline to the first team has weakened. Homegrown players are treated more favourably under UEFA's squad registration rules and do not count against PSR in the same way as transfer purchases, making academy investment doubly valuable.

**The recommendation:** United should increase investment in the academy and create structured pathways for academy graduates to access first-team minutes. Chido Obi, 17, has dominated the academy level and represents the type of talent that, if developed properly, could reduce transfer dependency. The Liverpool model is instructive: FSG's data-driven approach identified and developed young talent systematically, growing club value from £300m to £1.7bn. Homegrown players also generate stronger emotional connections with the fanbase — a brand asset that aligns with United's identity as a club rooted in youth development.

**Why this resolves the problem:** Academy graduates address PSR compliance (lower amortisation costs), reduce wage bill pressure (lower initial salaries), and maintain the brand narrative of authenticity that distinguishes United from state-backed rivals. The VRIO analysis identified the academy as currently offering only competitive parity; sustained investment could convert it into a genuine advantage.

---

### Recommendation 6 (Horizon 3): Execute the Stadium Project as a Strategic Transformation, Not Merely a Capital Project

**The problem:** The proposed new stadium is a £2 billion capital commitment at a time of financial fragility. If the club continues to lose money, it could reach a point where it has to rethink its plans, especially as the current iteration will cost over £1bn. Without resolving Horizon 1 and 2 problems first, the stadium risks becoming a financial burden rather than a strategic asset.

**The recommendation:** The stadium project should be sequenced as a Horizon 3 priority — contingent on Champions League revenue being restored (Recommendation 3) and PSR compliance being secured (Recommendations 2 and 4). The new 100,000-seat venue is expected to generate an additional £7.3 billion to the UK economy and would transform United's matchday revenue — currently the weakest of its three revenue streams. The club should pursue the government infrastructure co-investment model it has proposed: Manchester United's Chief Operating Officer says the club will pay £2 billion to build a new Old Trafford, but the UK government will be asked to support funding for local infrastructure and area regeneration. This public-private model reduces United's capital exposure while retaining the strategic upside.

**Why this resolves the problem:** A 100,000-seat stadium with premium hospitality would structurally increase matchday revenue by an estimated 30–40%, permanently improving United's revenue mix and reducing the relative importance of performance-sensitive broadcasting income. It is the single most powerful long-term lever available — but only if the financial platform to support it is built first.

## 9. Conclusion

Manchester United's strategic situation is serious but not irreversible. The club's global community of 1.1 billion fans and followers provides a massive worldwide platform that no rival can replicate in the short term — this is the strategic asset that must be protected above all others. But the VRIO analysis shows that brand alone cannot sustain competitive advantage if it is not backed by institutional capabilities (analytics, governance, academy) that translate commercial scale into sporting performance.

The central argument of this case study is that United's problems are structural, not cyclical, and therefore require structural solutions. Spending more money on transfers — the instinctive response — addresses symptoms rather than causes. The root causes are: a debt-laden ownership structure inherited from 2005; governance instability that prevents any strategy from being executed consistently; a performance-sensitive revenue model that punishes sporting failure with financial consequences; and an absence of the data-driven recruitment infrastructure that rivals have built over the past decade.

The Three Horizons framework provides the sequencing logic: stabilise first (governance, Champions League), build next (analytics, academy, contracts), transform last (stadium). Execute in that order, and the commercial giant and the sporting engine can be reconnected. Reverse the order — or attempt all three simultaneously without the financial platform to support them — and the decoupling between commercial supremacy and sporting decline will widen until even the brand can no longer compensate.
