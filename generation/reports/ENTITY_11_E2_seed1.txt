# Strategic Case Study: De Beers Group — Navigating Structural Disruption in the Natural Diamond Industry

## Anglo American Subsidiary Analysis | Strategic Management

---

## 1. INTRODUCTION AND CASE CONTEXT

De Beers Group, the world's most historically dominant diamond company, stands at a strategic inflection point that encapsulates some of the most compelling tensions in contemporary corporate strategy: technological substitution, shifting consumer values, geopolitical interdependence, and parent-company portfolio rationalisation. Founded in 1888, De Beers operates as an 85%-owned subsidiary of Anglo American plc, with the Government of Botswana holding the remaining 15% stake. Multinational mining company Anglo American has had control of De Beers since August 2012, when it purchased the Oppenheimer family's 40% stake, thus gaining an 85% ownership.

The strategic situation examined in this case study is the **structural crisis facing De Beers between 2022–2025**: a simultaneous collapse in natural diamond demand, the rapid rise of lab-grown diamond (LGD) substitutes, a deteriorating financial position, the renegotiation of its foundational Botswana partnership, and a parent company under existential M&A pressure that has decided to divest De Beers entirely. This is not a cyclical downturn but a **structural disruption** requiring a fundamental reorientation of strategy.

This analysis applies Porter's Five Forces, the Resource-Based View (RBV), a SWOT analysis, the BCG Growth-Share Matrix, and Ansoff's Growth Matrix to generate a coherent, evidence-grounded set of strategic recommendations.

## 2. SITUATIONAL BACKGROUND

### 2.1 Financial Deterioration

The severity of De Beers' crisis is best understood through its financial trajectory. Difficult trading conditions resulted in a year-on-year reduction in revenue of 23%, with total revenue of $3.3 billion in 2024 (2023: $4.3 billion), primarily due to a 25% reduction in rough diamond sales to $2.7 billion (2023: $3.6 billion). More starkly, the consequential impact of lower sales volumes, a lower average price index, and higher unit costs resulted in an underlying EBITDA of $(25) million in 2024 (2023: $72 million). The business has, in effect, moved from marginal profitability to operational loss within a single year.

This financial deterioration has cascaded upward to Anglo American. Anglo American has reduced the value of its diamond subsidiary, De Beers, by $2.9 billion, marking the second consecutive year of such writedowns. This adjustment reflects ongoing challenges in the diamond industry, including the rise of lab-grown diamonds and decreased consumer spending in China. Consequently, Anglo American reported a net loss of $3.1 billion in 2024.

### 2.2 The Parent Company's Strategic Pivot

De Beers' crisis does not exist in isolation. Anglo American itself faced an existential threat in 2024. Anglo's divestment of De Beers forms part of a comprehensive portfolio transformation designed to capitalise on the energy transition. The company has already demerged its platinum unit, Amplats, and is selling metallurgical coal assets to focus on copper and iron ore — metals essential for renewable energy infrastructure and electric vehicles. This pivot was catalysed by a hostile approach: in May 2024, Anglo American announced a strategic plan to divest less profitable assets and refocus on copper, a critical resource for electric vehicles and various industries. The trigger was BHP's attempted acquisition. Anglo's transformation strategy followed the company's successful defence against BHP's $49 billion takeover attempt in May 2024, which catalysed management's commitment to demonstrating value creation through focused operations.

The implication for De Beers is unambiguous: it is no longer a core asset. Anglo American is simultaneously exploring a direct sale and a potential IPO. The engagement of Morgan Stanley, Goldman Sachs, and Centerview as financial advisors demonstrates commitment to maximising competitive tension and achieving optimal pricing. This means De Beers must develop a credible standalone strategy — one that can either attract a buyer at a reasonable valuation or sustain an independent listing.

## 3. ENVIRONMENTAL ANALYSIS: PORTER'S FIVE FORCES

Porter's Five Forces (1980) provides the foundation for understanding the structural attractiveness of the industry in which De Beers operates. The framework argues that competitive intensity — and therefore long-run profitability — is determined by five structural forces. The analysis below demonstrates that the natural diamond industry has deteriorated materially across all five dimensions.

### Force 1: Threat of New Entrants — **Elevated**

Historically, De Beers used supply-chain control and marketing dominance to erect near-impenetrable barriers to entry. That position has been irrevocably altered by laboratory-grown diamonds. The threat of new entrants is high, due to the emergence of lab-grown diamond producers and online retailers. Unlike traditional mining, LGD production requires capital equipment rather than geological endowments, dramatically lowering entry barriers for new producers. The capital cost of a CVD (Chemical Vapour Deposition) reactor is a fraction of opening a new kimberlite mine, and Chinese manufacturers have entered at scale. A sharp increase in global supply, particularly from China, caused synthetic diamond prices to collapse. This structural change in entry barriers is permanent, not cyclical, because the technology is now widely diffused.

**Inferential link:** Because entry barriers have fallen permanently, De Beers cannot rely on supply restriction — historically its primary competitive weapon — to sustain pricing power. This forces a pivot toward differentiation strategies that lab entrants cannot replicate.

### Force 2: Bargaining Power of Suppliers — **Moderate but Shifting**

De Beers' primary "supplier" relationship is with the Government of Botswana through the Debswana joint venture. Botswana's share of the diamonds produced by Debswana, a 50-50 joint venture between the country's government and De Beers, will increase from 25% to 50% under the new agreement. Botswana will receive 10 billion pula ($712 million) in development funding, and the mining licences for Debswana are extended until 2054. While this secures long-run supply, the renegotiated terms represent a significant redistribution of value toward the supplier government. Botswana remains integral to De Beers' operations, supplying 70% of its annual rough diamonds. The government also holds a 15% stake in De Beers, underscoring the long-standing strategic partnership.

**Inferential link:** The Botswana government's increased share of rough diamond output (from 25% to 50%) reduces De Beers' control over its primary input, increasing the cost of goods and compressing margins precisely when the revenue side is also under pressure. This margin squeeze from both ends makes operational efficiency non-negotiable.

### Force 3: Bargaining Power of Buyers — **High and Rising**

Buyers — comprising both wholesale sightholders (rough diamond purchasers) and end consumers — have seen their bargaining power increase substantially. At the wholesale level, midstream inventory gluts have weakened De Beers' position. Retailers are very cautious as they restock, creating higher than normal levels of midstream inventory. At the consumer level, the existence of a chemically identical substitute at a fraction of the price gives buyers a credible outside option. The retail price of one-carat lab-grown diamonds stood at some $855 as of 2025, nearly a 76% decrease in price compared to 2018.

**Inferential link:** When buyers possess a credible, price-competitive substitute, the seller's ability to command a premium depends entirely on perceived non-price differentiation — provenance, emotional meaning, and brand. This directly informs the strategic priority De Beers must assign to marketing and traceability.

### Force 4: Threat of Substitutes — **Severe and Structural**

This is the most consequential force. The lab-grown diamond market's projected growth from $26.05 billion in 2024 to $97.85 billion by 2034 — representing a 14.15% CAGR — demonstrates the relentless pace of technological substitution. Crucially, this is not merely a price threat. The consumer behaviour shift particularly affects younger demographics who increasingly prioritise value over provenance. Millennials and Generation Z show greater openness to lab-grown alternatives, viewing them as equivalent products at superior price points. This generational preference change suggests the natural diamond premium may erode permanently rather than recovering cyclically.

The global rough diamond price index reflects this substitution pressure. Natural diamond prices have experienced a notable decline, with a 30% drop from their all-time high in 2022, and a 5.7% decrease in Q1 and Q2 2024. The global rough diamond index shows a 6% decline in 2024, hitting a 14-year low in mid-December 2023.

**Inferential link:** The permanence of the LGD threat — rooted in technology diffusion and generational attitude shifts — means that any strategy premised on a market "recovery" to 2022 price levels is analytically unsound. Strategy must be built around a permanently lower natural diamond price equilibrium.

### Force 5: Competitive Rivalry — **Intensifying**

As of 2023, the Russian diamond mining conglomerate ALROSA had the largest market share of any diamond mining company in the world at 31%, with De Beers in close second at 29% of global diamond production market share. Unlike previous eras when De Beers effectively acted as a cartel manager, today's competitive landscape features multiple large-scale producers with divergent strategic interests. Chinese demand for diamonds fell by up to 50% in 2024, and the recovery of the Chinese diamond market will hinge on a macroeconomic recovery that could be long in the making. With demand contracting and multiple well-capitalised rivals, pricing discipline across the industry has broken down.

**Inferential link:** The simultaneous contraction of demand and fragmentation of supply-side coordination means that industry-level profitability is structurally depressed. De Beers cannot solve this through unilateral action; it must instead pursue strategies that insulate it from industry-level price dynamics through differentiation or market segmentation.

**Five Forces Summary Verdict:** The natural diamond industry has moved from "attractive" to "structurally challenged." The combined effect of these forces explains why De Beers' EBITDA turned negative in 2024. Strategy must address each force directly, not assume conditions will revert.

## 4. INTERNAL ANALYSIS: RESOURCE-BASED VIEW (RBV) AND SWOT

### 4.1 Resource-Based View

The RBV (Barney, 1991) holds that sustainable competitive advantage derives from resources and capabilities that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN). Applying this framework to De Beers reveals a portfolio of assets that remain strategically significant but are under varying degrees of threat.

**Brand equity ("A Diamond Is Forever"):** De Beers coined "A Diamond Is Forever" in 1947, and it remains one of the most recognised advertising slogans in history. This brand carries genuine VRIN properties: it is valuable (it commands a price premium), rare (no competitor possesses equivalent brand heritage in diamonds), inimitable (decades of cultural embedding cannot be replicated quickly), and non-substitutable (no other asset performs the same function of linking natural diamonds to emotional permanence). De Beers has successfully established itself as a prominent diamond brand, particularly targeting women who love diamond jewellery. The brand's tagline, "Diamonds are forever," resonates with consumers and solidifies its position in the market. However, the brand's power is being eroded by generational shifts, making sustained investment non-negotiable.

**Geological asset base:** De Beers controls world-class mining assets, including the Jwaneng mine in Botswana, one of the world's most valuable diamond deposits. The Jwaneng mine alone holds reserves worth over $5 billion at conservative price assumptions, suggesting asset backing exists despite current cash flow challenges. These geological assets are inherently rare and inimitable, providing a long-run production foundation that LGD producers cannot replicate.

**Vertical integration and supply chain control:** De Beers possesses a vertically integrated business model, encompassing diamond mining, trading, and retail. This integration allows the company to have control over the entire supply chain, ensuring the quality and authenticity of its diamonds. This is a VRIN capability because it enables quality assurance and provenance verification at every stage — a capability that is directly relevant to the differentiation strategy required in the current environment.

**Tracr blockchain provenance platform:** De Beers has invested in a proprietary blockchain-based traceability system. Tracr uses blockchain technology to enable users to provide an unalterable record of a diamond's provenance. The platform now has over three million diamonds registered at source. Each diamond is assigned a unique digital identity, capturing critical attributes such as carat weight, colour, clarity and cut. This is a strategically critical capability: it is the technological infrastructure that makes provenance claims credible and verifiable, directly addressing the key differentiator between natural and lab-grown diamonds.

**Element Six (industrial/technological synthetic diamonds):** Element Six, part of De Beers Group, is establishing a transformational technology solutions business, leveraging synthetic diamonds to unlock new opportunities in high-value industries such as quantum technologies, semiconductors, and water purification. Element Six has been selected for a United States DARPA programme to advance semiconductor technologies and is also investing in Lightsynq, a quantum interconnect startup, to accelerate breakthroughs in quantum computing. This represents a genuinely valuable and rare capability: the application of synthetic diamond expertise to deep-tech markets where performance — not aesthetics — is the criterion.

### 4.2 SWOT Analysis

**Strengths:**
- Globally recognised brand with 130+ years of heritage
- World-class geological assets (Jwaneng, Venetia, Orapa mines)
- Vertically integrated value chain from mine to retail

**Weaknesses:**

- Total rough diamond sales volumes decreased by 28% to 17.9 million carats in 2024.
- De Beers is sitting on approximately $2 billion in unsold inventory, highlighting the scale of the demand shortfall.
- Over-reliance on a single commodity (natural diamonds) in a disrupted market
- Parent company divestiture creates governance uncertainty and potential underinvestment

**Opportunities:**
- India's growing middle class: In India, strong economic growth has underpinned positive natural diamond jewellery growth.
- Element Six's deep-tech pivot into semiconductors and quantum computing
- Tracr platform as an industry-wide provenance standard (licensing revenue)
- De Beers and the Government of Botswana have committed to co-investing in marketing efforts that emphasise the ethical and symbolic value of natural diamonds.
- Gen Z marketing through digital channels and emotional repositioning

**Threats:**
- The lab-grown diamond market is projected to grow from $26.05 billion in 2024 to $97.85 billion by 2034, representing a 14.15% CAGR.
- Chinese demand for diamonds fell by up to 50% in 2024.
- Anglo American's divestiture process creating strategic uncertainty
- Renegotiated Botswana terms compressing upstream margins
- ALROSA's continued production (despite Western sanctions) maintaining global supply pressure

A "Question Mark" in a commoditising market with no path to market leadership is a capital drain.

## 6. STRATEGIC OPTIONS ANALYSIS: ANSOFF MATRIX

Ansoff's Matrix (1957) provides a framework for evaluating growth options along two dimensions — products and markets. For De Beers, four strategic options present themselves:

**Option A — Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets):** Increase natural diamond market share in established markets (US, Europe) through intensified category marketing. De Beers is already pursuing this. The "Forever Present" holiday campaign follows De Beers and Signet Jewelers' collaborative campaign titled "Worth the Wait," focusing on soon-to-be-engaged millennial and Gen Z consumers, running nationally across digital platforms, social media, and out-of-home. Risk: **Low**. Return potential: **Moderate** (constrained by structural demand headwinds).

**Option B — Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets):** Expand natural diamond sales into high-growth emerging markets, particularly India. In India, strong economic growth has underpinned positive natural diamond jewellery growth. Risk: **Moderate** (requires distribution investment and cultural adaptation). Return potential: **High** (India's middle class is expanding rapidly and has a cultural affinity for gold and gemstones).

**Option C — Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets):** Leverage Element Six capabilities to develop synthetic diamond-based technology products for existing industrial customers. Element Six is establishing a transformational technology solutions business, leveraging synthetic diamonds to unlock new opportunities in high-value industries such as quantum technologies, semiconductors, and water purification. Risk: **Moderate-High** (requires deep-tech R&D investment and long commercialisation cycles). Return potential: **Very High** (semiconductor and quantum markets are structurally growing).

**Option D — Diversification (New Products, New Markets):** Expand Tracr as a standalone provenance-verification platform across luxury goods (watches, coloured gemstones, art). Risk: **High**. Return potential: **Moderate** (but provides resilience through revenue stream diversification).

**Recommended Priority Ranking:** B > C > A > D. Market development into India offers the highest near-term risk-adjusted return. Element Six's technology pivot is the highest long-term value creation opportunity. Market penetration through category marketing is necessary but insufficient alone. Diversification into non-diamond luxury provenance is a longer-term option.

## 7. STRATEGIC COHERENCE ASSESSMENT

The frameworks applied above converge on a consistent strategic diagnosis. The Five Forces analysis establishes that the natural diamond industry's structural attractiveness has deteriorated permanently, driven by LGD substitution and demand fragmentation — this is the "why" of De Beers' crisis. The RBV identifies the assets that remain genuinely defensible: brand heritage, Tracr provenance infrastructure, geological assets, and Element Six's deep-tech capabilities — these are the "what" of any viable strategy. The SWOT analysis maps these internal strengths against the external environment, identifying India, Gen Z marketing, and industrial diamonds as the key opportunity vectors. The BCG Matrix provides the capital allocation logic: harvest natural diamonds, invest in Element Six. The Ansoff Matrix operationalises the growth priorities: India first, deep-tech second.

The chain of reasoning is as follows:

1. **Industry structure is permanently impaired** (Five Forces) → therefore strategies premised on market recovery are insufficient.
2. **De Beers retains VRIN assets** (RBV) → therefore the company has a viable basis for differentiation, but only if those assets are actively deployed.
3. **Capital must be reallocated** (BCG) → from declining natural diamond volumes toward Element Six and India market development.
4. **Growth must be sequenced** (Ansoff) → near-term (India market development), medium-term (Element Six commercialisation), long-term (Tracr platform licensing).

## 8. RECOMMENDATIONS AND ACTION PLAN

### Recommendation 1: Accelerate India Market Development as the Primary Revenue Growth Engine

**Evidence base:** In India, strong economic growth has underpinned positive natural diamond jewellery growth, while Chinese demand fell by up to 50% in 2024. The strategic logic is straightforward: De Beers must rebalance its geographic demand exposure away from China (structurally impaired) toward India (structurally growing). India's cultural tradition of gifting jewellery at weddings and festivals provides a natural demand base.

**Action steps:**
- Establish a dedicated India Growth Unit within De Beers Brands, with a five-year revenue target
- Partner with leading Indian jewellery retailers (Tanishq, Kalyan Jewellers) to embed "A Diamond Is Forever" into Indian cultural narratives
- Deploy Tracr-verified diamonds as a premium tier in Indian retail, leveraging the provenance story to justify price premiums over domestic alternatives
- Invest in Hindi-language and regional-language digital marketing campaigns targeting 25–35 year old urban consumers

**Why this warrants priority:** India is the only major market where natural diamond jewellery demand is currently growing. Capturing this market before ALROSA or other competitors entrench themselves is a time-sensitive strategic imperative.

---

### Recommendation 2: Spin Element Six into a Standalone Deep-Tech Business Unit with Dedicated Capital

**Evidence base:** Element Six has been selected for a United States DARPA programme to advance semiconductor technologies and is also investing in Lightsynq, a quantum interconnect startup. Synthetic stones will remain in De Beers' portfolio, but through its Element Six division, the company will continue producing lab-grown diamonds for advanced industrial and technological applications — from semiconductors to quantum computing — where performance, not romance, is the measure of worth.

**Action steps:**
- Restructure Element Six as a separate P&L unit with its own management team and capital budget, insulated from the natural diamond business's financial pressures
- Pursue strategic partnerships with semiconductor manufacturers (Intel, TSMC) and quantum computing firms to secure long-term supply agreements
- Explore external funding (venture capital, government grants) for Element Six to reduce dependence on Anglo American's capital allocation decisions during the divestiture period
- File patents aggressively around CVD diamond applications in power electronics and quantum photonics

**Why this is strategically sound:** The BCG analysis identifies Element Six as a "Star." The RBV confirms its capabilities are VRIN — rare, inimitable expertise in synthetic diamond production for precision applications. Allowing this unit to be constrained by the natural diamond business's capital needs would destroy value. Ring-fencing it creates optionality: it can be retained post-separation, spun off, or sold to a deep-tech acquirer at a significant premium.

---

### Recommendation 3: Transform Tracr from an Internal Tool into an Industry-Wide Provenance Standard

**Evidence base:** Tracr uses blockchain technology to enable users to provide an unalterable record of a diamond's provenance, with over three million diamonds registered at source. Each diamond is assigned a unique digital identity, capturing critical attributes such as carat weight, colour, clarity and cut. Tracr, developed by De Beers in collaboration with Accenture, is an industry-first blockchain platform that creates a traceable and tamper-proof record of a diamond's provenance from the source.

**Action steps:**
- Open Tracr to the broader diamond industry on a licensing basis, positioning De Beers as the infrastructure provider for natural diamond authentication globally
- Mandate Tracr certification as a prerequisite for all De Beers sightholder relationships, creating a network effect that drives adoption
- Explore extension of the Tracr model to coloured gemstones (rubies, emeralds), expanding the addressable market and diversifying revenue
- Use Tracr data to build consumer-facing digital experiences (QR codes on jewellery that tell the diamond's story), reinforcing the emotional differentiation of natural over lab-grown

**Why this addresses the substitution threat:** The single most powerful differentiator between natural and lab-grown diamonds is provenance — a lab-grown diamond has no story. Tracr is the technological infrastructure that makes provenance claims credible. By making it an industry standard rather than a proprietary tool, De Beers converts a cost centre into a revenue stream while simultaneously raising the bar for the entire natural diamond category.

---

### Recommendation 4: Intensify Category Marketing Targeting Millennials and Gen Z Through Emotional Repositioning

**Evidence base:** De Beers has launched a comprehensive marketing initiative specifically tailored to capture the attention and loyalty of Gen Z, with a core objective to forge a deeper emotional connection between diamonds and younger consumers, moving beyond traditional notions of status and luxury. De Beers and the Government of Botswana have committed to co-investing in marketing efforts that emphasise the ethical and symbolic value of natural diamonds, including category marketing, promotional initiatives, and educational campaigns agreed upon annually.

**Action steps:**
- Reframe the natural diamond narrative around **irreplaceability and origin story** rather than merely "forever love" — the geological rarity (formed over billions of years, 150km underground) is a story that lab-grown diamonds structurally cannot tell
- Leverage the Botswana co-investment agreement to develop "Diamonds for Development" marketing content that connects diamond purchase to African community benefit — appealing directly to Gen Z's values around ethical consumption
- Increase digital and social media spend (TikTok, Instagram) to at least 60% of total marketing budget, with creator partnerships rather than traditional advertising
- Commission annual consumer research to track the natural-vs-lab-grown preference gap by age cohort, using data to continuously refine messaging

**Why this is necessary:** The consumer behaviour shift particularly affects younger demographics who increasingly prioritise value over provenance. This is a preference that can be shifted through marketing — De Beers has done it before (it literally invented the engagement ring norm). The difference today is that the channel is digital and the values are ethical, not merely aspirational.

---

### Recommendation 5: Manage the Separation Process to Maximise Standalone Value

**Evidence base:** Anglo has also prepared an IPO backup plan, reflecting renewed confidence in De Beers' long-term prospects. CEO Duncan Wanblad acknowledged the difficult market conditions and indicated that plans to spin off De Beers might be postponed to later in the year.

**Action steps:**
- Prioritise operational cost reduction to achieve the Origins Strategy target of $100 million in annual cost savings, improving EBITDA before any sale or IPO
- Secure the Botswana government as a strategic anchor investor in any IPO scenario — its 15% stake and reputational alignment make it a credible long-term partner that would reassure public market investors
- Develop a clear "De Beers standalone equity story" that articulates the three-pillar value proposition: (1) natural diamond mining with secured Botswana licences to 2054, (2) Element Six deep-tech growth, and (3) Tracr as a platform business
- Avoid a distressed sale that undervalues the geological asset base — the Jwaneng mine alone represents significant long-term value that current market conditions obscure

**Why this matters:** The separation process is itself a strategic variable. A well-managed separation that presents a credible standalone strategy will attract better buyers or achieve a higher IPO valuation than a distressed exit. De Beers' management must act as if they are building an independent company — because they are.

---

## 9. CONCLUSION

De Beers Group faces a strategic situation of rare complexity: structural industry disruption, financial distress, parent company divestiture, and geopolitical dependency — all simultaneously. However, the application of Porter's Five Forces, the RBV, SWOT, BCG, and Ansoff frameworks reveals that the company retains genuinely defensible assets and viable strategic pathways.

The core analytical conclusion is that De Beers cannot recover by waiting for the natural diamond market to normalise. The Five Forces analysis demonstrates that the structural forces — LGD substitution, buyer power, and competitive rivalry — have permanently altered the industry's profitability profile. Instead, De Beers must execute a three-horizon strategy: **defend** the natural diamond premium through provenance and marketing (Horizon 1); **develop** the India market as the primary growth engine (Horizon 2); and **transform** Element Six into a deep-tech business that is valued independently of diamond market cycles (Horizon 3).

The five recommendations form a coherent chain: Tracr makes provenance credible; provenance makes natural diamonds differentiated; differentiation justifies the marketing investment; India provides the growth market for that differentiated product; and Element Six provides the long-run growth engine that makes De Beers a viable standalone entity. Each recommendation addresses a specific force or weakness identified in the frameworks, and each connects to the others through a shared strategic logic: **move from commodity to platform, from volume to value, and from dependence on a single disrupted market to a diversified, differentiated business.**
