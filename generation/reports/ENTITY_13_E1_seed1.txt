# **Diageo plc: Navigating the Perfect Storm — A Strategic Case Study**

### *Premiumisation Under Pressure, Leadership Instability, and the Path to Renewal*

---

## 1. Introduction and Case Overview

Diageo plc is the world's largest premium spirits company, operating across nearly 180 countries with a portfolio of 13 billion-dollar brands including Johnnie Walker, Guinness, Don Julio, Smirnoff, Baileys, and Captain Morgan. Formed through the 1997 merger of Grand Metropolitan and Guinness, the company has long been regarded as one of the FTSE 100's most reliable compounders of shareholder value. Yet between 2022 and 2025, Diageo entered one of the most turbulent periods in its corporate history — a convergence of strategic missteps, macroeconomic headwinds, leadership instability, and structural shifts in consumer behaviour that erased more than half its market capitalisation.

Diageo's stock is now worth half what it was at the end of 2021, when its shares peaked at over £40, with shares falling 54.45% since then. Crew's departure comes by mutual agreement and follows a 44% drop in Diageo's share price during her tenure.

This case study frames the strategic situation as a **multi-front crisis**: a demand-side shock (consumer downtrading and moderation), a supply-side shock (tariffs and geopolitical disruption), a geographic concentration risk (over-reliance on North America and Latin America), and a leadership vacuum. The analysis proceeds through five theoretical lenses — PESTEL, Porter's Five Forces, the Resource-Based View (VRIO), the Ansoff Growth Matrix, and McKinsey's 7-S Framework — before converging on a coherent set of strategic recommendations.

## 2. Background: The Rise and Fall of Diageo's Growth Narrative

### 2.1 The Premiumisation Thesis

For most of the 2010s and early 2020s, Diageo's strategy was anchored to one powerful trend: premiumisation. In recent years, premiumisation has remained a central driver of the company's strategy. Consumers in many markets have shown interest in trading up to higher-quality spirits, and Diageo sought to capture this trend through super-premium and above offerings. The portfolio was structured as a deliberate value ladder. At the top sit reserve and prestige offerings such as Don Julio 1942, Johnnie Walker Blue Label, Casamigos, and rare single malts from its Scotch stable. This tiering allows Diageo to move consumers up the value ladder over time, a strategy it calls premiumisation. When the macro environment tightens, mainstream labels defend volume; when times are good, premium tiers drive mix.

In FY2024 Diageo remained a leading premium spirits group, stewarding icons like Johnnie Walker, Guinness and Don Julio across 180+ countries. Premiumisation and route-to-market scale anchor its position in the $1.1–1.2 trillion global beverage alcohol market.

### 2.2 The Unravelling: FY2024 Results

The fiscal year ending June 2024 revealed the cracks in this thesis. Organic net sales declined $129 million or 0.6%, positive price/mix of 2.9pps was more than offset by a 3.5% volume decline, primarily driven by a 21.1% decline in the Latin America and Caribbean region (LAC). Organic operating profit declined by $304 million or 4.8%, of which $302 million was attributable to LAC; organic operating margin contracted 130bps.

In 2024, the company generated net sales amounting to roughly 16.2 billion British pounds, the first decrease after several years of growth following the pandemic. The lion's share of Diageo's net sales were generated in North America. North America — Diageo's single largest market — compounded the LAC problem. The company witnessed organic sales decline of 21% and 3% in LAC and North America, respectively. However, organic sales improved 3%, 4% and 12%, respectively, in Europe, Asia Pacific and Africa.

### 2.3 Leadership Crisis

The performance deterioration triggered a leadership crisis. Diageo announced that Debra Crew has stepped down as Chief Executive Officer and as a Board Director with immediate effect, by mutual agreement. The Board has begun a comprehensive formal search process. Until a permanent appointment is made, Nik Jhangiani, Chief Financial Officer, will assume the role of Chief Executive Officer on an interim basis. Diageo has appointed former Group CEO of Tesco plc Sir Dave Lewis to the role of CEO and Executive Director, effective 1 January 2025. The appointment of a turnaround specialist — Lewis is celebrated for rescuing Tesco from its own accounting scandal — signals the Board's recognition that the company faces not merely a cyclical downturn but a structural strategic challenge.

## 3. Theoretical Framework 1: PESTEL Analysis — Mapping the Macro Environment

The PESTEL framework (Johnson, Whittington & Scholes, 2017) is used here to identify the external forces shaping Diageo's strategic context. The key insight is that several forces are moving simultaneously and in the same adverse direction, creating a compound external shock.

### 3.1 Political — Tariff Disruption

The most immediate political threat is US trade policy. Spirits maker Diageo said it is taking steps to deal with the potential impact of US tariffs on key supply chain regions and has removed its medium-term guidance due to macroeconomic and geopolitical uncertainty. The exposure is acute because Diageo's most valuable US categories are produced outside the United States. Tariffs on products in the US from Mexico and Canada could dent Diageo's operating profit by around $200m in the next half if implemented, according to the company's calculations. Tequila, for example, is a strong category in the US with Don Julio, Casamigos, Astral and DeLeon. Crown Royal is the top-selling Canadian whisky in the US. And products from Europe and the UK, such as Scotch whisky, are another important category.

Diageo says it has taken a number of actions to help mitigate the impact: including inventory management, supply chain optimisation and re-allocation of investments. As a result, it expects to mitigate around 50% of the hit. Even so, a $100m+ unmitigated profit headwind in a single half-year is material for a company already under investor scrutiny.

Brexit adds a further layer of political friction. As of 2024, Diageo faces significant regulatory challenges following Brexit, with increased customs documentation requirements and potential tariff impacts. The UK-EU Trade and Cooperation Agreement introduced additional compliance costs estimated at £750 million annually for spirits exporters.

### 3.2 Economic — Consumer Downtrading and the Moderation Shift

The economic environment is characterised by a squeeze on real incomes that has directly undermined the premiumisation thesis. Moderation trends are broadening, with "zebra striping" — alternating between full-strength and no-alcohol drinks — gaining momentum. No/low-alcohol volumes grew +9% in 2024, outpacing full-strength categories in most regions.

IWSR data shows that premiumisation persists globally, but is becoming more fragmented. Premium-and-above volumes (excluding national spirits) grew by +3% in 2024, led by beer and driven primarily by emerging markets such as South America, Africa, and the Middle East. This is a crucial nuance: premiumisation has not ended, but it has geographically migrated away from Diageo's core Western markets.

Worldwide volumes of spirits fell by -1% in 2024 (IWSR), with growth in value limited to +1%. The sector is undergoing profound transformation between economic pressures, changing lifestyles and the explosion of new categories.

### 3.3 Sociocultural — Health Consciousness and GLP-1 Drugs

A structural sociocultural shift is underway. Declines globally have been small but clear. According to IWSR, the compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of alcohol by volume between 2019 and 2024 was -1%. This reached -3% in China, -2% in Germany, Japan, and the UK, and -1% in the US.

GLP-1 weight-loss drugs (such as Ozempic) represent an emergent structural threat. Gen Z, those born between 1997 and 2012, have come of age during the rise of non-alcohol spirits and are known to be health-conscious and sober-curious. The no/low-alcohol category is already responding to this demand: the moderation trend is driving demand for non-alcoholic options, with sales approaching $1 billion in 2024 and projected to grow at a 4% CAGR from 2024 to 2028.

### 3.4 Technological — Digital and Route-to-Market

Technology is reshaping distribution and marketing. Diageo has invested in digital capabilities and direct-to-consumer (D2C) channels, but this remains a work in progress. The company's US route-to-market overhaul is explicitly cited as a strategic priority: Diageo has made good progress on its strategic initiatives, including its US route-to-market enhancements.

### 3.5 Environmental — Agave and Barley Supply Chain Risk

Climate-driven yield variability affected agave and barley prices in 2024–2025; tequila production costs rose materially amid significant agave price swings. With nearly 40% of sales from emerging markets, Diageo is exposed to currency volatility and trade-route disruptions that can erode reported earnings.

### 3.6 Legal — Regulatory and Reputational Risk

Diageo faced significant legal and reputational exposure from its relationship with Sean Combs around the Cîroc brand. Diageo is studying a sale of Cîroc following the settlement of a lawsuit with Combs where he accused the spirits company of racism in its alleged neglect of Cîroc Vodka and another liquor brand called DeLeón Tequila. This illustrates how legal entanglements can damage both brand equity and management bandwidth.

**PESTEL Summary:** The macro environment is characterised by a rare convergence of adverse forces — political (tariffs, Brexit), economic (downtrading), sociocultural (moderation, Gen Z sobriety), environmental (input cost inflation), and legal (brand controversies). This is not a simple cyclical trough; it has structural dimensions that require strategic recalibration.

## 4. Theoretical Framework 2: Porter's Five Forces — Industry Attractiveness

Porter's Five Forces (Porter, 1979) is used to assess whether the spirits industry remains structurally attractive and to identify where competitive pressure is intensifying.

### 4.1 Threat of New Entrants — **Moderate but Rising**

The premium spirits industry has historically had high barriers to entry: aged inventory requirements, brand heritage, and distribution scale. However, the craft spirits movement has partially eroded this moat. The number of small, local distilleries in the United States has grown significantly. This growth is being driven by a trend of consumers showing increasing interest in locally produced, regionally sourced products. As more brands enter the market, increased competition could negatively affect demand for premium and super-premium American whiskey brands.

That said, competing at Diageo's scale — with global distribution and billion-dollar marketing budgets — remains effectively impossible for new entrants. The threat is more relevant at the super-premium niche level.

### 4.2 Bargaining Power of Suppliers — **Moderate**

Diageo sources agricultural commodities (agave, barley, grain) from concentrated supply bases. The agave shortage is a live illustration: climate-driven yield variability affected agave and barley prices in 2024–2025; tequila production costs rose materially amid significant agave price swings. Diageo's scale gives it some purchasing leverage, but it cannot fully insulate itself from agricultural commodity cycles.

### 4.3 Bargaining Power of Buyers — **High**

Retail consolidation has significantly increased buyer power. Walmart, Costco, and Tesco control 59.2% of global alcohol retail distribution channels. These retailers negotiate prices aggressively, demanding volume discounts. In the on-trade (bars and restaurants), consolidation of pub chains and the rise of private-label RTDs similarly compress margins. Consumer power is also rising, as digital transparency makes brand switching easier.

### 4.4 Threat of Substitutes — **High and Structurally Increasing**

This is arguably the most strategically significant force. Substitutes operate at two levels: (a) category substitution (beer, wine, cannabis, RTDs replacing spirits); and (b) no/low alcohol substitution. The moderation trend is driving demand for non-alcoholic options, with sales approaching $1 billion in 2024 and projected to grow at a 4% CAGR from 2024 to 2028. Ready-to-drink (RTD) cocktails represent both a substitute and an opportunity, depending on whether Diageo can own the category.

### 4.5 Competitive Rivalry — **Intense**

Diageo's most prominent direct competitors include Pernod Ricard, Bacardi, Beam Suntory, Brown-Forman, Constellation Brands, LVMH's Moët Hennessy Division, Campari Group, William Grant & Sons, and Rémy Cointreau. These companies vie for market share through brand portfolios, strategic acquisitions, and market penetration efforts. Pernod Ricard, a French company, is Diageo's most direct competitor and the second-largest spirits company globally.

A notable recent development is the near-merger of Pernod Ricard and Brown-Forman: talks between Pernod Ricard and Brown-Forman have collapsed, easing the immediate threat of a larger rival at a time when the world's biggest spirits company is under pressure to reverse years of weak sales and lagging shareholder returns. While the collapse provides temporary relief, it signals that the competitive landscape is in motion and consolidation pressure remains.

**Five Forces Summary:** The industry remains attractive in aggregate — scale advantages, brand heritage, and the long-term growth of emerging-market middle classes preserve structural appeal. However, buyer power and the threat of substitutes are intensifying, and the competitive rivalry is at a high-water mark. Diageo's competitive position is therefore more contingent on internal strategic choices than it was a decade ago.

## 6. Theoretical Framework 4: Ansoff Growth Matrix — Strategic Direction Choices

The Ansoff Matrix (Ansoff, 1957) maps four strategic growth options along two dimensions: existing vs. new markets, and existing vs. new products. Diageo's current situation requires a deliberate choice about which quadrant to prioritise, given constrained capital and management bandwidth.

### 6.1 Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets)

This is the most capital-efficient growth path and should be the immediate priority in markets where Diageo already has scale. The US is the prime candidate: despite being Diageo's largest market, in the United States, Diageo has the largest share of the spirits market by volume. Winning back share in the US through improved route-to-market, competitive pricing, and targeted marketing is the lowest-risk growth lever. Guinness delivered double-digit growth and gained share in its three largest markets, while Guinness Microdraught opened new global distribution opportunities. In whisky, Johnnie Walker gained share of international whisky and Scotch.

### 6.2 Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets)

This is where the structural long-term opportunity lies. India is identified as a crucial growth market, expected to account for a quarter of new legal purchase age consumers by 2030. Diageo aims to rebalance growth beyond a soft North America in FY2024–FY2025 toward Europe, Africa, India and Travel Retail, targeting mid- to high-single-digit organic net sales growth over the cycle.

Africa is already delivering. Africa delivered solid results, with 10% growth in organic net sales. Strong performance was recorded in East Africa as well as South, West, and Central Africa, reflecting steady demand and distribution strength. The continent's favourable demographics and rising middle class make it a structural growth engine.

### 6.3 Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets)

Diageo's response to the no/low alcohol trend illustrates product development strategy. Diageo continues to strategically acquire brands that tap into emerging consumer trends, such as the September 2024 acquisition of a non-alcoholic spirits brand. This move highlights the company's commitment to innovation and expansion into the growing zero-proof beverage category. Guinness 0.0 and Captain Morgan Spiced Gold 0.0% are early moves in this space.

RTDs also represent a product development opportunity. The category's strong performance — ready-to-drink (RTD) beverages continue their strong performance — creates a natural extension for Diageo's existing brand equities.

### 6.4 Diversification (New Products, New Markets)

This is the highest-risk quadrant and should be approached selectively. The Cîroc-to-Lobos 1707 swap illustrates a disciplined form of diversification: exiting an underperforming asset while entering a high-growth category (tequila) with celebrity-driven brand equity. Diageo exchanged its majority stake in Cîroc for a controlling interest in Lobos 1707, a tequila and mezcal brand co-founded by NBA star LeBron James. This strategic shift reflects Diageo's efforts to revitalize its business amid changing consumer preferences.

**Ansoff Summary:** The matrix suggests a sequenced strategy: (1) prioritise market penetration in the US to stabilise the core; (2) accelerate market development in Africa and India; (3) selectively pursue product development in no/low alcohol and RTDs; and (4) use disciplined portfolio rationalisation (not broad diversification) to sharpen the brand mix.

## 7. Theoretical Framework 5: McKinsey 7-S Framework — Organisational Alignment

The McKinsey 7-S Framework (Peters & Waterman, 1982) examines whether an organisation's "hard" elements (Strategy, Structure, Systems) and "soft" elements (Shared Values, Skills, Style, Staff) are mutually aligned. Diageo's crisis is partly one of misalignment between these elements.

| **Element** | **Current State** | **Assessment** |
|---|---|---|
| **Strategy** | Premiumisation + geographic diversification | Partially valid; needs recalibration |
| **Structure** | Regional operating model | Created LAC blind spots; under review |
| **Systems** | Inventory management, RTM | Demonstrably failed in LAC and US |
| **Shared Values** | "Celebrating life, every day, everywhere" | Strong brand culture; ESG commitment |
| **Style** | Leadership in transition | High uncertainty; new CEO turnaround mandate |
| **Staff** | Deep spirits expertise | CMO departures signal talent risk |
| **Skills** | Brand building, distribution | Strong; digital/data capabilities lagging |

The most acute misalignment is between **Systems** and **Strategy**: Diageo's premiumisation strategy requires precise demand sensing and inventory management, yet the LAC crisis revealed a system that was unable to detect or respond to fast-changing consumer sentiment. The LAC decline was driven by fast-changing consumer sentiment in the prior year which resulted in elevated inventory levels and subsequent adjustments by wholesalers and customers in the fiscal year.

The leadership transition also creates a **Style-Strategy** misalignment risk. Brand competitiveness and affordability are key pillars of Sir Dave Lewis's turnaround plan for Diageo. The former Tesco CEO, who led a significant business and brand turnaround at the supermarket, joins a company facing a challenging environment. Lewis's Tesco playbook — price investment, supplier partnership, operational discipline — may need adaptation for a premium brand company where price cuts can permanently impair brand equity.

The **Accelerate programme** is explicitly designed to address the Systems and Structure misalignment: Diageo announced plans to implement a $500m cost savings programme over three years, to enable reinvestment in future growth and improved operating leverage. The alcohol giant announced the first phase of the programme, dubbed 'Accelerate', which intends to support a 'more agile' operating model. The target has since been raised: the cost savings programme target has been increased to c.$625 million, up from c.$500 million.

## 8. Synthesis: The Strategic Problem Statement

The five frameworks converge on a coherent diagnosis. Diageo faces a **strategic inflection point** driven by three interlocking problems:

1. **A demand-side structural shift**: The premiumisation supercycle that powered Diageo's growth is decelerating in its core Western markets, driven by consumer downtrading, health consciousness, and the rise of no/low alcohol alternatives. This is not purely cyclical — it has structural dimensions that require a portfolio and positioning response.

2. **A geographic concentration risk**: Over-reliance on North America and the mismanagement of the LAC opportunity exposed the fragility of Diageo's regional balance. The company's most resilient growth now comes from markets (Africa, India, Europe) that are historically under-resourced relative to their long-term potential.

3. **An organisational execution gap**: Diageo's brand and distribution assets are world-class (VRIO-confirmed), but the organisational systems and structure have failed to exploit them fully. The inventory crisis in LAC, the US route-to-market weakness, and the leadership instability are all symptoms of an organisation that grew faster than its management capabilities.

The strategic situation is therefore: *How does Diageo stabilise performance in its core markets, rebalance its geographic and portfolio mix toward structurally growing segments, and rebuild organisational effectiveness — all while managing a $200m+ tariff headwind and a CEO transition?*

### 9.1 Immediate Priority: Stabilise the US Core Through Affordability and RTM Excellence

### 9.5 Medium-Term: Complete Portfolio Rationalisation Around "Power Brands"

**Rationale (VRIO + Ansoff):** The VRIO analysis reveals that Diageo's competitive advantage is concentrated in a relatively small number of iconic brands. Maintaining a long tail of subscale brands dilutes management attention and marketing investment.

Guinness is a rare asset — it is one of the few spirits/beer brands with genuine cross-category appeal, growing no/low credentials, and exceptional African growth. Instead, in the interim, Diageo will provide more regular near-term guidance. This is prudent in the short term, but the long-term goal must be to rebuild the credibility that allows medium-term guidance to be reinstated. Analysts and investors price uncertainty with a discount; reducing uncertainty is itself a value-creation act.

The risk is that a turnaround mindset, optimised for cost discipline and short-term stability, conflicts with the investment in brand equity and emerging-market infrastructure that the long-term strategy requires. Managing this tension — between the urgency of stabilisation and the patience required for brand-led growth — is the central strategic challenge facing Diageo's new leadership.

The company that executes this agenda successfully will not merely recover its lost ground; it will emerge with a more resilient, geographically balanced, and future-proofed business model than the one that peaked in 2021.
