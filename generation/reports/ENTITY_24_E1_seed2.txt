# Mercedes-Benz at the Crossroads: Navigating the Electrification Dilemma and the Erosion of the China Premium

## A Strategic Case Study

---

## 1. Introduction and Case Overview

Mercedes-Benz Group AG — the world's oldest premium automotive brand, founded in 1926 — stands at one of the most consequential strategic inflection points in its near-century of history. Having spent the post-2019 era repositioning itself as the world's most desirable luxury car company, the firm now faces a simultaneous triple pressure: a structural decline in its most important single market (China), a painful reversal of its once-bold all-electric commitments, and the emergence of technologically sophisticated Chinese competitors that are rewriting the rules of the premium segment.

This case study examines the strategic situation confronting Mercedes-Benz through the lens of multiple analytical frameworks — Porter's Five Forces, SWOT analysis, the Resource-Based View (RBV/VRIO), the Ansoff Growth Matrix, and the Strategic Clock — to produce a coherent diagnosis of the firm's competitive position. The analysis concludes with a structured set of strategic recommendations.

## 2. The Strategic Situation: Evidence and Context

### 2.1 Financial Deterioration

The financial data tells a stark story. For 2024, the Mercedes-Benz Group reported revenue of €145.6 billion (down 4.5% year-on-year), EBIT of €13.6 billion (down 30.8% YoY), and net profit of €10.4 billion (down 28.4% YoY). This followed a stronger 2023, when a sharpened focus on desirable cars and vans combined with ongoing cost discipline resulted in Group EBIT of €19.7 billion and revenues of €153.2 billion. The speed of the deterioration — from €19.7 billion EBIT in 2023 to €13.6 billion in 2024 — signals not a cyclical blip but a structural challenge to the business model. The company's 2024 operating results showed a 31% decline to 13.6 billion euros, with passenger car segment margins falling from 12.6% to 8.1%.

### 2.2 The China Crisis

China is the epicentre of Mercedes-Benz's difficulties. The 3% decline to 1.98 million vehicles was led by a slump in deliveries in China, its largest market, where consumers are shunning models like the S-Class and Maybach in favour of local brands. Sales in the country dropped 7% to 683,600 cars from a year earlier. This is not a one-quarter anomaly: Mercedes-Benz had also participated in an aggressive price-cutting regime like its Chinese peers until the middle of last year, wherein it abandoned the price war in favour of a cost-cutting strategy. China's economy has slowed sharply since 2023, with luxury-car demand contracting (Mercedes-Benz China sales fell 12–27% in several quarters of 2024–2025). Domestic champions (BYD, NIO, Li Auto, etc.) have eroded the market share of foreign brands.

The competitive threat from Chinese domestic brands is particularly acute at the top end. At the Paris car show, FAW Group's Hongqi and BYD's Yangwang showed off limousines and SUVs that aim to compete for European customers against vehicles from Mercedes, Porsche and even Rolls-Royce. With the latest in digital technology alongside creature comforts like leather dashboards and champagne coolers — all for a competitive price — they threaten European manufacturers in a segment that is essential for profits.

### 2.3 The Electrification Reversal

Mercedes-Benz's strategic credibility has also been damaged by a high-profile reversal of its electrification commitments. Mercedes-Benz is pumping the brakes on its electrification strategy, pushing back its plans to go all-electric by 2030. The blueprint laid out three years ago predicted that half the vehicles the company sold would be electrified by 2025. That goal has now been pushed back five years to 2030. Compounding this, in 2024, Mercedes-Benz reported a 23% drop in battery-electric vehicle (BEV) sales, underscoring the challenges the company faces in this sector. A big part of the initial goal of 50% electric by 2025 was to be taken by all-electric vehicles, but now that seems to have changed too, with the company saying it expects plug-in hybrids to "stay relevant" for several more years.

## 3. Industry Analysis: Porter's Five Forces

Porter's Five Forces framework (1979) provides the foundation for understanding the structural attractiveness of the industry in which Mercedes-Benz competes and the sources of competitive pressure it faces.

### Force 1: Rivalry Among Existing Competitors — **HIGH and Intensifying**

In 2024, the company faces pressure from established rivals like BMW and Audi, as well as new entrants like Tesla and various Chinese EV manufacturers. The traditional German premium triad (Mercedes, BMW, Audi) has been joined by Tesla, which has disrupted pricing conventions in the premium EV segment, and by an increasingly capable cohort of Chinese brands. Rivalry is intensifying because the shift to electrification compresses traditional differentiation based on combustion engine refinement, while digital and software capabilities — areas where Mercedes is playing catch-up — become the new battleground.

### Force 2: Threat of New Entrants — **MODERATE but Rising**

The entry barriers of this industry are very high. The first is the brand equity that existing brands have developed over time. Since these brands have developed and become a symbol of prestige and repute, customers are not willing to try out new brands. The other entry barriers include huge initial investments, extensive marketing and advertisement costs. However, the transition to electrification has materially lowered certain barriers: battery-electric platforms require less mechanical complexity than ICE powertrains, enabling technology-first entrants (NIO, BYD's Yangwang) to achieve credible luxury positioning more rapidly than was previously possible.

### Force 3: Bargaining Power of Suppliers — **MODERATE**

Due to a variety of factors, including supplier scale, fragmentation, and a lack of forward integration, Mercedes suppliers have minimal negotiation leverage. Apart from a few large suppliers with significant negotiating strength, Mercedes-Benz suppliers have minimal bargaining power. However, the shift to EVs introduces a new and critical dependency: battery cell supply. As Mercedes-Benz Group shifts toward an all-electric fleet by 2030, it remains reliant on a few cell suppliers. This concentrated dependency represents a meaningful and growing risk to cost control.

### Force 4: Bargaining Power of Buyers — **MODERATE to HIGH**

In the ultra-luxury segment, buyers are relatively price-inelastic — they purchase status and experience, not just transport. However, the proliferation of credible alternatives (Chinese luxury EVs, Tesla's premium models) is expanding buyer choice and increasing switching options. The fact that Mercedes itself entered a price war in China before abandoning it confirms that buyer power in that market had grown to a level that threatened brand positioning.

### Force 5: Threat of Substitutes — **MODERATE**

Substitutes in the traditional sense (public transport, ride-hailing) pose limited threat to the ultra-luxury segment. However, the emerging software-defined vehicle represents a subtler substitution: a vehicle that competes primarily on digital experience rather than mechanical excellence. If Mercedes cannot match the in-car digital ecosystems offered by Chinese competitors or Tesla, its vehicles risk being perceived as technically inferior substitutes for the same price.

**Five Forces Conclusion:** The industry structure has deteriorated for incumbent European luxury OEMs. While entry barriers remain high in absolute terms, electrification has selectively lowered them in the digital and software dimensions, intensifying rivalry and expanding buyer choice. The net effect is structural pressure on margins — consistent with the observed decline from 12.6% to 8.1% RoS in the passenger car segment.

## 4. Internal Analysis: SWOT and the Resource-Based View (VRIO)

### 4.1 SWOT Analysis

**Strengths**

- **Brand equity:** By 2024, the Mercedes-Benz brand was valued at an impressive $58.8 billion, underscoring its significant global influence and market standing. This brand value confers pricing power: the Mercedes-Benz brand drives pricing power and 32% higher ASPs (average selling prices) versus non-luxury competitors.
- **Sub-brand architecture:** The new strategy will enhance the Mercedes-Benz brand's luxury status, raise the product portfolio's positioning and mix, and pursue significant growth for sub-brands AMG, Maybach, G and EQ.
- **Engineering heritage:** Mercedes-Benz boasts a rich history of more than a century, rooted in technological innovation and luxury. The brand's visual identity, symbolised by the iconic three-pointed star, is synonymous with high-quality engineering and premium vehicles.
- **Software investment:** By 2025, Mercedes-Benz intends to target €1 billion from software-enabled, features-on-demand.

**Weaknesses**

- **Margin erosion:** Passenger car RoS fell from 12.6% to 8.1% in 2024, well below the double-digit target.
- **EV sales underperformance:** A 23% drop in BEV sales in 2024 reveals that Mercedes's electric portfolio has not yet resonated at scale.
- **Strategic inconsistency:** The public reversal on all-electric timelines undermines investor and consumer confidence and signals reactive rather than proactive strategy-making.
- **China dependency:** With China representing Mercedes's single largest market, the concentrated geographic exposure amplifies the impact of regional demand shocks.

**Opportunities**

- **Luxury EV market growth:** The luxury car market is worth USD 567.65 billion in 2025 and is projected to reach USD 782.49 billion by 2030, registering a 6.63% CAGR.
- **Top-end mix enrichment:** Increasing the sales share of its top-end luxury segment by 60% compared to 2019 levels by 2026, focusing on high-margin vehicles.
- **Software monetisation:** The MB.OS platform creates recurring revenue opportunities through over-the-air (OTA) updates and features-on-demand that did not exist in the ICE era.
- **US market resilience:** Data indicates that the US could (or should) be a major focal point for Mercedes in the years to come. US sales grew 9% in 2024, providing a partial offset to the China decline.

**Threats**

- **Chinese competitive encroachment:** The most significant factor behind the sales drop was the 7% reduction in Mercedes-Benz's sales in China. High-end models such as the S-Class and Maybach saw a sharp decline in demand, as local consumers increasingly favoured local brands.
- **Geopolitical risk:** Trade tariffs, EU-China tensions, and the potential dissolution of joint ventures in China represent material risks to manufacturing costs and market access.
- **Technology disruption:** Software-defined vehicles from Tesla and Chinese OEMs threaten to redefine the premium experience around digital rather than mechanical attributes.

### 4.2 Resource-Based View (VRIO Framework)

The RBV (Barney, 1991) holds that sustainable competitive advantage derives from resources and capabilities that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and supported by the Organisation (VRIO). Applying this to Mercedes-Benz:

| Resource / Capability | Valuable? | Rare? | Inimitable? | Organised? | Competitive Implication |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Brand equity & heritage (~$58.8bn) | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ (130+ years) | ✅ | **Sustained advantage** |
| Engineering excellence (safety, ride quality) | ✅ | ✅ | Partially ✅ | ✅ | **Sustained advantage** |
| Sub-brand architecture (AMG, Maybach, G-Class) | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ | **Sustained advantage** |
| MB.OS / software platform | ✅ | Partially ✅ | ❌ (Tesla, NIO comparable) | Developing | **Temporary advantage** |
| China manufacturing JV | ✅ | ❌ | ❌ | At risk | **Competitive parity (eroding)** |
| Battery cell supply chain | ✅ | ❌ | ❌ | ❌ | **Competitive disadvantage** |

The VRIO analysis reveals a critical asymmetry: Mercedes's most inimitable resources are rooted in its analogue heritage (brand, craftsmanship, sub-brand prestige), while its most contested capabilities are precisely those that define the next generation of competition (software, digital experience, battery technology). The resource-based value comprises the physical, financial and human resources which help Mercedes-Benz attain competitive advantage. The tagline 'The Best or Nothing' successfully ties its heritage of excellence in style, performance, engineering, and safety. The strategic imperative is therefore to leverage the inimitable heritage resources as a platform from which to build the currently imitable digital capabilities before competitors fully commoditise the software layer.

## 5. Strategic Position Analysis: The Strategic Clock

Bowman's Strategic Clock maps competitive positions on axes of perceived value and price. Mercedes-Benz has historically occupied Position 5 — **Focused Differentiation** — offering the highest perceived value at a premium price, justified by brand heritage, engineering excellence, and exclusivity.

The current threat is a **clockwise drift** toward Position 6 (Risky High Margins — charging premium prices for declining perceived value) as Chinese competitors offer comparable or superior digital experiences at lower price points. The price war Mercedes briefly entered in China — before abandoning it — was an inadvertent move toward Position 3 (Hybrid Low Price/Added Value), which is structurally incompatible with a luxury positioning.

Mercedes's stated strategy — the "top-end" focus anchored in Maybach, AMG, and the G-Class — is a deliberate attempt to move to Position 5 on the clock and resist the centripetal pull toward commoditisation. Top-end vehicle focus: Elevate AMG, Maybach, G-Class, S-Class, and EQS to drive mix and halo effects across the lineup. This is strategically coherent: by concentrating volume in the segments where heritage-based inimitability is strongest, Mercedes can sustain premium pricing even as mid-market luxury becomes more competitive.

## 6. Growth Strategy Analysis: The Ansoff Matrix

Ansoff's Growth Matrix (1957) categorises growth strategies by the novelty of products and markets. Mercedes-Benz's current initiatives can be mapped as follows:

**Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets):**
Mercedes is pursuing penetration through its "Next Level Performance" programme. A comprehensive performance enhancement plan (Next Level Performance) aims to raise the competitiveness and resilience of Mercedes-Benz, seeking to return Mercedes-Benz Cars to a double-digit adjusted Return on Sales margin. The company plans to cut production costs by 10% until 2027. A 10% reduction in material costs is targeted through deeper localisation of parts in the China supply chain. Variable production costs and fixed costs are set to be reduced as well. This is defensive penetration — defending share and restoring margins in existing markets rather than growing them.

**Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets):**
The strategy is based on profitable growth through the introduction of luxury EVs, and as part of this strategy, it will launch the Mercedes-AMG PureSpeed, the first in the ultra-luxury brand "Mythos" series, in 2025. The MB.OS software platform represents another product development vector: from 2025 onwards, certain functionalities can be upgraded over the vehicle lifecycle through the Mercedes me Store. To explore new functions and make purchases, customers can access the Mercedes me Store via app, the web or from inside the car.

**Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets):**
The US market represents the most promising market development opportunity. Sales grew 9% in the US in 2024, while China declined. The Middle East and India represent further underpenetrated high-net-worth markets where Mercedes's heritage brand equity is a powerful asset.

**Diversification (New Products, New Markets):**
The most speculative but potentially transformative diversification is autonomous driving. The new S-Class with MB.OS, equipped with the NVIDIA DRIVE Hyperion architecture and full-stack NVIDIA DRIVE AV L4 software, is designed to support future robotaxi operations. This represents a genuine diversification beyond vehicle manufacturing into mobility services, albeit at an early stage.

**Ansoff Conclusion:** Mercedes's portfolio of initiatives spans all four quadrants, but the risk-adjusted priority should be: (1) penetration via cost discipline to restore margins; (2) product development via luxury EVs and software monetisation to rebuild differentiation; (3) market development toward the US and emerging markets to reduce China dependency. Diversification into autonomous mobility services should be pursued as a long-term option but should not absorb disproportionate near-term capital.

## 7. The Core Strategic Tension: Coherence Under Pressure

The analysis above reveals a central strategic tension that must be resolved for any recommendations to be coherent. Mercedes-Benz is simultaneously being pulled in two directions:

1. **The Heritage Pull:** Double down on what is inimitable — brand prestige, craftsmanship, exclusivity, the Maybach-AMG-G-Class constellation — and let the mid-market commoditise around it.

2. **The Technology Push:** Accelerate software-defined vehicle capabilities (MB.OS, autonomous driving, OTA monetisation) to remain relevant in an industry where digital experience is becoming the primary differentiator.

These are not mutually exclusive, but they require different capital allocation logic, different talent profiles, and different time horizons. The risk of trying to do both without prioritisation is strategic ambiguity — the condition in which a firm's actions send contradictory signals to customers, employees, and investors. The EV reversal is a symptom of this ambiguity: having committed to "electric only" architectures from 2025, the company then retreated under market pressure, undermining the credibility of both the technology push and the heritage narrative.

The analytical chain is therefore: **structural industry deterioration (Five Forces) → erosion of previously inimitable capabilities (VRIO) → margin compression (financial evidence) → strategic ambiguity (Strategic Clock drift) → need for a focused, coherent repositioning (Ansoff).**

## 8. Strategic Recommendations

The following recommendations are ordered by urgency and interconnected into a coherent action plan.

### Recommendation 1: Commit Unambiguously to the Ultra-Luxury Segment — and Mean It

**Rationale:** The VRIO analysis demonstrates that Mercedes's most durable competitive advantages lie in its heritage-based brand assets. The Strategic Clock analysis shows that the path to sustained profitability runs through Position 5 (focused differentiation), not through volume competition with Chinese OEMs. The Ansoff framework confirms that product development within the existing luxury customer base offers the highest risk-adjusted returns.

**Action:** Accelerate the "top-end" strategy with explicit volume targets. The share of top-end vehicle sales is projected to rise by around 60% by 2026, with a product offensive from Maybach, AMG and G-Class with dedicated EV characteristics and greater individualisation, and the launch of a "MYTHOS Series" of highly exclusive collectible vehicles. This trajectory should be locked in as a non-negotiable strategic commitment, not a contingent aspiration. Concretely, this means reducing the volume of entry-level Mercedes models (A-Class, B-Class equivalents) that dilute brand equity, accepting lower unit volumes in exchange for higher per-unit margins.

### Recommendation 2: Resolve the Electrification Ambiguity with a Tiered Platform Strategy

**Rationale:** The EV reversal has damaged strategic credibility. The root cause was a single, market-wide commitment that could not survive the heterogeneity of demand across geographies. A tiered approach — differentiating the electrification timeline by segment and geography — would restore coherence without abandoning the long-term direction.

**Action:** Mercedes-Benz is targeting an electrified vehicle (xEV) share of more than 30% by 2027. This interim target should be communicated as a floor, not a ceiling, for the ultra-luxury segment (Maybach, AMG, G-Class), where EV technology enhances rather than compromises the brand proposition (instant torque, near-silent cabin, over-the-air customisation). For the broader Mercedes-Benz passenger car range, plug-in hybrids should be retained as a "relevant" technology for several more years in markets where charging infrastructure remains immature. This is not a retreat from electrification — it is a market-segmented implementation that aligns technology deployment with genuine customer readiness.

### Recommendation 3: Accelerate MB.OS as a Revenue Platform, Not Just a Feature

**Rationale:** The software-defined vehicle is the single most important battleground in the next decade of automotive competition. Mercedes currently has a first-mover advantage in Level 3 autonomous driving (approved in 12 markets) and a proprietary OS (MB.OS). These capabilities are currently classified as "temporary advantage" in the VRIO analysis because they are not yet fully monetised or defended by network effects. Converting them into sustained advantages requires scale and speed.

**Action:** Mercedes-Benz's strategy of developing its own automotive OS aligns with the software-defined vehicle trend, targeting €1 billion from software-enabled, features-on-demand by 2025. This target should be treated as a starting point. The strategic logic of OTA monetisation is powerful: it converts a one-time vehicle sale into a recurring revenue relationship, fundamentally changing the economics of the luxury automotive business model. Mercedes should partner with luxury lifestyle brands (fashion, hospitality, wellness) to create exclusive in-car experiences available through the Mercedes me Store, creating a moat around the digital ecosystem that is as powerful as the physical brand heritage.

### Recommendation 4: Diversify Geographic Revenue Away from China Dependency

**Rationale:** China represented approximately 34% of Mercedes's global passenger car sales in recent years. The concentration of this exposure means that a single market's structural shift can materially impair Group results — as 2024 demonstrated. The Five Forces analysis identifies this as a buyer power risk: when one market accounts for a third of revenues, that market's consumers and regulators hold disproportionate leverage.

**Action:** Rebalance the geographic portfolio toward the US (where 2024 sales grew 9%), India (a high-growth luxury market with a rising affluent class), and the Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC) states (where Mercedes's brand heritage commands strong premium pricing). These projections reflect Mercedes-Benz's confidence in its ability to navigate current challenges while continuing to execute its strategic priorities around electrification, digitalisation, and luxury positioning. Simultaneously, in China, Mercedes should shift from volume defence to margin defence — maintaining presence in the ultra-luxury tier (Maybach, AMG) where domestic competitors have less established credibility, while ceding mid-market volume to domestic brands without engaging in margin-destructive price wars.

### Recommendation 5: Execute the "Next Level Performance" Cost Programme with Discipline

**Rationale:** Strategic repositioning requires financial headroom. Without restored margins, Mercedes cannot fund the R&D and brand investment needed to sustain its differentiated position. The cost programme is not a strategy in itself, but it is the necessary enabler of all the above recommendations.

**Action:** A 10% reduction in material costs is targeted through deeper localisation of parts in the China supply chain. Variable production costs and fixed costs are set to be reduced as well. Potential adjustments to the dealer network and production footprint are also being evaluated. The CFO's stated ambition to return to double-digit margins in the near future should be treated as a hard constraint on capital allocation decisions. Cost savings should be reinvested preferentially into MB.OS development, top-end product launches, and geographic diversification — not distributed entirely to shareholders through buybacks, however politically attractive that may be in the short term.

## 10. Conclusion

Mercedes-Benz's strategic situation is serious but not terminal. The firm possesses some of the most durable brand assets in global industry — assets that, by the VRIO framework's criteria, are genuinely valuable, rare, and inimitable. The challenge is not that these assets have lost their value; it is that the industry's competitive frontier has shifted to a domain (software, digital experience, EV platforms) where Mercedes's historical advantages do not automatically transfer.

The coherent strategic response is neither to abandon the heritage nor to ignore the technology shift, but to use the former to fund and legitimise the latter. A Maybach or AMG that offers the world's most refined electric powertrain, the most personalised in-car digital ecosystem, and the most exclusive ownership experience commands a price premium that no Chinese startup can match — not because of technology alone, but because of the 130 years of cultural capital embedded in the three-pointed star.

The analytical chain from Five Forces (deteriorating structure) through VRIO (heritage as anchor, software as frontier) through the Strategic Clock (stay at Position 5) through Ansoff (product development and selective market development) leads to a single, unified strategic logic: **Mercedes-Benz must become the world's most desirable luxury technology company, not merely the world's most prestigious car manufacturer.** The distinction is not semantic — it defines the competitive arena, the talent strategy, the capital allocation priorities, and ultimately the firm's relevance in a decade's time.
