# Diageo at the Crossroads: Navigating Structural Headwinds, Leadership Transition, and the Limits of Premiumisation

## A Strategic Case Study

---

## 1. INTRODUCTION AND CASE OVERVIEW

Diageo plc is the world's largest premium spirits company by net sales, operating from 132 sites in nearly 180 countries with a portfolio spanning Johnnie Walker Scotch whisky, Guinness stout, Don Julio tequila, Tanqueray gin, Smirnoff vodka, and Baileys liqueur. Founded through the 1997 merger of Guinness and Grand Metropolitan, Diageo has long been the archetype of a consumer-goods premiumisation story — systematically trading consumers up the price ladder and harvesting margin expansion.

Yet between 2023 and 2025, that model came under acute stress. In fiscal year 2024, organic net sales were down 0.6%, with positive price/mix performance mostly mitigating a decline in volume. Organic operating margin declined by 130 basis points, primarily driven by the Latin America and Caribbean (LAC) region. The crisis deepened in leadership: Diageo announced that Debra Crew had stepped down as Chief Executive Officer with immediate effect, by mutual agreement, with the Board beginning a comprehensive formal search process. Her successor, Sir Dave Lewis, was appointed CEO effective 1 January 2026 — a proven turnaround executive who served as Group CEO of Tesco plc from 2014 to 2020 and spent nearly three decades at Unilever.

**The strategic situation** is therefore a multi-front challenge: a cyclical consumer downturn has exposed structural weaknesses in Diageo's over-reliance on premiumisation in saturated Western markets; a leadership vacuum creates strategic ambiguity at a critical moment; and macro forces — US tariffs, currency volatility, and a secular moderation trend — are reshaping the total beverage alcohol (TBA) landscape. The question facing the incoming CEO is not simply how to return to growth, but *which growth model* is appropriate for the next decade.

This case study applies Porter's Five Forces, SWOT analysis, the Ansoff Growth Matrix, and a Value Chain lens to diagnose Diageo's position and prescribe a coherent strategic response.

## 2. INDUSTRY CONTEXT: PORTER'S FIVE FORCES

Understanding Diageo's strategic options requires first mapping the competitive forces reshaping the premium spirits industry.

### 2.1 Competitive Rivalry (HIGH)

The global spirits market is an oligopoly of global giants — Diageo, Pernod Ricard, LVMH Moët Hennessy, and Beam Suntory — each holding diversified portfolios of prestige brands. Rivalry is intense on marketing spend, brand equity, and route-to-market capability. Diageo brands have driven around 17% of total absolute dollar growth in the international spirits category since 2018, yet share gains are contested continuously. The entry of craft distillers in American whiskey and gin has fragmented the premium tier further, adding competitive noise even if individual entrants are small.

### 2.2 Threat of Substitutes (HIGH AND RISING)

The most structurally significant force is substitution — and it is accelerating in two directions simultaneously. First, *within* alcohol: the alcoholic beverage industry is evolving quickly, with the rise of low/no-alcohol drinks, the explosion of ready-to-drink (RTD) cocktails, and the continued premiumisation of spirits among the most impactful shifts. RTDs in particular have taken share from spirits in the US, with Diageo's own filings acknowledging that market share of spirits declined, primarily driven by ready-to-drink cocktails as a result of increased competitive activity. Second, *outside* alcohol: "sober curious" and moderation movements have gone mainstream, leading to a boom in low-alcohol and non-alcoholic beverages, with more consumers — especially millennials — actively reducing consumption. The forecasted volume CAGR for no-alcohol beverages between 2024 and 2028 is +7%, with the no/low-alcohol market in the top 10 global markets experiencing double-digit volume growth of +13% during 2024. This is not a niche trend; it is a structural shift in how younger cohorts relate to alcohol.

### 2.3 Bargaining Power of Buyers (MODERATE, SHIFTING)

Diageo sells primarily through distributors and retailers (off-trade) and on-trade operators. Retailer consolidation — particularly in the US three-tier distribution system — concentrates buyer power. Diageo's FY2025 US route-to-market transformation was explicitly designed to address this: investments in digital capabilities, supply chain, and transformational route-to-market changes are intended to drive long-term sustainable growth, with early benefits already visible from US route-to-market transformation. The LAC crisis illustrated what happens when the channel has too much power: elevated distributor inventory forced Diageo to offer trade discounts to clear stock, compressing margins. Price/mix declined 4% in LAC, reflecting higher trade investment to manage inventory towards appropriate levels and consumer downtrading.

### 2.4 Bargaining Power of Suppliers (LOW TO MODERATE)

Diageo's scale gives it negotiating leverage over most agricultural inputs. However, climate risk is elevating supplier power in key categories: climate-driven yield variability affected agave and barley prices in 2024–2025, with tequila production costs rising materially amid significant agave price swings. For Don Julio — one of Diageo's highest-margin growth brands — agave price inflation is a structural cost risk that cannot be fully passed to consumers in a value-conscious environment.

### 2.5 Threat of New Entrants (LOW FOR PREMIUM SCALE; MODERATE FOR CRAFT)

Building a global premium spirits brand requires decades of heritage, distribution infrastructure, and marketing capital — formidable barriers. Johnnie Walker's retail sales value increased over 400% since 2002, a trajectory that cannot be replicated quickly. However, the craft segment lowers entry barriers at the premium tier in specific categories (American single malt, artisan gin), and celebrity-backed spirits (e.g., Ryan Reynolds' Aviation Gin, acquired by Diageo itself) have demonstrated that brand equity can be built rapidly with the right cultural capital.

**Five Forces Summary:** The industry faces a deteriorating structural position — rising substitution from no/low and RTDs, increasing buyer power through retail consolidation, and cost inflation in key raw materials. This context is essential for evaluating Diageo's internal position.

## 3. INTERNAL ANALYSIS: SWOT

### 3.1 Strengths

**Portfolio Depth and Brand Equity.** Diageo possesses what strategists call a "portfolio of portfolios" — 13 billion-dollar brands and sales in nearly 180 countries, spanning beer, spirits, and liqueurs across price tiers. This diversification is a genuine competitive advantage: when Scotch faltered in LAC, Guinness and Don Julio partially compensated globally.

**Premiumisation Track Record.** Premium-plus brands comprised 63% of reported net sales in fiscal 2023, a 7 percentage point increase from fiscal 2019. This shift has structurally improved Diageo's gross margin profile and demonstrates the company's ability to execute brand elevation strategies over time.

**Emerging Market Footprint.** Diageo's 2025 expansion prioritises Asia-Pacific and Africa, with a major scale-up in India to capture premium scotch and local craft spirit demand. India is particularly compelling: within the next decade, some 150 million Indian consumers will enter the legal purchasing age for alcohol, with a growing middle class and changing alcohol consumption patterns presenting potential to expand repertoires and deliver new experiences.

**No/Low Alcohol Investment.** Diageo has invested in non-alcoholic brand extensions across its portfolio — from zero-alcohol Captain Morgan and Tanqueray to Guinness 0.0 — positioning it to capture the moderation trend rather than be disrupted by it. Guinness 0.0 has been a standout: Ireland net sales grew 7%, primarily driven by double-digit growth in Guinness, with strong share gains in the on-trade driven by effective brand building and the roll-out of Guinness 0.0 draught, now in more than 1,500 on-trade outlets.

### 3.2 Weaknesses

**Over-Concentration in North America.** North America accounts for approximately 39% of Diageo's global sales — a dangerous concentration in a market experiencing structural headwinds. The North American division saw a decline in sales, attributable to a cautious consumer environment and the impact of lapping inventory replenishment in the prior year.

**Inventory Management Failures.** The LAC crisis was fundamentally an inventory management and demand-sensing failure. The Latin America and Caribbean region experienced an "extraordinary period of growth" — increasing net sales value by 50% from 2019 to 2022 — attributed to the so-called "COVID super-cycle." Diageo shipped into this demand surge without adequately discounting post-pandemic normalisation, leaving distributors with excess stock that took over a year to clear. This reflects a weakness in real-time demand visibility and channel management.

**Elevated Leverage.** The leverage ratio stood at 3.1x net debt to EBITDA as at 31 December 2024, constraining Diageo's ability to pursue acquisitions or accelerate investment during the recovery phase. The Board has responded by initiating disposals: an agreement was announced to sell Diageo's 65% shareholding in East African Breweries plc and its Kenyan spirits business to Asahi Group Holdings, with estimated net proceeds after tax of $2.3 billion, expected to delever the balance sheet.

**Leadership Instability.** The departure of Debra Crew and the appointment of an interim CEO through end-2025 introduces strategic ambiguity precisely when decisive direction is needed. The Board's priorities for the new CEO are to deliver a return to consistent and sustainable growth, refresh the strategy to deliver shareholder value in a potentially evolving TBA landscape. Until Sir Dave Lewis takes the helm in January 2026, strategic commitments risk being deferred.

### 3.3 Opportunities

**India and Emerging Market Growth.** Diageo continued to benefit from consumer premiumisation in India, particularly in the prestige and above whisky segment, aided by smaller pack sizes and the potential tailwind of the UK–India Free Trade Agreement. India's whisky market is the world's largest by volume, and Diageo's United Spirits subsidiary has already repositioned its portfolio: Diageo India sold 32 mass-market brands in 2022 to focus almost entirely on Prestige and Above products, which now account for more than 90% of net sales.

**No/Low Alcohol Category Expansion.** Across 10 key markets, 61 million people were recruited into no-alcohol between 2022 and 2024, and 38 million into low-alcohol. Diageo's existing brand equity in Guinness, Tanqueray, and Captain Morgan gives it a right to win in these extensions without building new brands from scratch — a significant cost and time advantage.

**Africa's Long-Run Demographic Dividend.** Africa delivered organic net sales growth across all markets in fiscal 2025, led by Ghana. Africa's young, urbanising population represents the next generation of premium spirits consumers. Diageo's existing manufacturing and distribution infrastructure on the continent — built through decades of Guinness brewing operations — is a durable competitive asset.

### 3.4 Threats

**US Tariffs.** Diageo faces an approximate US$150 million unmitigated annual impact from the 10% tariff placed by US President Donald Trump on UK and European imports to the United States. Johnnie Walker, Tanqueray, and Ketel One are all produced outside the US and therefore directly exposed. This is not merely a cost issue — it threatens to erode Diageo's pricing power in its single largest market.

**Secular Moderation.** Gen Z, those born between 1997 and 2012, have come of age during the rise of non-alcohol spirits and are known to be health-conscious and sober-curious, with data suggesting they are more sensitive to price increases. If this cohort's moderation habits persist into peak consumption years (late 20s–40s), the long-run volume trajectory for premium spirits faces structural compression — not just cyclical softness.

**Geopolitical and Currency Volatility.** Medium-term guidance was removed due to current macroeconomic and geopolitical uncertainty in many key markets impacting the pace of recovery. With nearly 40% of sales from emerging markets, currency depreciation in Nigeria, Argentina, or India can rapidly erode reported earnings even when underlying business performance is sound.

## 4. STRATEGIC ANALYSIS: THE PREMIUMISATION MODEL UNDER PRESSURE

### 4.1 Diageo's Core Strategic Logic — and Its Limits

Diageo's dominant strategic logic has been **premiumisation**: shift the portfolio mix upward (from standard to premium to super-premium), price ahead of inflation, and harvest gross margin expansion. This logic was validated spectacularly between 2013 and 2022. However, the 2023–2025 period has exposed three structural limits to this model.

**Limit 1: Premiumisation requires a willing consumer.** In a cost-of-living crisis, consumers trade down. Spirits net sales in LAC declined 25%, primarily led by a double-digit decline in Scotch, particularly Buchanan's, Johnnie Walker Black Label and Old Parr, as well as a strong double-digit decline in Don Julio. These are Diageo's premium and super-premium brands — precisely the segment the strategy depended upon. When consumer confidence falters, the premium tier is disproportionately exposed.

**Limit 2: Premiumisation in mature markets has a ceiling.** Diageo holds 4.5% value share of total beverage alcohol, with an ambition to grow to 6% by 2030. In Western markets, spirits penetration is already high and the addressable market is not growing at the rate needed to sustain historical growth rates through premiumisation alone. The strategy requires new geographies or new occasions — not just higher prices in existing markets.

**Limit 3: Premium price points attract substitution.** At high price points, consumers have more alternatives — craft competitors, celebrity brands, and increasingly, premium no/low options. The RTD threat is emblematic: market share of spirits declined, primarily driven by ready-to-drink cocktails as a result of increased competitive activity — a category that often delivers a "premium experience" at a lower per-serve price than a full bottle of Scotch.

### 4.2 Ansoff Matrix: Where Should Diageo Grow?

The Ansoff Growth Matrix provides a disciplined framework for evaluating Diageo's strategic options across two dimensions: markets (existing vs. new) and products (existing vs. new).

| | **Existing Products** | **New Products** |
|---|---|---|
| **Existing Markets** | **Market Penetration** — deepen share in North America, Europe | **Product Development** — no/low extensions, RTD formats |
| **New Markets** | **Market Development** — India, Africa, Southeast Asia | **Diversification** — premium wellness beverages, cannabis-adjacent |

**Quadrant 1 — Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets):** The US route-to-market transformation falls here. Diageo has stepped up its route-to-market across several markets, including the most significant transformation in at least a decade in US Spirits. This is a necessary defensive move — improving execution in the existing core — but it is unlikely to deliver above-market growth rates on its own. The risk is that Diageo over-invests in defending a market where structural headwinds (tariffs, moderation) are intensifying.

**Quadrant 2 — Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets):** The no/low alcohol portfolio extension is Diageo's most strategically compelling product development opportunity. Diageo's moves in no- and low-alcohol are part of its Society 2030: Spirit of Progress ESG plan, enabling and reinforcing the breadth of choices that consumers have to moderate. However, Diageo has not yet set a specific sales target for this portfolio — a signal that it has not yet committed to treating no/low as a core growth engine rather than a defensive hedge. Given the IWSR's forecast of +$4 billion in incremental growth from the no-alcohol category by 2028, this ambiguity is strategically costly.

**Quadrant 3 — Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets):** India and Africa represent the highest-conviction growth opportunities. Africa delivered organic net sales growth across all markets in fiscal 2025, led by Ghana, while Latin America and the Caribbean saw improving conditions in Brazil and premiumisation-led growth in Mexico. The India opportunity is structurally superior: a large, young, urbanising middle class with rising income and a cultural affinity for whisky — Diageo's core competency. This quadrant carries moderate risk (execution, regulatory, currency) but offers the highest long-run volume upside.

**Quadrant 4 — Diversification:** Cannabis-adjacent beverages and premium wellness drinks have been explored by some competitors but represent high risk for Diageo. The company's competitive advantage is built on distillation heritage, brand equity, and alcohol distribution — capabilities that do not transfer cleanly to adjacent categories. This quadrant should be deprioritised.

**Ansoff Conclusion:** Diageo's growth strategy should prioritise **Quadrant 3 (Market Development in India and Africa)** as the primary long-run engine, **Quadrant 2 (No/Low Product Development)** as the strategic hedge against secular moderation, and **Quadrant 1 (Market Penetration in the US)** as a defensive necessity — but not a growth driver.

## 6. THE CENTRAL STRATEGIC TENSION: RESILIENCE VS. GROWTH

The analysis above reveals a central strategic tension that the incoming CEO must resolve. Diageo faces two competing imperatives:

1. **Resilience imperative:** Reduce leverage, improve channel management, defend core market share in North America and Europe, and weather the tariff and moderation headwinds.
2. **Growth imperative:** Accelerate in India and Africa, build the no/low portfolio into a genuine revenue line, and capture the next wave of TBA growth before competitors do.

These imperatives are not mutually exclusive, but they compete for capital, management attention, and organisational bandwidth. The FY2025 preliminary results noted that while there is clearly more to do, organic growth was ahead of competition — a fragile position that requires both defending and building simultaneously.

The risk of over-indexing on resilience is that Diageo cedes the India and Africa growth opportunities to Pernod Ricard and Beam Suntory, who are also investing aggressively in these markets. The risk of over-indexing on growth is that elevated leverage and weak North American execution leave Diageo exposed to a further demand shock or tariff escalation.

## 7. RECOMMENDATIONS AND ACTION PLAN

The following recommendations are structured around three time horizons — immediate (0–12 months), medium-term (1–3 years), and long-term (3–5 years) — and are directly derived from the preceding analysis.

### Recommendation 1: Resolve the Capital Structure to Restore Strategic Flexibility (Immediate)

**The case:** At 3.1x net debt/EBITDA, Diageo's leverage constrains its ability to invest in growth markets or respond to acquisition opportunities. The East African Breweries disposal generates approximately $2.3 billion in proceeds and will delever the balance sheet — this must be prioritised and completed swiftly. However, the disposal of African brewing assets is strategically double-edged: it reduces exposure to one of Diageo's highest-conviction long-run growth regions. The proceeds should therefore be ring-fenced for reinvestment in India and Africa's spirits businesses, not returned to shareholders via buybacks.

**Why the evidence warrants this:** The leverage ratio of 3.1x is above the typical investment-grade comfort zone for consumer staples companies and is already causing the Board to remove medium-term guidance. Medium-term guidance was removed due to current macroeconomic and geopolitical uncertainty in many key markets impacting the pace of recovery. Strategic ambiguity at the top of the capital structure propagates downward into brand investment decisions, hiring, and M&A — all of which are critical to recovery.

### Recommendation 2: Commit to No/Low as a Standalone Growth Engine, Not a Defensive Hedge (Immediate–Medium Term)

**The case:** Diageo currently treats its no/low portfolio as part of its ESG commitments and "responsible drinking" narrative rather than as a genuine revenue growth driver. This is strategically insufficient. The no-alcohol category is expected to deliver incremental growth of US$4 billion by 2028, with the no-alcohol segment recruiting more new consumers than its low-alcohol counterpart. Diageo should set an explicit revenue target for its no/low portfolio (e.g., 5% of net sales by 2028), invest behind Guinness 0.0 as the flagship, and extend the model to Tanqueray 0.0 and Captain Morgan 0.0 in the on-trade.

**Why the evidence warrants this:** Guinness 0.0 has already demonstrated proof of concept — the roll-out of Guinness 0.0 draught is now in more than 1,500 on-trade outlets — and Ireland's double-digit Guinness growth in FY2024 was partly attributable to this extension. The moderation trend is structural, not cyclical: Gen Z consumers are known to be health-conscious and sober-curious, and this cohort will represent the majority of new alcohol (and non-alcohol) consumers through the 2030s. Failing to capture them now risks permanent share loss to specialist no/low brands.

Diageo should accelerate evaluation of US-based production for its most tariff-exposed brands — particularly Ketel One vodka (Dutch origin) and Tanqueray gin (UK origin) — while protecting the provenance-dependent Scotch whisky portfolio, where geographic origin is legally protected and non-negotiable.

### Recommendation 6: Rebuild Demand-Sensing Capability to Prevent the Next LAC Crisis (Medium Term)

**The case:** The LAC inventory crisis was a demand-sensing and channel-management failure that cost Diageo over 20% organic sales decline in the region and significant management distraction. Diageo's new tech and data action plan for Latin America includes investment in technology and data to improve inventory visibility and prevent future channel overstocking. The Latin America and Caribbean region experienced an extraordinary period of growth — increasing net sales value by 50% from 2019 to 2022 — attributed to the COVID super-cycle, and Diageo's shipment decisions did not adequately discount the post-pandemic normalisation. Better demand sensing is not merely an operational improvement; it is a strategic necessity for a company operating in 180 markets with complex distribution chains.

## 8. CONCLUSION: A COHERENT STRATEGIC LOGIC

The analysis presented in this case study leads to a coherent strategic conclusion. Diageo's premiumisation model remains valid — but it must be **geographically rebalanced** (from North America/Europe to India/Africa), **categorically extended** (from spirits-only to include no/low), and **operationally reinforced** (through better demand sensing and channel management).

The Five Forces analysis establishes that the industry environment is deteriorating in mature markets (rising substitution from RTDs and no/low, buyer power through retail consolidation) but improving in emerging markets (growing middle classes, low spirits penetration). This external reality directly informs the Ansoff recommendation to prioritise market development in India and Africa over market penetration in North America.

The SWOT analysis confirms that Diageo's strengths (brand equity, distilling heritage, emerging market footprint) are best leveraged in the Ansoff Quadrant 3 and 2 strategies, while its weaknesses (leverage, channel management, leadership transition) require immediate remediation to preserve strategic optionality.

The Value Chain analysis identifies that Diageo's competitive advantage is concentrated in production heritage and brand building — and that the route-to-market weakness is the primary operational constraint on translating that advantage into market share gains.

The incoming CEO, Sir Dave Lewis, inherits a business with a broad portfolio of iconic brands in many of the largest categories in TBA, 13 billion-dollar brands and sales in nearly 180 countries — but one that must sharpen its strategy to accelerate growth. The recommendations above provide a coherent roadmap for doing so: deleverage to restore strategic flexibility, commit to no/low as a growth engine, accelerate India, mitigate tariff exposure, rationalise the portfolio, and rebuild demand-sensing capability. Each recommendation is grounded in the evidence, connected to the analytical frameworks, and sequenced to address the most urgent constraints first.

The strategic situation Diageo faces is genuinely difficult — but it is not existential. The fundamentals of the premium spirits industry remain attractive over the long run. The question is whether Diageo can execute a strategic pivot quickly enough to capture the next wave of growth before competitors do, while managing the cyclical and structural headwinds that are compressing performance today.
