## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This case study examines the strategic challenges confronting COMAC (Commercial Aircraft Corporation of China), a state-owned enterprise (SOE) and China's flagship commercial aircraft manufacturer. Despite receiving tens of billions of dollars in state funding and holding a domestic backlog of over 1,000 aircraft orders, COMAC faces a paradox: it is simultaneously the world's most heavily subsidised aircraft maker and one of its most commercially constrained. The company's flagship C919 narrowbody jet — positioned to challenge the Airbus A320neo and Boeing 737 MAX — has delivered only ~13–16 aircraft in 2024 against a target of 75, has no international type certification, and depends on Western suppliers for 50–60% of its components. Using a multi-framework strategic analysis, this case identifies the core tensions between COMAC's geopolitical ambitions and its operational realities, and proposes a phased action plan to resolve them.

## 2. CASE BACKGROUND & COMPANY PROFILE

### 2.1 Origins and Ownership Structure

COMAC was established in May 2008 in Shanghai as a direct response to China's strategic goal of developing an indigenous commercial aviation industry. It is a state-owned enterprise that reports directly to the **State Council of China**, not to a parent industrial conglomerate, underscoring its status as a national strategic priority. Its largest shareholder — with a 31.6% stake — is SASAC (the State-owned Assets Supervision and Administration Commission), with the Shanghai Municipal Government holding approximately 25% and AVIC (Aviation Industry Corporation of China) holding just under 25%.

COMAC is not publicly listed. As an unlisted SOE, it has no obligation to be transparent about its finances to outside investors, making precise financial analysis difficult. What is known is that it received approximately RMB 19 billion (~$2.7 billion) in initial paid-in capital, and by 2016, financial institutions had reportedly provided at least RMB 253.5 billion in total financing, including RMB 50 billion from the China Export-Import Bank specifically for R&D, manufacturing, and sales. COMAC has also issued at least RMB 55.5 billion in corporate bonds, largely held by state-linked institutions.

### 2.2 Product Portfolio

| Aircraft | Type | Status |
|---|---|---|
| ARJ21 (C909) | Regional jet (90 seats) | In service (domestic only) |
| C919 | Narrowbody (158–192 seats) | In service (China only, ~30 deliveries to date) |
| C929 | Widebody (280 seats, joint with Russia) | Development (stalled) |
| C939 | Ultra-widebody | Concept stage |

The C919 is COMAC's centrepiece — a narrowbody jet targeting the world's most lucrative and competitive segment of commercial aviation, currently dominated by Airbus (A320neo family) and Boeing (737 MAX family).

### 2.3 The Strategic Situation in Brief

COMAC's core strategic dilemma can be stated as follows:

> **COMAC has the political mandate, the financial backing, and the domestic market to become a global aircraft manufacturer — but it lacks the technology independence, international certification, MRO infrastructure, and operational track record to credibly compete beyond China's borders.**

The company is caught between two worlds: it is too politically significant to be allowed to fail, yet too commercially immature to compete on merit. This tension defines every strategic challenge it faces.

## 3. THE STRATEGIC SITUATION — KEY CHALLENGES

### Challenge 1: Catastrophic Production Underperformance
COMAC delivered only approximately 13–16 C919 aircraft in 2024 — against a stated target of 75. Its 2025 target has since been slashed by two-thirds to approximately 25 deliveries. This is not a demand problem — COMAC holds a domestic backlog exceeding 1,000 aircraft. The bottleneck is supply-side: the company is heavily dependent on foreign-sourced components, particularly the CFM International LEAP-1C engine (a joint venture between American GE Aerospace and France's Safran), which accounts for the single largest technology dependency.

### Challenge 2: Western Technology Dependency
The C919 relies on Western technology for an estimated **50–60% of its components**. These include:
- **Propulsion**: CFM LEAP-1C turbofan engines (GE Aerospace / Safran JV)
- **Avionics**: Systems from Honeywell, Rockwell Collins
- **Flight control systems**: Parker Aerospace
- **Nacelles**: Nexcelle (a Safran/GE joint venture)

In May 2025, the U.S. Commerce Department revoked export licences allowing American companies to supply COMAC with these technologies, citing trade tensions and China's restrictions on rare earth exports. Although exports partially resumed in August 2025, the disruption created lasting production backlogs and exposed COMAC's fundamental vulnerability.

### Challenge 3: International Certification Paralysis
The C919 holds only a **CAAC (Civil Aviation Administration of China) type certificate**, meaning it is legally permitted to operate only in Chinese airspace. EASA (the European Union Aviation Safety Agency) certification — essential for international sales — is now unlikely before **2028 and could slip to 2031**, according to European regulators. COMAC is not currently pursuing FAA certification at all. Without international certification, the C919 cannot be financed by non-Chinese lessors, operated by non-Chinese airlines, or sold to the vast majority of the world's carriers.

### Challenge 4: MRO and After-Sales Infrastructure Gap
For airlines to commit to a new aircraft type, they need confidence in a global Maintenance, Repair & Overhaul (MRO) network, spare parts availability, pilot training infrastructure, and technical support. COMAC has virtually none of this outside China. African and Southeast Asian airlines that might otherwise consider the C919 have explicitly stated they require EASA or FAA certification and a credible MRO network before purchase.

### Challenge 5: Governance and Innovation Constraints
As an SOE, COMAC faces structural governance constraints that limit the speed and agility of its decision-making. Innovation in aerospace requires tolerating failure, attracting global talent, and making rapid, market-responsive investment decisions — all of which sit uncomfortably within the bureaucratic incentive structures of state-owned enterprises. COMAC's opaque finances, political reporting lines, and national-champion status create pressures that can distort strategic priorities.

## 6. PORTER'S FIVE FORCES ANALYSIS

Porter's Five Forces provides a structural analysis of the commercial aircraft industry's competitive dynamics as they apply to COMAC.

### Force 1: Threat of New Entrants — **LOW**

The commercial aircraft industry has some of the highest barriers to entry of any sector:
- **Capital requirements**: Developing a new narrowbody aircraft costs $10–20 billion and takes 15–20 years
- **Regulatory barriers**: EASA/FAA certification is an enormous technical and bureaucratic hurdle
- **Technological complexity**: Requires mastery of aerodynamics, propulsion, avionics, materials science
- **Supply chain depth**: Thousands of tier-1, tier-2, and tier-3 suppliers needed
- **Customer switching costs**: Airlines invest heavily in pilot training, MRO infrastructure, and spare parts for a specific aircraft type

**Implication for COMAC**: These same barriers that protect Boeing and Airbus also protect COMAC within China. However, COMAC itself is a new entrant to the *international* market, meaning it faces all of these barriers from the other side.

### Force 2: Bargaining Power of Suppliers — **HIGH (and Rising)**

This is COMAC's most dangerous force:
- The CFM LEAP-1C engine is produced by a single JV (GE Aerospace + Safran) — COMAC has no alternative propulsion source currently certified for the C919
- Key avionics, flight control systems, and nacelles are sourced from a small number of Western suppliers
- US export controls have demonstrated that these suppliers can be cut off at any time by political decision
- The domestic CJ-1000A engine alternative is years from certification
- **Supplier power is existential** — the 2025 engine export ban directly caused COMAC's production collapse

### Force 3: Bargaining Power of Buyers — **MODERATE (Domestic) / HIGH (International)**

- **Domestic**: Chinese state airlines (Air China, China Eastern, China Southern) are effectively directed by the government to order C919s. Buyer power is artificially suppressed domestically.
- **International**: Completely different story. International airlines and lessors have full choice between Airbus and Boeing. They will only consider C919 if it has EASA/FAA certification, a proven safety record, competitive performance, and a global MRO network. Currently, none of these conditions are met.
- Non-Chinese lessors — who finance the majority of the world's aircraft purchases — explicitly refuse to finance C919s without international certification.

### Force 4: Threat of Substitutes — **LOW (for aircraft) / MODERATE (for specific routes)**

- No direct substitute exists for commercial jet aircraft on medium and long-haul routes
- High-speed rail competes on short-haul routes (particularly relevant in China, where HSR is highly developed)
- For COMAC specifically, the A320neo and 737 MAX are not "substitutes" in the traditional sense — they are direct competitors in the same product category
- Electric and hydrogen aircraft are decades away from commercial viability at scale

### Force 5: Competitive Rivalry — **EXTREMELY HIGH**

The narrowbody segment is the world's most competitive and lucrative aerospace market:

| Competitor | Strengths | COMAC's Position vs. Them |
|---|---|---|
| Airbus A320neo | 60%+ market share, EASA/FAA certified, global MRO, 7,000+ orders | Significantly inferior on all dimensions |
| Boeing 737 MAX | 40%+ market share, FAA/EASA certified, global MRO, despite quality issues | Significantly inferior on all dimensions |
| Embraer E-jets | Niche (smaller aircraft), strong in regional market | Different segment, less direct competition |

**Industry Rivalry Assessment**: The duopoly of Airbus and Boeing is deeply entrenched. Both companies have decades of operational data, certified aircraft, global MRO networks, and established customer relationships. COMAC is attempting to break into this duopoly from a position of significant technological and commercial disadvantage.

- Bundled offering: aircraft + pilot training + MRO investment + state bank financing
- Target: Nigeria (CAA already reviewing certification), Indonesia, Malaysia, Pakistan, UAE, Ethiopia
- Build regional MRO hubs in Singapore, Dubai, and Nairobi
- *KPI: 50 C919s in service outside China by 2028*

**Priority 2.3: Governance Reform — Commercial Autonomy**
- Restructure COMAC's internal governance to create a commercially-oriented subsidiary responsible for international sales and marketing
- This subsidiary should operate with greater management autonomy, market-responsive pricing authority, and international talent recruitment
- Consider a partial IPO of a commercial subsidiary to introduce market discipline and transparency
- *KPI: International sales unit established by 2026; first international order (non-BRI) by 2027*

## 12. CONCLUSION

COMAC's strategic situation is a textbook case of the tensions inherent in state-directed industrial policy meeting the realities of global commercial competition. The company has been given an extraordinary mandate — to break the Airbus-Boeing duopoly and establish China as a world-class commercial aircraft manufacturer — along with the financial resources to pursue it. Yet its strategic architecture is fundamentally misaligned with that mandate.

The PESTLE analysis reveals that geopolitical forces (particularly US export controls) represent an existential threat to COMAC's current product architecture. Porter's Five Forces shows that supplier power — driven by foreign component dependency — is the single most dangerous competitive force COMAC faces. The Value Chain analysis identifies two critical breaks (inbound logistics and after-sales service) that prevent international commercialisation. The SWOT analysis confirms that COMAC's greatest strengths (state backing, domestic market) are not the same as its greatest needs (technology independence, international certification). And the Ansoff Matrix counsels a disciplined sequencing: fix the foundation before expanding the footprint.

The recommended action plan is built on a clear strategic logic: **you cannot sell internationally what you cannot produce reliably, certify credibly, or support globally**. COMAC must resist the political temptation to claim global ambitions while its domestic production target is being met at one-fifth of the planned rate. The path to becoming a genuine third force in commercial aviation is real — but it runs through the engineering lab and the certification office, not the air show flight line.

If COMAC executes the phased strategy outlined above — stabilising production, achieving technology independence, securing EASA certification, and building a credible international support ecosystem — it has the resources, the market, and the geopolitical moment to become a genuine competitor to Airbus and Boeing by the mid-2030s. Failure to do so risks permanently confining China's aviation ambitions to its own airspace.
