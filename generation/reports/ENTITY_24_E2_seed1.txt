# **Strategic Case Study: Mercedes-Benz Group AG**
## *Navigating the Triple Squeeze: Electrification, China, and the Cost Structure Crisis (2023–2025)*

---

## **1. Introduction and Case Overview**

Mercedes-Benz Group AG, founded in 1926 and headquartered in Stuttgart, Germany, is one of the world's most iconic luxury automotive brands, symbolised by its three-pointed star. For most of the post-pandemic era, the company appeared invincible: record profits, surging demand for premium vehicles, and a bold strategic commitment to full electrification. Yet by 2024–2025, that picture had darkened dramatically. For the full year 2024, the Mercedes-Benz Group reported revenue of €145.6 billion (down 4.5% year-on-year), EBIT of €13.6 billion (down 30.8% year-on-year), and net profit of €10.4 billion (down 28.4% year-on-year). The deterioration accelerated into 2025: the 2025 financial year was defined by significant disruptions to the global automotive supply chain, with Mercedes-Benz Group reporting a 57% drop in full-year reported profit, with a major factor being a US$1.2 billion hit from global tariff costs, which significantly eroded margins in the core cars division.

This case study diagnoses the strategic situation facing Mercedes-Benz through three analytical lenses — **Porter's Five Forces**, the **Resource-Based View (RBV/VRIN)**, and the **Ansoff Growth Matrix** — and concludes with a coherent set of strategic recommendations.

The central argument is this: Mercedes-Benz faces a **triple squeeze** — a structural demand shock in China, a misaligned electrification strategy that underestimated market readiness, and a cost base exposed by geopolitical trade friction. These are not isolated incidents but interconnected failures of strategic foresight. The company possesses genuine, durable competitive resources, but must deploy them more selectively and with greater operational discipline if it is to restore double-digit margins.

## **2. The Strategic Situation: Evidence and Context**

### 2.1 Financial Deterioration

The financial decline is both sharp and multi-dimensional. The Mercedes-Benz Cars division reported an adjusted EBIT of €8.7 billion, a decline from €14.3 billion in 2023, primarily due to lower sales volumes, particularly in China, negative net pricing, and an unfavourable model mix. In Q4 2024, Mercedes-Benz's Cars division reported an adjusted Return on Sales (RoS) of 8.1%, down from 12.6% in 2023. This matters strategically because the company's own stated target is a double-digit RoS — meaning it is operating below its own strategic threshold. Earnings per share (EPS) followed a similar downward trend in 2024, decreasing by 24.3% to €10.19.

### 2.2 The China Collapse

China is the most critical battleground. Mercedes-Benz Group AG's car sales fell due to a deepening slump in China, where once-dominant Western brands are losing ground to new domestic challengers pushing into the luxury segment. Sales of top-end cars like the S-Class and Maybach sedans and sport utility vehicles fell 14% in 2024. This is particularly damaging because the top-end vehicle (TEV) segment is precisely the segment Mercedes had identified as its primary margin driver. Mercedes-Benz and its German peers experienced a 12% average decline in luxury EV deliveries in the first nine months of 2024. The competitive threat is structural: brands such as BYD, NIO, and Li Auto have successfully captured market share by offering "tech-laden" premium vehicles at aggressive price points.

### 2.3 The Electrification Misjudgement

Mercedes-Benz made an ambitious public commitment to electrification that has since been reversed. In July 2021, the Mercedes-Benz Group announced that "by the end of the 2020s, all new cars sold by the Mercedes-Benz Group (in markets where possible) will be EV-only," but in February 2024 the company revised that plan — the pace of electrification and the transition to all-electric BEVs will be determined by customer and market conditions, so the company will be tactically flexible and will continue to meet the needs for internal combustion engine vehicles well into the 2030s. The reason for this reversal is empirical: through 2024, sales of electrified vehicles were stagnant globally, hovering at just around 20 percent of overall volume, with the company noting "weak EV demand" as one of the reasons last year's sales results were down slightly.

### 2.4 Tariff and Geopolitical Exposure

The macro-environment has compounded internal difficulties. In the United States, a 25% Section 232 tariff raises the cost of German-built Mercedes models significantly if they are imported to what is one of the brand's largest markets. Beijing's retaliatory 10% tariff hit SUVs built in Alabama and exported to China, further dampening demand for American-made vehicles. These tariffs create a double bind: Mercedes is penalised for exporting *to* the US and penalised for exporting *from* the US to China. Persistent tariffs will erode margins, particularly as the company shifts production to lower-cost regions.

## **3. Analytical Framework 1: Porter's Five Forces**

Porter's Five Forces (1979) analyses the structural attractiveness of an industry by examining five competitive forces. Applied to Mercedes-Benz's premium automotive segment, this framework reveals why the company's environment has become structurally more hostile.

### Force 1: Competitive Rivalry — **HIGH and Intensifying**

The luxury and premium automobile industry is very competitive, with major competitors including BMW, Audi, Volvo, and Jaguar. But the rivalry has evolved beyond traditional German peers. Mercedes-Benz's long-term moats — brand equity, R&D capabilities, and a robust global distribution network — remain intact, but the market remains sceptical about its ability to restore profitability. Crucially, rivalry is now cross-dimensional: competitors include BMW, Audi, Porsche, and Tesla across performance, EVs, and software; Mercedes emphasises brand cachet, dealer network, captive finance, and a 140-year heritage as differentiators. The rivalry force is intensifying because the basis of competition is shifting from engineering excellence alone to software capability, digital services, and total cost of ownership — areas where Mercedes is still catching up.

**Strategic implication**: The shift in rivalry's basis means Mercedes cannot rely solely on its historical differentiators. Failure to achieve software parity with Tesla and Chinese OEMs will erode the brand's premium justification among younger, digitally-native buyers.

### Force 2: Threat of New Entrants — **MEDIUM (but rising in EV sub-segment)**

The entry barriers of this industry are very high. Brand equity that existing brands have developed over time is a key barrier. Since these brands have developed and become a symbol of prestige and repute, customers are not willing to try out new brands. Other entry barriers include huge initial investments, extensive marketing and advertisement costs. These structural barriers protect Mercedes in the traditional ICE luxury segment. However, the EV transition has partially reset entry barriers by reducing the mechanical complexity advantage of incumbents. Chinese OEMs — BYD, NIO, Li Auto — have entered the premium EV segment with technology-first propositions that circumvent the traditional brand heritage barrier. This is not a conventional "new entrant" threat but a **platform disruption**: new entrants are competing on a different value dimension (software, connectivity, AI) where heritage counts for less.

**Strategic implication**: Mercedes must treat Chinese EV brands as a distinct competitive category requiring a distinct response — not merely as inferior versions of traditional luxury rivals.

### Force 3: Bargaining Power of Buyers — **HIGH and Growing**

Customers in the premium and luxury automotive segment, where Mercedes-Benz operates, wield considerable power. Direct sales and agency models at Mercedes-Benz increase price transparency across markets, accelerating cross-border comparability and reducing dealer price dispersion. Online configurators make cross-brand comparisons simple, shortening purchase cycles and increasing churn. The evidence of buyer power in practice is stark: Mercedes was forced to offer discounts of up to $15,000 off on high-end EVs (e.g., the AMG EQS) to counter weak 2024 EV sales. Discounting of flagship EVs is a direct transfer of value to buyers and signals an inability to maintain pricing power in the EV segment — a critical weakness given that pricing power is the cornerstone of the luxury business model.

**Strategic implication**: The discounting of premium EVs is not merely a short-term sales tactic; it risks permanently damaging residual values and brand perception. Buyer power must be managed through product desirability and scarcity, not price concessions.

### Force 4: Bargaining Power of Suppliers — **MEDIUM (with specific risks)**

The automotive supply chain involves a limited number of specialised suppliers for high-value components such as battery cells, semiconductors, and rare-earth materials. Steel and aluminium tariffs impact the cost of raw materials used in vehicle production. The battery supply chain is a particular vulnerability: Mercedes has sought to mitigate this through partnerships (e.g., with SilaNano on battery technology and Shell on charging infrastructure), but the dependency on upstream suppliers for critical EV inputs remains a medium-to-high risk. The BEAT26 programme aims to drive sustainable cost reductions across the value chain through supply chain rationalisation, collaborating with suppliers to standardise components, reduce material costs, and adopt modular design principles.

**Strategic implication**: Supplier power is manageable but requires active governance. The BEAT26 programme is the right response, but its success depends on maintaining collaborative relationships rather than purely adversarial cost-cutting.

### Force 5: Threat of Substitutes — **MEDIUM and Growing**

Beyond rival cars, Mercedes faces substitution threats from urban mobility services (ride-hailing, car-sharing), public transport in dense urban markets, and — increasingly — the possibility that software-defined mobility platforms decouple the "driving experience" from vehicle ownership entirely. In China specifically, the rise of integrated mobility ecosystems (WeChat-embedded car services, autonomous taxis) represents a meaningful substitution risk for the urban luxury segment. This is not yet a dominant force but its trajectory is upward.

**Summary of Five Forces**: The overall structural attractiveness of the premium automotive segment is **declining**. Competitive rivalry is intensifying on new dimensions; buyer power is growing; new entrants are disrupting via software; and substitutes are proliferating. This structural deterioration explains why Mercedes's financial performance has weakened even as its brand remains strong — the industry itself is becoming less profitable.

## **4. Analytical Framework 2: Resource-Based View (RBV) and VRIN Analysis**

The Resource-Based View (Barney, 1991; Wernerfelt, 1984) argues that sustained competitive advantage derives from resources that are **Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN)**. This framework complements the Five Forces analysis by shifting focus from the external environment to Mercedes's internal capabilities — identifying which resources can serve as the foundation for strategic recovery.

### Resource 1: Brand Equity — **Valuable, Rare, Inimitable ✓ | Non-substitutable: Partially**

Mercedes-Benz Group AG maintains a strong position in the automotive industry due to several key competitive advantages. Its brand equity, built over a century, signifies quality, luxury, and engineering excellence, contributing to customer loyalty and premium pricing power. As of June 2024, Mercedes-Benz was valued at $22.8 billion USD, ranking fourth globally among automotive brands. This strong brand recognition allows for premium pricing and fosters significant customer loyalty.

However, the brand's VRIN status is under pressure. Mercedes-Benz's performance has been influenced by China's evolving market dynamics, leading to an 11% drop in brand value. This is significant: brand value is not permanently inimitable if it is not actively reinforced. The discounting of premium EVs and the public reversal of the all-electric commitment have introduced inconsistency into the brand narrative, which — if sustained — will erode the "non-substitutable" dimension of this resource. The brand remains VRIN-qualified, but only if strategic decisions are made consistently to protect its luxury positioning.

### Resource 2: Engineering and R&D Capability — **Valuable, Rare, Inimitable ✓ | Non-substitutable: Under Threat**

Mercedes-Benz's engineering heritage is a genuine VRIN resource in the ICE domain. The company's focus on innovation, particularly in EV architecture and autonomous driving, is a key differentiator. Its extensive patent portfolio creates barriers to entry for competitors. The partnership with NVIDIA is evidence of this: Mercedes-Benz and NVIDIA plan to create a revolutionary in-vehicle computing system and AI computing infrastructure, to be rolled out across the fleet of next-generation Mercedes-Benz vehicles from 2024, enabling them with upgradable automated driving functions.

However, the non-substitutability of this resource is threatened in the software domain. Chinese OEMs have demonstrated that software-defined vehicle capabilities can be developed faster by technology-native firms than by traditional OEMs. The MB.OS operating system is Mercedes's response: MB.OS standardises the hardware and software across the infotainment, autonomous driving, comfort, and driving/charging domains. Mercedes-Benz's MB.OS architecture, backed by a €1.3 billion investment announced in late 2023, has emerged as a key strategic initiative. This is the right direction, but the investment level must be assessed against competitors' software spending, particularly from Tesla and Chinese OEMs who invest a far higher proportion of revenue in software.

### Resource 3: Global Manufacturing and Distribution Network — **Valuable, Rare ✓ | Inimitable: Partially | Non-substitutable: Partially**

Mercedes operates a global manufacturing footprint that took decades to build. The Next Level Performance plan includes a 10% reduction in production costs by 2027 and a focus on local-for-local manufacturing, increasing from 60% to 70%. The shift to local-for-local production is strategically sound under conditions of geopolitical fragmentation — it converts a previously global-optimisation resource into a **regional resilience** resource. Mercedes will increasingly have plants serve regional markets instead of minimising cross-border exposure, optimising regional resilience, shortening supply chains, cutting down on complexity, and lowering geopolitical risk. This adaptation preserves the VRIN status of the manufacturing network by making it more relevant to the current environment.

### Resource 4: Top-End Vehicle (TEV) Portfolio — **Valuable, Rare, Inimitable ✓ | Non-substitutable ✓**

This is Mercedes's strongest VRIN resource in the current context. Mercedes-Benz plans to expand and protect the share of Top-End Vehicles (TEV) and aims to leverage the potential of its luxury brands such as G-Class, as the undisputed icon of adventurous luxury; Maybach as the epitome of sophisticated luxury. The "Economics of Desire" framework concentrates on Mercedes-Maybach, Mercedes-AMG and G-Class to lift margins and capture ultra-wealthy buyers, with a target to raise top-end sales share by about 60% versus 2019 by 2026. The Maybach and G-Class nameplates are genuinely non-substitutable: no competitor offers an equivalent combination of heritage, exclusivity, and engineering prestige at these price points. Critically, every second S-Class sold in China is now a Maybach, highlighting the success of the ultra-luxury segment — even as the broader China market contracts, the ultra-high-net-worth segment is proving more resilient.

**RBV Summary**: Mercedes possesses four genuine VRIN resources, but their durability is not guaranteed. Brand equity is eroding under strategic inconsistency; engineering capability faces software disruption; manufacturing networks are being restructured under geopolitical pressure. The TEV portfolio is the most durable VRIN resource and should be the strategic anchor of recovery.

## **5. Analytical Framework 3: Ansoff Growth Matrix**

The Ansoff Matrix (1957) identifies four strategic growth options — Market Penetration, Market Development, Product Development, and Diversification — each carrying a different risk profile. Applied to Mercedes-Benz's current situation, the matrix helps clarify which growth vectors are viable given the company's resource base and competitive environment.

### Quadrant 1: Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets) — **Viable but Limited**

In its core European and US markets, Mercedes has pursued market penetration through the "Next Level Performance" efficiency programme. The most concrete targets include a 10% reduction in production costs by 2027 and a return to a double-digit adjusted RoS margin for the Cars division. The "Next Level Performance" strategy targets operational efficiency and electrification to restore double-digit profit margins amid economic challenges and competition, with the programme aiming to permanently save the company €5 billion over the next three years. In the US, market penetration has shown early signs of success: the US market shows a 9% increase in car sales, contrasting with declines in China, Asia, and Europe.

**Assessment**: Market penetration is necessary but insufficient as a standalone strategy. Cost-cutting restores margins but does not address the structural revenue problem. It is a defensive measure, not a growth engine.

### Quadrant 2: Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets) — **High Priority**

The most significant market development opportunity is the recovery and deepening of the China position. Mercedes-Benz CFO Harald Wilhelm outlines a mid-term strategy to reach 600,000 annual units in China as the brand faces intense competition and margin pressure. The company plans to develop seven China-specific models and deepen its localisation efforts in this market, with its largest R&D hub outside Germany located in China. This is market development in the sense that it requires adapting the product-market fit for a market that has structurally changed — Chinese consumers now expect software-first, tech-laden vehicles, not merely badge-prestige. Market development also extends to emerging markets in Southeast Asia, India, and the Middle East, where rising high-net-worth populations create demand for aspirational luxury brands.

**Assessment**: China market development is high-risk but essential. The risk is that localisation investments may not be sufficient to overcome the structural preference shift toward domestic Chinese brands. The opportunity is that the ultra-luxury segment (Maybach, G-Class) remains less affected by Chinese domestic competition, as no domestic brand has yet replicated this positioning.

### Quadrant 3: Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets) — **Critical and Underway**

Product development is where Mercedes is investing most heavily. By 2025, Mercedes-Benz aims to achieve an xEV (battery and plug-in hybrid) share of 20–22% for its passenger car segment. The new CLA electric sedan is the centrepiece: the CLA EV is set for US/China launch in H2 2025, targeting a 500-mile range. The MB.OS operating system is a product development initiative that transforms the car from a mechanical product into a software platform: from 2025 onwards, certain functionalities can be upgraded over the vehicle lifecycle through the Mercedes me Store, with customers able to access new functions via app, the web, or from inside the car. The revenue potential is significant: by 2025, Mercedes-Benz intends to target €1 billion from software-enabled, features-on-demand.

The new "Mythos" ultra-luxury series represents product development at the top end: Mercedes will launch the Mercedes-AMG PureSpeed, the first in the ultra-luxury brand "Mythos" series, in 2025.

**Assessment**: Product development is the highest-potential growth vector, but it carries execution risk. The MB.OS and CLA EV must succeed simultaneously, and the company must demonstrate that it can be both a luxury brand and a credible software company — a dual identity that requires careful management.

### Quadrant 4: Diversification (New Products, New Markets) — **Selective and Cautious**

In 2025, "Other Revenue" — which encompasses digital subscriptions and "Digital Extras" — accounted for 13% of total group revenue ($19.42 billion). Winning the SDV race is now the primary lever for reclaiming the premium narrative against tech-centric disruptors. This represents a form of diversification: Mercedes is entering the software-as-a-service market, which is structurally different from vehicle manufacturing. The risk is that this diversification dilutes management focus and requires capabilities (software engineering talent, agile development, platform economics) that are culturally foreign to a traditional OEM.

**Assessment**: Diversification into software services is strategically necessary but should be managed as a **related diversification** — tightly linked to the vehicle ownership experience — rather than a standalone technology business. The €1 billion software revenue target is achievable only if the MB.OS platform is genuinely superior to competitors' offerings.

## **6. Synthesis: The Coherent Strategic Diagnosis**

The three frameworks converge on a single diagnosis. **The Five Forces analysis** shows that the luxury automotive industry is structurally deteriorating, with rising rivalry, growing buyer power, and disruptive new entrants. This explains *why* Mercedes's financial performance has weakened. **The RBV/VRIN analysis** shows that Mercedes possesses genuine, durable competitive resources — particularly the TEV portfolio and brand equity — but these resources are under threat from strategic inconsistency and software disruption. This explains *what* Mercedes must protect and build upon. **The Ansoff analysis** shows that the most viable growth paths are product development (MB.OS, new EVs, Mythos series) and targeted market development (China localisation, emerging markets), underpinned by market penetration through cost discipline. This explains *where* Mercedes should allocate resources.

The connecting logic is: because the industry structure is deteriorating (Five Forces), Mercedes cannot compete on volume or average-segment pricing; it must compete on the inimitable resources it possesses (RBV) — specifically the ultra-luxury positioning and software platform — and grow through product development and selective market development (Ansoff), not through broad-based volume recovery.

## **7. Strategic Recommendations**

### Recommendation 1: Anchor Strategy on the TEV Portfolio — *"Luxury First, Always"*

**Rationale**: The TEV portfolio (Maybach, AMG, G-Class) is Mercedes's most durable VRIN resource and is demonstrably more resilient to competitive pressure than the volume segment. Every second S-Class sold in China is now a Maybach — even in a contracting market. The "Economics of Desire" framework already points in this direction, but it must be elevated from a sub-strategy to the *primary* strategic identity. This means accepting lower overall unit volumes in exchange for higher per-unit margins, which is the correct trade-off given the structural deterioration in the mid-market.

**Action**: Accelerate the Mythos series launch; increase Maybach and G-Class production capacity; explicitly communicate to investors and dealers that Mercedes is a luxury house, not a volume OEM. Resist the temptation to use discounting to prop up volume figures, as this destroys the brand equity that is the foundation of the entire strategy.

### Recommendation 2: Resolve the EV Strategy Ambiguity — *"Profitable Electrification, Not Electrification at Any Cost"*

**Rationale**: The reversal of the all-electric commitment created strategic ambiguity that has damaged investor confidence and confused consumers. The company's revised plan states that the pace of electrification will be determined by customer and market conditions, with the company remaining tactically flexible. This is pragmatically correct but strategically incoherent as currently communicated. Mercedes must reframe this not as a retreat from electrification but as a **"profitable electrification"** strategy: only launch EVs where the business case is compelling, and use plug-in hybrids as the bridge technology. Mercedes-Benz's strategy stands out by focusing on the parallel development of fuel, plug-in hybrid, and fully electric vehicles to achieve profitability across all three powertrains, with plug-in hybrid sales growing by 13.7% year-on-year. This hybrid approach is the right one and should be the public narrative.

**Action**: Prioritise the CLA EV launch (targeting 500-mile range) as the proof-of-concept for premium electrification; position the electric G-Class as the halo EV product; expand the PHEV lineup for markets where full BEV adoption is slow. Communicate a clear, stable electrification roadmap that does not promise dates that will be reversed.

### Recommendation 3: Win the Software Race — *"MB.OS as the Platform, Not Just the Feature"*

**Rationale**: Winning the SDV race is now the primary lever for reclaiming the premium narrative against tech-centric disruptors. The €1.3 billion MB.OS investment is necessary but may not be sufficient. Mercedes must treat MB.OS as a **platform business** — one where the vehicle is the hardware and software subscriptions, over-the-air updates, and features-on-demand generate recurring revenue. From 2025 onwards, certain functionalities can be upgraded over the vehicle lifecycle through the Mercedes me Store. This is the correct model, but the catalogue of available features must be genuinely compelling — not merely incremental improvements to existing functions.

**Action**: Increase software R&D investment to at least match the proportion of revenue invested by Tesla; recruit top-tier software talent; consider a strategic partnership or acquisition in the AI/autonomous driving space to accelerate capability building. The NVIDIA partnership is a strong foundation — it should be deepened, not treated as a one-time announcement.

### Recommendation 4: Restructure the China Strategy — *"Localise Deeply or Compete Selectively"*

**Rationale**: Germany's automakers are struggling in the world's biggest auto market, where local manufacturers led by BYD Co. are taking over. Mercedes cannot win a volume war against domestic Chinese OEMs on their home turf. The correct strategy is to compete selectively in the segments where Chinese brands cannot yet match Mercedes: ultra-luxury (Maybach), iconic off-road (G-Class), and high-performance (AMG). Simultaneously, Mercedes must localise its mid-range products far more aggressively — the company plans to develop seven China-specific models and has its largest R&D hub outside Germany located in China. This dual-track approach — ultra-luxury defence + localised mid-range offence — is more coherent than either full retreat or undifferentiated volume competition.

**Action**: Establish a semi-autonomous China product team with full authority to design, engineer, and market China-specific vehicles; reduce the cost base at Beijing Benz by 20% (as already targeted); invest in partnerships with Chinese technology companies to embed locally-preferred software features (WeChat, Baidu maps, domestic AI assistants) into the MB.OS platform.

### Recommendation 5: Restructure the Cost Base Permanently — *"BEAT26 as Structural Reform, Not Crisis Management"*

**Rationale**: Mercedes' efficiency measures have already generated more than €3.5 billion in positive EBIT contribution in 2025, due to material cost savings and operational efficiency. This demonstrates that the restructuring is working, but the risk is that cost-cutting is treated as a temporary crisis response rather than a permanent reconfiguration of the operating model. The BEAT26 programme aims to drive sustainable cost reductions across the value chain through supply chain rationalisation, collaborating with suppliers to standardise components, reduce material costs, and adopt modular design principles.

**Action**: Institutionalise the BEAT26 disciplines as permanent operating principles; shift to modular vehicle architecture that allows multiple powertrains (BEV, PHEV, ICE) on the same platform to reduce R&D duplication; increase local-for-local manufacturing from 60% to 70% as targeted, which simultaneously reduces tariff exposure and supply chain complexity.

## **8. Conclusion**

Mercedes-Benz faces a strategic inflection point of genuine severity. The financial deterioration — revenue down 4.5%, EBIT down 30.8%, and net profit down 28.4% in 2024 alone — is not merely cyclical. It reflects structural forces: a deteriorating industry environment (Five Forces), competitive resources under threat (RBV), and a growth strategy that has been reactive rather than proactive (Ansoff).

The path forward is not to recover the 2021–2022 volume model but to complete the transition to a **luxury-first, software-enabled, regionally-resilient** business. Mercedes-Benz possesses the brand equity, engineering heritage, and TEV portfolio to make this transition — but only if strategic decisions are made with consistency and discipline. The greatest risk is not competition from BYD or Tesla; it is strategic drift — the temptation to chase volume with discounts, reverse commitments under pressure, and invest in too many directions simultaneously.

The three frameworks are not merely academic tools; they form a diagnostic chain. The Five Forces explain the *context* of pressure; the RBV identifies the *resources* that can resist it; the Ansoff Matrix defines the *direction* of investment. Together, they point to a strategy that is simultaneously more focused (luxury, TEV, software) and more disciplined (cost structure, China selectivity, EV realism) than what Mercedes has pursued in recent years. Executed with conviction, it offers a credible route back to the double-digit margins that define a true luxury house.
