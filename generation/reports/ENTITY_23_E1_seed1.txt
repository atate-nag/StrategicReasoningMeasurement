# STRATEGIC CASE STUDY: MANCHESTER UNITED FC
## *"The Fallen Giant: Diagnosing Structural Decline and Charting a Path to Renewal"*

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Manchester United Football Club sits at a strategic inflection point. Once the undisputed commercial and competitive hegemon of English football, the club has experienced a decade-long divergence between its formidable brand assets and deteriorating on-pitch performance — a divergence now threatening to erode the very commercial foundations that made it exceptional. This case study applies a structured analytical chain — PESTEL → Porter's Five Forces → VRIO → Bowman's Strategy Clock → Ansoff Matrix — to diagnose the root causes of United's strategic predicament. The analysis argues that the crisis is not merely managerial or sporting, but **structural**: the product of leveraged ownership, governance fragmentation, and a failure to reinvest commercial revenues into a coherent sporting model. A sequenced set of strategic recommendations follows.

---

## SECTION 1: BACKGROUND AND STRATEGIC CONTEXT

### 1.1 The Club and Its Heritage

Manchester United is one of the most powerful commercial engines in world football, consistently ranking among the most valuable clubs globally. Through 147 years of heritage, the club has won 69 trophies, including a record 20 English league titles, and claims a global community of 1.1 billion fans and followers. Independent valuations place the club's enterprise value in the $5–6 billion range alongside Real Madrid. The commercial model leverages heritage as a trust signal for multinational brands seeking cross-market resonance. Adidas extended its partnership in 2023 in a deal worth up to £900 million over 10 years, underscoring durability and demand.

Yet beneath this commercial architecture lies an increasingly acute strategic problem. Manchester United's 2023–24 season ended with confirmation of their worst-ever Premier League finish — eighth place — despite a final-day win against Brighton. Their 14 league defeats in a single season was their highest since the 1989–90 campaign.

### 1.2 The Strategic Situation: A Dual Crisis

The situation facing Manchester United can be characterised as a **dual crisis**: a *sporting crisis* (chronic underperformance relative to stated ambitions and peer clubs) intersecting with a *financial-structural crisis* (debt burden, PSR vulnerability, and governance fragmentation). These two crises are mutually reinforcing, creating a strategic trap from which escape requires coordinated action across multiple fronts simultaneously.

On 20 February 2024, Sir Jim Ratcliffe and his INEOS Group acquired a minority stake in the club, with the transaction including responsibility for football operations and a commitment to infrastructure investment. The Glazers currently maintain a 51% stake in Manchester United, following Sir Jim Ratcliffe's acquisition of a minority holding through INEOS. The resulting dual-principal structure — INEOS controlling sporting decisions while the Glazers retain financial authority — is a governance architecture with inherent strategic tension, as this analysis will demonstrate.

## SECTION 2: MACRO-ENVIRONMENTAL ANALYSIS (PESTEL)

### 2.1 Political

The Premier League operates under increasing regulatory scrutiny. The UK government's Football Governance Bill proposes an independent regulator with powers over ownership structures and financial sustainability. For United, this is directly relevant: the Glazer ownership model — built on a leveraged buyout that placed acquisition debt onto the club's balance sheet — is precisely the structure proposed regulations seek to constrain.

### 2.2 Economic

Macroeconomic pressures affect multiple revenue streams. A parent taking their child will face significant costs, and all but one Premier League club raised ticket prices ahead of the 2024–25 season. United's mid-season price hike provoked direct fan protest: the Manchester United Supporters' Trust wrote an open letter to club CEO Omar Berrada calling the mid-season price hikes — a first in the club's history — "a disgrace," with increases of as much as 40% for adults. At the macro level, however, the Premier League's new broadcast rights deal provides financial certainty for clubs throughout professional football until at least 2029, and the global reach of the Premier League continues to grow, with international broadcasting deals increasing approximately 23% in the new cycle.

### 2.3 Social

The new leadership inherited an institution defined by two parallel realities: on one side, Manchester United remained a global trophy asset, operating one of the most powerful commercial engines in world football; on the other, a decade of on-pitch decline had severely damaged the club's sporting credibility. Fan alienation has become a strategic risk: MUST called on Ratcliffe to "end the war on season ticket holders," describing on-field performances "amongst the worst in decades." An alienated fanbase undermines the authenticity of the brand proposition that sponsors pay a premium to associate with.

### 2.4 Technological

Technology is reshaping football's value chain. Data analytics and sports science are now core competitive capabilities in elite talent identification. Clubs such as Brighton and Brentford have demonstrated that analytically-driven recruitment models can deliver competitive performance at a fraction of the cost of traditional "marquee signing" strategies. Snapdragon's ROI calculation rests on the proposition that 1 billion-plus United fans, a jersey sold every 15 seconds, and a match at Old Trafford generate broadcast exposure equivalent to a Super Bowl advertisement — illustrating the digital and broadcast amplification potential of United's global platform, which remains significantly under-monetised.

### 2.5 Legal (PSR Regulation)

United's most acute legal-regulatory risk is the Premier League's Profit and Sustainability Rules. In September 2024, the club announced losses of £113.2 million for the 2023–24 financial year, following losses of £28.7 million and £115.5 million in the two preceding seasons. Premier League clubs were assessed over a three-year period ending 30 June 2024, during which clubs were only allowed to lose £105 million. Manchester United said they were "in danger" of breaching the Premier League's PSR — an admission made in a letter sent to fans groups in response to concerns over rising ticket prices. The club ultimately navigated this threshold through asset sales and cost restructuring, but the proximity to breach illustrates the fragility of the current financial model.

## SECTION 3: INDUSTRY ANALYSIS — PORTER'S FIVE FORCES

### 3.1 Threat of New Entrants — LOW to MODERATE

Entry barriers in elite football are structurally high. However, the threat is not zero: Newcastle's ownership has supported major investment in quality players, culminating in the 2024–25 season when their squad valuation overtook Manchester United's. State-backed ownership models represent a form of "new entry" into the elite competitive tier that bypasses traditional barriers through capital injection — fundamentally altering the competitive landscape.

### 3.2 Bargaining Power of Suppliers (Players) — HIGH

The primary "suppliers" in football are elite players and their agents. Players' wages in the Premier League reached £1.2 billion, accounting for 62% of revenue, reflecting the extraordinary bargaining power of top talent. For United specifically, this power is acutely felt: the club's wage bill reached £365 million in 2023–24, yet on-pitch output did not reflect this expenditure — representing a severe misallocation of scarce resources.

### 3.3 Bargaining Power of Buyers (Fans & Sponsors) — LOW to MODERATE

Fans have limited individual bargaining power, but collective fan action represents a meaningful check on pricing power, as the 2024–25 controversy demonstrated. Sponsors hold moderate bargaining power: in 2023–24, the club extended its long-standing partnership with Adidas for another decade, through to 2035, worth £90 million annually, subject to a £10 million reduction if United fail to qualify for the Champions League. This clause directly links commercial income to sporting performance — a critical strategic dependency.

### 3.4 Competitive Rivalry — VERY HIGH

Other challengers, such as Brighton and Brentford, have sought to build models based on the identification, development, and trading of talent, with reinvestment sustaining competitiveness. In 2024, Sir Jim Ratcliffe and INEOS now hold control of football operations, but the competitive landscape has been fundamentally altered by owners willing to absorb losses at a scale that a leveraged, debt-burdened club cannot match.

**Porter's Five Forces Summary:** The industry structure is characterised by very high competitive rivalry and supplier power, with moderate buyer and substitution threats. For a club with structural financial constraints, this is an unfavourable configuration that demands differentiation through sustainable competitive advantages rather than financial muscle alone.

## SECTION 4: INTERNAL ANALYSIS — THE VRIO FRAMEWORK

The VRIO framework (Barney, 1991) evaluates resources and capabilities against four criteria: **V**alue, **R**arity, **I**nimitability, and **O**rganisation.

### 4.1 Global Brand and Fan Community → *Sustained Competitive Advantage (Under-Exploited)*

Through 147 years of heritage, Manchester United has won 69 trophies, enabling the development of what the club believes is one of the world's leading sports brands and a global community of 1.1 billion fans and followers. This resource is **valuable** (unmatched commercial platform), **rare** (only Real Madrid and Barcelona possess comparable reach), and **inimitable** (built over a century of cultural moments that cannot be replicated). However, it is only **partially organised**: commercial income of £333 million represents respectable year-on-year growth, but zoom out and the picture is more troubling — the shine has come off the United badge somewhat in recent years. The gap between 1.1 billion followers and the depth of commercial engagement represents an organisational failure to fully exploit this resource.

**Verdict: Sustained competitive advantage — but under-exploited.**

### 4.2 Commercial Infrastructure and Sponsorship Portfolio → *Temporary Advantage, Performance-Contingent*

Of United's £333 million total commercial revenue, sponsorship contributed more than half at £188 million, up from £178 million the year before. Qualcomm's Snapdragon front-of-shirt sponsorship starting 2024–25 aligns the badge with cutting-edge mobile ecosystems, enhancing global visibility and tech relevance. This infrastructure is valuable and partially rare, but its sustainability is contingent on on-pitch performance, as the Adidas penalty clause illustrates.

**Verdict: Temporary competitive advantage, increasingly contingent on sporting performance.**

### 4.3 Financial Structure (Debt Burden) → *Competitive Disadvantage*

Over half of Premier League debt is at just three clubs — Everton £1.0 billion, Tottenham £851 million, and Manchester United £547 million — with United's burden representing the lingering effects of the Glazers' leveraged buyout. The Manchester United Supporters' Trust's analysis of the numbers indicates that debt interest payments will eventually increase to a staggering £50 million per year. This is not a resource — it is a structural drain on investment capacity.

**Verdict: Competitive disadvantage — the most significant internal constraint on strategic options.**

### 4.4 Youth Academy → *Competitive Advantage Requiring Greater Commitment*

The academy has historically produced world-class talent (Beckham, Scholes, Giggs, Rashford, Mainoo, Garnacho) and represents a cost-efficient pathway to first-team quality. It is valuable, moderately rare, and partially inimitable through its heritage and recruitment reputation. However, its output has been inconsistent and has not been systematically leveraged as a strategic priority. **Verdict: Competitive advantage — requires greater organisational commitment.**

### 4.5 Old Trafford Infrastructure → *Potential Sustained Advantage, Currently Unrealised*

Matchday revenue climbed £23 million to £160 million in 2024–25, driven by five extra home fixtures. The stadium is a valuable, rare, and inimitable asset. However, it is ageing and was excluded from UEFA Euro 2028 hosting consideration. In March 2025, Manchester United confirmed they are planning to build a new 100,000-seat stadium next to the current Old Trafford. Sir Jim Ratcliffe has publicly said that he wants to build a "Wembley of the North" for the Red Devils.

**Verdict: Potential sustained advantage — currently unrealised due to organisational failure to invest.**

## SECTION 5: COMPETITIVE POSITIONING — BOWMAN'S STRATEGY CLOCK

Bowman's Strategy Clock maps organisations on two dimensions: **perceived value** (to customers and stakeholders) and **price**. It identifies eight competitive positions, of which positions 1–5 are broadly sustainable.

Historically, Manchester United occupied **Position 5: Focused Differentiation** — delivering the highest perceived value (trophy-winning football, iconic players, global brand experience) at premium prices. This is the most defensible and value-creating position in a premium market.

The club's current position has drifted toward **Position 6: Increased Price / Standard Value** — maintaining and in some cases increasing premium pricing while delivering declining perceived value. The mid-season price hikes — as much as 40% for adults — represent a first in the club's history, implemented in the same season as the club's worst-ever Premier League finish. This is identified by Bowman as an unstable and ultimately unsustainable position: it erodes customer loyalty, damages brand equity, and invites competitive substitution.

The strategic imperative is therefore to **return to Position 5** — through genuine value restoration (on-pitch performance, fan experience, and stadium quality) rather than through price reduction, which would damage the premium brand positioning that underpins commercial revenue.

## SECTION 6: STRATEGIC DIAGNOSIS — THE CORE PROBLEM

The analytical frameworks converge on a coherent diagnosis:

> **Manchester United is caught in a value destruction spiral driven by structural financial constraints, governance fragmentation, and a decade-long failure to build a coherent sporting model — all of which are now threatening to erode the commercial assets that constitute the club's only remaining source of sustainable competitive advantage.**

The causal chain is explicit:

```
Glazer LBO (2005)
    → £547m net debt + rising interest payments
        → Chronic underinvestment in squad, infrastructure, and football operations
            → Managerial instability (7 permanent managers in 12 years post-Ferguson)
                → Absence of coherent sporting identity
                    → Deteriorating on-pitch performance (8th place, 2023-24)
                        → Loss of Champions League revenue
                            → PSR pressure → further investment constraint
                                → Ticket price hikes → fan alienation
                                    → Brand erosion → commercial partner attrition risk
                                        → Further revenue decline → closes the spiral
```

An examination of 2023–24 domestic league finishes illustrates the close alignment between sporting performance and financial valuation — seven of the ten most valuable clubs secured top-two positions in their respective leagues, underscoring the premium accorded to consistent success. Manchester United's eighth-place Premier League finish stands out as an anomaly: despite sub-par on-field results, the club retains its valuation, but this cannot be sustained indefinitely.

INEOS's arrival introduced a leaner operating model, and that shift has been visible and, at times, contentious. Following an initial round of job losses, further redundancies were confirmed in early 2024. Manchester United expects to make cost savings of between £40 million and £45 million a year due to its restructuring. While necessary, cost-cutting alone cannot resolve a structural problem rooted in the absence of investment.

The INEOS intervention is a necessary but insufficient condition for strategic renewal. The dual-principal governance structure — INEOS controlling football operations while the Glazers retain financial authority — creates a strategic paradox: the party with operational accountability lacks the financial authority to fully fund the solution.

## SECTION 7: STRATEGIC OPTIONS — ANSOFF MATRIX

The Ansoff Matrix structures growth options along two dimensions: existing vs. new products, and existing vs. new markets.

| | **Existing Markets** | **New Markets** |

| **Existing Product** | **Option A:** Market Penetration — deepen competitive performance in Premier League/Champions League | **Option B:** Market Development — monetise global fan base in high-growth markets (Asia, Americas, Africa) |
| **New Product** | **Option C:** Product Development — transform Old Trafford precinct into a 365-day commercial destination | **Option D:** Diversification — adjacent entertainment/media businesses |

**Option A** is the most direct route to restoring commercial value but requires significant upfront investment that the current financial structure constrains. **Option B** is a medium-risk parallel track that reduces dependence on domestic performance. **Option C** is the long-term structural investment that transforms the revenue ceiling. **Option D** is not recommended in the current period — management attention must remain focused on the core crisis.

**Recommended Approach:** Options A, B, and C in sequence and, where possible, in parallel.

## SECTION 8: STRATEGIC RECOMMENDATIONS

### IMMEDIATE TERM (0–18 months): Stabilise, Restructure, and Rebuild Trust

---

**Recommendation 1: Resolve the Governance Paradox**

The dual-principal ownership structure is the single most important strategic constraint. INEOS must negotiate either (a) a formal framework agreement with the Glazers granting INEOS full operational and financial authority over football operations, with clear accountability metrics; or (b) accelerate the path to majority ownership. The Glazer family is reportedly considering a peak-price sale of their majority stake, with discussions remaining internal and no formal sale process underway. INEOS should actively facilitate this process.

*Rationale:* The VRIO analysis identifies the brand and commercial infrastructure as the club's core competitive advantages, but both are being eroded by the inability to invest in the on-pitch product. The governance structure is the proximate cause of this inability. All other recommendations are contingent on this one.

---

**Recommendation 2: Execute the Wage Bill Reset Decisively**

Manchester United are preparing a massive financial reset by offloading several senior stars this summer, aiming to slash more than £1 million per week from the payroll to fund a squad overhaul. Casemiro's exit at the end of the season has been confirmed, while Jadon Sancho is tipped to leave permanently. This process must be executed swiftly and completely, with freed capital ring-fenced for targeted reinvestment in positions identified by Amorim's coaching staff — particularly central midfield and wing-back roles for the 3-4-3 system. Manchester United's wage bill fell to £313 million in 2024–25, down from £365 million the previous year, partly driven by the absence of Champions League participation. This trajectory must continue, with the wage-to-revenue ratio reduced toward the UEFA-recommended 70% threshold.

---

**Recommendation 3: Rebuild the Fan Social Contract**

Freeze ticket prices for at least two seasons. Establish a formal Fan Advisory Board with genuine (not merely consultative) input into pricing and stadium experience decisions. MUST's statement advised: "By freezing prices and avoiding major disruptive policy changes now, you have the opportunity to signal that you value the unique role of fans in Manchester United's success. Together, we can build a stronger future for the club. The consequences of ignoring this are significant, but the benefits of getting it right are immeasurable."

*Rationale:* The Bowman analysis identifies the club's drift toward Position 6 (high price, declining value) as strategically unsustainable. Fan alienation threatens the authentic supporter culture that constitutes a core element of the brand proposition sponsors pay to associate with.

### MEDIUM TERM (18 months–4 years): Reinvest in the Sporting Model

---

**Recommendation 4: Commit to a Three-Year Coaching Cycle Under Amorim**

Ruben Amorim has a distinct philosophy, and recent signings have been smarter. With a structured 3-4-3 formation, a disciplined build-up style, and an emphasis on versatile, game-aware players, Amorim has the potential to bring much-needed stability and tactical clarity to Old Trafford. Ownership must make an explicit, public commitment to a minimum three-year sporting project, aligning all transfer activity to the requirements of his system. Empower the Director of Football (Jason Wilcox) to build a data-driven recruitment infrastructure capable of identifying value in non-marquee markets, replicating the model that has enabled Brighton and Brentford to compete above their financial weight.

*Rationale:* The PESTEL analysis identifies data analytics as a core competitive capability. United's historical reliance on expensive marquee signings — many of which have failed to deliver — represents a strategic misallocation of scarce resources.

---

**Recommendation 5: Prioritise Champions League Return as the Commercial Anchor**

The strategic importance of Champions League participation cannot be overstated. The Adidas deal's £10 million penalty clause for non-qualification, combined with the direct broadcasting distributions from UEFA competition, means that Champions League football is the mechanism through which the brand asset is continuously renewed. Arsenal, Manchester City, Manchester United, and Aston Villa have secured top-five finishes and will all play in the UEFA Champions League next season — a return that must now be consolidated and built upon. All sporting investment decisions should be evaluated against a single criterion: does this improve the probability of a sustained top-four Premier League finish?

---

**Recommendation 6: Accelerate Digital Fan Monetisation**

Launch a structured programme to convert the 1.1 billion global followers into active commercial relationships. This should include: (a) a tiered global digital membership scheme replacing the current fragmented fan engagement model; (b) investment in localised content production for key growth markets (India, Indonesia, Nigeria, USA); (c) a direct-to-consumer streaming offering for non-broadcast content. Manchester United are searched approximately 65.2 million times around the world each month, ranking first in the EPL fanbase size and engagement index, and gaining 33.9% of all EPL team digital engagement across combined platforms. Even a modest conversion of this passive following into paid digital membership would be transformative at the current scale of operations.

*Rationale:* The VRIO analysis identifies the global fan base as valuable, rare, and inimitable — but currently under-organised. This recommendation directly addresses the organisational gap.

### LONG TERM (4–10 years): Structural Transformation

---

**Recommendation 7: Deliver the New Stadium as a Strategic Asset**

Manchester United confirmed in March 2025 that they are planning to build a new 100,000-seat stadium next to the current Old Trafford. A survey of season ticket holders revealed that 52% of the club's core fanbase would prefer a new stadium over a renovated Old Trafford. Structure the financing to avoid repeating the Glazer-era error of loading acquisition debt onto the club — explore public-private partnership models with Greater Manchester Combined Authority, given the regional regeneration benefits. An Old Trafford Regeneration Task Force was set up by Ratcliffe in March 2024, featuring Sebastian Coe, former United captain Gary Neville, and Manchester Mayor Andy Burnham. This collaborative governance model should be deepened and formalised.

*Rationale:* A new stadium directly increases matchday revenue, unlocks new hospitality tiers, and requalifies the club for major tournament hosting. The VRIO analysis shows Old Trafford is a valuable, rare, and inimitable resource — the failure is in the "Organised" dimension, which a new stadium directly addresses.

**Recommendation 8: Pursue Full Ownership Clarity**

Facilitate the transition to a single, accountable ownership structure. The Glazers' leveraged buyout placed a massive burden on the club's finances — raising £600 million in loans secured against the club's assets, bringing £60 million in annual repayment obligations. The Glazers chose to use revenue generated by Manchester United itself to service this debt, meaning syphoning away millions every season. This structural burden must be resolved for the club to compete on equal terms. The Football Governance Bill's proposed independent regulator will increasingly scrutinise such ownership structures; proactive governance reform is preferable to regulatory compulsion.

## SECTION 10: CONCLUSION

Manchester United's strategic situation is serious but not irrecoverable. The club retains extraordinary assets — a global brand of rare depth, a commercial infrastructure generating over £333 million annually, a historic academy, and a fanbase of 1.1 billion that no competitor can replicate. These are the foundations of a sustainable competitive advantage. But they are being eroded by a structural financial burden, a governance architecture that prevents decisive action, and a decade of sporting underperformance that has begun to damage the commercial foundations themselves.

The analytical chain is explicit: **governance dysfunction → financial constraint → sporting decline → commercial erosion → further constraint**. Breaking this chain requires action at the governance level first, followed by coordinated sporting and commercial investment, and anchored by a long-term infrastructure commitment that transforms the club's revenue-generating capacity.

The appointment of Ruben Amorim and the arrival of INEOS represent the beginning of a genuine strategic transition. But beginnings are not sufficient. The eight recommendations in this case study constitute a coherent, sequenced plan — one that respects the club's real constraints, builds on its genuine competitive advantages, and charts a credible path from fallen giant to renewed force. Although Manchester United's on-pitch decline has eroded certain revenue sources and competitive standing, the club's historic legacy, expansive fan base, and robust commercial infrastructure continue to underpin its position as a "blue-chip" football asset. The strategic logic of renewal is available — what is required is the governance clarity, the financial discipline, and the institutional courage to execute it.
