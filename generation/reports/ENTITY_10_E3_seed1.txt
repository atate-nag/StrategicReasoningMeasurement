# COMAC AND THE C919: NAVIGATING GEOPOLITICAL TURBULENCE IN COMMERCIAL AEROSPACE

## A Strategic Case Study

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

China's Commercial Aircraft Corporation (COMAC) occupies a uniquely paradoxical strategic position: it is simultaneously a national champion endowed with virtually unlimited state capital, and a technologically constrained manufacturer unable to fill its own order book. This case study examines COMAC's strategic situation through Porter's Five Forces, a Resource-Based View (RBV)/Dynamic Capabilities analysis, a SWOT framework, and Ansoff's Growth Matrix. The analysis reveals that COMAC's core strategic problem is not financial — it is a **technology-dependency trap** in which state-backed demand creation has outpaced indigenous capability development. The case concludes with a sequenced, evidence-based action plan.

---

## 1. CASE BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### 1.1 The Organisation

COMAC's state involvement manifests through SASAC's (State-owned Assets Supervision and Administration Commission) oversight of strategic decisions, governance, and resource allocation, positioning COMAC as a national champion to foster self-reliance in commercial aviation amid geopolitical constraints on foreign technology access. COMAC was established in 2008 and produces three commercial aircraft programmes: the ARJ21/C909 regional jet, the C919 narrow-body, and the C929 wide-body under development.

### 1.2 The Strategic Situation

The global aviation industry is dominated by Boeing and Airbus, together accounting for over 93% of the single-aisle aircraft segment. This duopoly has set the standards for innovation, safety, and service infrastructure. COMAC's C919 was designed to challenge this duopoly directly by targeting the world's fastest-growing aviation market — China itself.

The C919, China's first domestically developed jet built to international airworthiness standards, entered commercial service in May 2023 with China Eastern Airlines. Since then, it has operated exclusively with Chinese airlines, apart from a limited charter appearance in Hong Kong in 2024.

The strategic problem crystallised rapidly after commercial entry: production targets have been serially missed, Western certification has proved elusive, and escalating US-China technology decoupling has exposed a critical vulnerability at the heart of the programme.

## 2. INDUSTRY ANALYSIS: PORTER'S FIVE FORCES

Porter's Five Forces framework (1980) is applied to assess the structural attractiveness of the commercial narrow-body aircraft manufacturing industry and COMAC's position within it. The purpose is to explain *why* the industry is structurally hostile to new entrants and what this means for COMAC's competitive viability.

### Force 1: Threat of New Entrants — **Very Low**

Commercial aircraft manufacturing presents some of the highest barriers to entry of any industry. Capital requirements are enormous, certification timelines span decades, and learning-curve effects are profound. COMAC's struggle to scale production reflects challenges that took Boeing and Airbus decades to overcome. Complex aerospace supply chains, quality control at volume, and workforce training all require sustained investment and operational maturity. This evidence warrants the conclusion that even a state-backed entrant with effectively unlimited capital — as COMAC is — faces structural barriers that money alone cannot dissolve. The implication is that COMAC's difficulties are not a temporary ramp-up problem but a structural feature of the industry.

### Force 2: Bargaining Power of Suppliers — **High (and Rising)**

The core issue lies in COMAC's reliance on foreign suppliers for 50–60% of the C919's components. The aircraft depends on Western technology, including CFM International's LEAP-1C turbofan engines, produced by a GE Aerospace and Safran joint venture. The concentration of supply in a single Western-controlled engine source gives those suppliers extraordinary leverage. This leverage is not merely commercial — it is geopolitical. The CFM International LEAP-1C engine alone represents roughly 30% of total aircraft cost. Because COMAC has no certified substitute engine, the supplier's bargaining power is near-absolute: when the US suspended export licences, production stopped. This directly explains why COMAC's delivery shortfalls are structurally rooted in supplier dependency rather than in manufacturing incompetence per se.

### Force 3: Bargaining Power of Buyers — **Moderate (Domestically Constrained)**

Within China, buyer power is structurally suppressed by government policy. There are clear instructions by the Chinese government to order less from Western manufacturers. COMAC has amassed more than 1,000 orders for the C919 from Chinese airlines and lessors. This state-directed demand means Chinese carriers — themselves largely state-owned — have limited ability to exercise genuine buyer power. However, this is a double-edged dynamic: it creates an artificially captive market that does not discipline COMAC to improve quality or reduce cost in the way a genuinely competitive market would. Internationally, buyer power is high: Western carriers have been slow to adopt COMAC aircraft, citing concerns about certification, reliability, and long-term maintenance. This contrast between a captive domestic market and a sceptical international one is a key structural tension in COMAC's strategy.

### Force 4: Threat of Substitutes — **Low to Moderate**

There is no near-term substitute technology for narrow-body commercial jets. High-speed rail competes on select short-haul domestic routes in China, but cannot substitute for medium-range aviation. The more relevant substitute threat is *within* the aircraft category: airlines can substitute the C919 with the A320neo or 737 MAX. Airbus plans to boost A320 production to 75 aircraft monthly by 2027, and its order book stands at a record 8,769 jets, of which narrowbody aircraft make up 89%. This means the substitutes (Airbus and Boeing products) are not only available but are being produced at scale with deep aftermarket ecosystems, making the switching cost from C919 back to Western aircraft low for any airline not under government instruction.

### Force 5: Competitive Rivalry — **Intense (Duopoly with High Exit Barriers)**

The duopoly structure creates intense rivalry precisely because there are only two established players defending enormous market positions. Together, Boeing and Airbus account for roughly 80% of the active commercial aircraft fleet worldwide. Both incumbents have decades of accumulated manufacturing know-how, global MRO networks, and certified supply chains that create powerful switching costs for airlines. COMAC's entry into this rivalry is complicated by the fact that it is perceived by the incumbents not merely as a commercial competitor but as a geopolitically motivated state enterprise, intensifying the competitive response.

**Five Forces Summary:** The industry is structurally hostile to new entrants. The one structural advantage COMAC possesses — captive domestic demand — is a product of state intervention rather than competitive merit. This finding connects directly to the RBV analysis: COMAC must convert state-granted advantages into genuine, inimitable capabilities or remain perpetually vulnerable.

## 3. INTERNAL ANALYSIS: RESOURCE-BASED VIEW AND DYNAMIC CAPABILITIES

The Resource-Based View (Barney, 1991) holds that sustained competitive advantage derives from resources that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN). Teece et al.'s (1997) Dynamic Capabilities framework extends this by asking whether a firm can *sense, seize, and reconfigure* resources as the environment changes. Applied to COMAC, this framework reveals a stark gap between the resources COMAC holds and those it needs.

### 3.1 COMAC's Resources: An Audit

**Financial Resources (Valuable, but not Rare or Inimitable):**
The most significant recent financial event was the USD 6.2 billion capital injection from eight state-owned shareholders in late 2024, lifting registered capital from CNY 50.1 billion to CNY 94.1 billion. This injection is valuable — it funds production ramp-up and R&D — but it is not a source of competitive advantage because Boeing and Airbus also have access to deep capital markets. Financial resources are necessary but not sufficient for advantage.

**State-Backed Market Access (Valuable and Rare, but Potentially Inimitable by Rivals):**
Financial support includes extensive subsidies, with estimates indicating up to $72 billion directed toward development of programs like the C919, supplemented by corporate bonds totalling RMB 55.5 billion. The combination of captive domestic demand and state subsidy represents a rare resource. However, the WTO-incompatibility of these subsidies creates a long-term strategic liability: China's only subsidy notification since joining the WTO in 2001 was submitted in 2006 and did not include a single subsidy program administered by sub-central governments. This evidence warrants the inference that COMAC's subsidy advantage is legally fragile and could face international challenge if COMAC ever attempts to compete in Western markets.

**Technological Capabilities (Weak — the Critical Gap):**
COMAC is far behind, and it is not catching up. In fact, the gap is growing, as Western aerospace firms are already focused on developing the technologies of tomorrow, such as supersonics, while China is still struggling to master the technologies of today. This is the most strategically significant finding from the RBV analysis: COMAC's technological capabilities fail the VRIN test entirely. They are neither rare (Western firms possess superior versions), nor inimitable (they depend on licensed or imported components), nor non-substitutable (the C919 can be substituted by A320neo or 737 MAX). The inescapable conclusion is that COMAC currently possesses no technology-based competitive advantage.

**Manufacturing Capability (Developing, but Immature):**
COMAC had shipped just 13 of its flagship C919 aircraft in 2024 despite lofty targets. This performance — fewer than one aircraft per month — against an order book of over 1,000 units demonstrates that manufacturing capability has not yet reached the operational maturity required for competitive viability. The gap between order intake and delivery capacity is itself a strategic vulnerability: it erodes airline confidence and gives rivals time to lock in long-term commitments.

### 3.2 Dynamic Capabilities Assessment

**Sensing:** COMAC has correctly identified the strategic imperative. In 2024, COMAC was working on a plan to become more self-sufficient in terms of suppliers, with a long-term goal of replacing all US-export-controlled components, because American export restrictions were affecting the ability of COMAC to boost jet output. This sensing capability — recognising the dependency risk — is present.

**Seizing:** The response has been to invest in indigenous engine development. The development of the ACAE CJ-1000A reflects China's strategic imperative to attain self-sufficiency in aero-engine technology, a domain long dominated by Western manufacturers, as articulated in national industrial policies. Launched under the "Made in China 2025" framework in 2015, this initiative set explicit targets for 70% domestic content in core aviation components by 2025. However, the seizing capability is slow: the CJ-1000A has not yet achieved commercial certification.

**Reconfiguring:** This is where COMAC's dynamic capability is weakest. The moderate scenario — most likely — is that the CJ-1000A achieves certification but faces reliability concerns and higher maintenance costs compared to Western alternatives. Chinese airlines use it primarily on domestic routes for strategic reasons, while international routes and export customers prefer Western-powered aircraft. This evidence warrants the conclusion that even successful indigenisation may not produce a globally competitive capability, only a domestically deployable one — a partial reconfiguration at best.

## 4. SWOT ANALYSIS

The SWOT analysis synthesises the external (Five Forces) and internal (RBV) findings into a structured strategic assessment.

### Strengths

- **Captive domestic market:** COMAC has amassed more than 1,000 orders for the C919 from Chinese airlines and lessors, providing a revenue pipeline that no pure commercial competitor could match.
- **Unlimited state financial backing:** COMAC received a capital injection of 44 billion yuan (US$6.2 billion) from its stakeholders, boosting its total registered capital by 88% to more than 94 billion yuan.
- **World's largest aviation growth market:** China will overtake North America and Europe to become the largest market for aviation services by 2043, with its market value almost tripling from US$23bn in 2024 to US$61bn in 2043.
- **Geopolitical leverage via BRI:** The Belt and Road Initiative is now entering phase 2.0 amid the intensifying US-China trade war and global supply chain restructuring, with Chinese President Xi Jinping's state visits to Southeast Asia opening doors for COMAC to enter the ASEAN market.

### Weaknesses

- **Critical engine dependency:** By value, approximately 60% of the C919 comes from Western components, and the CFM International LEAP-1C engine alone represents roughly 30% of total aircraft cost. This dependency is not merely commercial but existential: engine supply suspension directly halts production.
- **Production immaturity:** COMAC delivered just 13 C919 aircraft in 2024 despite lofty targets — a rate that makes scaling to competitive volumes within this decade highly uncertain.
- **Absent global MRO network:** In the near term, maintenance will rely heavily on foreign OEM partnerships for engines and avionics. COMAC lacks a similar global network to Airbus or Boeing, potentially causing delays in servicing. Airlines evaluating the C919 for international routes must factor in this support gap.
- **No Western certification:** COMAC has been open about its ambition to take the aircraft overseas, but Western certification has proved far more challenging. Without FAA or EASA approval, the C919 is structurally excluded from the world's most lucrative aviation markets.

### Opportunities

- **EASA certification progress:** In August 2024, EASA completed its fourth round of on-site assessments, a critical step in evaluating the C919's regulatory compliance. Achieving EASA approval would unlock European and many non-Western markets that defer to EASA standards.
- **Southeast Asian market entry:** The C919 could operate in Southeast Asia without European or US certifications, provided it meets the regulatory standards of individual countries, offering a near-term internationalisation pathway.
- **Boeing's competitive weakness:** Boeing struggles with production limits due to regulatory oversight and quality control measures. Boeing's reputational and operational difficulties create a window for COMAC to consolidate its domestic position and expand regionally before Boeing recovers.
- **Growing domestic traffic:** In 2024, the Civil Aviation Administration of China (CAAC) reported record passenger traffic of 730 million, with a year-on-year increase of 17.9%. This demand surge creates urgent need for additional capacity that COMAC is positioned — if it can scale — to supply.

### Threats

- **US technology sanctions:** U.S. sanctions, imposed in May 2025, suspended export licences for CFM LEAP engine parts, threatening COMAC's 50-aircraft annual production target. This is the single most acute near-term threat to the programme.
- **Widening technology gap:** The gap between COMAC and Western aerospace firms is growing, as Western firms are already focused on developing the technologies of tomorrow while China is still struggling to master the technologies of today.
- **Geopolitical fragility of the order book:** The 1,000+ C919 orders are overwhelmingly from state-directed Chinese carriers. Any relaxation of government mandates to buy domestic — or any shift in political priorities — could expose the order book as structurally shallow.
- **C929 programme risk:** Russia formally exited the C929 joint venture, and COMAC assumed full control, re-evaluating the programme as a primarily Chinese project. Developing a wide-body aircraft independently, while still struggling to master narrow-body production, represents a severe resource allocation challenge.

## 5. STRATEGIC POSITION AND GROWTH OPTIONS: ANSOFF'S MATRIX

Ansoff's Matrix (1957) provides a framework for evaluating COMAC's growth options across two dimensions: markets (existing vs. new) and products (existing vs. new). This framework is used here not merely to categorise options but to assess their risk-adjusted feasibility given the constraints identified above.

### Quadrant 1: Market Penetration (Existing Product, Existing Market)

COMAC's immediate priority must be deepening penetration of the Chinese domestic market with the C919. Comac itself is apparently targeting 70 aircraft delivered by the end of the decade. This is the lowest-risk growth path because the demand is state-guaranteed. However, the bottleneck is supply, not demand — production must be scaled before penetration can deepen. This quadrant is *necessary but insufficient*: it generates revenue and manufacturing learning but does not reduce the technology-dependency risk that is COMAC's existential threat.

### Quadrant 2: Market Development (Existing Product, New Markets)

International market development is COMAC's medium-term strategic priority, but it faces the highest regulatory and credibility barriers. Chinese civil aviation authorities express optimism about receiving EU certification in 2025, and industry experts emphasise that obtaining type certifications from the FAA and EASA will significantly boost the C919's international expansion. The Southeast Asian corridor offers a more accessible entry point: COMAC is making a high-profile push into Southeast Asian skies leveraging BRI 2.0 diplomatic relationships. This quadrant is strategically critical because international market development is the only path to genuine competitive validation — a C919 that flies only in China will never be taken seriously as a global competitor.

### Quadrant 3: Product Development (New Product, Existing Market)

COMAC is pursuing product development through the C929 wide-body, but this is the riskiest quadrant given current operational immaturity. In 2023, COMAC continued the C929 programme independently following the withdrawal of Russia's UAC. Pursuing a wide-body development programme while unable to produce 20 narrow-body aircraft per month represents a dangerous dispersion of engineering and managerial attention. The analytical implication is that product development should be sequenced *after* C919 production is stabilised, not pursued in parallel.

### Quadrant 4: Diversification (New Product, New Market)

This quadrant is not strategically relevant for COMAC in the near term. The organisation lacks the operational foundations to support simultaneous product and market innovation. Diversification would compound existing capability deficits.

**Ansoff Summary:** The evidence warrants a sequenced strategy: prioritise Market Penetration (stabilise C919 production) → then Market Development (Southeast Asia, then EASA-certified markets) → then Product Development (C929), with Diversification deferred indefinitely.

## 6. THE CORE STRATEGIC PROBLEM: THE TECHNOLOGY-DEPENDENCY TRAP

The five frameworks converge on a single diagnosis. COMAC is caught in what can be termed a **technology-dependency trap**: state intervention has created a demand structure (1,000+ orders, state-directed airline procurement) that exceeds COMAC's indigenous supply capability, and the gap is filled by Western components whose supply is subject to geopolitical interruption. This trap has three self-reinforcing dimensions:

1. **Production bottleneck:** In June and July 2025, escalating U.S.-China trade tensions led to a temporary U.S. export halt on LEAP engines, stalling production. Although exports resumed in August, the disruption created a lasting barrier to scaling. Each interruption delays the manufacturing learning curve that would eventually enable competitive production rates.

2. **Certification exclusion:** Without EASA or FAA certification, COMAC cannot access the international markets that would provide the revenue and volume needed to fund indigenous R&D at the scale required to close the technology gap.

3. **Credibility deficit:** While the C919's competitive pricing and support from Beijing are driving demand, Western carriers have been slow to adopt COMAC aircraft, citing concerns about certification, reliability, and long-term maintenance. The credibility deficit reinforces certification exclusion: regulators and airlines interpret delivery delays as evidence of systemic quality problems, not merely supply-chain disruptions.

These three dimensions interact: production bottlenecks prevent volume accumulation, which prevents credibility building, which prevents certification, which prevents international revenue, which limits R&D investment, which perpetuates the technology gap. Breaking this cycle requires simultaneous interventions at multiple points.

## 7. RECOMMENDATIONS AND ACTION PLAN

The following recommendations are sequenced by time horizon and derived directly from the analytical findings above. Each recommendation states the strategic rationale (the *why*) connecting evidence to proposed action.

### Recommendation 1: Emergency Supply Chain Diversification (Immediate: 0–18 months)

**The problem:** Trade friction with Washington threatens to prevent COMAC from securing core parts for the programme. A single-source engine dependency on a US-French joint venture is an unacceptable strategic risk for a state enterprise operating in an environment of active technology decoupling.

**The action:** COMAC should immediately negotiate dual-sourcing agreements with European engine suppliers (e.g., CFM's French parent Safran, or Rolls-Royce for future variants) to reduce US-controlled content in the supply chain. Simultaneously, it should accelerate the procurement of European avionics, hydraulics, and structural components to reduce the share of US-export-controlled parts below the threshold that triggers sanctions exposure.

**The warrant:** Reducing US-controlled content below critical thresholds does not require indigenous capability — it requires supplier substitution. This is achievable in the near term and directly addresses the production bottleneck identified in the Five Forces and RBV analyses.

### Recommendation 2: Accelerate CJ-1000A Certification on a Parallel Track (Medium-term: 2–5 years)

**The problem:** The CJ-1000A was launched under the "Made in China 2025" framework with explicit targets for 70% domestic content in core aviation components by 2025, but these targets have not been met. The engine remains uncertified for commercial use.

**The action:** Ring-fence a dedicated CJ-1000A development and certification budget, insulated from C919 production pressures. Establish a dedicated flight-test fleet for the CJ-1000A variant of the C919, operated exclusively on domestic routes, to accumulate the in-service reliability data required for CAAC certification and, eventually, EASA assessment.

**The warrant:** The RBV analysis shows that COMAC's only path to a VRIN-qualifying technological capability is indigenous engine mastery. By adopting a strategy that emphasises both importation and domestic innovation — "introducing first then localising" — AECC has set out to create an indigenous powerhouse capable of meeting international standards. This "introduce-then-localise" logic is sound but requires sustained, protected investment to succeed.

### Recommendation 3: Pursue a "Southeast Asia First" Internationalisation Strategy (Medium-term: 2–4 years)

**The problem:** COMAC cannot access Western markets without EASA/FAA certification, but remaining exclusively domestic prevents the credibility accumulation needed to achieve that certification.

**The action:** Leverage BRI diplomatic relationships to place C919s with Southeast Asian carriers — particularly in markets where CAAC certification is recognised or where bilateral aviation agreements can be negotiated. Reports suggest the C919 could operate in Southeast Asia without European or US certifications, provided it meets the regulatory standards of individual countries. COMAC should establish dedicated MRO hubs in Singapore and Bangkok to support these operations, addressing the maintenance network gap identified in the analysis.

**The warrant:** International operations, even in non-Western-certified markets, generate three strategic goods simultaneously: revenue diversity, in-service reliability data (which supports EASA certification), and commercial credibility with global lessors and financiers. The BRI framework provides the diplomatic infrastructure for this strategy at low incremental cost.

### Recommendation 4: Prioritise EASA Certification Over FAA Certification (Medium-term: 3–5 years)

**The problem:** FAA certification is effectively unattainable in the current US-China political environment. Pursuing it diverts resources from more achievable regulatory milestones.

**The action:** Concentrate regulatory resources on EASA certification. In August 2024, EASA completed its fourth round of on-site assessments, indicating that the process is advanced. COMAC should assign its best regulatory engineers exclusively to the EASA process and accept EASA's technical requirements as the de facto global standard for C919 design modifications.

**The warrant:** EASA certification unlocks not only European carriers but also the majority of non-US international markets, since most regulators outside China defer to either EASA or FAA type certificates. Achieving EASA approval would therefore be a strategic multiplier, breaking the certification-exclusion dimension of the technology-dependency trap.

### Recommendation 5: Rationalise the C929 Programme (Strategic: 3–7 years)

**The problem:** COMAC assumed full control of the C929 following Russia's withdrawal and is now attempting to develop a wide-body aircraft independently. This is a severe resource allocation risk given that C919 production has not yet been stabilised.

**The action:** Suspend active C929 development beyond conceptual design until C919 annual deliveries reach a sustained rate of at least 50 aircraft per year. Redirect C929 engineering talent to C919 production engineering and CJ-1000A certification. When C929 development resumes, seek partnership with a non-US technology partner (e.g., a European airframe or systems supplier) to avoid replicating the single-source dependency problem.

**The warrant:** The Ansoff analysis shows that Product Development (C929) should be sequenced after Market Penetration (C919 production stabilisation). Pursuing both simultaneously violates basic resource allocation discipline and risks producing two mediocre programmes rather than one competitive one.

### Recommendation 6: Build a Global MRO Network Proactively (Long-term: 5–10 years)

**The problem:** COMAC lacks a global MRO network, potentially causing delays in servicing — a barrier that airlines cite explicitly when evaluating the C919 for international routes.

**The action:** Establish joint-venture MRO facilities with local partners in key target markets (Southeast Asia, Middle East, Africa) ahead of fleet entry. Model this on the approach used by Embraer, which built regional MRO partnerships as a precondition for international sales, not as an afterthought.

**The warrant:** The Five Forces analysis shows that buyer power in international markets is high precisely because alternatives (A320neo, 737 MAX) come with established global support networks. Reducing this asymmetry is a necessary condition for competitive parity. Airlines will not commit to a fleet type that cannot be maintained cost-effectively, regardless of purchase price.

## 9. CONCLUSION

COMAC's strategic situation is best described as a race against time and technology. The state has provided the financial runway; what COMAC must now build is the operational and technological capability to use it before geopolitical conditions foreclose the options. Although China has gained ground and in some cases caught up with others — for example, in high-speed rail and telecom — the story is different in commercial aircraft. The frameworks applied here converge on the same prescription: COMAC must break the technology-dependency trap by sequencing its strategic priorities — supply-chain resilience first, production stabilisation second, Southeast Asian internationalisation third, EASA certification fourth, and wide-body development last. A strategy that attempts all of these simultaneously, without the operational foundations to support them, risks achieving none.

The C919 is not merely a commercial aircraft. It is China's most visible industrial-policy bet. Whether it succeeds or fails will depend less on how much money the state provides and more on whether COMAC can transform state-granted advantages into genuinely inimitable capabilities — the core test of competitive advantage in any industry.
