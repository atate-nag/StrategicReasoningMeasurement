# STRATEGIC CASE STUDY: DE BEERS GROUP
## *Anglo American's Diamond Subsidiary at the Crossroads: Navigating Disruption, Defending Differentiation, and Charting a Path to Recovery (2022–2025)*

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

De Beers Group — Anglo American's majority-owned diamond subsidiary — entered one of the most acute strategic crises in its 130-year history between 2022 and 2025. A simultaneous demand contraction across its two largest markets (the United States and China), the rapid commoditisation of lab-grown diamonds (LGDs), structural oversupply, and the parent company's decision to divest the business combined to create a multi-front strategic emergency. This case study applies Porter's Five Forces, Porter's Generic Strategies, the Ansoff Growth Matrix, and a VRIO resource-based analysis to diagnose the situation, before offering a coherent, evidence-grounded set of strategic recommendations.

---

## SECTION 1: SITUATIONAL BACKGROUND

### 1.1 The Company and Its Parent

De Beers Group is the world's largest natural diamond company by value, with mining operations in Botswana, South Africa, Namibia, and Canada, as well as midstream trading and downstream branded jewellery businesses. Anglo American currently owns 85% of De Beers, with the remaining 15% held by the government of Botswana. The relationship between De Beers and Anglo American has been central to the subsidiary's strategic autonomy — and, as of 2024, to its uncertain future, as Anglo American announced plans to divest.

### 1.2 The Financial Deterioration

The scale of De Beers' financial decline between 2023 and 2024 is the primary indicator that a strategic inflection point has been reached. Revenue declined 23% year-over-year to $3.29 billion in 2024, with rough diamond sales falling 25% to $2.7 billion. Production volumes dropped 26% in Q4 2024 to 5.8 million carats, reflecting deliberate capacity reductions in response to weakening demand. The profitability collapse is particularly instructive: the consequential impact of lower sales volumes, a lower average price index and higher unit costs resulted in an underlying EBITDA of $(25) million in 2024, compared to $72 million positive in 2023. This swing from marginal profitability to a loss at the EBITDA level signals that De Beers' cost structure has become misaligned with its revenue base — a structural, not merely cyclical, problem.

Further upstream, De Beers reported a 21% decline in total revenue from $2.8bn in H1 2023 to $2.2bn in H1 2024, with a 22% year-on-year decrease in total rough diamond sales. Economic challenges in China led to low consumer confidence in the diamond market, while the US experienced similar economic uncertainty preventing diamond purchases. The combination of two simultaneous demand shocks in its two largest markets makes this a structural challenge, not a temporary trough.

### 1.3 The Parent's Strategic Pivot

The divestment decision is not incidental; it is itself a strategic signal about where Anglo American sees value. Anglo American's proposed changes would reshape it into a more tightly focused miner centred on copper, iron ore and certain platinum group metals assets. Capital allocation optimisation redirects 60% of post-divestment proceeds to copper expansion. This means De Beers must now operate as a business preparing for ownership transition, which constrains capital availability and creates governance uncertainty — a significant strategic liability.

## SECTION 2: ENVIRONMENTAL ANALYSIS — PORTER'S FIVE FORCES

Porter's Five Forces framework (Porter, 1980) is used here to assess the structural attractiveness of the natural diamond industry as De Beers currently experiences it. The analysis reveals an industry whose structural attractiveness has deteriorated materially since 2020.

### Force 1: Threat of New Entrants — MODERATE TO HIGH

Historically, the natural diamond industry had formidable barriers to entry: the capital requirements for deep mining, the geological scarcity of kimberlite deposits, and De Beers' near-monopolistic control of the supply chain. However, the emergence of lab-grown diamonds has effectively created a parallel industry with dramatically lower entry barriers. Wholesale prices for a two-carat lab-grown diamond fell 34% from 2020 to 2024, while retail prices dropped 25%, squeezing margins. De Beers CEO Al Cook noted that lab-grown diamonds had shifted to "low-margin fashion accessories." The ease of scaling LGD production — particularly via Chemical Vapour Deposition (CVD) technology from Asian manufacturers — means that the threat of new entrants into the broader gem-quality diamond *market* (if not the natural diamond sub-segment) is high and escalating.

### Force 2: Bargaining Power of Buyers — HIGH AND RISING

Among centre stones, 52% were lab-grown in 2024, up from 12% in 2019. This rapid substitution demonstrates that a large segment of buyers — particularly in the bridal category — now exercise genuine choice between natural and lab-grown alternatives. Where substitutes are readily available and price differentials are large, buyer power is inherently high. Retailers, too, are gaining leverage: while a proportion of natural diamond demand continues to be affected by lab-grown diamonds as a result of prevailing retail margins, such margins are expected to be unsustainable in light of increasing lab-grown diamond supply volumes, greater levels of competition, and growing consumer awareness of lab-grown diamond price trends. This suggests that the current retail margin structure is itself a transitional distortion, not a durable equilibrium.

### Force 3: Threat of Substitutes — VERY HIGH

This is the most structurally significant force. Lab-grown diamonds are chemically, physically, and optically identical to mined diamonds. By 2024, lab diamonds became 90% cheaper than natural diamonds of the same quality. Since 2020, prices for lab diamonds have fallen by 74%. The speed and depth of this price collapse means that the functional substitute is now dramatically cheaper — a situation that, in most industries, would predict rapid and sustained market share loss for the premium product. The key question — addressed in the recommendations — is whether natural diamonds can be repositioned as a *non-substitutable* luxury good rather than a commodity gem.

### Force 4: Bargaining Power of Suppliers — LOW (for De Beers itself)

De Beers controls its own mines, which reduces upstream supplier power for the company itself. However, the broader industry faces a supply-side complication: De Beers' rough diamond production was reduced by 22% to 24.7 million carats in 2024 (2023: 31.9 million carats), reflecting a proactive production response to a prolonged period of weak demand. The fact that De Beers must deliberately curtail its own production to manage price levels reveals a loss of pricing power — a sign that the supply-demand balance has shifted structurally against producers.

### Force 5: Rivalry Among Existing Competitors — HIGH

Demand for smaller and lower-quality diamonds experienced pressure in light of the growing supply from other producers — most likely a reference to Angola's mining industry, which sold large quantities of lower-value rough at discounts. Angola's aggressive market entry, combined with Russian producer ALROSA's continued production despite Western sanctions, has intensified rivalry at the rough diamond level. The result is a structurally unattractive competitive environment: high substitution threat, rising buyer power, intensifying rivalry, and a new class of entrant (LGD producers) that competes on price rather than differentiation.

**Five Forces Summary:** The natural diamond industry's structural attractiveness has deteriorated significantly. The only mitigating factor is the potential for product differentiation — specifically, the argument that natural diamonds can be repositioned as irreplaceable luxury assets rather than commodity gemstones. Whether De Beers can execute this repositioning is the central strategic question.

## SECTION 3: INTERNAL ANALYSIS — VRIO FRAMEWORK

The VRIO framework (Barney, 1991) assesses whether De Beers' resources and capabilities can generate sustained competitive advantage. Resources are evaluated on four criteria: Value, Rarity, Imitability, and Organisation.

| Resource / Capability | Valuable? | Rare? | Inimitable? | Organised to Exploit? | Competitive Implication |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Brand heritage ("A Diamond is Forever") | Yes | Yes | Partially | Partially | Temporary advantage |
| Tracr blockchain traceability platform | Yes | Yes | Partially | Yes | Sustained advantage (if scaled) |
| Mine-to-market vertical integration | Yes | No | No | Yes | Competitive parity |
| Rough diamond production assets | Yes | No | No | Yes | Competitive parity |
| De Beers Jewellers / high jewellery capability | Yes | Yes | Yes | Partially | Potential sustained advantage |
| Category marketing expertise | Yes | Yes | Partially | Yes | Temporary advantage |

**Brand heritage** is De Beers' most historically powerful resource. The "Diamonds are Forever" campaign, first launched in 1947, has been ranked among the top 100 advertising campaigns of all time. The brand has been deliberately revived: in November 2024, De Beers launched 'Forever Present', a natural diamonds campaign for the holiday season, which revived the iconic 'A Diamond is Forever' tagline. However, brand heritage alone is not inimitable in the modern media environment, where challenger brands can rapidly build emotional associations.

**Tracr** is De Beers' most strategically distinctive capability. De Beers made a substantial $20 million investment in Tracr, the blockchain-based diamond traceability initiative, poised to dramatically expand Tracr's capabilities and reach, furthering its mission to bring greater transparency and accountability to the diamond supply chain. Crucially, the platform is gaining independent credibility: GIA acquired a 30% stake in De Beers' Tracr blockchain platform, marking a key step in Tracr's transition towards becoming an independent, industry-wide solution. The investment builds on GIA's existing collaboration with Tracr, which began in 2023 with the inclusion of provenance information from Tracr on eligible GIA diamond grading reports. GIA's involvement transforms Tracr from a proprietary De Beers tool into a potential industry standard — a much more powerful strategic position, since industry-standard infrastructure confers ongoing influence even as De Beers' ownership stake dilutes.

**Category marketing** is another genuine capability. De Beers announced a collaboration with Signet Jewelers in May 2024. Signet is the world's largest retailer of diamond jewellery, and its iconic retail brands include KAY Jewelers, Zales, Jared, Diamonds Direct and Blue Nile. By combining Signet's trusted brands, digital capabilities and extensive store network with De Beers' expertise in creating iconic campaigns, the collaboration aims to showcase the rarity and timelessness of natural diamonds for a new generation, ahead of an expected 25% increase in engagements over the next three years. This collaboration leverages De Beers' marketing capability through Signet's distribution reach — a smart asset-light approach to category demand generation.

**VRIO Conclusion:** De Beers' most defensible competitive advantages are Tracr (traceability as a differentiator) and its high jewellery maison capability. Brand heritage is valuable but not inimitable. The company's mining assets provide parity, not advantage. This analysis implies that De Beers' strategy should concentrate investment on the resources that are genuinely rare and inimitable.

## SECTION 4: STRATEGIC DIAGNOSIS

### 4.1 The Core Strategic Problem

Synthesising the Five Forces and VRIO analyses produces a clear diagnosis: **De Beers is caught in a strategic identity crisis between a commodity business model (rough diamond producer) and a luxury brand model (natural diamond experience provider).** The commodity model is structurally threatened by LGD competition and oversupply. The luxury model is theoretically defensible — but only if De Beers can successfully execute the repositioning of natural diamonds as non-substitutable luxury assets.

The financial data supports this diagnosis. The EBITDA collapse to $(25) million in 2024 is not primarily a volume problem (though volumes fell); it is a *margin* problem driven by the increase in unit cost to $93/ct (2023: $71/ct), primarily driven by lower production volumes. This cost-volume dynamic — where fixed mining costs spread over fewer carats as production is deliberately curtailed — is the classic trap of a commodity producer trying to hold price by restricting supply. The strategy works only if demand is price-inelastic, which it is not when a near-perfect substitute exists at 90% lower cost.

### 4.2 The Lightbox Reversal as Strategic Signal

De Beers' 2018 launch of Lightbox — its own lab-grown diamond brand — was a defensive competitive move designed to capture LGD market share and signal to consumers that LGDs were fashion accessories, not fine jewellery. The subsequent closure of Lightbox is the clearest possible strategic signal. De Beers announced plans to wind down its lab-grown diamond jewellery brand Lightbox, marking a strategic pivot back to its core focus on natural diamonds. The decision comes amid mounting challenges in the diamond industry, including tepid demand, tariff uncertainty, and a prolonged decline in diamond prices. The closure is strategically rational: Lightbox faced mounting headwinds, with wholesale prices for a two-carat lab-grown diamond falling 34% from 2020 to 2024, while retail prices dropped 25%, squeezing margins. Continuing to operate in a commoditising, margin-free segment would have diluted the De Beers brand and consumed capital better deployed in natural diamond differentiation. The Lightbox closure thus represents a necessary, if belated, strategic clarification.

### 4.3 The "Origins" Strategy: Assessment

De Beers' management response to this crisis is its "Origins" strategy, announced in May 2024. The strategy consists of: (i) focusing upstream investments on the major projects that will deliver the highest returns; (ii) integrating the midstream to deliver greater efficiency; (iii) resetting the downstream by reinvigorating category marketing and evolving proprietary brands through development of De Beers Jewellers into a leading high jewellery maison business and refocusing Forevermark on the fast-growing Indian market; and (iv) pivoting synthetics. The strategy is underpinned by a plan to reduce overhead costs by $100 million per year.

The Origins strategy is directionally correct — it moves De Beers toward the luxury/differentiation end of the strategic spectrum — but it faces three execution risks: (1) insufficient speed relative to the rate of market deterioration; (2) governance uncertainty created by the Anglo American divestiture process; and (3) the risk that the India pivot is insufficient to offset the combined weakness of the US and China markets.

## SECTION 5: PORTER'S GENERIC STRATEGIES — POSITIONING ANALYSIS

Porter (1980) identifies three generic competitive strategies: Cost Leadership, Differentiation, and Focus. A firm that fails to commit to one of these is "stuck in the middle" and earns below-average returns. De Beers' current situation is precisely this: it retains the cost structure of a large-scale commodity miner while attempting to execute a differentiation strategy in the downstream. This ambiguity is strategically dangerous.

**The case for Differentiation Focus:** The VRIO analysis establishes that De Beers' inimitable resources — traceability technology, high jewellery expertise, and 130-year brand heritage — are all on the differentiation side. Consumer research supports this direction: 75% of consumers believe natural diamonds hold their value better due to their rarity and finite supply, and 73% view each stone as one of a kind. Nearly three-quarters of respondents (72%) expressed that natural diamonds bring them joy. Furthermore, the rapid price decline of lab-grown diamonds fundamentally altered consumer perception — initially marketed as "ethical luxury," lab-grown diamonds increasingly became viewed as industrial commodities rather than luxury goods. This commoditisation of LGDs is, paradoxically, De Beers' greatest strategic opportunity: it creates a clear and widening perceptual gap between LGDs and natural diamonds, which De Beers can exploit.

**The case against Cost Leadership:** De Beers cannot win a cost leadership contest against LGD producers. By 2024, lab diamonds became 90% cheaper than natural diamonds of the same quality, and since 2020, prices for lab diamonds have fallen by 74%. No mining operation — however efficiently run — can match the economics of a scalable manufacturing process. Attempting cost competition would require writing down mining assets and destroying the very scarcity narrative that underpins natural diamond value.

**Conclusion on Generic Strategy:** De Beers must commit fully to **Differentiation Focus** — targeting the premium, emotionally engaged, luxury consumer segment with an offering built on provenance, rarity, and irreplaceability. This is not a new strategy; it is the *completion* of a transition that the company has been executing inconsistently.

## SECTION 6: ANSOFF MATRIX — GROWTH VECTOR ANALYSIS

The Ansoff Matrix (Ansoff, 1957) maps growth options across existing/new products and existing/new markets. Each quadrant carries a different risk profile.

**Market Penetration (existing products, existing markets):** De Beers' collaboration with Signet Jewelers targets the US market — its largest existing market — with a campaign aimed at Zillennial consumers ahead of a projected engagement boom. The collaboration with Signet, the world's largest retailer of diamond jewellery, aims to showcase the rarity and timelessness of natural diamonds for a new generation, ahead of an expected 25% increase in engagements over the next three years. This is the lowest-risk growth vector and should be the near-term priority, given that the US remains the world's largest diamond jewellery market.

**Market Development (existing products, new markets):** The India pivot represents De Beers' highest-conviction market development bet. India is emerging as the most critical growth market for De Beers, and the brand is going all out to target a rising consumer base, with new stores, campaigns, and partnerships. This is supported by consumer data: surveys conducted in 2024–25 found that around 65–70% of Indian diamond jewellery buyers still prefer natural diamonds over lab-grown alternatives. India's large and growing middle class, its cultural affinity for gold and gemstones, and the relatively lower penetration of LGDs make it the most structurally attractive growth market available to De Beers.

**Product Development (new products, existing markets):** The ORIGIN – De Beers Group polished diamond initiative represents a product development move. First unveiled at the 2024 JCK Show, ORIGIN – De Beers Group is a new branded polished diamond offering designed to enable retailers to tell the individual stories of natural diamonds sourced by De Beers Group, strengthening the connection between a specific stone and its mine of origin. This is a high-value move: it converts a commodity (a rough diamond) into a differentiated product (a story-bearing stone with verified provenance), directly leveraging the Tracr capability.

**Diversification (new products, new markets):** The highest-risk quadrant. De Beers has wisely retreated from diversification (Lightbox was an attempt to enter the LGD market) and should not re-enter it. The diversification experiment failed, confirming that De Beers' competitive advantages do not transfer to the LGD segment.

**Ansoff Conclusion:** The strategic priority order should be: (1) Market Penetration via Signet collaboration in the US; (2) Market Development in India via Forevermark refocus; (3) Product Development via ORIGIN provenance-based polished diamond branding. Diversification should be explicitly ruled out.

## SECTION 7: THE DIVESTITURE DIMENSION — STAKEHOLDER AND GOVERNANCE ANALYSIS

The Anglo American divestiture process introduces a governance dimension that is inseparable from the strategic analysis. Anglo values De Beers at about $5 billion, though analysts at UBS estimate the sale could generate between $3 billion and $4 billion, including deferred payments, given challenging market conditions. This valuation gap — between what Anglo American seeks and what the market will pay — reflects the strategic uncertainty documented throughout this analysis. A buyer acquiring De Beers at current market conditions would be betting on the successful execution of the Origins strategy; the valuation discount prices in execution risk.

The Botswana dimension adds further complexity. Botswana's government has signalled a bold move to acquire a controlling stake in De Beers, adding new complexity to the ongoing divestment process. Currently holding a 15% stake in De Beers and a 50% share in the Debswana joint venture, Botswana plays a pivotal role. A Botswana-controlled De Beers would face different strategic constraints: a government owner would likely prioritise production volumes and employment over brand repositioning, potentially undermining the differentiation strategy. This is a significant strategic risk that any recommendation must address.

## SECTION 8: STRATEGIC RECOMMENDATIONS

The following recommendations are derived directly from the analytical frameworks applied above. Each recommendation is linked to the specific diagnostic finding that warrants it.

### Recommendation 1: Fully Commit to Differentiation Focus — Eliminate Strategic Ambiguity

**Warrant:** The Five Forces analysis shows that cost competition with LGDs is structurally impossible. The VRIO analysis identifies De Beers' inimitable resources as lying exclusively on the differentiation side. Porter's framework warns that "stuck in the middle" firms earn below-average returns. De Beers must therefore eliminate any residual ambiguity about its generic strategy and fully commit to premium differentiation.

**Action:** Accelerate the development of De Beers Jewellers as a high jewellery maison, explicitly targeting the UHNW (ultra-high-net-worth) segment where price sensitivity is lowest and brand provenance matters most. De Beers' downstream strategy includes resetting the downstream by reinvigorating category marketing and evolving proprietary brands through development of De Beers Jewellers into a leading high jewellery maison business. This should be the flagship strategic initiative, resourced accordingly.

### Recommendation 2: Scale Tracr as the Industry Standard for Provenance — Convert a Capability into a Platform

**Warrant:** The VRIO analysis identifies Tracr as De Beers' most genuinely inimitable resource. This can create a competitive advantage for retailers who offer Tracr-traced diamonds, attracting consumers willing to pay a premium for ethically sourced products. The GIA partnership has begun the transition from proprietary tool to industry standard, which is strategically superior. By bringing an independent, globally recognised grading authority like GIA into Tracr's ownership, the platform sheds its proprietary image, encouraging broader industry trust and adoption. An industry-standard traceability platform gives De Beers ongoing structural influence over the natural diamond supply chain even as its mining market share evolves.

**Action:** Accelerate Tracr adoption by offering it to all natural diamond producers, not just De Beers-sourced stones. This converts a defensive asset into an offensive platform — one that De Beers controls architecturally but that serves the entire industry. Connect Tracr data directly to the ORIGIN polished diamond product and to Forevermark certification, creating an end-to-end provenance narrative that no LGD can replicate.

### Recommendation 3: Execute the India Market Development Strategy with Urgency

**Warrant:** The Ansoff analysis identifies India as the highest-potential market development opportunity, supported by the consumer preference data showing 65–70% preference for natural diamonds. India is emerging as the most critical growth market for De Beers, and the brand is going all out to target a rising consumer base, with new stores, campaigns, and partnerships. The Forevermark refocus on India is already underway; the recommendation is to accelerate and resource it as a strategic priority rather than a subsidiary initiative.

**Action:** Redirect Forevermark marketing budgets toward India with a specific focus on self-purchase (non-bridal) occasions, which represent a growing demand category. Non-bridal purchases now account for 75% of demand value, while bridal (engagement and wedding jewellery) is 25%. This is a structural shift in demand that De Beers must capture in India before LGD producers establish the self-purchase narrative there.

### Recommendation 4: Resolve the Governance Uncertainty — Advocate for a Strategically Aligned Owner

**Warrant:** The divestiture process creates governance uncertainty that directly impairs strategy execution. A Botswana government-controlled De Beers, prioritising production volumes, would undermine the differentiation strategy. A financially motivated private equity owner would likely cut the marketing and technology investment that the differentiation strategy requires. The optimal owner is a luxury goods conglomerate or a long-horizon institutional investor that can provide patient capital for brand building.

**Action:** De Beers' management should actively shape the sale process by articulating the strategic logic of the Origins strategy to potential buyers and by structuring any sale to include long-term commitments to the Tracr platform and category marketing investment. Wanblad said he expects the sale process to conclude within six months but did not rule out spinning off De Beers if negotiations fail. A spin-off — creating an independently listed De Beers — may in fact be the governance structure most conducive to long-term differentiation strategy execution, as it would allow De Beers to raise capital directly from investors who understand and believe in the luxury repositioning thesis.

### Recommendation 5: Maintain Production Discipline — Do Not Sacrifice Price for Volume

**Warrant:** The Five Forces analysis establishes that De Beers faces a substitution threat that is price-elastic: when natural diamond prices fall, consumers substitute toward LGDs. Conversely, maintaining high prices reinforces the luxury narrative and preserves the scarcity premium. The mining operations delivered steady operational performance at lower output levels as the business continued to reconfigure production in response to prevailing market conditions. Rough diamond production was reduced by 22% to 24.7 million carats in 2024, reflecting a proactive production response to a prolonged period of weak demand. This production discipline is correct and should be maintained, even if it further depresses short-term EBITDA.

**Action:** Set explicit price floors for rough diamond categories and accept lower volumes rather than clearing inventory at distressed prices. The luxury goods precedent — Hermès and Rolex deliberately restrict supply to maintain price and desirability — is the relevant strategic analogy. De Beers must accept that it is no longer a volume business and manage accordingly.

## CONCLUSION

De Beers Group faces a genuinely existential strategic challenge: the commoditisation of its core product by a technologically superior substitute, against a backdrop of demand contraction in its two largest markets and governance uncertainty created by Anglo American's divestiture. The analytical frameworks applied here converge on a single strategic imperative: De Beers must complete its transition from commodity diamond producer to luxury natural diamond brand, and it must do so with urgency and resource commitment. The company possesses the necessary raw materials — a 130-year brand, a blockchain traceability platform gaining industry-standard status, and a growing Indian market with strong natural diamond preferences — but these advantages will erode unless the strategic ambiguity of the current position is resolved. The Origins strategy points in the right direction; the recommendations above specify how to execute it with the coherence and conviction that the situation demands.
