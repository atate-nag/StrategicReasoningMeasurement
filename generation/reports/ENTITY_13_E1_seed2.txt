# Diageo PLC: A Strategic Case Study

## *From Growth Plateau to Structural Reckoning — Diagnosing a Multi-Front Crisis and Charting a Path to Recovery*

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Diageo PLC, the world's largest premium spirits company, entered fiscal year 2024 (year ending June 2024) in a position that was, superficially, one of enduring strength: over 200 brands, distribution in nearly 180 countries, and a portfolio anchored by icons such as Johnnie Walker, Guinness, and Don Julio. Yet beneath this surface, a convergence of strategic pressures — a catastrophic inventory management failure in Latin America and the Caribbean (LAC), a softening North American consumer, a structural shift towards moderation and no/low alcohol, intensifying competitive rivalry, and a leadership vacuum — exposed deep vulnerabilities in the company's operating model and strategic positioning.

This case study argues that Diageo's situation is not merely a cyclical dip but a **multi-front strategic crisis** that requires simultaneous action across four dimensions: (1) operational repair of the distribution and inventory architecture; (2) portfolio rebalancing towards resilient and emerging growth categories; (3) organisational redesign for market agility; and (4) proactive repositioning ahead of the structural moderation megatrend. Using Porter's Five Forces, the Resource-Based View (RBV), the Ansoff Growth Matrix, and a PESTEL analysis, this study develops an integrated diagnosis and a set of prioritised recommendations.

## PART I: SITUATIONAL OVERVIEW — THE STRATEGIC CONTEXT

### 1.1 Company Profile

Diageo is a global leader in beverage alcohol with an outstanding collection of brands including Johnnie Walker, Crown Royal, JeB and Buchanan's whiskies, Smirnoff, Cîroc and Ketel One vodkas, Captain Morgan, Baileys, Don Julio, Tanqueray and Guinness. Diageo is a global company, and its products are sold in nearly 180 countries around the world. The company is listed on both the London Stock Exchange (DGE) and the New York Stock Exchange (DEO).

Diageo has a portfolio of over 200 brands and sales in nearly 180 countries, bringing together some of the most iconic brands from around the globe that consumers have enjoyed for generations. Tequila is the company's third largest business area globally, preceded by Scotch and beer.

### 1.2 The Financial Deterioration: FY2024–FY2025

The scale of Diageo's recent difficulties is best illustrated through its financial trajectory. In fiscal 24, organic net sales were down 0.6%, with positive price/mix performance mostly mitigating a decline in volume. Excluding the Latin America and Caribbean region (LAC), the business grew organic net sales by 1.8%. Organic operating margin declined by 130bps, primarily driven by LAC.

By FY2025 (year ending June 2025), the headline picture remained troubled. Reported operating profit declined 27.8% and reported operating profit margin declined 819bps, primarily due to exceptional impairment and restructuring costs, unfavourable foreign exchange and a decline in organic operating margin. Net sales for FY2025 were $20,245m, essentially flat year-on-year. Organic net sales returned to growth and increased $101 million or 1.0%, driven by positive price/mix of 1.2pps, partially offset by a 0.2% volume decline — a fragile recovery masking deeper structural problems.

The most recent half-year data (H1 FY2026, ending December 2025) shows the crisis deepening in key geographies. Solid organic net sales growth in Europe, LAC and Africa was offset by weakness in CWS impacting Asia Pacific results and softer performance in North America as US Spirits declined reflecting weak consumer confidence.

## PART II: PESTEL ANALYSIS — THE MACRO-ENVIRONMENT

A PESTEL analysis identifies the external forces shaping the strategic landscape within which Diageo must operate.

### 2.1 Political

The global geopolitical environment is increasingly hostile to multinational spirits companies. Tariff risk has become acute: Diageo faces potential tariffs on UK and EU spirits exports to the United States, its single largest market (approximately 39% of net sales). Government regulations, including taxation, import/export policies, and restrictions on alcohol advertising, significantly influence the market. Stringent laws in some countries may pose challenges, while regulatory changes can favour or constrain market access.

### 2.2 Economic

The macroeconomic environment has been characterised by elevated inflation, rising interest rates, and a resulting squeeze on consumer disposable incomes in key Western markets. Weak consumer confidence in many key markets, including the US and China, has meant that the resulting pressure on consumer wallets continues to weigh on the spirits sector. In emerging markets, currency volatility — particularly in LAC — has created additional headwinds, with unfavourable foreign exchange movements compounding organic sales weakness.

### 2.3 Social

The most structurally significant social trend is the rise of the "sober-curious" consumer. Social norms around alcohol are changing, contributing to a rapid expansion of the non-alcoholic drinks market and the rise of the "sober curious." Across major global markets, no- and low-alcohol volumes are forecast to grow about 4% annually from 2024 to 2028, with the no-alcohol segment leading at +7%. Critically, in 2024 alone, the no-alcohol segment attracted an estimated 61 million new consumers globally (compared to 2022) — far more than the 38 million recruited by low-alcohol options. Generational dynamics compound this: Millennials fuel premium spirits growth, but Gen Z's sober curiosity raises long-term questions for the category.

### 2.4 Technological

Digital transformation is reshaping both consumer engagement and supply chain management. Online spirits penetration more than doubled since 2020 in key markets; global beverage alcohol e-commerce exceeded $50 billion in 2024. Diageo is leveraging AI in marketing: the company reduced non-working development costs from 21% to 14% of A&P spend using AI and agile methods. In supply chain management, Diageo has deployed RFID technology: RFID labels are being used to track certain cases as they move through the distribution network, directly addressing the inventory visibility failures that caused the LAC crisis.

### 2.5 Environmental

Sustainability is both a regulatory and reputational imperative. Diageo has set targets of a 50% reduction in Scope 1 and 2 emissions by 2030. Climate change poses direct risks to agricultural inputs — agave supply for tequila and barley for Scotch whisky are both vulnerable to extreme weather events, representing a strategic supply chain risk that is not fully priced into medium-term planning.

### 2.6 Legal

Regulators intensified scrutiny of health claims, calorie counts, and ingredient transparency on alcohol labels in 2024. The Cîroc situation — in which Diageo became entangled in legal disputes arising from its partnership with Sean Combs — illustrates the reputational and legal risks of celebrity brand partnerships. In May 2023, Combs filed a lawsuit against Diageo for allegedly neglecting his two liquor brands, DeLeón and Cîroc. This ultimately led to Diageo unwinding the partnership, taking a material impairment charge, and diverting management attention at a critical period.

## PART III: PORTER'S FIVE FORCES — INDUSTRY ATTRACTIVENESS

Porter's Five Forces framework assesses the structural attractiveness of the premium spirits industry and Diageo's position within it.

### 3.1 Competitive Rivalry — HIGH

Diageo's most prominent direct competitors in the spirits sector include Pernod Ricard, Bacardi, Beam Suntory, Brown-Forman, Constellation Brands, LVMH's Moët Hennessy Division, Campari Group, William Grant & Sons, and Rémy Cointreau. Rivalry is intensifying as the overall category growth rate slows. In a challenging year within the spirits industry, Brown-Forman remained agile and focused on the long-term growth of its brands, with organic results reflecting inventory reductions across the entire spirits value chain. The fact that multiple major players simultaneously experienced inventory destocking confirms that competitive rivalry is structurally elevated, with no single firm able to escape category-wide dynamics.

In tequila — Diageo's most important growth category — competitive pressure is acute. A big challenge has been competition in tequila: affecting brands such as Don Julio and Casamigos, with Diageo's US tequila sales plunging 23% over H1 FY2026.

### 3.2 Threat of New Entrants — MODERATE (but rising in craft)

The premium spirits industry has historically had high barriers to entry: aged inventory requirements (a bottle of 12-year Scotch whisky requires 12 years of capital tied up in maturing stock), brand equity built over decades, and complex global distribution networks. Diageo's distribution network includes a vast sales force and an unrivalled number of venues scattered throughout the planet, creating a barrier to entry for smaller brands. However, the craft spirits movement has lowered barriers in the premium-but-local segment, and private equity-backed challenger brands (e.g., Aviation Gin, Monkey 47) have demonstrated that heritage is not an insurmountable moat.

### 3.3 Threat of Substitutes — HIGH and RISING

The threat of substitutes is the most structurally significant force acting on Diageo's long-term position. Substitutes operate on two levels:

- **Within alcohol**: beer, wine, RTDs, and hard seltzers compete for the same occasions as spirits.
- **Outside alcohol**: the no/low alcohol category is growing at 4-7% annually and sales of no- or low-alcohol beer, cider, wine, and spirits were up by 23% in the UK in 2023-24, according to Waitrose data. This is not a niche: it represents a structural shift in how consumers — particularly younger ones — relate to alcohol.

### 3.4 Bargaining Power of Buyers — HIGH

Walmart, Costco, and Tesco control 59.2% of global alcohol retail distribution channels. These retailers negotiate prices aggressively, demanding volume discounts. The concentration of retail buying power gives major retailers significant leverage over pricing, shelf placement, and promotional terms. In the on-trade channel, consolidation of hospitality groups creates similar dynamics.

### 3.5 Bargaining Power of Suppliers — MODERATE

Diageo sources agricultural inputs — barley, agave, grapes, sugarcane — from geographically concentrated supply bases. Agave, in particular, has a 7-10 year growing cycle, meaning supply cannot be rapidly adjusted to demand. However, Diageo's scale and long-term supplier relationships provide some mitigation. End-to-end control — from sourcing grain and agave to global logistics — supports consistent quality, premium pricing and scale advantages that smaller competitors cannot match.

**Five Forces Summary**: The premium spirits industry remains attractive due to strong brand loyalty, pricing power, and the premiumisation trend. However, the combination of high rivalry, rising substitute threats (especially no/low alcohol), and concentrated buyer power means that competitive advantage must be actively defended, not assumed.

## PART IV: RESOURCE-BASED VIEW — DIAGEO'S COMPETITIVE CAPABILITIES

The Resource-Based View (Barney, 1991) holds that sustainable competitive advantage derives from resources and capabilities that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN). Diageo's strategic assets can be assessed against this framework.

### 4.1 Brand Portfolio — VRIN ✓

Diageo's brand portfolio is its most powerful strategic asset. Diageo holds a top-tier global position in premium spirits and stout, with distribution in over 180 countries and marquee brands like Johnnie Walker, Guinness, Baileys and Tanqueray driving strong brand loyalty and premiumisation-led mix gains. The heritage depth of these brands — Guinness dates to 1759, Johnnie Walker to 1820 — is genuinely inimitable. These brands carry cultural meaning that cannot be manufactured quickly by rivals.

### 4.2 Maturing Inventory — VRIN ✓

Diageo holds one of the world's largest stocks of maturing Scotch whisky, representing billions of dollars in inventory that has been accumulating over decades. Diageo's competitive edge rests on deep brand equity, unmatched Scotch maturing inventory and scale advantages that underpin production, distribution and M&A capacity. This is a genuinely rare and inimitable resource — competitors cannot replicate it without decades of investment.

### 4.3 Global Distribution Network — Valuable, Rare ✓ but Vulnerable

Diageo's Route to Consumer targets Africa and Asia for volume and premiumisation opportunities, while US efforts emphasise premium boutique retail for Prestige brands. However, the LAC crisis revealed that distribution network strength is only as good as the quality of information flowing through it. When distributor sell-out data is not visible to the manufacturer, inventory accumulates invisibly until it becomes a crisis. This represents a critical capability gap.

### 4.4 Innovation and Brand-Building Capability — Valuable but Inconsistent

Diageo's track record shows it to be an expert in innovation and brand building, vital in order to first make the right products and then be able to take those products to consumers. However, the Cîroc episode illustrates the limits of this capability when over-reliant on celebrity endorsement rather than intrinsic brand equity. The Casamigos acquisition (2017, ~$1bn) and Don Julio portfolio have been genuine successes, but the recent tequila slowdown in the US suggests that even Diageo's strongest growth engine is not immune to category saturation.

### 4.5 Capability Gap: Distribution Intelligence

The LAC crisis is best understood through the RBV lens as a **capability gap in distribution intelligence**. Diageo lacked real-time visibility of sell-out (consumer purchase) data versus sell-in (shipments to distributors), allowing inventory to build unsustainably in the channel. This gap is not merely operational — it is strategic, because it prevented management from making timely portfolio and pricing decisions.

## PART V: THE LATIN AMERICA & CARIBBEAN CRISIS — A DEEP DIVE

The LAC situation warrants detailed analysis as it is both the proximate cause of Diageo's financial deterioration and a diagnostic window into broader organisational failures.

### 5.1 What Happened

Reported net sales of $11.0 billion declined 1.4% or $158 million, due to a $167 million unfavourable foreign exchange impact and an organic net sales decline of $67 million or 0.6%, driven by a $310 million or 23% decline in Latin America and Caribbean.

The first half of fiscal 24 was challenging for Diageo and the sector, particularly as it lapped strong growth in the prior year and faced an uneven global consumer environment. The LAC region encompasses five divisions: Brazil, Mexico, Central America and the Caribbean, Colombia and Venezuela (Andean), and South LAC (Argentina, Bolivia, Chile, Ecuador, Paraguay, Peru, and Uruguay).

### 5.2 Root Cause Analysis

The crisis had two interacting causes:

1. **Macroeconomic**: Currency devaluations across the region (particularly in Argentina) compressed real consumer purchasing power, causing downtrading from premium to value spirits.

2. **Operational/Strategic**: Diageo had been shipping product to distributors at rates significantly above end-consumer demand, allowing channel inventory to build to unsustainable levels. When the macroeconomic shock hit, distributors stopped ordering — not because consumers had entirely stopped drinking, but because they were working through existing stock. This is a classic **bullwhip effect**: small fluctuations in consumer demand became amplified into large swings in manufacturer shipments due to lack of information transparency in the supply chain.

In January 2024, Diageo outlined its action plan for LAC: focused on expanding inventory visibility in the region and incentivising top distributors to report sell-out information, giving the company better position to forecast future requirements.

### 5.3 Strategic Implications

The LAC crisis is not simply a regional operational failure. It reveals a **systemic weakness in Diageo's route-to-market architecture**: in markets where Diageo does not own its distribution, it has been flying blind on consumer demand. This same vulnerability exists, to varying degrees, in other emerging markets. The fact that the North American market also experienced inventory-related headwinds — the decline in North America was attributable to a cautious consumer environment and the impact of lapping inventory replenishment in the prior year — suggests the problem is not unique to LAC.

## PART VI: ANSOFF MATRIX — GROWTH STRATEGY ANALYSIS

The Ansoff Growth Matrix (Ansoff, 1957) provides a framework for evaluating Diageo's strategic options across four quadrants: Market Penetration, Market Development, Product Development, and Diversification.

### 6.1 Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets)

In its core developed markets — North America and Europe — Diageo's primary strategic lever is **premiumisation**: shifting consumers from standard to premium and super-premium variants within existing categories. Diageo is accelerating into the super-premium plus segment — underscored by its 2024 Don Papa Rum acquisition — as it chases higher margins amid shifting consumer tastes toward quality over quantity.

However, the premiumisation strategy faces headwinds. It is the super-premium-and-above that has been growing, while premium has been under pressure — meaning Diageo must continue pushing its portfolio upmarket while managing the risk that economic pressure causes consumers to trade down.

### 6.2 Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets)

Africa and Asia represent Diageo's most important market development opportunities. In Africa, Diageo's strategy is to grow its beers fast and its spirits faster, with its operating model seeking to build resilience, agility and strength into its African businesses as they develop. Diageo's 2025 expansion prioritises Asia-Pacific and Africa, with a major scale-up in India to capture premium Scotch and local craft spirit demand.

These markets offer demographic tailwinds — a growing middle class, rising disposable incomes, and an increasing affinity for Western premium brands — that are structurally more favourable than the mature Western markets where Diageo generates the majority of its current revenue.

### 6.3 Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets)

The no/low alcohol category represents Diageo's most strategically important product development opportunity. Guinness 0.0 delivered double-digit growth in FY2025. Diageo's investment into Guinness 0.0 will now total €60m in the three years since its launch. The alcohol-free alternative has experienced an almost 50% increase in sales between the end of February 2023 and the end of February 2024.

RTDs represent a second product development vector. Lewis has said Diageo missed significant opportunities in the US in recent years, particularly in the ready-to-drink cocktails segment. Diageo initiated a more targeted strategy for RTDs in selective key markets, with some encouraging early signs.

### 6.4 Diversification (New Products, New Markets)

Diageo continues to strategically acquire brands that tap into emerging consumer trends, such as the September 2024 acquisition of a non-alcoholic spirits brand. This represents a cautious move into related diversification — leveraging existing distribution and brand-building capabilities in adjacent categories. The risk is that diversification dilutes management focus at a time when the core business requires urgent repair.

## PART VII: THE LEADERSHIP DIMENSION

No strategic analysis of Diageo's situation would be complete without examining the leadership context, which is itself a strategic variable.

Diageo confirmed the appointment of Sir Dave Lewis, former Group CEO of Tesco, as its new CEO and Executive Director, effective 1 January 2025. Dave is a proven CEO with extensive marketing and brand-building experience, with an outstanding track record leading global consumer businesses, growing world-class brands, and providing operational and financial rigour. He served as Group CEO of Tesco plc from 2014 to 2020, where he transformed the business.

The Tesco parallel is instructive. Lewis's turnaround of Tesco involved brutal simplification — reducing SKU complexity, improving supply chain transparency, rebuilding supplier relationships, and restoring price competitiveness. Brand competitiveness and affordability are key pillars of Sir Dave Lewis's turnaround plan for Diageo.

Lewis has moved quickly. Diageo is combining its Africa and Europe divisions into a single unit as part of the restructuring, and Lewis plans to give greater power to managing directors who should hold more decision-making capabilities for their markets. This decentralisation logic directly addresses the organisational rigidity that allowed the LAC crisis to develop undetected.

The **Accelerate programme** is the operational expression of Lewis's strategy. The Accelerate programme was announced in May 2025 and is a three-year programme aiming to create a more agile global operating model with cash delivery, cost savings and deleveraging targets. Diageo is increasing its cost savings target by c.$125 million, to c.$625 million over the next three years. Savings are earmarked for reinvestment: savings are earmarked for "feet on the street" expansion in the US, a market where Diageo's premium tequila is under competitive pressure.

## PART VIII: INTEGRATED STRATEGIC DIAGNOSIS

Drawing together the preceding analyses, Diageo's strategic situation can be characterised as a **convergence of four distinct but interrelated crises**:

| Crisis Type | Manifestation | Root Cause |
|---|---|---|
| **Operational** | LAC inventory collapse; North America destocking | Lack of real-time sell-out data; bullwhip effect |
| **Competitive** | US tequila share loss; Cîroc reputational damage | Over-reliance on celebrity equity; category saturation |
| **Structural** | No/low alcohol megatrend; Gen Z moderation | Demographic and social shift; portfolio under-indexed to growth |
| **Organisational** | Slow response to LAC; leadership vacuum | Centralised decision-making; insufficient market agility |

These crises are not independent. The organisational rigidity that slowed the LAC response is the same rigidity that caused Diageo to miss the RTD opportunity in the US. The over-reliance on premiumisation as a singular growth strategy made the company vulnerable when economic pressure caused consumers to trade down. The leadership vacuum following the departure of the previous CEO compounded all of the above.

The **coherent thread** connecting these crises is this: Diageo built a strategy for a world of rising consumer confidence, accelerating premiumisation, and benign macroeconomics. When that world changed — abruptly and simultaneously across multiple geographies — the company lacked both the information architecture to detect the change quickly and the organisational agility to respond to it.

## PART IX: RECOMMENDATIONS AND ACTION PLAN

The following recommendations are structured around four strategic priorities, sequenced by urgency and time horizon.

---

### PRIORITY 1: REBUILD DISTRIBUTION INTELLIGENCE (Immediate: 0–12 months)

**The Recommendation**: Diageo must complete the rollout of a real-time sell-out data infrastructure across all Tier 1 and Tier 2 markets. This means mandating distributor sell-out reporting as a condition of commercial terms, deploying RFID tracking across the supply chain, and building a central demand-sensing capability that can detect inventory build-up before it becomes a crisis.

**The Logic**: The LAC crisis was fundamentally an information failure. Diageo's action plan for LAC was focused on expanding inventory visibility in the region and incentivising top distributors to report sell-out information, giving the company better position to forecast future requirements. This is the right approach, but it must be applied globally, not just in LAC. The North American inventory overhang and the China CWS exposure both suggest that the same blind spot exists in other markets. Without real-time demand visibility, Diageo will continue to be surprised by regional crises.

**The Mechanism**: Leverage Diageo's scale to make sell-out data reporting a non-negotiable commercial requirement. Use the Accelerate programme savings to fund the technology investment. Establish a Global Demand Intelligence function reporting directly to the CFO.

---

### PRIORITY 2: PORTFOLIO RATIONALISATION AND REBALANCING (Short-term: 6–18 months)

**The Recommendation**: Diageo should undertake a rigorous BCG-matrix-style review of its 200+ brand portfolio, with the explicit goal of concentrating investment in (a) its 13 billion-dollar brands; (b) the no/low alcohol segment; and (c) RTDs. Non-core brands that do not meet minimum return thresholds or strategic fit criteria should be divested.

**The Logic**: A portfolio of 200+ brands is a management distraction and a capital allocation inefficiency. Strategic acquisitions to enter or strengthen presence in emerging categories, such as the non-alcoholic spirits market, and divestment of non-core assets to optimise the brand portfolio and resource allocation are both necessary. The Cîroc episode illustrates the downside of maintaining brands that are not genuinely strategic: they consume management attention, create reputational risk, and deliver sub-threshold returns.

On the positive side, Guinness 0.0's growth trajectory — volume sales of Guinness 0.0 draught increased nearly 50% between February 2023 and February 2024 — demonstrates that Diageo's brand equity can be successfully extended into no/low alcohol. This playbook should be applied more aggressively across the portfolio: Tanqueray 0.0, Captain Morgan 0.0, and a Johnnie Walker 0.0 (whisky-flavoured non-alcoholic spirit) are all logical extensions.

**The Mechanism**: Commission a portfolio review within the first 90 days of the new CEO's tenure. Apply a consistent framework: Strategic Fit × Return on Capital × Market Growth Rate. Set a target of reducing the active brand portfolio by 20-25% within 18 months.

---

### PRIORITY 3: NORTH AMERICA COMPETITIVE REPAIR (Short-term: 6–24 months)

**The Recommendation**: Diageo must address its competitive position in North America — its largest market — through three simultaneous actions: (a) route-to-market enhancement; (b) tequila portfolio repositioning; and (c) RTD market entry at scale.

**The Logic**: New CEO Dave Lewis has said Diageo missed significant opportunities in the US in recent years, particularly in the ready-to-drink cocktails segment. North America generates approximately 39% of Diageo's net sales, and the structural health of this market is critical to the group's overall recovery. Diageo is adjusting prices for its Casamigos Tequila and launching an RTD under the brand, and is also putting more efforts into Astral, a brand sold at a lower price point than Casamigos or Don Julio — this is the right directional move but needs to be executed with greater urgency and resource commitment.

On route-to-market: Diageo's distribution is anchored by strategic wholesalers such as RNDC and Southern Glazer's in the US. The company needs to invest in "feet on the street" — direct sales force capability that can activate brands at the point of sale, particularly in the on-trade channel where premium spirits are most effectively marketed.

**The Mechanism**: Ring-fence a portion of the Accelerate programme savings specifically for North American route-to-market investment. Set clear KPIs for US market share recovery in tequila and whisky within 24 months. Use the FIFA World Cup 2026 sponsorship — Diageo has secured the first official spirits supporter partnership for the FIFA World Cup 2026 — as a platform to drive brand awareness across North America and LAC simultaneously.

### PRIORITY 4: STRUCTURAL POSITIONING FOR THE MODERATION MEGATREND (Medium-term: 18–36 months)

**The Recommendation**: Diageo should formally adopt a "Total Beverage Alcohol Plus" (TBA+) positioning that explicitly encompasses no/low alcohol as a core growth pillar, not a peripheral CSR initiative. This requires investment in dedicated no/low innovation capability, a separate commercial team focused on the no/low channel, and a shift in marketing investment towards moderation-positive messaging.

**The Logic**: The no- and low-alcohol beer, wine, and spirits market is expected to increase at a 4% compound annual growth rate, with growth projections from the IWSR suggesting that the sober movement is likely to continue over the next few years. The rise of alcohol-free and low-alcohol beverages reflects the evolving "sober-curious" movement. A growing segment of consumers is prioritising health and mindfulness over habitual or socially motivated drinking.

Diageo has the brand equity and distribution infrastructure to be the dominant player in no/low alcohol. Guinness 0.0 is proof of concept. The risk is that the company treats no/low as a defensive hedge rather than a genuine growth opportunity, under-investing relative to the scale of the structural shift. Diageo's strategic priorities include capitalising on the moderation trend with non-alcoholic offerings, leveraging its broad portfolio of 13 billion-dollar brands.

**The Mechanism**: Establish a dedicated No/Low Innovation Centre of Excellence, with a budget ring-fenced from the core A&P allocation. Set a target of 10% of net sales from no/low products by 2030. Partner with health and wellness platforms (fitness apps, nutrition brands) to reach the sober-curious consumer where they already are.

## CONCLUSION

Diageo's strategic situation is serious but not irreversible. The company possesses genuinely rare and inimitable assets — its brand portfolio, its maturing whisky inventory, its global distribution infrastructure — that no competitor can replicate quickly. The crisis is fundamentally one of **execution and adaptation**, not of fundamental strategic obsolescence.

The analytical chain developed in this case study can be summarised as follows:

1. **PESTEL** reveals that Diageo faces a convergence of macroeconomic headwinds, structural social change (moderation), and political risk (tariffs) that collectively reduce the tailwinds that sustained its growth through the 2010s.

2. **Porter's Five Forces** shows that while the premium spirits industry remains structurally attractive, competitive rivalry is intensifying and the threat of substitutes — particularly no/low alcohol — is rising materially.

3. **RBV analysis** identifies Diageo's brand portfolio and maturing inventory as genuine VRIN assets, but also exposes a critical capability gap in distribution intelligence that allowed the LAC crisis to develop undetected.

4. **Ansoff analysis** maps the growth options: premiumisation (market penetration), Africa/Asia (market development), no/low/RTDs (product development), and adjacent acquisitions (diversification) — with the clearest near-term returns available from product development and market development.

5. **The Accelerate programme** and Dave Lewis's leadership represent the right strategic direction — decentralisation, cost discipline, and reinvestment in commercial capability — but must be executed with sufficient speed and resource commitment to arrest the competitive deterioration in North America before it becomes structural.

The fundamental strategic imperative is this: Diageo must **repair its operational foundations** while simultaneously **repositioning its portfolio** for a world in which moderation is not a niche but a mainstream consumer behaviour. Companies that treat these as sequential tasks — fix operations first, then innovate — will find that the market has moved on before the repair is complete. The two must proceed in parallel, which is precisely the organisational challenge that the Accelerate programme, if executed with discipline, is designed to address.
