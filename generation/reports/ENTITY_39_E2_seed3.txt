# Strategic Case Study: Trustpilot and the Integrity Paradox — When the Asset Is Also the Liability

---

## 1. Introduction and Situational Overview

Trustpilot Group plc, founded in 2007, is a Danish consumer business that operates a review website hosting reviews of businesses worldwide. As of June 2025, Trustpilot hosts 330 million reviews and has 60 million monthly active users, offering freemium services to businesses. Trustpilot was listed on the London Stock Exchange in March 2021, signalling its ambition to scale from a European review aggregator into the global "Universal Symbol of Trust."

Financially, the company has been on a strong trajectory. Revenue grew 18% in constant currency to $210.7 million in FY2024 (2023: $176.4 million), and Trustpilot delivered a record adjusted EBITDA of $24.1 million (2023: $15.5 million). Profit before tax was $5.2 million, reversing a $1.9 million loss the prior year.

Yet beneath this headline performance lies a structural strategic tension that this case study will analyse: **Trustpilot's openness — the very characteristic that gives it scale and consumer credibility — simultaneously makes it vulnerable to manipulation, regulatory sanction, and competitive substitution.** This tension is what we term the **Integrity Paradox**: the platform's core asset (open, voluminous, independent reviews) is also its core liability.

The central strategic question is: *How should Trustpilot resolve the trade-off between openness (which drives scale and consumer trust) and verification (which drives business confidence and regulatory compliance) without undermining either side of its two-sided market?*

## 2. Analytical Framework

This case study applies four complementary analytical lenses in sequence, each building on the last:

1. **Two-Sided Platform Theory** (Rochet & Tirole, 2003) — to diagnose the structural logic of the business model and where the tension originates.

3. **SWOT Analysis** — to synthesise internal capabilities and external pressures into a coherent strategic position.
4. **The Strategic Options Matrix** — to evaluate and prioritise the recommended course of action.

## 3. Two-Sided Platform Analysis: Where the Tension Originates

### 3.1 The Platform Structure

Trustpilot operates an open, two-sided review platform where consumers submit reviews of recent experiences and businesses claim profiles, invite reviews, and showcase ratings to boost conversion and trust. This is a textbook two-sided market: the platform creates value by connecting two distinct user groups — consumers (who seek reliable purchasing signals) and businesses (who seek reputation management tools) — and must attract and retain both simultaneously.

The theoretical foundation here is Rochet and Tirole's (2003) insight that in two-sided markets, platforms must choose a **price structure, not merely a price level**. Consistent with this, Trustpilot aggregates verified reviews, boosts visibility in search, and sells subscription tools that help companies capture, analyse, and act on customer sentiment; this flywheel underpins its monetisation and growth. Consumers receive the service free of charge (subsidised side), while businesses pay subscription fees (monetised side) — a pricing structure that maximises consumer-side participation to generate the data asset that businesses pay for.

### 3.2 The Network Effect Flywheel

The platform connects consumers with more than 1.1 million registered domains, driving transparent feedback loops that influence global spending. This scale matters because, as Rochet and Tirole (2003) note, platforms exhibit **cross-side network effects**: more consumers posting reviews increases the value to businesses (richer data, stronger social proof), and more businesses on the platform increases the value to consumers (broader coverage). The flywheel is self-reinforcing — up to a point.

In 2024, more than 61 million reviews were written on Trustpilot, a 15% year-on-year increase, with 301 million total active reviews representing a 23% increase year-on-year. This scale is a genuine moat: it is very difficult for a new entrant to replicate 330 million reviews organically.

### 3.3 Where the Paradox Emerges

The Integrity Paradox emerges directly from the platform's open architecture. Trustpilot operates what is called an 'open' platform, which means that anyone with a Trustpilot account can leave a review on any business, whether they've had an experience with that business or not, leaving businesses vulnerable to fake review attacks and rogue opinions. This openness is what enables the massive consumer-side scale — but it is precisely what enables manipulation.

Two-sided platform theory predicts that when one side of a platform can engage in behaviour that degrades value for the other side, the platform faces a **governance problem** that, if unresolved, triggers a collapse of cross-side network effects. Fake reviews degrade consumer-side trust; degraded consumer trust reduces the value businesses receive from the platform; reduced business confidence reduces subscription revenue. The causal chain is direct, and the data confirm the problem is material: Trustpilot removed 4.5 million fake reviews in 2024, amounting to 7.4% of the total number of reviews submitted, up from 6.1% in 2023.

Furthermore, independent analysis suggests the problem may be deeper than Trustpilot's own reporting indicates. SafePaper's analysis found the Transparency Company determined up to 14% of 70 million reviews were likely fake, with 2.3 million suspected AI-generated. Even within Trustpilot's own guidelines, a further manipulation is documented: SafePaper documented legitimate businesses manipulating within guidelines — when users clicked four or five stars, reviews went to Trustpilot; below four stars redirected to internal surveys. Technically compliant, yet engineered so only satisfied customers post publicly. This practice — **review gating** — is explicitly prohibited by Trustpilot's own business guidelines, which state that businesses must not be "selective with invitations, by picking and choosing which customers you invite" to include "only those people who you know have had a positive experience." Yet enforcement of this rule is structurally difficult precisely because Trustpilot's paid tools give businesses the capability to segment their customer base before sending invitations.

This creates a **principal-agent problem** at the heart of the business model: Trustpilot's paying customers (businesses) have financial incentives to game the system, while Trustpilot's credibility with its non-paying users (consumers) depends on preventing that gaming. The platform is simultaneously the agent of both principals, and their interests conflict.

## 4. Porter's Five Forces Analysis

Porter's Five Forces framework (1979) identifies the structural determinants of industry profitability and competitive pressure. Applied to Trustpilot, it reveals why the Integrity Paradox is strategically dangerous rather than merely reputationally inconvenient.

### Force 1: Threat of New Entrants — **Moderate to Low**

The scale moat created by 330 million reviews is substantial. However, the threat is not zero. Feefo starts at £149, whereas Trustpilot Plus starts at £259 (which is their entry plan). Closed-platform competitors such as Feefo compete on a fundamentally different value proposition — verified reviews — at a lower price point, targeting the segment of businesses most damaged by Trustpilot's openness. The barrier to entry for a *verified* platform is lower than for an *open* one, because verified platforms do not need to achieve consumer-side critical mass before they can offer businesses a credible product. This means the threat of entry is most acute in the premium, verification-focused segment, which is precisely where regulatory pressure is pushing the market.

### Force 2: Bargaining Power of Buyers (Businesses) — **Moderate and Rising**

Trustpilot's paid tiers — starting around $250/month for basic business features — put meaningful review management out of reach for smaller operations. SMB customers are price-sensitive and have increasing alternatives. More importantly, one verified reviewer wrote that they "had to pay to remove libelous reviews" from competitors, with multiple reviewers reporting feeling pressured during renewal negotiations. This indicates that Trustpilot's negotiating leverage over businesses is partly coercive — businesses pay partly to manage reputational risk that the open platform itself creates. This is a structurally fragile source of revenue: it depends on the continuation of the very problem (fake/competitor reviews) that Trustpilot is publicly committed to solving. If Trustpilot succeeds in eliminating fake reviews, it removes a key driver of business subscription renewals.

### Force 3: Bargaining Power of Suppliers — **Low**

Trustpilot's "suppliers" are consumers who write reviews — and they contribute for free. This is a structural advantage. However, if consumer trust in the platform declines (due to fake review scandals), supply of genuine reviews could dry up, which would be existential. The platform's supplier relationships are therefore mediated entirely through trust, making the Integrity Paradox a supplier-side risk as much as a regulatory one.

### Force 4: Threat of Substitutes — **High**

This is the most acute competitive force. Substitutes operate across multiple dimensions:

- **Google Reviews**: Google Reviews is Trustpilot's top competitor, leveraging search and Maps dominance to capture local feedback. Trustpilot counters with stronger verification, business tools, and SEO value. Google Reviews is free, ubiquitous, and directly integrated into the world's dominant search engine — a formidable substitute for SMBs.
- **Closed/verified platforms**: Feefo uses a closed-loop system — invitations go out after purchase, and only that customer can respond. No fake reviews, no competitor sabotage, no disgruntled non-customers. Reviews are always authentic because Feefo verifies the purchase before accepting the review. This is a direct challenge to Trustpilot's credibility proposition.
- **Social commerce**: TikTok and Instagram are shifting discovery and reputation toward social proof formats, forcing Trustpilot to adapt how review data is surfaced outside its domain.

The substitution threat is compounded by a structural weakness: having millions of reviews on Trustpilot will not make a business easier to find for target audiences — nobody ends up on a Trustpilot page listing businesses in a given category through organic search in the way they do on Google Maps or Yelp. This undermines Trustpilot's discoverability value proposition for SMBs.

### Force 5: Rivalry Among Existing Competitors — **Moderate but Intensifying**

Yotpo integrates tightly with platforms like Shopify to drive retention and monetisation, positioning itself as a growth tool beyond pure reviews. G2 and Capterra specialise in technical, feature-rich reviews for software buyers, limiting Trustpilot's influence in B2B software procurement decisions. The competitive landscape is fragmenting by vertical and use case, meaning Trustpilot faces not one competitor but a portfolio of specialists each attacking a different segment.

**Five Forces Summary**: The most dangerous forces are the **threat of substitutes** (high, multi-directional) and **rising buyer power** (driven by price sensitivity and growing alternatives). The scale moat reduces the threat of new entrants but does not eliminate it, particularly in the verified-review segment. The overall implication is that Trustpilot's competitive position is defensible but not durable without resolving the Integrity Paradox — because the paradox is what makes substitutes attractive.

## 5. SWOT Analysis

The SWOT framework synthesises the preceding analysis into a coherent strategic picture, distinguishing internal factors (Strengths, Weaknesses) from external ones (Opportunities, Threats).

### Strengths

**S1 — Scale and network effects**: 301 million total active reviews and 61 million written in 2024 alone create a data asset that competitors cannot replicate quickly. Scale also improves the accuracy of fraud detection models, creating a virtuous cycle.

**S2 — Revenue growth and profitability inflection**: Revenue of $210.7 million, adjusted EBITDA of $24.1 million, and a return to profit before tax of $5.2 million demonstrate that the business model can generate returns at scale — a critical signal for investors and a source of capital for strategic investment.

**S3 — AI-driven integrity infrastructure**: In 2024, 90% of detected fake reviews were removed automatically by technology using machine learning, neural networks, and generative AI to identify patterns linked to fabricated content — 53% more reviews removed automatically than in 2023. This is a genuine technical capability that is improving.

**S4 — Enterprise integration momentum**: Trustpilot's 2023 strategy to integrate directly with platforms like Shopify Plus and Stripe fuelled a 22% year-on-year surge in enterprise revenue by Q4 2024.

**S5 — Regulatory engagement**: In 2023 alone, Trustpilot eliminated over 3.3 million fraudulent reviews and brought legal action against review sellers, being cited 27 times in the FTC's final ruling on fake reviews. This positions Trustpilot as a regulatory partner rather than a target — a valuable strategic asset.

### Weaknesses

**W1 — The open platform vulnerability**: As established above, the open architecture leaves businesses vulnerable to fake review attacks and rogue opinions, and independent research shows over a third of consumers (36%) do not trust reviews on open platforms. This is a fundamental trust deficit that the business model structurally generates.

**W2 — Review gating and selective invitation**: SafePaper documented legitimate businesses manipulating within guidelines — technically compliant, yet engineered so only satisfied customers post publicly. Trustpilot's inability to prevent this practice without undermining its business model is a structural weakness.

**W3 — SMB pricing vulnerability**: Trustpilot's paid tiers starting around $250/month put meaningful review management out of reach for smaller operations, making the SMB segment price-sensitive and churn-prone, particularly during economic downturns.

**W4 — Coercive subscription dynamic**: The dynamic whereby businesses pay partly to manage reputational risks created by the open platform is reputationally fragile and strategically self-defeating.

### Opportunities

**O1 — Regulatory tailwinds**: The UK Digital Markets, Competition and Consumers Act 2024 (DMCC Act), which came into force in April 2025, makes it illegal to write or commission fake reviews, offer undisclosed incentives for positive feedback, or publish reviews that haven't been properly verified for authenticity. This creates a level playing field that rewards platforms with strong integrity infrastructure — precisely Trustpilot's stated investment area.

**O2 — North American expansion**: Trustpilot Group expects FY2024 bookings of $239 million, up 23% year-on-year, reflecting a strong performance across focus markets, particularly in North America. North America remains underpenetrated relative to Europe, representing a large addressable market.

**O3 — AI-powered review intelligence**: Trustpilot operates as an open-review ecosystem combining AdTech and SaaS to monetise reputation services, AI sentiment tools, and subscription tiers for businesses. The growing sophistication of AI creates opportunities to offer businesses richer analytics — sentiment analysis, competitive benchmarking, service recovery — that transform Trustpilot from a review host into a strategic intelligence platform.

**O4 — AI search citation**: The rise of AI-powered search (Google AI Overviews, ChatGPT, Perplexity) creates a new distribution channel. Trustpilot's content is being used by AI search providers, contributing to a surge in revenues. If Trustpilot's reviews become a trusted source for AI-generated answers, the platform's strategic value multiplies beyond its own traffic.

### Threats

**T1 — Regulatory sanction**: In March 2026, Italy's competition authority (AGCM) fined Trustpilot €4 million for failing to adequately verify the authenticity of its reviews and misleading consumers about how its services work, with the company saying it would appeal. This is not an isolated incident — it signals a regulatory trend that could impose material costs and reputational damage.

**T2 — Sophisticated AI-generated fake reviews**: SafePaper's analysis identified 2.3 million suspected AI-generated reviews in a single year. As generative AI lowers the cost of producing convincing fake reviews at scale, the arms race between fraud and detection intensifies.

**T3 — Platform disintermediation**: Trustpilot inadvertently uses review content to outrank brands in Google search, meaning the investment businesses put into building brand awareness goes to Trustpilot, which then promotes their closest competitors on their Trustpilot merchant page. This creates structural resentment among businesses and incentivises them to seek alternatives.

**T4 — SMB budget volatility**: SMB marketing-budget cuts during economic softness can slow new bookings and upsells; in 2023–2024, SMB spend volatility illustrated vulnerability in the online review platform strategy.

## 6. The Core Strategic Problem: A Synthesised Diagnosis

The SWOT and Five Forces analyses converge on a single diagnosis. Trustpilot's competitive position rests on a **trust asset** — the credibility of its reviews in the eyes of consumers. That trust asset is what businesses pay for. But the open architecture that generates scale (and therefore the trust asset) also enables manipulation that erodes it. The Integrity Paradox is therefore not a peripheral compliance issue: it is the central strategic problem.

The paradox has three dimensions that must be addressed simultaneously:

1. **The Governance Dimension**: Trustpilot cannot fully police its paying customers (businesses) without reducing their willingness to pay, and cannot fully police non-paying users (consumers) without reducing review volume.
2. **The Regulatory Dimension**: The Italian fine and the DMCC Act signal that regulators are no longer willing to accept self-regulation as sufficient. The cost of inaction is rising.
3. **The Competitive Dimension**: Every fake review scandal is an advertisement for verified-review competitors like Feefo. The longer the paradox persists, the more it drives the premium segment toward closed platforms.

The strategic implication is clear: **Trustpilot must move from a posture of reactive detection to one of proactive structural verification, without abandoning the openness that generates its scale advantage.** This is a genuine strategic dilemma — not a false one — because full verification would eliminate the open platform's consumer-side scale, while pure openness invites regulatory sanction and competitive substitution.

## 7. Strategic Options Analysis

Three broad strategic options present themselves:

| Option | Description | Key Risk |
|---|---|---|
| **A — Status Quo Plus** | Incrementally improve AI detection; maintain open platform; increase lobbying | Regulatory sanction escalates; verified competitors capture premium segment |
| **B — Full Verification Pivot** | Shift to closed, purchase-verified model like Feefo | Destroys consumer-side scale; triggers network effect collapse |
| **C — Tiered Trust Architecture** | Introduce verification tiers alongside open reviews; differentiate product by trust level | Execution complexity; risk of consumer confusion |

**Option A** is the path of least resistance but is strategically untenable. The Italian fine demonstrates that regulators have moved beyond accepting incremental improvement, and the DMCC Act creates legal liability for platforms that cannot demonstrate adequate verification. Moreover, the data show that fake review rates are *rising* in absolute terms even as detection improves — from 3.3 million removed in 2023 to 4.5 million in 2024 — because the volume of submitted reviews is growing faster than fraud prevention capacity.

**Option B** is strategically suicidal. The consumer-side scale of 330 million reviews is Trustpilot's primary competitive moat. Abandoning openness would hand that moat to Google Reviews, which already operates at far greater scale and at zero cost to businesses. Trustpilot cannot win a verification-only competition against Feefo (which has a 17-year head start in that model) and cannot win a free-review competition against Google.

**Option C — the Tiered Trust Architecture** — is the strategically correct response. It resolves the paradox by making the trade-off *explicit and visible* rather than pretending it does not exist. The logic is as follows: if Trustpilot cannot eliminate the tension between openness and verification, it should productise that tension — creating a spectrum of trust signals that serve different consumer needs and business budgets, while maintaining the open platform's scale advantage.

## 8. Recommendations: A Tiered Trust Architecture

The following action plan operationalises Option C across four strategic pillars.

### Pillar 1: Introduce a Three-Tier Review Verification System

Trustpilot should introduce a publicly visible, consumer-facing trust taxonomy for every review on the platform:

- **Tier 1 — Open Review**: Any consumer can post; AI-moderated; labelled as "Community Review."
- **Tier 2 — Invited Review**: Posted in response to a business invitation; labelled "Invited Review" with timestamp relative to purchase.
- **Tier 3 — Verified Review**: Purchase cryptographically confirmed via API integration with the business's transaction system (Shopify, Stripe, etc.); labelled "Verified Purchase Review."

**Why this resolves the paradox**: It preserves the open platform's consumer-side scale (Tier 1 remains open) while giving businesses a credible, regulatorily compliant product (Tier 3) and giving consumers the information they need to calibrate their trust. In a recent study, over a third of consumers (36%) do not trust reviews on open platforms — but this distrust is likely concentrated among consumers who cannot distinguish verified from unverified reviews. Making the distinction visible addresses the root cause of distrust without eliminating openness.

This approach is also consistent with the DMCC Act's requirements. The DMCC Act makes it illegal to publish reviews that haven't been properly verified for authenticity — but it does not prohibit open reviews; it requires that their provenance be disclosed. A tiered system satisfies this requirement transparently.

### Pillar 2: Restructure Business Subscriptions Around Verification, Not Reputation Management

Currently, Trustpilot's subscription model partially monetises businesses' anxiety about reputational risk on the open platform — a coercive dynamic that is both ethically questionable and strategically fragile. The subscription model should be restructured so that businesses pay for **verification capability and analytical intelligence**, not for damage limitation.

Concretely, this means:

- **Tier 3 Verification Access** (API integration with transaction systems) should be the primary value proposition of paid subscriptions, replacing the current emphasis on review invitation tools.
- **AI-powered sentiment analytics** — identifying service failure patterns, competitive benchmarking, customer journey insights — should be bundled into higher subscription tiers, monetising the data asset rather than the anxiety.

The rationale is grounded in the Five Forces analysis: the coercive subscription dynamic increases buyer bargaining power (businesses resent paying to manage risks the platform creates) and makes the product substitutable (businesses can switch to Google Reviews, which creates no such risk). Restructuring around genuine value creation — verified reviews and actionable intelligence — reduces churn and increases switching costs.

Trustpilot's 2023 strategy to integrate directly with platforms like Shopify Plus and Stripe fuelled a 22% year-on-year surge in enterprise revenue, demonstrating that deep integration is commercially viable. This pillar accelerates and systematises that strategy.

### Pillar 3: Establish a Regulatory Compliance Moat

Trustpilot's existing regulatory engagement is a strength that should be converted into a durable competitive advantage. Trustpilot was cited 27 times in the FTC's final ruling on fake reviews, and in 2024, it became a founding member of the Coalition for Trusted Reviews, a cross-industry body that has helped promote best practices in the reviews industry and brought together experts and policymakers.

The strategic logic is to shape regulation in ways that raise the compliance cost for competitors more than for Trustpilot. Specifically, Trustpilot should advocate for:

- **Mandatory provenance disclosure** (already required by DMCC Act): Trustpilot's tiered system already satisfies this; closed competitors like Feefo satisfy it differently; Google Reviews currently does not satisfy it at all, creating a regulatory gap that Trustpilot can exploit.
- **Platform liability for systematic review gating**: Trustpilot's own guidelines already prohibit review gating, but enforcement is weak. Advocating for regulatory enforcement of anti-gating rules would reduce the practice across the industry, levelling the playing field while allowing Trustpilot to demonstrate superior compliance.

This approach converts regulatory pressure from a threat (T1 in the SWOT) into a competitive weapon — a classic example of what Porter (1979) described as using industry structure to shape competitive dynamics in one's favour.

### Pillar 4: Invest in AI-Powered Trust Signals for the Post-Search Era

The rise of AI-powered search is both an opportunity and a threat. Trustpilot's content is being used by AI search providers, contributing to a surge in revenues. However, if AI search engines begin to treat all review platforms equivalently — or if they develop their own review aggregation — Trustpilot's distribution advantage could erode rapidly.

Trustpilot should invest in becoming the **authoritative structured data source** for AI search, by:

- Publishing a public API that AI search providers can use to access verified (Tier 3) reviews with full provenance metadata. This creates a data licensing revenue stream while embedding Trustpilot's content in AI-generated answers.
- Developing a **Trust Score API** that businesses can embed in their own digital properties — websites, apps, email signatures — distributing Trustpilot's trust signals beyond its own domain and reducing dependency on search traffic.

The strategic rationale is that Trustpilot's long-term value lies not in being a destination website (where Google Reviews is a superior competitor) but in being the **trust infrastructure layer** of global e-commerce — a position that is defensible, scalable, and consistent with the stated mission of becoming the "Universal Symbol of Trust."

## 10. Conclusion

This case study has demonstrated, through a sequential application of two-sided platform theory, Porter's Five Forces, and SWOT analysis, that Trustpilot's strategic situation is defined by a single structural tension: the **Integrity Paradox**. The openness that generates the scale advantage enabling Trustpilot's network effects also enables the manipulation that erodes the trust asset those network effects depend upon.

The analysis shows that this paradox cannot be resolved by incremental improvements to fraud detection (Option A), nor by abandoning openness for a verified-only model (Option B). The only strategically coherent response is a **Tiered Trust Architecture** (Option C) that makes the trade-off between openness and verification explicit, visible, and commercially productive.

The four pillars of the recommended action plan — tiered verification, restructured subscriptions, regulatory moat-building, and AI distribution — are not independent initiatives. They form a coherent chain: verification tiers create the product differentiation that justifies subscription restructuring; subscription restructuring funds the AI investment that enables better verification; better verification enables regulatory advocacy; regulatory advocacy shapes the competitive environment in Trustpilot's favour. Each step is warranted by the preceding analysis, and each step reinforces the others.

Trustpilot's mission is to become the Universal Symbol of Trust, and its strong financial results are evidence that the approach is working. But financial momentum is not the same as strategic durability. The Integrity Paradox, if unresolved, will eventually reverse that momentum — as the Italian fine and the DMCC Act already signal. The window to act proactively, rather than reactively, is now.
