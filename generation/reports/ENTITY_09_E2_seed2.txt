# Strategic Case Study: Charoen Pokphand Foods (CPF) — Navigating the Tension Between Commodity Vulnerability and Sustainable Competitive Advantage

---

## 1. Introduction and Case Framing

This case study analyses the strategic situation facing Charoen Pokphand Foods Public Company Limited (CPF), a Thai-headquartered multinational agro-industrial and food corporation. The central strategic problem is a structural one: CPF's vertically integrated model, which has historically been its greatest source of competitive advantage, simultaneously exposes it to acute commodity-cycle risk and an emerging sustainability-legitimacy deficit. The 2023 near-collapse of profitability — followed by a dramatic 2024 recovery — makes this tension visible and urgent. The case uses Porter's Five Forces, the Resource-Based View (RBV), the Value Chain, PESTEL analysis, and the Ansoff Matrix to diagnose the situation and generate a coherent action plan.

---

## 2. Company Background

Charoen Pokphand Foods Public Company Limited and its subsidiaries conduct business under the vision "Kitchen of the World," with a commitment to creating sustainable food security for the world through developing high-quality, safe, and nutritious products. CPF is the world's largest producer of feed and shrimp, and a global top-three producer of poultry and pork.

CPF operates a vertically integrated agro-industrial and food business in animal proteins, with main animals being swine, broiler, shrimp, and layers. The company and its joint investments have a manufacturing base in 17 countries. Of CPF's 2024 revenue, 63% came from overseas operations in 13 countries, 31% from Thailand, and 6% from exports to over 50 countries.

Financially, total sales revenue in 2024 amounted to THB 580,747 million, with total assets valued at THB 876,724 million as of the end of 2024. CPF reported a net profit of THB 19.5 billion in 2024, a significant 476% turnaround from the previous year's net loss.

## 3. The Strategic Situation: A Crisis That Reveals a Structural Problem

### 3.1 The 2023 Collapse and 2024 Recovery

The 2023 financial year crystallised CPF's core strategic vulnerability. CPF's sales revenue in 2023 was THB 585,844 million, a drop of 4.6% or THB 28,353 million from 2022, comprising 62% from international operations, 32% from Thailand domestic operations, and 6% from Thailand export operations. More critically, gross profit in 2023 amounted to THB 61,201 million, a decrease of 25% from the previous year; the gross profit margin fell from 13.3% in 2022 to 10.4% in 2023.

The proximate cause was a commodity price shock. The main reason for the decline in revenue was lower swine prices than in 2022 and translation losses from overseas subsidiaries caused by the volatility of foreign exchange. Specifically, swine prices in Thailand decreased by approximately 31% in 2023, due in part to illegal pork imports which forced the price of swine below the cost of operation. 2023 profits were also down because of the increase in production costs, including feed raw material prices and the costs of energy. The 2023 Annual Report acknowledged these headwinds directly: challenges included a less-than-anticipated rise in purchasing power, heightened agricultural raw material expenses due to political tensions, escalated interest rates, and an oversupply of meats across several nations.

Recovery came in 2024 as commodity conditions normalised. Improvements in financial performance over the past quarter represent a recovery in the multinational agri-food group's businesses; the gross profit margin of around 15% was driven mainly by lower animal production costs — particularly for raw materials — as well as improvements in cost management and production efficiency, and higher prices for pig meat.

**The analytical inference here is critical**: the speed with which CPF's profitability collapsed in 2023 and rebounded in 2024 demonstrates that earnings are primarily driven by exogenous commodity prices rather than by durable, internally controlled competitive advantage. A business whose profitability is structurally tied to the price of corn, soybean meal, and live animal market prices — rather than to brand premium, proprietary technology, or customer switching costs — faces persistent earnings volatility. This is the central strategic problem.

### 3.2 The Leverage Dimension

Charoen Pokphand's total debt-to-equity ratio is 196.32%. High financial leverage amplifies the operational risk described above: when commodity margins compress, as in 2023, the fixed cost of debt service becomes a disproportionate burden, threatening solvency as well as profitability. This means that CPF's strategic situation is not merely a cyclical inconvenience but a structural vulnerability that requires deliberate strategic action.

## 4. External Environment Analysis: PESTEL

**Political:** CPF operates across 17 countries, creating regulatory heterogeneity. The 2023 illegal pork import problem in Thailand — which contributed to a 31% domestic swine price decline — illustrates how trade policy in one market can devastate the profitability of an integrated producer. The salient human rights issues identified through CPF's own due diligence include working conditions, health and safety of employees, community health and safety, data privacy, and use of illegal labour (child labour, forced labour, and illegal migrant workers) in supply chains, sub-contractors, and suppliers. These issues have created regulatory and reputational exposure across Western markets.

**Economic:** With a burgeoning population, rising income levels, and increasing demand for protein-rich foods, the poultry market in Asia is poised for significant growth; the demand for chicken meat in Asia is expected to reach up to 154 million metric tons by 2031. The Asia-Pacific region is expected to witness substantial growth in the poultry products market, registering a CAGR of 7.8% by 2028. This macro tailwind is favourable for CPF's core business.

**Social:** The Asia Pacific region is witnessing a surge in protein consumption, driven by rising health consciousness, an aging population, and a shift towards plant-based diets. Consumer preferences are bifurcating: growing middle classes in Asia demand more animal protein, while health- and environment-conscious consumers in developed markets increasingly seek alternatives. CPF must serve both.

**Technological:** CP Foods is integrating AI and cutting-edge innovations across all production processes, aiming to empower employees to become 'innovators' who contribute valuable advancements to both the organisation and society. CP Foods is pushing forward its "Smart Farm" scheme, whereby digital and Blockchain technology and innovations are being applied for greater farming efficiency. Technology adoption is both an opportunity and a competitive necessity, as rivals globally are making similar investments.

**Environmental:** CPF is the first food processing company in the world with both near- and long-term Forest, Land and Agriculture (FLAG) science-based targets validated by the Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi); CPF commits to a 42% reduction and 90% reduction of all three scopes of greenhouse gas non-FLAG emissions by 2030 and 2050 respectively. However, only 59% of key raw materials are currently traceable (in operations in Thailand, Laos, and Cambodia). The gap between the commitment and the current state of traceability represents an execution risk.

**Legal:** CPF's supply chain controversies have triggered legal exposure. In January 2017, the United States District Court ruled on multiple grounds in favour of CP Foods in relation to litigation brought against them, which claimed damages related to the alleged presence of human rights abuses in the supply chain for Thai shrimp. While CPF prevailed legally, the reputational damage was significant: in Australia, Woolworths stocks only CPF-Vietnam products and Metcash, wholesale supplier to IGA, has eliminated CPF SKUs from their inventory. This illustrates how ESG failures translate directly into market access losses, making supply chain integrity a strategic — not merely ethical — imperative.

## 5. Industry Analysis: Porter's Five Forces

**Threat of New Entrants — LOW to MODERATE.** CPF's scale and vertical integration create formidable entry barriers. In Thailand, CPF holds a dominant position with approximately 42% market share in animal feed as of 2024, more than double that of its nearest competitor, bolstered by integrated operations. Replicating this integrated infrastructure across 17 countries requires decades of capital investment and relationship-building. However, in individual product segments (e.g., plant-based protein), entry barriers are lower, as new technology-driven entrants face fewer legacy infrastructure requirements.

**Bargaining Power of Suppliers — MODERATE to HIGH.** CPF's dependence on globally traded commodities limits its pricing power over inputs. CPF is heavily reliant on the import of raw materials needed for animal feed production; soybeans and corn are essential components for feed manufacturing, sourced primarily from the USA, Brazil, and Argentina. Corn and soybean meal make up approximately 40% and 24% of raw material costs respectively. These inputs are priced on global commodity markets, meaning CPF cannot negotiate price — it must absorb or hedge. This is a structural weakness that vertical integration does not eliminate.

**Bargaining Power of Buyers — MODERATE.** CPF sells to a diversified customer base — retailers, food service operators, industrial buyers, and end consumers — across more than 50 countries. This diversification moderates buyer power. However, in Western retail markets, large supermarket chains (e.g., Walmart, Tesco, Carrefour) hold significant leverage. The loss of Australian retail shelf space following supply chain controversies demonstrates how buyer power can be weaponised by ESG concerns.

**Threat of Substitutes — GROWING.** CPF's CEO noted that "plant-based protein trends are growing rapidly" and that "the demands of today's consumers are becoming more complex," requiring products that resemble meat in all aspects such as texture, taste, and nutritional value. China expanded the production of plant-based meat alternatives using soy and pea proteins; in Southeast Asia, countries including Thailand and Vietnam saw strong growth in functional beverages. The substitute threat is not yet existential for CPF's core business, but it is growing fastest in the high-margin, value-added segment that CPF needs to pivot toward.

**Competitive Rivalry — HIGH.** Major rivals in agribusiness include Betagro (Thailand), BRF and JBS (global poultry/meat), and New Hope Group (China) for feed and livestock. In global commodity protein markets, competition is primarily on cost and scale. CPF's advantage here is its vertical integration, but rivals like JBS and Tyson are similarly large and integrated. Rivalry is intensifying in value-added food segments, where CPF must compete on brand and innovation rather than cost.

**Five Forces Summary:** The overall industry structure is moderately attractive for an incumbent of CPF's scale, but the commodity input cost structure and growing substitute threat mean that sustained profitability requires a deliberate move up the value chain — from commodity protein production toward differentiated, branded, value-added food products.

## 6. Internal Analysis: Resource-Based View (RBV) and Value Chain

### 6.1 Resource-Based View

The RBV framework (Barney, 1991) asks whether a firm's resources are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN). CPF's key resources are assessed below.

**Vertical Integration Architecture — VRIN.** CP Foods has established a value chain from the upstream to the downstream of the food industry, from livestock feed to stock breeding, fisheries, processed foods, and retail. CPF's integrated food process is designed to deliver top-quality products in nutrition, taste, food safety, and traceability, with the "Sustainovation" concept leveraging technology and innovation to improve efficiency and minimise environmental impact. This architecture is valuable (it enables cost control and quality consistency), rare (few competitors span the entire chain at this scale), inimitable (it took decades to build), and non-substitutable (no single alternative resource replicates its function). It is CPF's most durable competitive asset.

**Scale and Market Position — Valuable, Rare, partially Imitable.** CPF is the world's largest producer of feed and shrimp and a global top-three producer of poultry and pork. Scale enables cost advantages in procurement, processing, and logistics. However, scale alone is not sufficient for sustained competitive advantage if it is not paired with differentiation, because scale-based competition invites rivals to achieve similar scale.

**Technology and Innovation Capability — Valuable, increasingly Rare.** CP Foods is integrating AI and cutting-edge innovations across all production processes through the Feed Sustainovation 2024 initiative. The Smart Farm scheme applies digital and Blockchain technology for greater farming efficiency, ensuring good health for animals and meeting international animal welfare standards. If CPF can build proprietary AI-driven farming and supply chain management systems, this resource could become inimitable.

**Brand and Consumer Trust — Weak in Global Consumer Markets.** CPF's brands (Chester's, Five Stars, Meat Zero) have strong regional recognition but limited global consumer brand equity. The supply chain controversies have actively damaged trust in Western markets. This is a resource gap — the absence of a strong global consumer brand is a strategic liability as CPF attempts to move toward premium, value-added products.

### 6.2 Value Chain Analysis

Porter's Value Chain distinguishes primary activities (inbound logistics, operations, outbound logistics, marketing and sales, service) from support activities (firm infrastructure, HR, technology, procurement).

**Primary Activities:**
- *Inbound Logistics:* CPF sources soybeans, corn, and fishmeal from global commodity markets. This is the value chain's most exposed point — commodity price volatility enters here. The fact that only 59% of key raw materials are currently traceable also means that supply chain risks (ESG, quality, disruption) are not fully visible.
- *Operations:* Farming operations were the largest revenue generator at 55%, followed by animal feed at 23% and food products at 22%. The dominance of farming and feed (78% of revenue) over processed food (22%) means that most of CPF's value creation still occurs in the lower-margin, commodity-exposed segments of the chain. This is the structural imbalance that strategy must address.
- *Outbound Logistics:* The company distributes products through various channels including its own channels such as Chester's restaurants, Five Stars product points of sale, and through CP Group's wholesale and retail stores such as Makro, Lotus, and 7-Eleven. Access to CP Group's retail infrastructure is a significant advantage that most standalone food companies do not possess.
- *Marketing and Sales:* CPF's consumer brand portfolio is underdeveloped relative to its production scale. The Meat Zero plant-based brand represents an attempt to build consumer-facing equity, but its scale remains limited.

**Support Activities:**
- *Technology Development:* The "Sustainovation" strategy and Feed Sustainovation 2024 initiative represent genuine investment in technology as a support activity. The CP Group Digital Transformation Day 2024 highlighted positive trends in digital business revenue and the increasing adoption of automation, underscoring the impact of technology on the business model.
- *Human Resource Management:* CPF employs approximately 12,000 foreign workers in Thailand, creating ongoing labour rights exposure. The company has established a Labour Voice Hotline in partnership with the Labour Rights Promotion Network Foundation to manage this risk.
- *Procurement:* Global commodity procurement is both a strength (scale enables volume discounts) and a weakness (exposure to global price cycles). Hedging and long-term supply contracts are partial mitigants.

**Value Chain Conclusion:** The value chain analysis reveals that CPF's margin improvement opportunity lies in shifting the revenue mix from commodity protein (feed + farming) toward processed and branded food products. CPF's 2024 revenue was THB 580,747 million, with farming operations at 55%, animal feed at 23%, and food products at only 22%. A strategic objective of increasing the food products share from 22% to 35% over five years would structurally reduce commodity exposure and improve margin quality.

## 7. Strategic Options Analysis: Ansoff Matrix

The Ansoff Matrix maps CPF's growth options across existing/new products and existing/new markets.

**Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets):** CPF can deepen share in its existing 17-country footprint, particularly in high-growth Southeast Asian markets. South and Southeast Asia are projected to be the epicentres of growth in the global poultry market, with an expected market expansion of 30% between 2023 and 2030. This is a low-risk option but does not address the commodity-cycle structural problem, because it simply scales up the same vulnerable model.

**Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets):** CPF aims to expand in 17 overseas markets where the company is currently operating — especially in China, Vietnam, India, the Philippines, Russia, and the United States. In recent years, CPF has invested significantly in acquiring value-added food companies in the USA, UK, and Continental Europe. This strategy carries moderate risk and is already being executed. However, Western market entry amplifies ESG scrutiny, meaning market development and ESG improvement are strategically interdependent.

**Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets):** This is the most strategically important quadrant. CPF launched plant-based products under the Meat Zero brand and has received recognition from the market in both Thailand and the Asian market, with plans to roll out to the USA and European markets. CPF partnered with Future Meat Technologies to develop cultured meat products for the Asian market, describing cultured meat as "a key part of our ambition to be one of the leading alternative protein companies." Product development into alternative proteins is high-risk but strategically necessary to address the substitute threat and build premium brand equity.

**Diversification (New Products, New Markets):** CPF's parent, CP Group, already operates in retail (7-Eleven, Lotus's), telecommunications (True Corporation), and financial services. CPF itself should avoid unrelated diversification, which would dilute management focus. Related diversification into functional foods, nutraceuticals, or food technology platforms is more appropriate.

## 8. The Core Strategic Tension: A Coherent Diagnosis

Synthesising the above analyses, CPF faces a three-part strategic tension:

1. **Commodity Dependence vs. Margin Quality:** The vertical integration model that built CPF's scale is now a liability when commodity cycles turn adverse. The 2023 experience — where a 31% fall in Thai pork prices wiped out profitability despite revenue of THB 585 billion — demonstrates that scale without differentiation does not protect margins.

2. **Sustainability Commitment vs. Execution Gap:** CPF has made credible, externally validated sustainability commitments (SBTi-approved targets, zero deforestation pledges). But the gap between commitment and execution — only 59% raw material traceability, ongoing labour rights concerns, a criminal defamation suit against an environmental activist — creates legitimacy risk that can materialise as market access loss, as demonstrated in Australia.

3. **Global Ambition vs. Brand Weakness:** CPF's vision of becoming the "Kitchen of the World" requires consumer-facing brand equity in global markets. Currently, CPF is primarily a B2B supplier in most international markets, making it a price-taker rather than a price-setter. Without consumer brand investment, the global expansion strategy will remain structurally vulnerable to buyer power from large retailers.

## 9. Recommendations and Action Plan

The following recommendations are designed to resolve the three-part strategic tension identified above. They form a coherent chain: each recommendation addresses one dimension of the tension, and together they reinforce one another.

### Recommendation 1: Rebalance the Revenue Portfolio Toward Value-Added Food (Addresses Tension 1)

**What:** Set an explicit strategic target to increase the food products segment from 22% of revenue to 35% within five years, through organic product development and targeted M&A.

**Why the evidence warrants this:** The value chain analysis shows that food products (22% of revenue) generate higher margins than feed and farming (78% of revenue). The Five Forces analysis shows that competitive rivalry in commodity protein is intense and price-based, while rivalry in branded food is based on innovation and consumer trust — a more defensible basis for advantage. The Ansoff Matrix identifies product development as the highest-priority growth vector. CPF has already demonstrated the M&A capability required, having acquired value-added food companies in the USA, UK, and Continental Europe. The FY2024 Q1 outlook explicitly identifies "high value product portfolio expansion" as a key growth target.

**How:** Accelerate the Meat Zero brand rollout globally; acquire or build processed food capabilities in China and India (the two largest protein demand growth markets); and redirect a portion of the ~THB 20 billion annual capex from commodity farming infrastructure toward food processing and branding.

### Recommendation 2: Close the Sustainability Execution Gap (Addresses Tension 2)

**What:** Achieve 100% traceability of key raw materials by 2027 (ahead of the existing 2025 deforestation-free target), and establish an independently audited annual ESG performance report linked to executive compensation.

**Why the evidence warrants this:** The PESTEL analysis shows that ESG failures have already resulted in concrete market access losses (Australian retail exclusions). CPF has committed to zero deforestation across its primary deforestation-linked commodities by fiscal year 2025. Yet only 59% of key raw materials are currently traceable. The gap between the 2025 commitment and the current 59% traceability level means that the commitment is at risk of not being met — creating a credibility crisis that would be worse than having made no commitment at all. CPF has already implemented SAP Sustainability Control Tower to automate data collection and generate auditable GHG metrics. This infrastructure should be extended to supply chain traceability.

**How:** Mandate that all Tier 1 and Tier 2 suppliers achieve certification under CPF's Sustainable Sourcing Policy by 2026; extend the Labour Voice Hotline to all international operations (not just Thailand); and commission annual third-party audits of supply chain ESG compliance.

### Recommendation 3: Build Consumer Brand Equity in Priority Markets (Addresses Tension 3)

**What:** Invest in consumer brand building in three priority markets — China, India, and the UK/Europe — using the Meat Zero and CP brand platforms, with a target of achieving top-three brand recognition in the alternative protein category in Asia by 2028.

**Why the evidence warrants this:** The RBV analysis identifies CPF's brand weakness as the primary resource gap preventing a move from B2B commodity supplier to B2C branded food company. Internationally, CP Group often competes as a private-label supplier in Western markets, facing limitations in premium branding and local rivals in high-margin categories. Private-label supply is structurally low-margin and subject to buyer power. CPF's CEO confirmed that Meat Zero has "received exceptional recognition from the market both in Thailand and the Asian market," with plans to roll out to the USA and European markets. This provides a platform to build from. The alternative protein market is growing rapidly, with China expanding the production of plant-based meat alternatives and Southeast Asia seeing strong growth in functional food products. CPF's manufacturing scale gives it a cost advantage in this segment that pure-play alternative protein startups lack.

**How:** Establish dedicated brand management teams in China, India, and Europe; partner with local distributors who have existing consumer relationships; and invest in digital marketing to build direct consumer engagement. Leverage CP Group's 7-Eleven and Lotus's retail network in Thailand and Southeast Asia as a captive distribution channel for new branded products.

### Recommendation 4: Implement Commodity Risk Management Infrastructure (Addresses Tension 1 — Defensive)

**What:** Establish a formal commodity risk management function with authority to use financial hedging instruments (futures, options) and long-term supply contracts to lock in input costs for 6–12 months forward, targeting a reduction in earnings volatility.

**Why the evidence warrants this:** The 2023 crisis was partly caused by the inability to pass through rising feed raw material costs to meat prices. The overall economic conditions in various countries impacted purchasing power, resulting in prices being unable to reflect the increasing costs. Corn and soybean meal make up approximately 40% and 24% of raw material costs respectively — meaning that a 20% increase in grain prices (a realistic scenario in any given year) can erase a significant portion of operating profit. Hedging does not eliminate commodity risk, but it converts unpredictable spot-price exposure into manageable, budgetable cost certainty, giving management time to execute the longer-term strategic repositioning.

**How:** Appoint a Chief Commodity Risk Officer; implement a policy requiring 50% of the following quarter's raw material requirements to be covered by forward contracts; and report commodity risk exposure in quarterly investor briefings.

## 10. Conclusion

CPF's strategic situation is best understood not as a cyclical problem requiring patience, but as a structural vulnerability requiring deliberate strategic repositioning. The 2023 near-collapse of profitability — caused by commodity price movements that CPF could not control — and the 2024 recovery — driven by the same forces reversing — demonstrate that the current business model generates earnings that are more a function of global commodity markets than of CPF's own strategic choices. This is the fundamental problem.

The analysis chain runs as follows: the PESTEL and Five Forces analyses identify the external environment as simultaneously offering large growth opportunities (Asian protein demand, alternative protein trends) and acute threats (commodity volatility, ESG regulatory pressure, substitute products). The RBV and Value Chain analyses reveal that CPF's core competitive advantage — vertical integration — is real and durable, but that it is currently deployed primarily in the low-margin, commodity-exposed segments of the value chain. The Ansoff Matrix identifies product development and selective market development as the priority strategic vectors. Together, these analyses lead to four recommendations that form a coherent strategic programme: rebalance the revenue portfolio toward value-added food, close the sustainability execution gap, build consumer brand equity, and implement commodity risk management. Each recommendation is warranted by specific evidence, and together they address the three-part strategic tension at the heart of CPF's situation.

CPF's stated commitment to "Sustainovation" — leveraging technology, science, and innovation to improve efficiency and minimise environmental impact — provides the strategic vocabulary for this repositioning. The task now is to translate that vocabulary into a resource allocation pattern that structurally reduces CPF's dependence on commodity price cycles and builds the brand equity and sustainability credentials required to compete — and win — in the global food markets of the next decade.
