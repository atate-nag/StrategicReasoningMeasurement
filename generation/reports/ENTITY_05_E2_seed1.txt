# Bayer AG: A Strategic Case Study
## *"The Monsanto Inheritance: Navigating Litigation, Leverage, and the Patent Cliff"*

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Bayer AG finds itself at a strategic inflection point shaped decisively by a single acquisition decision made in 2018. The €54 billion purchase of Monsanto — intended to create an integrated agrochemical and pharmaceutical powerhouse — instead unleashed a cascade of litigation, debt, and reputational damage that has consumed managerial bandwidth, suppressed shareholder value, and constrained strategic flexibility for over half a decade. Simultaneously, Bayer's pharmaceutical division faces a structural revenue cliff as two of its most profitable drugs lose patent protection, while its Crop Science division confronts intensifying competitive pressure. This case study applies **Porter's Five Forces**, a **SWOT analysis**, and **Ansoff's Growth Matrix** to diagnose the strategic situation, before drawing on the **Resource-Based View (RBV)** and the **McKinsey 7-S framework** to evaluate the internal transformation underway. The analysis concludes with a set of prioritised strategic recommendations.

## PART 1: CASE BACKGROUND AND STRATEGIC CONTEXT

### 1.1 Company Profile

Bayer AG, founded in 1863 and headquartered in Leverkusen, Germany, is a global life-sciences company operating across three divisions: Pharmaceuticals, Consumer Health, and Crop Science. In 2024, the Group reported sales of €46.606 billion. The company is listed on the Frankfurt Stock Exchange and employs approximately 100,000 people worldwide.

### 1.2 The Monsanto Acquisition: Strategic Rationale and Execution Failure

In 2018, Bayer AG, the German pharmaceutical and life sciences company, completed its $63 billion acquisition of US-based agricultural giant Monsanto Company. The strategic logic was coherent in isolation: combining Bayer's existing crop protection chemistry with Monsanto's world-leading genetically modified seed portfolio would create an integrated "field-to-yield" platform. At the heart of Monsanto's product line was Roundup, a glyphosate-based herbicide that had long been the subject of health and environmental controversy.

However, the deal rapidly became what legal analysts now describe as "a textbook example of how a strategic decision can turn into a financial nightmare." In August 2018, just two months after the Bayer-Monsanto merger was finalized, a San Francisco jury awarded a school groundskeeper, Dewayne Johnson, $250 million in punitive damages, finding that Roundup had caused his non-Hodgkin's lymphoma. A federal judge gave the green light for a class-action investor lawsuit alleging that Bayer AG misled investors over the risks of the acquisition and failed to properly perform due diligence before buying Monsanto.

The due diligence failure is particularly striking given the timeline. Bayer had approximately two years from the time it started until closing — so it was not because they were in a rush. Yet the lawsuit claims that during 2017 and 2018, as the filing of new Roundup cancer lawsuits was escalating, the ability of Bayer management to conduct due diligence into Monsanto and the litigation risks was "severely restricted."

### 1.3 The Accumulating Financial Burden

The financial consequences have been severe and protracted. To date, Bayer has paid over $10 billion to settle a portion of the claims but faces approximately 67,000 lawsuits that remain pending as of May 2025. The company increased its provision and liabilities for litigation from €7.8 billion to €11.8 billion, including €9.6 billion for glyphosate alone. In February 2026, Bayer's Monsanto subsidiary filed a proposed $7.25 billion class action settlement to resolve tens of thousands of cancer claims tied to its Roundup weedkiller, structured as capped annual payments declining over 21 years.

Without litigation costs, Bayer's CFO acknowledged the company "would have generated about €5 billion of cash flow on average per year." Net financial debt as of December 31, 2025, came in at €29.843 billion, representing a reduction of 8.5 percent against year-end 2024. The share price has declined by more than 50% since the acquisition, destroying tens of billions of euros of market capitalisation.

## PART 2: EXTERNAL ENVIRONMENT ANALYSIS

### 2.1 Porter's Five Forces Analysis

**Force 1: Competitive Rivalry (HIGH)**

Bayer competes in two structurally distinct but equally competitive arenas. Companies like Johnson & Johnson, Pfizer, Novartis, Roche, AstraZeneca, and Bristol Myers Squibb are major competitors. Roche's Vabysmo, a direct competitor to Bayer's Eylea, generated approximately $4.5 billion in sales in 2024. In crop science, Bayer and Corteva lead the market, with their shares 16–20 percentage points higher than BASF, which ranks third.

*Inferential step:* High rivalry compresses margins and reduces pricing power. Because Bayer is simultaneously managing a debt burden and litigation costs, it has less financial flexibility to compete aggressively on R&D investment or price compared with rivals operating from stronger balance sheets. This makes competitive position self-reinforcing in a negative direction: financial stress → constrained investment → weakening competitive position.

**Force 2: Threat of New Entrants (LOW)**

In both pharmaceuticals and crop science, barriers to entry are exceptionally high. The pharmaceutical and crop science industries require high capital requirements in R&D, licensing of drugs, and patenting, making these industries non-lucrative for new market entrants. However, biosimilar sales reached $17.6 billion in 2025, cutting biologic prices by 25–40% — a form of substitution that erodes the value of established biologics rather than creating direct new entrants.

*Inferential step:* Low threat of new entrants is a structural advantage for Bayer, but it does not protect against the more immediate threat of patent expiry, which effectively converts Bayer's own products into commodities.

**Force 3: Bargaining Power of Buyers (MODERATE–HIGH)**

Bayer's own 2025 results explicitly note "negative pricing developments in connection with patent expirations and the Inflation Reduction Act." In crop science, consolidation among large agricultural distributors increases buyer leverage. *Inferential step:* Rising buyer power means Bayer cannot rely on price increases to offset volume or pipeline pressures, reinforcing the imperative to launch genuinely differentiated, patent-protected products.

**Force 4: Bargaining Power of Suppliers (MODERATE)**

In pharmaceuticals, specialist API suppliers and contract research organisations hold meaningful leverage for complex biologics. Bayer's scale provides some countervailing purchasing power, but this force does not represent a primary strategic threat.

**Force 5: Threat of Substitutes (MODERATE AND RISING)**

Bio-inputs are growing from $11.5 billion in 2024 to a projected $20 billion by 2029, and see-and-spray robotics can cut herbicide use by 70–90% per field. These represent structural substitution risks to Bayer's core chemical herbicide business, including glyphosate.

*Inferential step:* The substitution threat in crop science is particularly strategic because it targets the very product category — glyphosate — that has generated the litigation liability. If precision agriculture reduces herbicide volume, Roundup's commercial value diminishes while its legal liability persists, worsening Bayer's risk-adjusted return from the Monsanto acquisition.

### 2.2 PESTEL Highlights

**Political/Legal:** A favorable ruling by the U.S. Supreme Court on the federal preemption question could largely end the Roundup litigation. The main legal question is whether state-based failure-to-warn claims are preempted by federal EPA approval of Roundup labels. The U.S. Solicitor General supports Supreme Court review and agrees with Bayer's arguments on preemption, with a decision expected by summer 2026. Additionally, the executive order President Trump signed in February 2025 protecting and encouraging the production of glyphosate is a major victory for Bayer AG.

**Economic:** Net financial debt amounted to €32.626 billion as of December 31, 2024, and the company intends to pay out the legal minimum in dividends to further reduce debt and gain greater flexibility.

**Technological:** The shift toward biological crop inputs and digital agriculture represents both a threat to chemical crop protection and an opportunity for Bayer's Seeds & Traits business.

**Environmental/Social:** The reputational damage associated with the Monsanto brand — which Bayer attempted to retire — creates an ongoing ESG liability affecting institutional investor appetite.

## PART 3: INTERNAL ANALYSIS

### 3.1 SWOT Analysis

**Strengths:**
- **Scale and diversification:** Bayer's diverse portfolio across pharmaceuticals, consumer health, and crop science cushions against market-specific risks. Their global presence supports adaptability to various regional demands, with 2023 sales reaching €47.6 billion.
- **R&D capability:** In 2024, Bayer's R&D expenses were approximately €5.5 billion, reflecting its commitment to innovation.
- **Pharmaceutical pipeline momentum:** Sales at Pharmaceuticals increased by 1.7% in 2025. Nubeqa and Kerendia registered significant gains, while the Radiology business and Mirena product family continued to deliver very strong performance.
- **Crop Science leadership:** Bayer and Corteva together lead the global agrochemical market by a substantial margin over third-placed BASF.

**Weaknesses:**
- **Litigation liability:** ~67,000 Roundup lawsuits pending as of mid-2025, with total provisions of €11.8 billion.
- **Debt burden:** Bayer's debt-to-equity ratio for the three months ending June 30, 2025 was 1.04.
- **Pharmaceutical patent cliff:** Bayer lost its US patent on Xarelto in 2025, which alone accounted for an estimated €1bn–€1.5bn of lost 2025 revenues. The company is set to lose its patent on Eylea in 2027. Together, Xarelto and Eylea chalked up €6.62 billion in 2019 revenues. It would be "mathematically impossible" for growth from newer drugs to compensate for their loss of patent protection.
- **Bureaucratic inertia:** CEO Bill Anderson identified "a hierarchical bureaucracy that blocks progress" as one of Bayer's core strategic problems.

**Opportunities:**
- **Supreme Court preemption ruling:** A favourable ruling in *Monsanto v. Durnell* could largely end the Roundup litigation.
- **Pipeline launches:** Bayer is planning to launch the heart drug Beyonttra (acoramidis) and elinzanetant, a non-hormonal treatment for menopause symptoms. The Pharmaceuticals Division is projected to return to sales growth from 2027 onwards and expand margins beginning in 2028.
- **Biological agriculture:** The Crop Science division is focused on executing its Five-Year Framework, with the Plenexos insecticide recently launched in Latin America, highlighting the division's focus on innovative crop solutions.
- **DSO cost savings:** The DSO model promises significant cost savings — €500 million in 2025 and €2 billion in 2026.

**Threats:**
- **Adverse Supreme Court ruling:** If preemption is rejected, the litigation tail could extend indefinitely.
- **Biosimilar competition:** Xarelto saw sales fall 32.7% in the three months ended September 30, 2025. Pressure is also building as Bayer faces a new biosimilar threat for Eylea in Europe as of November 2025.
- **Crop pricing pressure:** The global crop protection market experienced significant price deflation in 2023–2024 due to destocking and competitive pressure, particularly in Latin America.
- **Capital market pressure:** With the stock trading at multi-decade lows, Bayer faces potential activist pressure.

### 3.2 Resource-Based View (RBV) Analysis

The RBV framework (Barney, 1991) asks which of Bayer's resources are **Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN)** — and therefore the basis for sustainable competitive advantage.

| Resource | Valuable? | Rare? | Inimitable? | Non-substitutable? | Implication |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Pharmaceutical R&D capability | ✓ | Partially | Partially | Partially | Competitive parity; not distinctive |
| Crop Science integrated seed/chemistry platform | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | Partially | Core source of advantage |
| Consumer Health brand portfolio (Aspirin, Claritin) | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | Partially | Durable cash generator |
| Glyphosate/Roundup franchise | ✓ (declining) | No | No | No | Liability-generating commodity |
| DSO operating model | ✓ | ✓ (currently) | Partially | Partially | Potential advantage if sustained |
| Balance sheet capacity | No | No | — | — | Structural weakness |

*Inferential step:* The RBV analysis reveals that Bayer's most genuinely inimitable advantage lies in its integrated Crop Science platform and its consumer health brands. The glyphosate franchise — the asset that justified the Monsanto premium — has been transformed by litigation from a valuable resource into a liability-generating commodity. This represents a fundamental destruction of the RBV logic underpinning the acquisition: Bayer paid a control premium for an asset that is neither rare (glyphosate is off-patent) nor non-substitutable (biological herbicides are growing rapidly), while inheriting a litigation tail that has consumed more capital than the asset itself generates.

### 3.3 McKinsey 7-S Framework: The DSO Transformation

In 2023, Bill Anderson became CEO of Bayer AG, a 160-year-old life sciences giant looking to strengthen its pharma pipeline, manage debt, and cut through bureaucracy. His bold response: Dynamic Shared Ownership (DSO), a radical model replacing traditional hierarchies with self-organizing teams. By 2025, Bayer was on track to cut €2 billion in costs.

Evaluated through the McKinsey 7-S lens:

- **Strategy:** Anderson aims to redesign Bayer "from management to Mission, from alignment to action, from CEO to customers."
- **Structure:** Bayer has reduced its headcount by more than 12,000, with a 7.3% reduction over the last 12 months, primarily eliminating management and coordination roles.
- **Systems:** The DSO model, rolled out in January 2024, emphasises flattening hierarchies, decentralising decision-making, and fostering cross-functional collaboration.
- **Style:** Anderson's leadership explicitly targets speed and customer-centricity over compliance and alignment.
- **Shared Values:** A cultural shift from bureaucratic alignment to entrepreneurial accountability.

*Inferential step:* The 7-S analysis reveals coherent internal alignment — strategy, structure, systems, and style are all being reconfigured in the same direction. The key risk, as the Harvard Business School case on DSO acknowledges, is whether "fewer bosses" can truly drive innovation in complex, regulated industries where institutional knowledge matters enormously. The €500 million in savings delivered in 2025 and the 20.8% EBITDA increase in Q3 2025 suggest early momentum, but the model remains unproven at scale.

## PART 4: STRATEGIC POSITION SYNTHESIS

### 4.1 Ansoff Matrix: Where Is Bayer Growing?

**Market Penetration (Existing products, Existing markets):** Bayer is pursuing penetration through Nubeqa (oncology) and Kerendia (cardio-renal) — both growing significantly in established markets. This is the lowest-risk near-term pharmaceutical strategy.

**Product Development (New products, Existing markets):** The launch of Beyonttra, Lynkuet, and pursuit of asundexian approval represent product development within established therapeutic areas. This is the core medium-term pharmaceutical growth strategy.

**Market Development (Existing products, New markets):** The biological crop protection portfolio expansion — including Plenexos in Latin America — represents market development in new customer segments.

**Diversification (New products, New markets):** Bayer has largely avoided diversification, consistent with Anderson's decision to maintain the three-division structure. Higher investments in early-stage research and in cell and gene therapy and chemoproteomics technologies represent early-stage diversification within the pharmaceutical division.

*Inferential step:* Bayer's Ansoff positioning is appropriately conservative given its financial constraints. The company cannot afford the capital requirements of aggressive diversification while carrying €30 billion in debt and €11.8 billion in litigation provisions. Concentration on market penetration and product development is rational, but it means Bayer's growth is entirely dependent on its existing pipeline — which, as the pharma chief has acknowledged, cannot fully replace Xarelto and Eylea revenues in the near term.

### 4.2 The Core Strategic Problem: A Compound Constraint

The analysis converges on a compound strategic constraint with three mutually reinforcing components:

1. **The Litigation Constraint:** ~67,000 pending lawsuits and €11.8 billion in provisions absorb cash, management attention, and reputational capital.
2. **The Debt Constraint:** €30 billion in net financial debt limits strategic flexibility, preventing transformative M&A and constraining R&D acceleration.
3. **The Pipeline Constraint:** The pharmaceutical division faces a structural revenue trough in 2025–2027 as Xarelto and Eylea revenues decline faster than pipeline products can compensate.

These three constraints are interdependent: litigation absorbs cash that could reduce debt; debt constrains pipeline investment; pipeline weakness reduces earnings needed to service debt and fund litigation reserves. Breaking any one link is necessary but insufficient — all three must be addressed in parallel.

## PART 5: STRATEGIC RECOMMENDATIONS

### Recommendation 1: Pursue an Aggressive but Structured Litigation Resolution *(Priority: CRITICAL, Horizon: 2025–2026)*

**What:** Pursue the proposed $7.25 billion nationwide class action settlement with maximum urgency, while simultaneously pursuing the Supreme Court preemption argument in *Monsanto v. Durnell* as a parallel and reinforcing strategy. The proposed class, combined with Supreme Court review, are "independently necessary and mutually reinforcing steps in the company's multipronged strategy to significantly contain the Roundup litigation."

**Why the evidence warrants this:** The litigation is the primary value-destruction mechanism. The proposed class settlement, structured as declining capped annual payments over 21 years, gives the company control and certainty over the litigation. A favourable Supreme Court ruling on preemption could largely end the Roundup litigation at no incremental cost. The backing of the U.S. Solicitor General will be important to the Court's consideration of the petition.

**Risk and Mitigation:** Court approval is not guaranteed, and opt-out rates among current claimants could undermine effectiveness. Bayer is reportedly exploring the possibility of placing its Monsanto subsidiary into Chapter 11 bankruptcy as a means to isolate and manage these liabilities — this should be maintained as a structural backstop if both primary tracks fail.

---

### Recommendation 2: Accelerate Pharmaceutical Pipeline Commercialisation Through Focused Partnering *(Priority: HIGH, Horizon: 2025–2028)*

**What:** Prioritise rapid commercialisation of Nubeqa, Kerendia, Beyonttra, Lynkuet, and asundexian through targeted co-promotion and licensing partnerships in markets where Bayer lacks commercial scale.

**Why the evidence warrants this:** With sales of its top-selling product Xarelto tumbling because of generic competition, Bayer isn't projecting growth for the pharma division until 2027. Bayer is relying on five assets to drive growth and reclaim near-term profits, including Beyonttra for ATTR-CM, Lynkuet for menopause management, and asundexian for secondary stroke. Partnering reduces capital requirements, accelerates market penetration, and generates upfront milestone payments that can be applied to debt reduction — directly addressing the compound constraint.

**Risk and Mitigation:** Partnering dilutes long-term revenue. Mitigation: structure deals with performance milestones and co-promotion rights in Bayer's core geographies, while licensing out secondary markets.

---

### Recommendation 3: Execute DSO Rigorously but Ring-Fence Scientific Capability *(Priority: HIGH, Horizon: 2024–2026)*

**What:** Continue the DSO restructuring to deliver the targeted €2 billion in annual savings from 2026, but implement a "ring-fencing" protocol that explicitly protects R&D scientists, regulatory specialists, and clinical development teams.

**Why the evidence warrants this:** By eliminating 12,000 roles — primarily in management and coordination — across its Pharmaceuticals, Crop Science, and Consumer Health divisions, the company aims to create a leaner, faster organisation. Early results are positive: Dynamic Shared Ownership is generating returns, with the operating model delivering benefits on the top and bottom line. "Our organisation continues to get leaner and more efficient, and we're seeing benefits in terms of speed and focus." However, questions remain — could fewer bosses truly drive innovation, or was this an unsustainable gamble? In pharmaceuticals and crop science, institutional knowledge about regulatory pathways and clinical data resides in experienced specialists, not management layers.

**Risk and Mitigation:** Define explicit "protected roles" in R&D and regulatory affairs. Apply DSO's decentralisation principles to commercial and administrative functions while maintaining structured oversight in regulated scientific functions.

### Recommendation 4: Commit to the Crop Science Five-Year Framework — Not a Fire Sale *(Priority: MEDIUM-HIGH, Horizon: 2025–2030)*

**What:** Execute the Crop Science Five-Year Framework in full, with particular emphasis on accelerating the biological crop protection portfolio, while resisting pressure to divest the division at distressed valuations.

**Why the evidence warrants this:** The RBV analysis identifies the integrated seed-and-chemistry platform as Bayer's most genuinely rare and inimitable resource. Selling it under financial duress would crystallise a permanent loss of competitive position in a structurally growing market. Bio-inputs are growing from $11.5 billion in 2024 to a projected $20 billion by 2029 — a trajectory that rewards incumbents who invest now. The Crop Science division is focused on executing its Five-Year Framework, with innovative crop solutions like Plenexos being launched in Latin America.

**Risk and Mitigation:** Sustained capital investment is required during balance-sheet stress. Mitigation: ring-fence a minimum annual Crop Science R&D budget; explore selective asset sales of non-core geographies or commodity chemistry assets to fund biological platform investment.

### Recommendation 5: Evaluate Consumer Health Separation as a Medium-Term Capital Event *(Priority: MEDIUM, Horizon: 2027–2029)*

**What:** Conduct a formal strategic review of Consumer Health separation — whether through IPO, trade sale, or spin-off — targeted for execution once litigation provisions are materially reduced and the pharmaceutical pipeline revenue inflection is visible.

**Why the evidence warrants this:** CEO Anderson considered a potential sale of Consumer Health but concluded that the tax losses would be significant, and Bayer would lose a business that's reliably generating cash flow — a rational decision given immediate liquidity needs. However, Consumer Health operates on a fundamentally different business model from prescription pharmaceuticals and agrochemicals, requiring consumer marketing and retail distribution capabilities distinct from Bayer's core scientific competencies. A dedicated Consumer Health entity could attract a higher valuation multiple as a pure-play OTC business. By 2027–2028, if litigation is substantially contained and the pharmaceutical pipeline is generating visible growth, the tax cost of separation will be more manageable relative to the strategic benefits.

**Risk and Mitigation:** Do not execute before 2027. Use the intervening period to maximise Consumer Health EBITDA through DSO efficiencies, improving the separation multiple.

## PART 6: CONCLUSION

Bayer's strategic situation is one of the most analytically rich in contemporary corporate history: a firm of genuine scientific capability and market leadership, trapped in a compound constraint of its own making. The Monsanto acquisition was not strategically irrational — the logic of an integrated agricultural platform was coherent — but it was executed with a catastrophic failure of legal due diligence that has cost the company more than the original purchase price in litigation settlements and provisions alone, while simultaneously destroying over half its market capitalisation.

The path forward is not a single transformative move but a **sequenced programme of constraint relief**: first, resolve the litigation; second, bridge the pharmaceutical revenue trough through pipeline acceleration and partnering; third, deliver DSO cost savings while protecting scientific capability; fourth, invest in the Crop Science biological platform for long-term competitive positioning; and fifth, evaluate Consumer Health separation once the balance sheet permits.

The analytical frameworks converge on a single overriding inference: Bayer's competitive advantage is real — in its integrated crop science platform, its consumer health brands, and its pharmaceutical pipeline — but it is currently obscured by the financial and reputational consequences of the Monsanto acquisition. **The strategic task is not to reinvent Bayer, but to excavate it.**
