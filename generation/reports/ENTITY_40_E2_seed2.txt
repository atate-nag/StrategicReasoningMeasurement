# Vattenfall AB: Navigating the Dual-Mandate Dilemma in Sweden's Energy Transition

## A Strategic Case Study

---

## 1. Introduction and Case Overview

Vattenfall AB is wholly owned by the Swedish state, and its headquarters are located in Sweden. It is a European energy company with approximately 21,000 employees. Beyond Sweden, the company generates power in Denmark, Finland, Germany, the Netherlands, and the United Kingdom. For over a century, Vattenfall has been the centrepiece of Sweden's energy infrastructure — first as a hydropower agency electrifying a rural nation, then as a nuclear operator, and now as a company caught between two contradictory imperatives: behaving as a commercially competitive European utility while simultaneously serving as the Swedish state's primary instrument of energy and climate policy.

This tension — what this case study terms the **dual-mandate dilemma** — has become acutely visible in the 2023–2025 period. On one hand, Vattenfall has set science-based targets for 2030 and 2040 to transition away from fossil fuels and align its business with the requirements to limit global warming to 1.5°C. On the other hand, Vattenfall has decided to pause the development of the offshore wind power project Swedish Kriegers Flak until further notice, due to unviable investment prerequisites in Sweden, revealing that commercial logic and public-interest ambition do not always align. Meanwhile, Sweden's industrial electricity demand could increase from 134 TWh in 2020 to 349 TWh in 2050, making Vattenfall's strategic choices consequential not just for shareholders but for the entire Swedish economy.

The central strategic question this case examines is: **How should Vattenfall reconcile its commercial obligations with its public-interest mandate in a period of energy market volatility, rising demand, and a contested energy mix?**

---

## 2. Background and Historical Context

In 1909, the Swedish government created Vattenfall, a publicly-owned 'commercial government agency' to accelerate the electrification of Sweden. From its founding until the mid-1970s, Vattenfall's business was largely restricted to Sweden, with a focus on hydroelectric power generation. Only in 1974 did the company begin to build nuclear reactors in Sweden, eventually owning seven of Sweden's 12 reactors. A first step in the liberalisation process was the corporatisation of the Swedish state-owned utility Vattenfall in 1992. The deregulatory process culminated with the new Electricity Law, which entered into force in 1996.

This historical trajectory is analytically significant: Vattenfall was not designed as a market actor but was progressively *converted* into one without ever fully shedding its public-service identity. This structural ambiguity — commercial form, public purpose — is the root of the strategic tensions examined below.

In 1997, Vattenfall's mission was defined in more detail by parliament: 'Investments in electricity and heat production from renewable energy sources should be prominent in Vattenfall's long-term development and investment plans'. It was also emphasised that 'said efforts are to be made within the framework of requirements for greater commercialism'. Parliament's phrasing — public goals *within* commercial constraints — encodes the dual mandate directly into Vattenfall's charter, and the strategic tension has never been resolved.

## 3. The Strategic Situation: Four Interlocking Pressures

### 3.1 Financial Performance Under Falling Prices

The Swedish state-owned power utility Vattenfall posted a revenue of SEK 246bn (€22bn, -15% compared to 2023) and profit of SEK 33bn (€3bn, ×3.3) in 2024. While the profit figure appears strong, this is partly an accounting effect from asset divestments rather than underlying operational improvement: the underlying operating profit for the full year 2024 was relatively unchanged compared to 2023 and amounted to SEK 19.8 billion. This matters strategically because 2024 was a year characterised by declining electricity prices and geopolitical and economic uncertainties. Vattenfall's underlying earnings are therefore structurally exposed to wholesale price volatility — a core vulnerability for a company that must also fund long-horizon capital projects in nuclear and offshore wind.

### 3.2 The Offshore Wind Paradox

Vattenfall's offshore wind strategy has experienced two high-profile setbacks that reveal a deeper strategic contradiction. In 2023, Vattenfall stopped development of its 1.4GW Norfolk Boreas offshore wind project due to higher inflation and capital costs, which have affected the entire energy sector, but the geopolitical situation has made offshore wind and its supply chain particularly vulnerable. Overall, cost increases of up to 40% were seen. This forced Vattenfall to divest the Norfolk Offshore Wind Zone: Vattenfall signed an agreement with RWE to divest the Norfolk Offshore Wind Zone, with an agreed purchase price corresponding to an enterprise value of £963m.

In Sweden itself, Vattenfall has chosen to put the 640 MW Kriegers Flak offshore wind farm on hold until further notice because the investment conditions in Sweden are not feasible at this time. The reason was structural, not cyclical: Vattenfall said it had paused construction of Sweden's Kriegers Flak offshore wind project as a lack of clarity on how it will connect to the national grid made it unprofitable. This is a critical finding: it is not simply market prices that are blocking green investment, but the inadequacy of Sweden's own grid infrastructure — a problem the state-owner has not resolved.

### 3.3 The Nuclear Renaissance and Its Financing Risk

Simultaneously, Vattenfall is pivoting toward a nuclear-led strategy. The owners of the Forsmark and Ringhals nuclear power plants in Sweden have taken a directional decision to extend the operating lifetime of the plants' reactors from 60 to 80 years, enabling fossil-free generation into the 2060s. Beyond life extension, Swedish utility Vattenfall has submitted an application for state financing to construct new nuclear reactors at its Ringhals facility, the first such request under the government's support framework aimed at expanding nuclear power capacity. The application comes through Videberg Kraft AB, a majority-owned subsidiary: Vattenfall, backed by several large Swedish industrial corporations through the Industrikraft i Sverige AB consortium, plans to build new reactors at Ringhals.

The financing model is telling. The government has promised SEK 400 billion ($38.2 billion) in credit guarantees to companies that plan to build new reactors. Vattenfall's CEO has been explicit that this is a prerequisite: a future investment decision will, among other things, require a reasonable risk-sharing model with the state. This is necessary to lower financing costs and thereby enable a reasonable cost for electricity production that customers are prepared to pay. In other words, Vattenfall's nuclear ambitions are only commercially viable if the state underwrites the downside risk — a direct expression of the dual-mandate dilemma, where the state must act simultaneously as owner, regulator, and risk-bearer.

### 3.4 The North–South Price Zone Crisis

A structural market failure is eroding public trust and threatening Vattenfall's social licence. On the afternoon of December 11, 2024, the spot price in southern Sweden reached 5 kronor per kilowatt hour, while northern Sweden remained at near-zero prices — a disparity of 167 times. The cause is infrastructural: one of Sweden's core challenges is that while the south has a strong grid but limited production, the north has abundant generation capacity but weaker grid capacity. Resolving this requires Svenska kraftnät, Sweden's transmission system operator, plans to invest SEK 225 billion over the next ten years to strengthen and expand the national electricity transmission network, but this investment horizon extends well beyond the immediate crisis. The price disparity directly affects Vattenfall's public legitimacy: as a state-owned utility, it is blamed by consumers and politicians for high prices even when the root cause lies in grid infrastructure it does not control.

## 4. Analytical Framework Application

### 4.1 PESTEL Analysis

**Political:** The Swedish State-owned enterprises, which are ultimately owned by the Swedish people, make up a significant part of the business sector in Sweden. The Government must actively manage state-owned enterprises. The government's 2025 ownership policy signals a shift: "It's important that commercially driven state-owned enterprises deliver value to taxpayers. Therefore, the new ownership policy emphasises profitability, adaptability, and responsibility." This tightening of the commercial mandate increases pressure on Vattenfall to deliver financial returns even as it pursues capital-intensive long-term projects.

**Economic:** Falling wholesale electricity prices in 2024 compressed margins. The underlying operating profit for the full year 2024 was relatively unchanged compared to 2023 at SEK 19.8 billion, despite a year of declining electricity prices. Rising capital costs — particularly the 40% cost inflation in offshore wind supply chains — make new investment decisions precarious.

**Social:** The north–south price disparity generates significant public discontent. The Swedish government believes that these structural issues are undermining the country's energy security and social cohesion. As the state-owned incumbent, Vattenfall absorbs political blame even for failures outside its direct control.

**Technological:** The energy landscape is being reshaped by two competing technologies — offshore wind and small modular reactors (SMRs). During 2025, Vattenfall took final investment decisions for the Nordlicht I and II offshore wind farms in the North Sea, Germany, and progressed plans for new nuclear power by selecting two small modular reactor suppliers. Vattenfall must develop competencies in both, creating a portfolio management challenge.

**Environmental:** Vattenfall's goal is net-zero by 2040, which means at least a 90% reduction in absolute emissions across all emission scopes. Any residual emissions will be neutralised by permanently removing CO₂ from the atmosphere.

**Legal/Regulatory:** Grid connection rules in Sweden are insufficiently developed for offshore wind, as the Kriegers Flak pause demonstrates. The regulatory framework has not kept pace with the commercial reality of large-scale offshore development.

### 4.2 Porter's Five Forces

**Threat of New Entrants (Moderate):** The energy sector requires enormous capital, long permitting timelines, and specialised technical expertise — all of which create high barriers to entry. However, Fortum faces robust competition from large, established energy incumbents, many of which are state-backed. These players, like Vattenfall in Sweden, command substantial financial muscle and own vast, critical infrastructure networks. The real threat of entry is not from startups but from other state-backed European utilities expanding into Nordic markets.

**Bargaining Power of Suppliers (High and Rising):** The Norfolk Boreas exit illustrates supplier power acutely: higher inflation and capital costs are affecting the entire energy sector, but the geopolitical situation has made offshore wind and its supply chain particularly vulnerable. Supply chain concentration in turbine manufacturing (dominated by Vestas, Siemens Gamesa, and GE Vernova) gives suppliers significant leverage over project economics.

**Bargaining Power of Buyers (Moderate, but Growing):** Large industrial customers are increasingly able to negotiate long-term Power Purchase Agreements (PPAs) directly with generators, bypassing spot markets. Vattenfall's ability to optimise dispatch across both customer loads and generation assets increases its competitive advantage in wind auctions, by enabling stable revenues through Corporate PPAs with its customers. This capability is a genuine differentiator, but it also means Vattenfall's revenues increasingly depend on the creditworthiness of a concentrated set of industrial buyers.

**Threat of Substitutes (Low-to-Moderate):** In the short term, there is no substitute for grid electricity at scale. However, distributed generation (rooftop solar, battery storage, demand-response systems) is eroding the traditional utility model at the margin, particularly for residential customers.

**Competitive Rivalry (High):** In Germany and the Netherlands, Vattenfall competes directly with RWE, E.ON, Orsted, and Ørsted — all of whom have comparable or superior balance sheets. In Sweden, Vattenfall holds a dominant position in generation, but the competition authorities and OECD have criticised the joint ownership structure of Swedish nuclear power.

### 4.3 Resource-Based View (RBV) and Core Competencies

The RBV framework (Barney, 1991) argues that sustained competitive advantage derives from resources that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN). Applying this to Vattenfall:

| Resource | Valuable? | Rare? | Inimitable? | Non-substitutable? | Strategic Implication |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Hydro & nuclear asset base | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | **Core strategic asset** — low-carbon, dispatchable baseload |
| State ownership / credit access | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | Partially | Enables financing at sub-market rates; also constrains agility |
| Offshore wind development capability | ✓ | Partially | Partially | Partially | Eroding — UK exit signals capability gaps in cost management |
| Grid optimisation & PPA structuring | ✓ | Partially | Partially | ✓ | Growing differentiator as markets tighten |

The RBV analysis reveals that Vattenfall's most durable competitive advantage lies in its existing low-carbon dispatchable generation — hydro and nuclear — not in offshore wind, where it faces cost-competitiveness challenges against rivals with deeper supply-chain relationships. This has significant implications for strategic prioritisation.

### 4.4 Principal-Agent Theory and the Dual-Mandate Dilemma

The most analytically distinctive feature of Vattenfall's situation is the principal-agent problem embedded in state ownership. The Swedish government (principal) delegates operational authority to Vattenfall's board and management (agent), but the principal itself has multiple, partially conflicting objectives: maximising commercial returns to fund public services; achieving climate targets; ensuring energy security; and maintaining affordable prices for citizens.

Vattenfall has transformed from a national jewel with a high level of social responsibility and low prices into a profit-seeking company — and critics argue the state owner had no control and was weak. This critique captures the agency problem precisely: when the state's ownership objectives are ambiguous, management cannot optimise for any single goal, and accountability is diffused.

The 2025 ownership policy attempts to address this: state-owned enterprises have to act on a commercial basis, sustainably, and in an exemplary manner that safeguards public trust. But "commercial basis" and "safeguarding public trust" can point in opposite directions — as the Kriegers Flak pause (commercially rational) versus the public need for southern Swedish renewable capacity (publicly necessary) illustrates.

### 4.5 Stakeholder Analysis (Freeman, 1984)

Vattenfall's stakeholders can be mapped on a power/interest matrix:

**High Power, High Interest (Manage Closely):**
- **Swedish Government** — owner, regulator, and risk-sharer; sets the strategic mandate
- **Industrial customers (Industrikraft consortium)** — co-investors in nuclear; critical demand anchor for new capacity

**High Power, Lower Interest (Keep Satisfied):**
- **EU regulators** — state aid rules constrain the government's ability to underwrite nuclear risk; the SEK 400bn guarantee framework must comply with EU competition law
- **Capital markets / credit rating agencies** — though Vattenfall is state-owned, its debt is rated and its cost of capital matters for project viability

**Lower Power, High Interest (Keep Informed):**
- **Residential consumers** — acutely affected by north–south price disparities; politically vocal
- **Environmental NGOs** — scrutinise nuclear expansion and any residual fossil fuel exposure

**Lower Power, Lower Interest (Monitor):**
- **Competitors (RWE, Orsted, E.ON)** — shape the competitive context but do not directly govern Vattenfall's choices

The stakeholder map reveals a structural risk: the groups with the highest political salience (residential consumers, NGOs) have the least formal power, while the groups with the most formal power (government, industrial consortium) have interests that may diverge from the public interest in affordable, reliable energy.

### 4.6 Ansoff Matrix: Growth Strategy Analysis

Vattenfall's recent strategic moves can be mapped onto the Ansoff Matrix:

- **Market Penetration (existing products, existing markets):** Life-extension of Forsmark and Ringhals reactors — the owners have taken a directional decision to extend the operating lifetime of the plants' reactors from 60 to 80 years, extracting more value from existing assets in existing markets. This is the lowest-risk quadrant and is currently the most commercially rational move.

- **Market Development (existing products, new markets):** Vattenfall's offshore wind expansion into Germany (Nordlicht I and II) represents taking an existing capability into a new geography. During 2025, Vattenfall took final investment decisions for the Nordlicht I and II offshore wind farms in the North Sea, Germany.

- **Product Development (new products, existing markets):** SMR deployment at Ringhals — introducing a new reactor technology into the existing Swedish nuclear market. This is high-risk: the technology is unproven at commercial scale, and the financing model requires state support.

- **Diversification (new products, new markets):** The Berlin heat business divestment signals a deliberate *retreat* from diversification. Vattenfall has finalised the strategic review of its Berlin heat business and has decided to sell the entire district heating business to the State of Berlin, refocusing on core electricity generation.

The pattern is coherent: Vattenfall is retreating from diversification and market development in high-cost geographies (UK, Berlin heat) while doubling down on market penetration (nuclear life extension) and selective market development in Germany. The SMR bet is the most strategically adventurous move, and its risk profile is only manageable because of state risk-sharing.

## 5. The Core Strategic Tension: A Synthesis

Drawing the analytical threads together, Vattenfall faces a **trilemma** — three goals that cannot all be simultaneously maximised:

1. **Commercial profitability** (required by the 2025 state ownership policy and necessary to fund capital investment)
2. **Energy security and affordability** (required by the public interest, particularly given the north–south price crisis)
3. **Decarbonisation leadership** (required by the net-zero 2040 commitment and Sweden's climate obligations)

The offshore wind situation illustrates the trilemma starkly. Building Kriegers Flak would serve goals (2) and (3) — adding renewable capacity in southern Sweden where prices are highest. But it fails goal (1) because grid connection rules make it commercially unviable. Vattenfall, acting commercially, pauses the project. The state, which owns Vattenfall and also regulates the grid, has created the very conditions that prevent its own instrument from delivering the public good it was designed to provide. This is a **policy coordination failure**, not merely a corporate strategy failure.

The nuclear pivot addresses goal (1) — nuclear provides stable, long-run revenues — and goal (2) — dispatchable baseload enhances energy security. But it risks goal (3) in the short-to-medium term, because new nuclear capacity will not come online until the late 2030s at the earliest, leaving a decade-long gap in which decarbonisation depends on wind capacity that Vattenfall is currently pausing.

## 6. Recommendations and Action Plan

The following recommendations are derived directly from the analytical chain above. Each addresses a specific failure identified in the analysis, and each is grounded in the evidence.

### Recommendation 1: Resolve the Grid-Investment Coordination Failure Through a Joint Venture with Svenska kraftnät

**The problem:** The Kriegers Flak pause demonstrates that Vattenfall's renewable investment decisions are being blocked by grid infrastructure that another state entity (Svenska kraftnät) controls. Svenska kraftnät plans to invest SEK 225 billion over the next ten years to strengthen and expand the national electricity transmission network, but this timeline is too slow to unlock Vattenfall's offshore wind pipeline.

**The recommendation:** The Swedish government should mandate a formal coordination mechanism — a joint investment vehicle or co-planning protocol — between Vattenfall and Svenska kraftnät, so that offshore wind development and grid connection are planned and financed simultaneously rather than sequentially. This directly addresses the principal-agent problem: the state, as owner of both entities, is the only actor with the authority to impose coordination. Without this, the 10-year lead times for high-voltage infrastructure will continue to block renewable investment in southern Sweden, perpetuating the north–south price disparity and undermining both goals (2) and (3) of the trilemma.

**Why the evidence warrants this:** The Kriegers Flak pause was not caused by a failure of Vattenfall's commercial judgement — it was caused by a regulatory gap in grid connection rules. The RBV analysis shows that grid access is a non-substitutable resource; without it, even a well-capitalised utility cannot deploy renewable capacity. Coordination between the state's energy instruments is therefore a prerequisite for any renewable investment strategy.

### Recommendation 2: Ring-Fence the Nuclear Programme Within a Separate Financing Structure, Insulated from Commercial Earnings Volatility

**The problem:** Vattenfall's nuclear new-build ambitions require multi-decade capital commitments, but the company's underlying operating profit is exposed to short-term wholesale price swings. A future investment decision will require a reasonable risk-sharing model with the state. This is necessary to lower financing costs and thereby enable a reasonable cost for electricity production.

**The recommendation:** The Videberg Kraft structure — a majority-owned subsidiary with industrial co-investors — is the right vehicle, but it should be further insulated from Vattenfall's consolidated balance sheet through a dedicated project finance structure backed by the government's SEK 400bn guarantee framework. The government has promised SEK 400 billion in credit guarantees to companies that plan to build new reactors, and Vattenfall has already applied for this support. The recommendation is to accelerate the legal and financial structuring of this guarantee so that nuclear investment decisions are decoupled from annual earnings cycles.

**Why the evidence warrants this:** The Ansoff analysis shows that SMR deployment is in the highest-risk quadrant (product development). The RBV analysis shows that state-backed financing is one of Vattenfall's rarest and most inimitable resources. Using this resource to de-risk nuclear investment is strategically rational — it converts a public-balance-sheet strength into a commercial-investment enabler. Failure to ring-fence the nuclear programme risks a scenario where a bad earnings year (driven by low wholesale prices) delays or cancels a multi-decade infrastructure investment, imposing enormous social costs.

### Recommendation 3: Establish Explicit, Publicly Reported Dual-Mandate Metrics to Resolve the Principal-Agent Ambiguity

**The problem:** The principal-agent analysis shows that Vattenfall's management cannot optimise for ambiguous goals. The 2025 ownership policy's call for enterprises to act "commercially" while "safeguarding public trust" provides insufficient operational guidance. Without clear metrics for both mandates, management will default to the more easily measurable goal — financial return — at the expense of the public-interest mandate.

**The recommendation:** The Swedish government should require Vattenfall to publish an annual **Dual Mandate Scorecard** alongside its financial results, reporting on: (a) commercial metrics (ROCE, dividend yield, debt/equity); (b) public-interest metrics (contribution to Swedish renewable capacity additions, north–south price convergence, CO₂ emissions per TWh, share of electricity affordable to low-income households). These metrics should be set by parliament, not by Vattenfall's board, to ensure they reflect the principal's actual objectives rather than the agent's preferences.

**Why the evidence warrants this:** Stakeholder analysis reveals that residential consumers — the group most affected by Vattenfall's public-interest performance — have the least formal power in the governance structure. Mandated public reporting shifts accountability: it makes the dual mandate visible, measurable, and politically salient, creating incentives for management to balance both goals rather than trading one off against the other silently.

### Recommendation 4: Accelerate the Corporate PPA Strategy to Bridge the Decarbonisation Gap Before New Nuclear Comes Online

**The problem:** New nuclear capacity will not be operational until the late 2030s at the earliest. In the intervening decade, Sweden's rapidly growing electricity demand — steel and metal works alone are expected to increase electricity demand from 7 to 42 TWh/year by 2035, mainly by replacing fossil coal with hydrogen — must be met by some combination of existing capacity and new renewables. The Kriegers Flak pause creates a supply gap precisely where demand is growing fastest.

**The recommendation:** Vattenfall should aggressively expand its Corporate PPA programme, contracting long-term supply agreements with the large industrial customers (steel, battery manufacturing, data centres) whose demand growth is driving the electricity gap. The ability to optimise dispatch across both customer loads and generation assets increases Vattenfall's competitive advantage in wind auctions, by enabling stable revenues through Corporate PPAs. Long-term PPAs provide the revenue certainty needed to justify offshore wind investment even in volatile wholesale markets — directly addressing the commercial viability problem that caused the Kriegers Flak pause.

**Why the evidence warrants this:** The Porter's Five Forces analysis identifies rising buyer power as a structural trend, as large industrials increasingly negotiate directly with generators. Vattenfall can convert this threat into a strategic asset: by locking in long-term industrial customers through PPAs, it creates the revenue floor that makes otherwise-marginal wind projects bankable. This bridges the gap between the current renewable investment freeze and the eventual nuclear renaissance, serving all three goals of the trilemma simultaneously.

### Recommendation 5: Formalise the Strategic Retreat from Non-Core Geographies and Redeploy Capital to Sweden and Core Nordic Markets

**The problem:** The Norfolk and Berlin divestments represent an implicit strategic refocusing, but it has not been articulated as a coherent portfolio strategy. Without an explicit framework, the next management team may repeat the cycle of international expansion and costly exit.

**The recommendation:** Vattenfall should formally adopt a **Core Nordic Plus** geographic strategy, defining Sweden, Denmark, Finland, and Germany (where offshore wind regulation is more favourable) as primary markets, and committing not to enter new geographies without explicit board-level approval against defined criteria (grid connection clarity, regulatory stability, supply chain access). Through strategic divestments of the heating operations in Berlin and wind power projects off the coast of Norfolk in the UK, Vattenfall is strengthening its preparedness for future investments. This logic should be formalised into a standing portfolio governance policy.

**Why the evidence warrants this:** The Ansoff analysis shows that Vattenfall's failed diversification and market development moves (Berlin heat, UK offshore wind) destroyed value — the Norfolk exit crystallised a major impairment, and the Berlin heat sale, while at a positive price, represented a decade of management distraction. The RBV analysis confirms that Vattenfall's VRIN resources (hydro/nuclear assets, state credit access, Nordic market knowledge) are geographically concentrated. Deploying capital in geographies where these resources do not apply exposes the company to competitors with superior local capabilities.

## 7. Conclusion

Vattenfall's strategic situation in 2024–2025 is not primarily a story of corporate mismanagement. It is a story of **institutional design under stress**: a company created to serve the public interest, progressively commercialised, and now required to simultaneously maximise financial returns, lead Sweden's energy transition, and ensure affordable, reliable electricity for citizens and industry. These goals are not always compatible, and the current governance architecture — a single state owner with multiple objectives, a board accountable for financial performance, and a regulatory environment that has not kept pace with the energy transition — is insufficient to manage the tension.

The analytical chain constructed above leads to five recommendations that address the root causes rather than the symptoms: coordinating grid investment with generation planning; ring-fencing nuclear financing from earnings volatility; clarifying the dual mandate through public metrics; using PPAs to bridge the supply gap; and formalising geographic focus. Together, these recommendations would not eliminate the dual-mandate dilemma — that is a structural feature of state ownership — but they would create the institutional conditions under which the dilemma can be managed transparently and accountably, rather than resolved invisibly in favour of whichever goal is most easily measured in any given quarter.
