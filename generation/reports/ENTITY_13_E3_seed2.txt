# DIAGEO PLC: NAVIGATING THE POST-BOOM INFLECTION — A STRATEGIC CASE STUDY

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This case study analyses the strategic situation facing Diageo plc between fiscal year 2024 and fiscal year 2026. The central problem is a multi-year volume and profit decline caused by the convergence of five forces: a post-pandemic inventory correction in the company's largest market (North America), macro-economic demand weakness in key emerging and developed markets, structural consumer headwinds (health consciousness, GLP-1 drugs, moderation trends), escalating geopolitical risk (US tariffs), and a high leverage ratio that constrains strategic flexibility. The analysis employs a PESTEL scan, Porter's Five Forces, a Resource-Based View (RBV), Ansoff's Growth Matrix, and a BCG-style portfolio assessment to diagnose the situation and generate a coherent set of strategic recommendations.

---

## 1. SITUATIONAL OVERVIEW

Diageo is the world's largest premium spirits company by revenue. It holds a portfolio of over 200 brands sold in nearly 180 countries, including 13 billion-dollar brands such as Johnnie Walker, Smirnoff, Guinness, Don Julio, Crown Royal, Baileys, and Tanqueray, with number-one global positions in scotch, vodka, tequila, Canadian whisky, liqueurs, and gin.

Despite this scale, the company entered a sharp downturn from fiscal 2024. In FY2024, organic net sales declined $129 million (–0.6%), with a positive price/mix of 2.9 percentage points more than offset by a 3.5% volume decline, primarily driven by a 21.1% decline in the Latin America and Caribbean (LAC) region; organic operating profit fell $304 million (–4.8%). The recovery in FY2025 was modest and fragile: organic net sales grew only 1.7%, driven by volume growth of 0.9% and positive price/mix of 0.8%. Heading into FY2026, the company's H1 results showed renewed pressure: reported net sales of $10.5 billion declined 4.0%, with organic net sales declining 2.8%, driven by organic volume down 0.9% and negative price/mix of 1.9%.

Crucially, management itself has signalled strategic uncertainty: medium-term guidance was removed due to current macroeconomic and geopolitical uncertainty in many key markets impacting the pace of recovery. This withdrawal of guidance is a material signal that the strategic situation is not simply cyclical but structurally contested, warranting systematic analysis.

## 2. PESTEL ANALYSIS

A PESTEL scan identifies the macro-environmental forces shaping Diageo's strategic options, providing the evidentiary foundation for each subsequent framework.

### 2.1 Political

The most acute political risk is US tariff policy. Diageo warned that President Donald Trump's tariffs will further complicate its business, and the company scrapped its medium-term sales target amid uncertainty over tariffs' impact and flagging demand in some markets. The exposure is concentrated: North America is Diageo's largest division, representing more than a third of net sales, pulling in around $7.9 billion annually, with the portfolio leaning into premium and high-growth categories such as whiskey and tequila. In the US, around 45% of Diageo's net sales come from products that are imported. Quantifying the financial impact, the company's guidance for the expected impact of tariffs from UK and European imports is approximately $200 million pre-mitigation on an annualised basis, assuming current tariffs of 10% on UK imports and 15% on European imports, with Mexican and Canadian spirits imports remaining exempt under USMCA; some analysts estimate additional costs of $600 million annually.

**Analytical connection:** Because North America generates close to 50% of operating profit (see Section 3.3), tariff-driven cost inflation in this region is disproportionately threatening to group profitability. This constrains the pricing lever (Section 4) and makes geographic diversification (Section 6) more urgent.

### 2.2 Economic

The spirits industry is experiencing a cyclical hangover from pandemic-era over-ordering. "High inventory levels are expected to persist into 2024 and potentially beyond, with normalisation now not expected until 2025 or early 2026," according to Marten Lodewijks, president of the US Division at IWSR. The macro data confirm this: spirits declined by 2% in volume in the US in 2023, the first dip in the category in almost 30 years, with forecasts showing a predicted CAGR of only 1% between 2023 and 2028. In emerging markets, the picture is mixed: in Greater China, there was a strong double-digit decline in both volume and net sales, driven by Chinese white spirits with reduced consumption occasions across the baijiu category, primarily as a result of market policy, adversely impacting Diageo's net sales in the region by approximately 13% and group net sales by approximately 2.5%.

**Analytical connection:** The economic environment confirms that volume recovery will be slow and geographically uneven. This means Diageo cannot rely on market tailwinds to restore growth; it must actively reposition its portfolio and geography mix, as analysed under Ansoff (Section 5).

### 2.3 Social

The most structurally significant social trend is consumer moderation. Overall industry value rose to $543 billion in 2024 and is projected to climb to $806 billion by 2033, driven by premium pricing rather than volume. However, volume headwinds are real: roughly half of UK consumers now prefer "mid-strength" drinks, with 76% of Brits moderating their intake, and 1 in 3 pub visits now alcohol-free. The no- and low-alcohol segment is capturing this shift: in 2024 alone, the no-alcohol segment attracted an estimated 61 million new consumers globally compared to 2022 — far more than the 38 million recruited by low-alcohol options.

An emerging and potentially structural disruptor is the rise of GLP-1 weight-loss drugs. Patients taking semaglutide, the main component of Ozempic, drank less frequently, consumed fewer drinks, and were less likely to binge, according to a 2025 study from the University of Southern California.

**Analytical connection:** These social forces mean the long-run volume growth assumption underpinning Diageo's premiumisation strategy is under challenge. If consumers drink less but better, the strategy of trading up within premium tiers remains valid; but if GLP-1 drugs and moderation trends suppress even premium consumption occasions, the value pool itself shrinks. This makes the no/low alcohol adjacency (Section 6) strategically important as a hedge.

### 2.4 Technological

Digital commerce is reshaping the route-to-market. E-commerce is booming, with projections showing global alcohol e-commerce revenue surpassing $36 billion by 2028. Diageo has acknowledged this, with strategy documents noting investment in digital capabilities and AI-driven analytics. The company is fully utilising AI to organise data for better analytics to help deliver efficiencies in a sustainable manner.

**Analytical connection:** Digital commerce and data analytics represent an area where Diageo's scale advantage in brand investment can be leveraged (see RBV, Section 4), but only if the company builds proprietary first-party consumer data assets, which remains a gap.

### 2.5 Environmental

Climate-driven yield variability affected agave and barley prices in 2024–2025; tequila production costs rose materially amid significant agave price swings. This is directly relevant because tequila (Don Julio, Casamigos) is one of Diageo's highest-growth, highest-margin categories. Supply-chain concentration in Mexico for agave creates input cost volatility that cannot be fully hedged.

**Analytical connection:** Environmental risk in the supply chain reinforces the case for geographic and category diversification of the portfolio (Section 5) and for the Accelerate cost programme (Section 6).

### 2.6 Legal/Regulatory

Regulation of alcohol is tightening globally. Diageo's own annual report acknowledges that it must "enable and reinforce the breadth of choices that consumers have to moderate." Diageo has invested significantly in non-alcoholic brand extensions, including zero-alcohol Captain Morgan, Tanqueray, and Guinness 0.0, as part of its Spirit of Progress ESG plan. Regulatory pressure on alcohol advertising, minimum unit pricing in the UK and Ireland, and health labelling requirements in the EU all represent incremental headwinds.

**Analytical connection:** Regulatory risk reinforces the no/low alcohol strategic opportunity identified under Social (Section 2.3) and the Ansoff analysis (Section 5).

## 3. PORTER'S FIVE FORCES

Porter's framework assesses the structural attractiveness of the premium spirits industry and Diageo's position within it, explaining *why* the company is experiencing margin pressure even as it holds market leadership.

### 3.1 Competitive Rivalry (HIGH)

Diageo operates in a highly competitive global alcoholic beverage market, facing significant challenges from direct rivals including Pernod Ricard, Bacardi, Beam Suntory, Brown-Forman, Constellation Brands, LVMH's Moët Hennessy Division, Campari Group, William Grant & Sons, and Rémy Cointreau. Pernod Ricard, the second-largest spirits company globally, is Diageo's most direct competitor, competing head-to-head across whiskies, vodka, cognac, and agave spirits. Rivalry is intensified because premium spirits is a high-fixed-cost, brand-intensive industry where market share is contested through sustained advertising and promotional spend. Diageo's own data show it grew or held market share in only 65% of measured markets in FY2025, meaning it is losing ground in 35% of its measured footprint — a signal that competitive rivalry is eroding its position.

**Analytical connection:** High rivalry means that any reduction in marketing investment — which Diageo has used as a cost-saving lever — risks permanent share loss to rivals. This creates a strategic tension between short-term margin protection and long-term brand equity, addressed in Recommendation 3 (Section 6).

### 3.2 Threat of New Entrants (MODERATE-LOW)

Capital requirements for distilling, brand-building, and global distribution are substantial, creating high barriers to entry at the premium global scale. However, the threat from craft distillers is non-trivial at the super-premium and artisanal tier. The spirits industry faces potential bankruptcies, lawsuits, and the collapse of some whiskey investment schemes, with a "bright spot" being savvy craft whiskey players. Celebrity-backed brands also represent a disruptive entry vector: Flecha Azul, the upscale tequila label backed by Mark Wahlberg, leaped 57% to 94,000 cases in 2024.

**Analytical connection:** The celebrity-brand phenomenon threatens Diageo's pricing power at the ultra-premium tier, making the formation of the Diageo Luxury Group (Section 5) a directly relevant competitive response.

### 3.3 Bargaining Power of Buyers (MODERATE-HIGH)

Buyers include global retail chains (e.g., Walmart, Tesco), on-trade operators (bars, restaurants), and distributors. Consolidation in retail and on-trade increases buyer power. In the US three-tier distribution system, distributors hold significant leverage. Diageo's response has been to invest in route-to-market capabilities; however, its transition to direct distribution in France from a Moët Hennessy joint venture caused disruption, with Diageo's performance in France adversely impacted by the transition to a new distribution model for its malts and luxury brands, with the firm moving to direct distribution by Diageo France in March 2024.

**Analytical connection:** Route-to-market transitions are a strategic vulnerability. The France case demonstrates that even a well-resourced company can lose sales during distribution model changes, reinforcing the need for careful sequencing of any further restructuring.

### 3.4 Bargaining Power of Suppliers (LOW-MODERATE)

For most inputs (glass, packaging, grain), Diageo's scale gives it negotiating power. However, for category-specific inputs, power shifts: agave for tequila is geographically concentrated in Jalisco, Mexico, and climate-driven yield variability caused tequila production costs to rise materially amid significant agave price swings in 2024–2025. Scotch whisky's long maturation cycle (minimum three years, often 10–25 years for premium expressions) means supply is structurally inflexible.

**Analytical connection:** Supplier power in agave and aged whisky creates asymmetric risk: when demand falls (as now), Diageo cannot reduce supply-side commitments quickly, creating inventory and cost pressure. This is a structural constraint on the speed of any recovery.

### 3.5 Threat of Substitutes (HIGH and RISING)

Substitutes include wine, beer, RTD cocktails, cannabis-infused beverages, and no/low alcohol products. In 2024, the growth within spirits was almost entirely attributable to the tequila and ready-to-drink (RTD) spirits and cocktails categories, with almost every other category experiencing a decline from 2023 sales. The GLP-1 drug trend (Section 2.3) represents a novel and potentially structural substitute for alcohol consumption occasions.

**Analytical connection:** The high and rising threat from substitutes means Diageo cannot assume that its premium spirits positioning alone will protect volumes. The RTD category is both a substitute (cannibalising premium spirits occasions) and an opportunity (if Diageo can capture the RTD consumer), making it a priority for Ansoff market development (Section 5).

## 4. RESOURCE-BASED VIEW (RBV)

The RBV asks which of Diageo's internal resources and capabilities are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN), and whether the current strategy deploys them effectively.

### 4.1 Brand Portfolio (VRIN: HIGH)

Diageo has a portfolio of over 200 brands with 13 billion-dollar brands, holding number-one global positions in scotch, vodka, tequila, Canadian whisky, liqueurs, and gin. These positions took decades to build and cannot be replicated quickly. The brand portfolio is the company's most durable competitive advantage. However, brand equity is not self-sustaining: it requires consistent investment. Diageo's own data show that the company's marketing spending had been growing in recent years alongside growth in net sales, but 2024 marked a decline in marketing spending, down nearly 3% compared to the previous year. Cutting marketing to protect short-term margins risks eroding the very asset that underpins long-run competitive advantage — a classic VRIN erosion trap.

**Warrant:** The RBV logic is that sustainable competitive advantage requires not merely possessing VRIN resources but actively investing in them. Reducing marketing spend on billion-dollar brands is therefore strategically self-defeating unless the savings are redeployed into higher-return brand-building activities.

### 4.2 Global Distribution Network (VRIN: HIGH)

Operating in over 180 countries, Diageo's extensive distribution network is a key differentiator, ensuring its products are widely available and supporting its market position. Building equivalent distribution depth would require decades and billions of dollars for any new entrant or smaller competitor, making this a genuinely rare and inimitable capability.

**Analytical connection:** The distribution network is the mechanism by which Diageo can execute geographic diversification (Ansoff, Section 5) into high-growth markets such as India and Africa without the full cost of market entry, since the infrastructure already exists.

### 4.3 Brand-Building Capability (VRIN: MODERATE-HIGH)

Diageo invests heavily in consumer insights, marketing, and digital capabilities to drive brand growth, with innovation in non-alcoholic beverages and RTD offerings showcasing adaptability. The Guinness brand is the clearest proof point: Guinness delivered double-digit growth for an eighth consecutive half, supported by brand-building expertise, innovation, and growing global momentum. This track record demonstrates that Diageo's brand-building capability, when deployed effectively, can sustain growth even in a difficult market environment.

**Warrant:** The contrast between Guinness (eight consecutive halves of double-digit growth) and Johnnie Walker (double-digit declines in certain markets) suggests the capability is present but unevenly applied. The strategic implication is that portfolio prioritisation — concentrating brand-building resources on the highest-return brands — is more important than across-the-board spending.

### 4.4 Premiumisation Knowledge and Price Architecture (VRIN: MODERATE)

In the last 10 years, premium and above spirits grew from 26% of category value to almost 35%, with the super-premium-plus tier growing in value more than two times faster than other price tiers. Diageo has built deep expertise in managing price ladders across categories, as evidenced by its scotch portfolio spanning value ($15) through luxury ($250+) price tiers. However, this capability is increasingly shared by rivals, reducing its rarity.

**Analytical connection:** Because premiumisation knowledge is becoming less rare, Diageo must move up the price ladder faster than competitors, which is the strategic logic behind the Diageo Luxury Group (Section 5).

### 4.5 Financial Leverage as a Constraint (NOT a Resource)

One internal factor that is *limiting* rather than enabling strategy is Diageo's balance sheet. Net debt as at 30 June 2025 was $21.9 billion, with a leverage ratio of 3.4x net debt to adjusted EBITDA, in line with guidance of 3.3–3.5x. This leverage level is above the company's own target range of 2.5–3.0x, which means the balance sheet is constraining strategic flexibility for acquisitions and investment. The Accelerate programme directly addresses this: the cost savings programme target was increased to approximately $625 million over three years, with a firm focus on productivity, driving cash, and growth.

**Warrant:** High leverage during a revenue downturn is doubly dangerous — it reduces the company's ability to invest counter-cyclically in brand-building (Section 4.1) while increasing financial risk. Deleveraging is therefore not merely a financial objective but a strategic enabler for all other recommendations.

## 5. ANSOFF MATRIX: DIAGEO'S GROWTH OPTIONS

The Ansoff Matrix maps Diageo's growth options across existing/new products and existing/new markets, providing a structured way to evaluate the risk-return profile of each strategic direction.

### 5.1 Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets): MEDIUM PRIORITY

In North America — Diageo's most important market — the company is recovering but fragile. Diageo outperformed the market in North America with high-quality share growth and positive organic net sales growth, driven by strong execution and momentum in Don Julio and Crown Royal. This indicates that market penetration through superior execution is achievable. However, the structural inventory correction means volume growth will be slow: normalisation of US spirits inventory is not expected until 2025 or early 2026.

**Warrant:** Market penetration is the lowest-risk Ansoff option and should be the baseline. But given the structural headwinds in North America and China, it cannot alone restore the growth rates needed to deleverage the balance sheet. It is a necessary but insufficient strategy.

### 5.2 Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets): HIGH PRIORITY

India and Africa represent the most compelling market development opportunities. India (United Spirits) and Africa are central to volume-led premiumisation, with India growing double-digit despite recent excise increases in the state of Maharashtra. India grew double-digit with growth across the portfolio despite recent excise increases. Africa is a long-term structural opportunity: Diageo's existing East Africa Beer business (EABL), while subject to a recent disposal transaction, demonstrates deep market knowledge that can be leveraged for spirits growth.

**Warrant:** India and Africa exhibit the demographic and middle-class growth trends that underpin the long-run spirits premiumisation thesis. Diageo sees a long-term trend of consumers choosing to switch to spirits from beer and wine, supported by favourable population demographics and the increasing size of the middle class in key markets globally. Deploying existing brands (Johnnie Walker, Guinness) through existing distribution infrastructure into these high-growth markets is the highest expected-value growth option available.

### 5.3 Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets): HIGH PRIORITY

The RTD (ready-to-drink) and no/low alcohol categories represent the most important product development vectors. In 2024, growth within spirits was almost entirely attributable to the tequila and RTD spirits and cocktails categories. Diageo has already moved in this direction: Q1 FY2026 showed strong momentum in RTDs and RTS, particularly Smirnoff Ice. On no/low alcohol, Diageo has doubled Guinness 0.0 production capacity, with increased investment to help grow the reach of Guinness 0.0 as consumers increasingly opt to moderate their drinking.

**Warrant:** Product development in RTD and no/low categories directly addresses the substitution threat identified in Porter's Five Forces (Section 3.5). By building these categories internally, Diageo converts a competitive threat into a growth opportunity, while leveraging its existing brand equity (RBV, Section 4.1) — Guinness 0.0 succeeds precisely because the Guinness brand is trusted.

### 5.4 Diversification (New Products, New Markets): LOW PRIORITY

The Diageo Luxury Group represents a focused diversification into the ultra-luxury tier. In November 2024, Diageo announced the formation of the Diageo Luxury Group, a new global division consolidating all brands priced at USD $100 and above, along with 15 brand homes and distillery visitor experiences, to reflect a renewed focus on the fastest-growing spirits price tier. The key focus is on accelerating the growth of brands that retail at US$100 and above, led by managing director Julie Bramham, former global brand director of Johnnie Walker.

**Warrant:** The luxury tier is structurally attractive — the super-premium-plus tier has grown in value more than 50% faster than other price tiers in the category, gaining approximately 600 basis points of share of international spirits retail sales value since 2014. However, this is also the tier most exposed to economic downturns and the celebrity-brand disruption identified in Porter's analysis (Section 3.2). Diversification into luxury should be pursued selectively, not as a wholesale strategic pivot.

## 6. BCG PORTFOLIO ANALYSIS

Mapping Diageo's key brands onto a BCG-style matrix (relative market share vs. market growth) reveals a portfolio in transition that requires active management rather than passive maintenance.

**Don Julio** is the clearest Star: a nearly 1-million-case surge by Diageo-owned Don Julio lifted tequila's above-$40 luxury tier to another year of growth in 2024. Investment here is warranted by both high market share and high category growth.

**Guinness** is also a Star, with a distinctive competitive moat: Diageo needs its leading stout beer Guinness to keep growing fast, and is pushing a zero-alcohol alternative, with Guinness having been a much-needed bright spot in earnings for the world's top spirits maker.

**Johnnie Walker** is transitioning from Cash Cow to Question Mark. Its decline in China and parts of the US is concerning for a brand that represents the core of Diageo's scotch franchise. Excluding the impact of the Cîroc transaction, organic net sales growth was 1.5%. This portfolio rationalisation is strategically correct: divesting declining brands frees capital for Stars and Question Marks.

**Analytical connection:** The BCG analysis confirms that Diageo's portfolio is bifurcating — a handful of high-growth Stars (Don Julio, Guinness, RTD) coexist with a larger number of mature or declining brands. The strategic implication is to accelerate resource reallocation from Cash Cows and Dogs toward Stars and high-potential Question Marks, which is the portfolio logic underlying the Accelerate programme and the Diageo Luxury Group.

## 7. STRATEGIC RECOMMENDATIONS

The preceding analysis generates a coherent chain of recommendations. Each is warranted by the evidence assembled above.

### Recommendation 1: Protect and Accelerate the Stars — Don Julio and Guinness

**What:** Ring-fence marketing and capital investment for Don Julio and Guinness, treating them as the primary growth engines for the next three to five years. Expand Guinness 0.0 to draught in global on-trade accounts. Extend Don Julio's price architecture upward into the $200+ tier.

**Why (Warrant):** The RBV analysis (Section 4.1) establishes that brand equity is Diageo's primary VRIN resource. The BCG analysis (Section 6) identifies Don Julio and Guinness as the Stars with the highest return on investment. Porter's rivalry analysis (Section 3.1) shows that share losses in 35% of measured markets are occurring even now — protecting the Stars from rivalry requires sustained, not reduced, investment. The Ansoff market development strategy (Section 5.2) can be executed most effectively behind these two brands, which have proven global resonance.

**Risk mitigation:** Tariff exposure on Don Julio (Mexican origin) is real, but Mexican and Canadian spirits imports remain exempt under USMCA, limiting immediate tariff risk to Don Julio. Guinness is brewed in multiple markets, further reducing tariff exposure.

### Recommendation 2: Accelerate Geographic Pivot to India and Africa

**What:** Increase capital allocation and management bandwidth to India and Africa, targeting double-digit organic net sales growth in both markets within three years. Prioritise Johnnie Walker and Guinness as the anchor brands for market development in these geographies, leveraging existing distribution infrastructure.

**Why (Warrant):** The Ansoff analysis (Section 5.2) identifies market development as the highest-priority growth option given structural headwinds in North America and China. Diageo's long-term thesis — consumers switching to spirits from beer and wine, supported by favourable population demographics and the increasing size of the middle class — is most intact in India and Africa. The RBV (Section 4.2) confirms that Diageo's distribution network already exists in these markets, meaning the marginal cost of market development is lower than entering new geographies from scratch. India's double-digit growth in FY2025 (Section 5.2) provides empirical validation that the opportunity is real and accessible.

### Recommendation 3: Build a Credible No/Low Alcohol Platform

**What:** Establish no/low alcohol as a formal strategic business unit (SBU), not merely an ESG footnote. Scale Guinness 0.0 to draught in at least 10,000 on-trade venues globally within two years. Extend the no/low format to Tanqueray and Captain Morgan through accelerated product development. Set a revenue target for the no/low portfolio (e.g., 5% of net sales by FY2028).

**Why (Warrant):** The PESTEL analysis (Sections 2.3, 2.6) identifies moderation, health consciousness, and GLP-1 drug adoption as structural — not cyclical — consumer shifts. Porter's substitution analysis (Section 3.5) confirms that no/low products are both a threat and an opportunity. The Ansoff product development logic (Section 5.3) shows that Diageo can leverage its existing brand equity (Guinness, Tanqueray) in no/low, reducing the risk of this move. IWSR analysts describe the current environment as a "transformative period of growth" for no/low products, bringing in 61 million new consumers globally in 2024 alone. Failing to build scale here cedes the category to pure-play no/low brands, permanently shrinking Diageo's total addressable market.

### Recommendation 4: Deleverage the Balance Sheet Through Disciplined Portfolio Rationalisation

**What:** Continue disposing of non-strategic, non-core brands (following the Cîroc precedent) to reduce net debt from 3.4x to within the 2.5–3.0x target range within 18 months. Redeploy disposal proceeds to reduce debt rather than fund acquisitions. Maintain the Accelerate cost savings programme target of $625 million.

**Why (Warrant):** The RBV analysis (Section 4.5) identifies high leverage as the binding constraint on strategic flexibility. The Accelerate programme targets approximately $625 million in cost savings over three years, with a firm focus on productivity, driving cash, and growth. The company remains committed to returning to well within its target leverage ratio range of 2.5–3.0x through disposals of non-strategic, non-core assets, alongside delivering positive operating leverage and tighter capital discipline. Until leverage is within the target range, Diageo's ability to invest counter-cyclically in brand-building (Recommendation 1) and geographic expansion (Recommendation 2) is constrained. Deleveraging is therefore a strategic prerequisite, not merely a financial housekeeping task.

### Recommendation 5: Develop a Tariff Mitigation Strategy for North America

**What:** Accelerate investment in US-based production capacity for categories currently imported (e.g., scotch-inspired American whiskey expressions, US-bottled gin). Pursue pricing actions selectively on premium and super-premium tiers where demand is less elastic. Hedge input cost exposure through multi-year supply contracts.

**Why (Warrant):** The PESTEL analysis (Section 2.1) quantifies tariff exposure at up to $200 million annually pre-mitigation, with some analyst estimates as high as $600 million. Diageo expects to be able to mitigate around half of the tariff impact on operating profit on an ongoing basis through actions taken to date, before any pricing. However, the remaining 50% of unmitigated impact will directly compress margins in Diageo's most profitable market. Porter's buyer power analysis (Section 3.3) indicates that passing the full tariff cost to distributors and retailers is not feasible without volume loss. Therefore, a combination of production localisation, selective pricing, and cost efficiency is required. This recommendation directly connects to the deleveraging objective (Recommendation 4): tariff-driven margin compression will slow debt reduction unless proactively mitigated.

## CONCLUSION

Diageo's strategic situation is best understood as a multi-layered crisis of confidence: a cyclical volume correction in North America has coincided with structural consumer moderation trends, a high-leverage balance sheet that limits strategic flexibility, and geopolitical tariff risk concentrated in its most profitable market. The analysis reveals, however, that Diageo's VRIN resources — its brand portfolio, global distribution network, and brand-building capability — remain intact and, in some cases (Guinness, Don Julio), are demonstrably growing. The strategic path forward is therefore not transformation but *prioritisation*: concentrate resources on the Stars, accelerate into structurally growing geographies (India, Africa), build a credible no/low alcohol platform to hedge structural volume risk, deleverage the balance sheet to restore strategic freedom, and mitigate tariff risk through production localisation and selective pricing. Each of these recommendations is warranted by the evidence, connected to the analytical frameworks, and coherent as a system — because deleveraging enables investment, investment in Stars drives share, share gains in India and Africa offset North American weakness, and the no/low platform hedges the long-run volume risk that threatens the entire premiumisation thesis.
