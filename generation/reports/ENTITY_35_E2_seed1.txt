# Shell plc: Strategic Case Study — The Dual Legitimacy Crisis

## *Navigating the Tension Between Fossil Fuel Profitability and the Energy Transition*

---

## 1. INTRODUCTION AND CASE OVERVIEW

Shell plc stands at one of the most consequential strategic crossroads in its 120-year history. Shell is a global group of energy and petrochemical companies, employing 85,000 people across more than 70 countries. It is simultaneously one of the world's most profitable energy companies and one of the most scrutinised for its role in climate change. The strategic situation examined in this case study is what we term Shell's **Dual Legitimacy Crisis**: the company must maintain financial legitimacy with capital markets (which demand returns, growth, and capital discipline) while preserving social and regulatory legitimacy with governments, courts, and civil society (which demand credible decarbonisation). These two pressures are not simply in tension — they are pulling Shell's strategy in structurally opposite directions. The central question is whether Shell's current strategic response — its "More Value with Less Emissions" pivot — resolves this tension, or merely defers it.

---

## 2. SITUATIONAL ANALYSIS

### 2.1 The Strategic Situation: A Pivot Under Pressure

Under CEO Wael Sawan, appointed in 2023, Shell articulated a strategic philosophy built on three principles. Shell's focus on performance, discipline, and simplification is driving clear choices about where it can have the greatest impact through the energy transition and create the most value for investors and customers. This reorientation was not merely rhetorical. Shell took concrete portfolio decisions: it retreated from offshore wind, doubled down on LNG, and aggressively returned capital to shareholders.

Shell, one of the world's largest oil and gas companies, announced it will not pursue new offshore wind projects, signalling a notable shift in its renewable energy strategy. Simultaneously, in June 2023, Shell announced a scale down of its low-carbon investments, from 33% of total investments in 2022 to 15%–20% over 2023–25. These moves were accompanied by a weakening of climate commitments: Shell lowered its Net Carbon Intensity (NCI) target and is now targeting a 15–20% reduction in the NCI of the energy products it sells by 2030, compared with 2016, against 20% previously.

The financial rationale was clear. Shell generated free cash flow of $40 billion across the full year 2024, higher than 2023, in a lower price environment. Its continued focus on simplification helped to deliver over $3 billion in structural cost reductions since 2022, meeting its target ahead of schedule.

Yet this pivot simultaneously intensified the social legitimacy problem, as detailed below.

---

### 2.2 PESTEL Analysis

**Political:** The geopolitical environment has, paradoxically, strengthened the case for fossil fuels in the short term. The ongoing conflict in Eastern Europe has led to significant volatility in global oil and gas prices, with Brent crude futures fluctuating around $80–$90 per barrel in early 2024, impacting Shell's operational costs and revenue streams. Energy security concerns across Europe and Asia have elevated natural gas — and LNG in particular — as a strategic priority for governments, creating a policy tailwind for Shell's LNG-first strategy. However, the political environment is bifurcated: while fossil fuel producers benefit from energy security narratives, the EU's carbon border adjustment mechanism and tightening emissions trading systems impose long-run cost pressures on high-carbon assets.

**Environmental/Legal:** The most dramatic environmental-legal event of the period was the *Milieudefensie v Shell* appellate ruling. On 12 November 2024, a Dutch appeals court ruled that Shell does not have to reduce its CO₂ emissions by 45% by 2030 compared to 2019 levels, as previously ordered by the Hague District Court on 26 May 2021. Shell now has the right to adjust its own emissions reduction targets as it sees fit. This provided immediate legal relief. However, the appeals court maintained that large companies that have contributed to causing dangerous climate change have a special duty to others on this planet to help combat it, and that the court can intervene if a company fails to fulfil its duty of care to limit CO₂ emissions. The ruling thus removed a specific mandatory target while preserving the principle of corporate climate liability — a distinction that matters enormously for Shell's long-run legal exposure.

**Economic:** Shell's cash flow from operating activities for the full year 2024 was $54.7 billion, primarily driven by Adjusted EBITDA, and working capital inflows of $2.1 billion, partly offset by tax payments of $12.0 billion. This financial strength is the foundation of Shell's current strategy, but it is contingent on oil and gas price levels that are inherently volatile. The company's capital expenditure for 2024 was $21.1 billion, and at its 2025 Capital Markets Day, Shell committed to invest for growth while maintaining capital discipline, with spend lowered to $20–22 billion per year for 2025–2028.

**Social:** Public and investor expectations around climate action remain high, even as Shell retreats from them. ClientEarth argued that Shell's board is persisting with a transition strategy that is fundamentally flawed, leaving the company seriously exposed to the risks that climate change poses to Shell's future success, despite the Board's legal duty to manage those risks. This claim received support from major institutional investors including UK pension funds.

**Technological:** The rise of renewables and electric vehicles creates substitution risk for Shell's downstream products over the medium term. At the same time, the energy demands of artificial intelligence data centres are generating new demand for gas-fired power, partially offsetting this threat. Shell is expecting more LNG consumption worldwide to come from the impact of artificial intelligence and efforts to cut emissions in heavy industries and transportation.

### 2.3 Porter's Five Forces Analysis

**Threat of New Entrants (Low):** The oil and gas industry has extraordinarily high barriers to entry — capital requirements for exploration and production run into tens of billions of dollars, access to reserves is geologically constrained, and regulatory licences are difficult to obtain. Shell's reliance on highly specialised resources and its global infrastructure network constitute durable barriers that new entrants cannot readily replicate.

**Bargaining Power of Suppliers (Moderate):** Shell's suppliers include oilfield services companies (e.g., SLB, Halliburton), equipment manufacturers, and sovereign resource owners. OPEC+ nations, as resource controllers, wield significant upstream pricing power. However, Shell's scale and diversified sourcing reduce dependence on any single supplier.

**Bargaining Power of Buyers (Moderate–High):** Shell operates in a dynamic energy landscape where intense competition, significant buyer power, and the looming threat of substitutes heavily influence its strategic decisions. In commodity markets, buyers can switch between suppliers with relative ease, limiting Shell's pricing power in refining and chemicals. In LNG, however, long-term contracts with Asian buyers partially insulate Shell from spot market buyer power.

**Threat of Substitutes (Rising):** Renewable energy, battery storage, and electric vehicles represent growing substitutes for Shell's core products. The pace of substitution is policy-dependent and slower than many forecasts predicted, but the directional threat is unambiguous. Shell's retreat from offshore wind means it is less positioned to capture value from the substitute technologies it faces.

**Competitive Rivalry (High):** Major oil and gas companies are relatively equal in size, power, and capabilities, which increases the intensity of rivalry and can manifest itself in a price war if a competitor tries to gain market share. Shell competes directly with ExxonMobil, Chevron, TotalEnergies, and bp, all of which are simultaneously managing similar energy transition pressures. TotalEnergies, notably, has pursued a more aggressive renewables strategy while also growing LNG, making it a benchmark competitor.

### 2.4 Resource-Based View (RBV) Analysis

The Resource-Based View (Barney, 1991) holds that sustainable competitive advantage derives from resources and capabilities that are **Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN)**. Applying this framework to Shell reveals both the foundations of its current pivot and the risks embedded within it.

**Shell's VRIN Resources:**

*Trading and Optimisation Capability:* Shell's most distinctive resource is arguably its global trading operation. Shell became one of the world's largest traders and suppliers of sustainable aviation fuel in 2024, achieving this because of its long-term agreements with producers, the strength of its customer relationships, and strategic investments in logistics around key terminals and airports. More broadly, Shell sells nearly three times the energy products that it produces, meeting customers' demand for oil and gas and low-carbon products through its integrated model. This 3:1 trading-to-production ratio is rare among oil majors and represents a genuinely inimitable organisational capability built over decades — it requires deep market intelligence, counterparty relationships, risk management systems, and logistics infrastructure that cannot be quickly replicated.

*LNG Infrastructure and Market Position:* Shell is the world's largest private LNG trader. Its integrated position — from upstream gas production through liquefaction to shipping, regasification, and marketing — constitutes a value chain that is difficult to replicate. Wael Sawan stated that Shell wants to become the world's leading integrated gas and LNG business and the most customer-focused energy marketer and trader, while sustaining a material level of liquids production. This aspiration is grounded in genuine competitive position, not aspiration alone.

*Brand and Scale:* Shell employs 85,000 people across more than 70 countries, giving it operational reach and customer relationships that constitute a rare and valuable resource. Its brand carries trust in both B2B and B2C markets.

**RBV Diagnosis:** Shell's pivot towards LNG and trading is strategically coherent from an RBV perspective — it concentrates investment in areas where Shell's resources are most VRIN-compliant. The retreat from offshore wind is also explicable: in renewables, Shell's resources are not rare or inimitable (capital is available to many players; technology is commoditising). The RBV logic thus supports the portfolio focus. However, the RBV also exposes a gap: Shell's net-zero commitment by 2050 requires capabilities in clean energy that it is currently *divesting* rather than building, creating a future resource deficit.

## 3. THE DUAL LEGITIMACY CRISIS: CORE STRATEGIC TENSION

The frameworks above converge on a single, central diagnosis. Shell faces two simultaneous legitimacy deficits that its current strategy addresses asymmetrically.

### 3.1 Financial Legitimacy: Largely Restored

Shell's financial legitimacy with capital markets has been substantially rebuilt under Sawan. Shell increased its structural cost reduction target, aiming for cumulative $5–7 billion by the end of 2028 compared to 2022. It has also committed to enhancing shareholder distributions from 30–40% to 40–50% of cash flow from operations through the cycle, continuing to prioritise share buybacks while maintaining a 4% per annum progressive dividend policy. The quarterly evidence supports this: total shareholder distributions in Q3 2024 amounted to $5.7 billion, comprising repurchases of shares of $3.5 billion and cash dividends paid to Shell plc shareholders of $2.1 billion.

The capital discipline is equally visible: Shell's focus on "more value with less emissions" through disciplined capital allocation and structural cost reductions, which achieved $3.1 billion since 2022 by the end of 2024, is designed to ensure resilience.

### 3.2 Social/Regulatory Legitimacy: Structurally Weakened

The social legitimacy deficit has deepened precisely as financial legitimacy has been restored. The mechanism is straightforward: Shell's portfolio pivot — retreating from low-carbon investments and weakening emissions targets — has widened the gap between its stated 2050 net-zero ambition and its near-term actions.

Follow This, a shareholder activist group, noted that Shell's retired and watered-down targets will take Shell even further away from Paris alignment than it was before. After another year with record-breaking global temperatures, backtracking on essential emissions reduction targets clearly shows what Wael Sawan means by a "ruthless focus."

The credibility gap is structural, not merely reputational. ClientEarth observed that although Shell has an ambition to reach net-zero by 2050 in absolute terms, Shell's "intensity" pledges for 2030 and 2035 are not the same as commitments to achieve near-term absolute reductions in fossil fuel business in line with climate science. Instead, the gaps in Shell's pledges permit continued fossil fuel sales for the next 14 years.

The distinction between intensity-based and absolute targets matters strategically: an intensity reduction is compatible with *growing* total emissions if volumes increase, which is precisely Shell's plan (LNG sales growth of 4–5% per year). This means Shell's emissions trajectory may diverge from its stated ambition even as it reports progress against its chosen metrics.

## 4. STRATEGIC OPTIONS ANALYSIS

The Dual Legitimacy Crisis generates three archetypal strategic responses, each with distinct trade-offs:

### Option A: Accelerate the Fossil Fuel Pivot ("Extraction Maximiser")
Fully lean into hydrocarbons, maximise returns while the energy transition is slow, and exit climate commitments explicitly. This maximises short-run financial legitimacy but abandons social legitimacy entirely, exposing Shell to regulatory risk, stranded asset risk, and loss of access to capital from ESG-mandated investors.

### Option B: Genuine Transition Leadership ("Green Transformer")
Redirect capital aggressively into low-carbon technologies, accept lower near-term returns, and build the capabilities needed for a net-zero world. This restores social legitimacy but risks financial legitimacy if the energy transition is slower than expected, destroying shareholder value in the interim.

### Option C: Integrated Transition Trader ("Ambidextrous Integrator") — *Shell's Current Path*
Use the cash flows from hydrocarbons to fund a selective, capability-led transition, concentrating on areas where Shell's VRIN resources (trading, LNG, logistics) provide competitive advantage in both the current and future energy system. This is Shell's explicit strategy: Shell's energy transition plans cover all its businesses. In Integrated Gas, Shell is growing its world-leading LNG business with lower carbon intensity. In Upstream, it is reducing emissions from oil and gas production. In Downstream and Renewables and Energy Solutions, Shell is growing sales of low-carbon products and solutions such as biofuels, electric vehicle charging and renewable power, while investing in hydrogen and other fuels of the future.

The analytical verdict is that Option C is the most defensible *in principle*, but Shell's current execution of it is **asymmetrically weighted towards financial legitimacy**, creating the social legitimacy deficit identified above.

## 5. DEMAND-SIDE VALIDATION: IS THE LNG BET SOUND?

A critical test of Shell's strategy is whether the LNG demand thesis is empirically warranted. The evidence is mixed but broadly supportive in the medium term.

Shell predicts that annual LNG demand will skyrocket to between 630 and 718 million tonnes by 2040, fuelled by Asia's relentless industrial expansion, the push for cleaner energy in heavy industries and transportation, and the burgeoning energy demands of artificial intelligence. Independent analysis supports this directionally: gas demand in Asia is projected to reach approximately 1.2 trillion cubic metres by 2030, contributing more than half of global demand growth and peaking around 2040 at approximately 1.38 trillion cubic metres.

The reasoning is sound: natural gas emits roughly half the CO₂ of coal per unit of energy, making it a credible bridge fuel for Asian economies transitioning away from coal. Natural gas emits about half as much carbon dioxide as coal to produce the same amount of energy, making it an important "partner fuel" in the energy transition, particularly for developing economies which prioritise reliable, affordable fuel.

However, the demand thesis carries a significant downside scenario. The World Energy Outlook 2025 shows LNG demand growth slowing from 80 billion cubic metres per year, as witnessed in the past decade, to 70 billion cubic metres per year to 2035 under the Current Policies scenario, and even lower under the Stated Policies scenario, indicating the high potential for gas to be replaced by a combination of renewable energy expansion, efficiency increases, and electrification.

This creates a **timing risk**: Shell is investing to grow LNG sales by 4–5% per year through 2030, but if the energy transition accelerates — driven by falling renewable costs, battery storage breakthroughs, or more aggressive policy — LNG assets could become stranded before they are fully depreciated. The RBV analysis reinforces this: Shell's LNG infrastructure is VRIN today, but its value depends on the persistence of gas demand, which is not guaranteed.

## 6. RECOMMENDATIONS AND ACTION PLAN

The following recommendations are designed to resolve the Dual Legitimacy Crisis by rebalancing Shell's strategy without sacrificing the financial discipline that has been legitimately restored. They are grounded in the analytical frameworks above and derive from the specific gaps they identify.

---

### Recommendation 1: Restore Absolute Emissions Targets Alongside Intensity Metrics
**Analytical basis:** The credibility gap identified in the social legitimacy analysis stems directly from the substitution of intensity-based targets for absolute ones. Intensity improvements are compatible with growing total emissions, which undermines the net-zero claim. The *Milieudefensie* appellate ruling confirmed Shell's legal duty of care, even while removing the specific 45% mandate. This duty will be enforced again if Shell's emissions trajectory diverges visibly from its stated ambition.

**Action:** Shell should establish an absolute Scope 3 emissions cap — not just an intensity reduction — for the period 2025–2030, consistent with a 1.5°C pathway. This does not require Shell to exit LNG; it requires Shell to offset LNG growth with accelerated reductions elsewhere (e.g., chemicals divestiture, upstream portfolio rationalisation). AXA IM expressed concern about the lack of absolute Scope 3 emissions reduction targets and what it sees as an excessive reliance on nature-based offsets, even though Shell retired its former 2030 target. Reinstating an absolute target would directly address this institutional investor concern, reducing the risk of capital withdrawal by ESG-mandated funds.

---

### Recommendation 2: Redirect the Trading Capability Towards Low-Carbon Commodities at Scale
**Analytical basis:** The RBV analysis identified Shell's trading and optimisation capability as its most distinctive VRIN resource. Currently, this capability is deployed overwhelmingly in fossil fuel markets. The sustainable aviation fuel (SAF) example demonstrates that the capability is transferable: Shell became one of the world's largest traders and suppliers of sustainable aviation fuel in 2024 because of its long-term agreements with producers, the strength of its customer relationships, and strategic investments in logistics around key terminals and airports. This model — using trading infrastructure rather than asset ownership — is capital-light and scalable.

**Action:** Shell should systematically apply its trading model to carbon credits, green hydrogen, renewable power purchase agreements (PPAs), and biofuels — not as niche activities but as core business lines. The 3:1 trading-to-production ratio that distinguishes Shell in fossil fuels should become a target in clean energy markets. This directly addresses the social legitimacy deficit (demonstrating genuine transition activity) while leveraging the financial strength already established (capital-light model preserves cash returns). The offshore wind retreat is strategically defensible; the failure to redirect that capital and capability into tradeable clean energy markets is not.

---

### Recommendation 3: Implement Scenario-Gated Capital Allocation for LNG
**Analytical basis:** The demand-side analysis reveals that Shell's LNG bet is directionally sound but carries significant timing risk under accelerated transition scenarios. The current strategy commits $20–22 billion per year in capital expenditure through 2028 without explicit scenario-gating. This is a strategic vulnerability: if LNG demand peaks earlier than Shell's central forecast (as the IEA's Stated Policies scenario implies), capital deployed from 2026–2028 may generate sub-threshold returns.

**Action:** Shell should adopt a **real options approach** to LNG capital allocation, structuring investments in tranches with explicit decision gates tied to observable transition indicators (e.g., renewable cost curves, Asian coal phase-out policy milestones, EV penetration rates). Investments in long-lived LNG infrastructure (liquefaction trains, shipping) should be stress-tested against a scenario in which LNG demand peaks by 2035 rather than 2040. Projects that are viable only under the high-demand scenario should be deferred or structured as joint ventures to distribute stranded asset risk. This recommendation directly addresses the timing risk identified in Section 5 while preserving the financial discipline that has restored capital market confidence.

---

### Recommendation 4: Resolve the Chemicals and Products Drag Decisively
**Analytical basis:** Shell's Chemicals and Products segment has been a persistent value destroyer relative to its integrated gas and upstream businesses. Shell's focus on disciplined capital allocation and structural cost reductions achieved $3.1 billion since 2022 by the end of 2024. However, the impairments in chemicals have been substantial: the Singapore Energy and Chemicals Park was impaired following a sales agreement, and European chemicals assets face structural margin pressure from energy costs and demand weakness. Retaining these assets consumes capital, management attention, and organisational complexity — all of which are in tension with the "simplification" principle.

**Action:** Shell should accelerate the divestiture of non-core chemicals assets, particularly in Europe, where structural energy cost disadvantages are persistent. Proceeds should be redeployed into the trading capability expansion recommended above (Recommendation 2) and into shareholder distributions, maintaining the financial legitimacy already established. This is consistent with Shell's own stated direction — for its chemical assets, Shell's strategy includes exploring strategic and partnership opportunities in the United States, while some businesses could be shuttered in Europe — but the pace should be accelerated to remove the drag on returns and focus.

---

### Recommendation 5: Institutionalise a Climate Credibility Governance Mechanism
**Analytical basis:** The social legitimacy deficit is partly a governance problem: Shell's climate targets have been repeatedly revised downward, eroding trust with institutional investors, regulators, and civil society. Follow This noted that Shell's retired and watered-down targets will take Shell even further away from Paris alignment than it was before, and that backtracking on essential emissions reduction targets clearly shows what Wael Sawan means by a "ruthless focus." The legal risk is not eliminated: the *Milieudefensie* court confirmed Shell's duty of care, and future litigation will test whether Shell's self-set targets constitute adequate discharge of that duty.

**Action:** Shell should establish an independent **Climate Transition Oversight Board** — comprising independent directors, credible climate scientists, and institutional investor representatives — with a formal mandate to review and publicly certify the consistency of Shell's capital allocation decisions with its net-zero 2050 commitment. This body should have the authority to flag inconsistencies between investment decisions and stated climate ambitions, with public reporting obligations. This mechanism directly addresses the governance gap that enables target dilution and would substantially strengthen Shell's position in future litigation by demonstrating procedural good faith.

## 7. CONCLUSION: THE COHERENCE TEST

The analysis in this case study is built on a chain of connected inferences:

1. Shell faces a **Dual Legitimacy Crisis** — financial and social — that its current strategy addresses asymmetrically (PESTEL, situational analysis).

2. The financial pivot is grounded in genuine VRIN resources — trading capability and LNG position — making it strategically coherent from an RBV perspective. But the retreat from low-carbon investment creates a future resource deficit inconsistent with the 2050 net-zero commitment.

3. Porter's Five Forces confirm that rivalry is high, substitutes are rising, and Shell's competitive advantage in renewables is weak — validating the LNG focus. But they also confirm that the threat of substitutes will intensify, making the LNG timing risk real.

4. The LNG demand thesis is empirically supported in the medium term but faces a credible downside scenario under accelerated transition, creating a capital allocation risk that is not currently scenario-gated.

5. The five recommendations flow directly from these gaps: they restore the social legitimacy deficit (Recommendations 1, 5), leverage the VRIN trading capability more fully (Recommendation 2), address the capital allocation timing risk (Recommendation 3), and remove the structural drag on financial performance (Recommendation 4).

Shell's "More Value with Less Emissions" strategy is not wrong — it is incomplete. The word "less" is doing too much work. A strategy that grows LNG volumes by 4–5% per year while reducing only the *intensity* of emissions is not a transition strategy; it is a growth strategy with transition branding. The recommendations above are designed to close that gap — not by abandoning Shell's financial discipline, which is legitimate and necessary, but by ensuring that the social and regulatory legitimacy on which Shell's long-run licence to operate depends is rebuilt with equal rigour.
