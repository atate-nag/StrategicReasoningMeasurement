# ExxonMobil at the Crossroads: Doubling Down on Hydrocarbons While Building a New Energy Business
## A Strategic Case Study

---

## 1. Introduction and Case Framing

ExxonMobil Corporation is the world's largest publicly traded integrated oil and gas company, yet it stands at one of the most consequential strategic inflection points in its 140-year history. The central strategic tension is this: ExxonMobil delivered $33.7 billion in earnings and $55.0 billion in cash flow from operations in 2024 — its third-best year in a decade, powered almost entirely by hydrocarbons. Yet the long-run legitimacy and viability of that business model is being contested by energy-transition pressures, activist shareholders, and regulatory change. The firm's response — a "dual-track" strategy of deepening its hydrocarbon cost position while selectively building low-carbon businesses — raises a fundamental strategic question: **Is ExxonMobil's current strategy coherent, and does it create a durable competitive advantage, or does it expose the firm to existential stranded-asset risk?**

This case study analyses ExxonMobil's strategic situation through four complementary frameworks — Porter's Five Forces (industry structure), the VRIO/Resource-Based View (internal capabilities), the Ansoff Growth Matrix (strategic choices), and Johnson, Scholes & Whittington's Strategic Position–Choices–Action (SAF) model — before generating a set of integrated recommendations.

## 2. The Strategic Situation

### 2.1 The Macro-Environment: Demand Uncertainty as the Core Threat

The most consequential external variable for ExxonMobil is the trajectory of global oil demand. The firm and its critics hold radically different views. In its long-term outlook published in August 2024, ExxonMobil forecast that oil demand, rather than shrinking, would remain close to 100 million barrels per day through 2050, requiring high levels of investment in exploration and development. ExxonMobil's own Global Outlook reinforces this: there is no credible energy transition scenario that does not include a role for oil and natural gas, and its Global Outlook shows an approximately 25% increase in energy use in developing countries versus 2024, driven by population growth and rising living standards.

However, the International Energy Agency presents a more disruptive picture. Total global oil supply capacity is forecast to rise to nearly 114 million barrels a day by 2030 — a staggering 8 million barrels per day above projected global demand — which would result in levels of spare capacity never seen before. This structural oversupply risk, if realised, would compress margins across the industry and disproportionately harm high-cost producers. The strategic implication is direct: ExxonMobil's entire capital-allocation logic — which prioritises scale and volume in hydrocarbons — is predicated on a demand forecast that is contested by the industry's most authoritative external body. This is not merely an academic disagreement; it is the central risk that makes the firm's strategic choices high-stakes.

### 2.2 The Competitive Context: Acquisitions, Scale, and the Pioneer Deal

ExxonMobil has responded to this uncertain environment with an aggressive consolidation strategy. Pioneer synergies are expected to be $4 billion annually, double initial estimates, and due to technology advancements and improved capital efficiency, the company expects to double production in the Permian Basin by 2030 versus 2024 to approximately 2.5 million oil-equivalent barrels per day.

Simultaneously, the company achieved record production in the Permian and Guyana, and record sales volumes of high-value products in 2024, and relative to 2019, cumulative structural cost savings totalled $12.1 billion, including an additional $2.4 billion in 2024. These are not incidental operational achievements; they represent a deliberate strategic bet that ExxonMobil can be the *last producer standing* in a potentially shrinking market — a cost-leadership logic that only pays off if demand does not collapse faster than costs fall.

### 2.3 The Governance and Legitimacy Challenge

ExxonMobil's strategy faces a parallel challenge from within its own ownership structure. Engine No. 1 challenged ExxonMobil's "all oil" strategy and succeeded in imposing its views through the election of three of its candidates to the Board of Directors at the 2021 Annual General Meeting. This was not merely symbolic: it demonstrated that institutional investors regard the firm's climate strategy as a material financial risk, not simply a reputational concern.

The firm's response to continued activist pressure has been confrontational. ExxonMobil sued Arjuna Capital and Follow This over their shareholder proposal to raise the greenhouse gas emission reduction bar on January 22, 2024, arguing that the content was the same as proposals submitted in 2022 and 2023. While legally defensible, this posture carries a reputational cost and signals strategic defensiveness rather than proactive adaptation. Critics have also highlighted a critical omission in ExxonMobil's climate commitments: "By not making any commitment to reduce the emissions that come from burning oil and gas, known as Scope 3, ExxonMobil is shifting blame for the bulk of its emissions onto consumers who are using its products exactly as the company intended." Since Scope 3 emissions represent the vast majority of a fossil fuel company's total climate impact, this gap between stated ambition and measurable commitment undermines the credibility of the firm's energy-transition narrative.

## 3. Framework Analysis

### 3.1 Porter's Five Forces: Industry Structure

**Threat of New Entrants — Low.** The integrated oil and gas industry has enormous capital requirements, long project lead times, regulatory complexity, and proprietary geological knowledge. These structural barriers are nearly prohibitive for new entrants at scale, which protects ExxonMobil's market position. However, this force does not protect ExxonMobil from *substitution* — a conceptually distinct but related threat.

**Threat of Substitutes — High and Rising.** The existential threat to ExxonMobil is not a new oil company but the electrification of transport and the decarbonisation of industry. The petrochemical industry is now poised to become the dominant source of oil demand growth from 2026 onwards, with the industry on track to consume one in every six barrels of oil by 2030 — suggesting that demand is shifting *within* the oil complex from transport fuels (vulnerable to EV substitution) toward chemicals (more structurally resilient). This nuance matters: ExxonMobil's integrated chemicals business is better positioned than a pure-play fuel producer, but the overall substitution pressure remains elevated.

**Bargaining Power of Suppliers — Moderate to High.** Suppliers offering cutting-edge technologies, crucial for efficiency and cost reduction, gain leverage over ExxonMobil. The company's reliance on advanced solutions for exploration and refining enhances supplier influence, particularly as providers of advanced carbon capture technologies can command higher prices due to the substantial investment required and the lack of readily available alternatives. This is a growing constraint as ExxonMobil expands into CCS and hydrogen, where it is a *buyer* of emerging technology, not a developer.

**Bargaining Power of Buyers — Moderate.** Oil and gas are largely commoditised, which limits pricing power. However, ExxonMobil's downstream integration and chemicals business allow it to capture value across the supply chain, partially offsetting commodity-price exposure.

**Competitive Rivalry — Intense.** The integrated majors — Shell, BP, TotalEnergies, Chevron — all compete for the same assets, talent, and capital. Critically, rivals have made divergent strategic choices: European majors have invested more heavily in renewables, while US majors (ExxonMobil, Chevron) have doubled down on hydrocarbons. A critical analysis by Engine No. 1 specifically noted that while most European oil companies, like TotalEnergies, are investing heavily in renewable energy to reduce their products' carbon footprint, ExxonMobil has not followed this path. This divergence creates a natural experiment: if the energy transition accelerates, European majors' renewable portfolios will prove prescient; if demand remains robust, ExxonMobil's cost-leadership position will be vindicated.

**Five Forces Summary:** The industry structure is fundamentally unfavourable over the long run due to rising substitution threats, but ExxonMobil's scale and integration provide a defensible near-to-medium-term position. The key strategic implication is that competitive advantage must be built on *cost* (to survive demand erosion) and *transition optionality* (to avoid stranded assets). ExxonMobil's current strategy addresses the first but only partially addresses the second.

### 3.2 VRIO Analysis: Internal Resources and Capabilities

The Resource-Based View (Barney, 1991) holds that sustained competitive advantage derives from resources that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Organised (VRIO). ExxonMobil's key resources are assessed below.

**Scale and Integrated Value Chain — Valuable, Rare, Partially Inimitable.**
ExxonMobil's competitive advantages are deeply rooted in its vast scale, technological prowess, integrated operational model, and robust financial health, with extensive global operations across upstream, downstream, and chemical segments. The integration across the value chain is particularly important: "Our focus is on increasing overall value by strengthening our upstream business and further integrating it with the downstream and chemical segments to take advantage of our unique capabilities across the value chain," as ExxonMobil's own leadership has stated. This integration is rare among pure-play producers but is shared with other supermajors, limiting its inimitability as a source of *unique* advantage.

**Structural Cost Reduction Capability — Valuable, Rare, Inimitable.**
Cumulative structural cost savings of $12.1 billion since 2019, including $2.4 billion in 2024, have more than offset inflation and growth costs. This is not merely operational efficiency; it reflects an organisational capability to systematically redesign processes and workflows. This capability, embedded in ExxonMobil's engineering culture, is difficult to replicate quickly and represents a genuine source of sustained advantage — *particularly* if the industry enters a prolonged period of lower oil prices driven by oversupply.

**Permian Basin Resource Base — Valuable, Rare, Inimitable.**
ExxonMobil is leveraging the Pioneer acquisition, cost-cutting innovations, and infrastructure investments to drive long-term high-margin, low-emission production growth from the Permian, with the company expecting significant cash flow contributions reinforcing its shareholder return programs and investment in new technologies. The Permian position is geologically unique — the resource quality, density, and infrastructure cannot be replicated elsewhere — making this a textbook VRIO resource.

**CCS Infrastructure (Denbury) — Valuable, Rare, Potentially Inimitable.**
With the acquisition of Denbury, ExxonMobil gained ownership of the largest CO₂ pipeline network in the US, spanning an impressive 1,300 miles, with nearly 925 miles of CO₂ pipelines strategically located in Louisiana, Texas, and Mississippi. This is a first-mover advantage in physical CCS infrastructure. ExxonMobil is assembling what it describes as the world's first large-scale system for carbon capture and storage — a network of pipelines and storage sites that stretches across Texas, Louisiana and Mississippi. Because pipeline infrastructure benefits from natural monopoly characteristics (high fixed costs, network effects), this asset is difficult to replicate and could become a durable competitive moat in the emerging CCS market.

**Lithium and Advanced Materials — Valuable, Not Yet Proven.**
ExxonMobil plans to tap lithium-rich brines deep underground, using processes similar to those used in its existing production and refining businesses, with direct lithium extraction expected to have as low as approximately two-thirds less carbon intensity than hard rock mining. This leverages existing subsurface competencies into an adjacent market. However, the resource is not yet commercially proven at scale, and the competitive advantage relative to dedicated lithium producers remains unestablished.

**VRIO Summary:** ExxonMobil's most durable advantages are its cost-reduction capability, its Permian resource base, and its nascent CCS infrastructure. These are the three pillars around which a coherent long-term strategy should be organised. The firm's newer bets (lithium, hydrogen, advanced materials) are strategically logical extensions but are not yet proven sources of competitive advantage.

### 3.3 Ansoff Growth Matrix: Strategic Choices Under Uncertainty

The Ansoff Matrix maps growth strategies along two dimensions — existing vs. new markets, and existing vs. new products — to assess risk and strategic logic.

**Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets):** This is ExxonMobil's dominant current strategy. ExxonMobil produced 4.3 million barrels of oil equivalent per day in 2024 — the highest output from the major in over a decade — and plans to double Permian Basin output to 2.3 million bpd by 2030. This strategy is rational *if* ExxonMobil's demand forecast is correct and *if* its cost position allows it to be profitable even at lower price points. The Pioneer acquisition amplifies this bet. However, market penetration in a potentially maturing market concentrates rather than diversifies risk.

**Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets):** Gross production in Guyana increased 58% year-over-year with the addition of the Prosperity FPSO, generating increased profits for all stakeholders. Guyana represents a new geographic market with existing product (oil), and it demonstrates ExxonMobil's ability to develop frontier resources. This is a lower-risk extension of its core competency.

**Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets):** ExxonMobil's CCS business targets its existing industrial customers — refiners, petrochemical manufacturers, power generators — with a new service (carbon storage). CF Industries signed commercial agreements with ExxonMobil to capture and permanently store up to approximately 2.5 million metric tons of CO₂ emissions annually from manufacturing complexes in Louisiana and Mississippi. This is the most strategically coherent of ExxonMobil's new ventures: it leverages existing relationships, existing infrastructure, and existing engineering capabilities to sell a new product to a known customer base.

**Diversification (New Products, New Markets):** ExxonMobil's broader portfolio of new low-carbon businesses, including Proxxima materials, carbon products, lithium, and CCS-enabled data centres, is estimated to carry $13 billion in earnings potential by 2040. These represent genuine diversification — new technologies in new markets. The risk is highest here, but so is the long-run optionality value. The strategic logic is that ExxonMobil's engineering and project management capabilities are transferable across these domains. However, the firm must be careful not to spread capital too thinly across too many speculative ventures simultaneously.

**Ansoff Summary:** ExxonMobil's portfolio spans all four quadrants, but the capital weighting is heavily skewed toward market penetration. The company's capital allocation framework dedicates approximately $20 billion between 2025–2030 toward low-carbon business segments, representing a strategic pivot toward services-based revenue streams alongside traditional production. Yet this $20 billion over five years ($4 billion/year) compares with total capex guidance of approximately $28 billion per year, meaning low-carbon investment represents roughly 14% of total capital — a proportion that critics argue is insufficient for a genuine energy transition strategy.

### 3.4 Johnson, Scholes & Whittington: Strategic Position, Choices, and Action (SAF)

The SAF framework evaluates strategy along three dimensions: Strategic Position (what is the environment and what are our capabilities?), Strategic Choices (what options are available?), and Strategy in Action (how is the strategy being implemented?). It also applies the Suitability–Acceptability–Feasibility (SAF) test to evaluate strategic options.

**Strategic Position:** ExxonMobil occupies a position of short-run financial strength but long-run strategic ambiguity. Its financial position is robust: ExxonMobil delivered industry-leading total shareholder returns of 11%, 25%, and 14% for the last one, three, and five years respectively, and plans to extend its annual $20 billion share-repurchase program. However, its strategic position is contested by the legitimacy challenge described above. The firm's stated purpose — "The company's objective to play a leading role in the energy transition" as stated in its 2024 10-K — is undermined by the absence of binding Scope 3 commitments, creating a credibility gap between stated position and observable action.

**Strategic Choices — SAF Test:**

*Option A: Accelerate Hydrocarbon Production (Current Core Strategy)*
- **Suitability:** Suitable if demand remains robust through 2030+. ExxonMobil's own forecast supports this. However, the IEA's oversupply warning introduces significant suitability risk.
- **Acceptability:** Partially acceptable to shareholders (strong near-term returns) but increasingly unacceptable to a growing cohort of ESG-oriented institutional investors, as evidenced by the Engine No. 1 episode.
- **Feasibility:** Highly feasible — ExxonMobil has the assets, capital, and operational capability. The Pioneer integration is on track.

*Option B: Accelerate Low-Carbon Transition (European Major Model)*
- **Suitability:** Suitable in a fast-transition scenario, but ExxonMobil lacks the renewable energy capabilities of BP or Shell, and the returns from renewables have disappointed European majors.
- **Acceptability:** More acceptable to ESG investors but potentially unacceptable to value-oriented investors who have rewarded ExxonMobil's hydrocarbon focus.
- **Feasibility:** Partially feasible in CCS and hydrogen, where ExxonMobil has genuine technical capabilities. Less feasible in solar/wind, where it has no established position.

*Option C: Hybrid "Advantaged Hydrocarbons + Selective Low-Carbon" (Current Stated Strategy)*
- **Suitability:** Suitable across a range of demand scenarios — the cost-leadership position in hydrocarbons provides resilience in slow-transition scenarios, while CCS and lithium provide optionality in fast-transition scenarios.
- **Acceptability:** Acceptable to most shareholders, but only if the low-carbon investments are credible and the Scope 3 gap is addressed.
- **Feasibility:** Feasible, given ExxonMobil's financial strength and engineering capabilities. The Denbury CCS infrastructure and Pioneer Permian assets are both operational.

**Strategy in Action:** The implementation of Option C is currently uneven. The hydrocarbon side is executing well — record Permian production, Pioneer integration ahead of synergy targets. The low-carbon side is making progress: ExxonMobil prioritised its point-source CCS business, highlighted by the successful launch of its commercial operation with CF Industries in Louisiana, which now captures and stores up to 2 million metric tons of CO₂ per year. However, the firm's confrontational posture toward shareholder activists — ExxonMobil's lawsuit against climate activists has received much scrutiny from company investors, many of whom pushed fellow investors to vote against its CEO and board slate — risks undermining the stakeholder management dimension of its strategy in action.

## 4. Synthesis: The Core Strategic Problem

The analysis across all four frameworks converges on a single core problem: **ExxonMobil's strategy is directionally correct but insufficiently differentiated and credibly communicated.**

- **Porter's Five Forces** shows that the firm's survival in a potentially oversupplied market depends on being the lowest-cost producer — a logic the Pioneer acquisition supports — but that substitution risk is not adequately hedged.
- **VRIO** reveals that ExxonMobil's most durable new asset is its CCS infrastructure, which is valuable, rare, and inimitable — but it is under-capitalised relative to its strategic importance.
- **Ansoff** shows that the firm's capital allocation is overwhelmingly concentrated in market penetration (high risk if demand peaks) with insufficient investment in product development and diversification.
- **SAF** confirms that the hybrid Option C is the most suitable, acceptable, and feasible strategy, but its implementation has a credibility gap — particularly on Scope 3 emissions — that threatens its long-run acceptability to capital markets.

The strategic risk is therefore not that ExxonMobil has chosen the wrong direction, but that it is executing a coherent strategy *incompletely*, leaving itself exposed to both financial stranded-asset risk (if demand peaks sooner than forecast) and reputational/governance risk (if the Scope 3 credibility gap widens).

## 5. Recommendations and Action Plan

The following recommendations are derived directly from the framework analysis and are ordered by urgency and strategic impact.

---

### Recommendation 1: Formalise a Scope 3 Pathway with Conditional Milestones
**Derived from:** SAF acceptability test; VRIO legitimacy gap; Five Forces (buyer/investor power)

**Rationale:** The absence of Scope 3 commitments is ExxonMobil's most significant governance vulnerability. It feeds activist shareholder campaigns, creates regulatory exposure, and undermines the credibility of the firm's energy-transition narrative. The firm's own 10-K acknowledges that its net-zero ambition is "contingent on technology development and supportive prices" — a formulation that is strategically honest but communicatively weak. A conditional Scope 3 pathway — with explicit milestones tied to CCS deployment rates, policy triggers, and technology readiness levels — would address the credibility gap without committing to targets that are currently technically unachievable. This approach converts a liability into a demonstration of strategic rigour.

**Action:** Commission an independent technical panel to establish a Scope 3 reduction pathway by 2026, linked explicitly to CCS throughput volumes and government carbon-pricing developments. Publish this alongside the annual sustainability report.

---

### Recommendation 2: Rebalance Capital Allocation Toward CCS Infrastructure — the Firm's Strongest VRIO Asset
**Derived from:** VRIO (CCS as rare and inimitable); Ansoff (product development quadrant); Five Forces (first-mover advantage in CCS)

**Rationale:** ExxonMobil's CCS infrastructure — built on the Denbury acquisition — is its single most strategically differentiated asset in the energy-transition space. The broader portfolio of new low-carbon businesses, including CCS-enabled data centres, carries $13 billion in earnings potential by 2040. Yet CCS currently receives a disproportionately small share of total capital relative to its strategic importance and first-mover advantage. Unlike renewables (where ExxonMobil has no established position) or lithium (where the competitive advantage is unproven), CCS directly leverages the firm's existing engineering capabilities, customer relationships, and physical infrastructure. The natural monopoly characteristics of CO₂ pipeline networks mean that the window for establishing a dominant position is time-limited.

**Action:** Increase the CCS capital allocation within the $20 billion low-carbon envelope from its current level to at least 40% of that envelope by 2027. Accelerate customer contracting — the current offtake pipeline of 6.7 million metric tons should be scaled to 15 million metric tons by 2028 — to create a defensible revenue base before competitors can replicate the infrastructure.

---

### Recommendation 3: Adopt a Scenario-Contingent Capital Discipline Framework for Upstream
**Derived from:** Ansoff (market penetration risk); Five Forces (oversupply threat); Porter's generic strategies (cost leadership must be paired with price-floor discipline)

**Rationale:** The IEA forecasts total supply capacity rising to nearly 114 million barrels a day by 2030 — 8 million barrels per day above projected demand — which, if realised, would compress oil prices significantly. ExxonMobil's current plan to double Permian production by 2030 is rational under its own demand forecast but potentially value-destructive under the IEA's. A scenario-contingent capital framework — with pre-defined production and investment triggers linked to oil price bands and demand indicators — would allow ExxonMobil to preserve its cost-leadership position while avoiding the trap of overproducing into a structurally oversupplied market. This is not a retreat from hydrocarbons; it is disciplined capital stewardship.

**Action:** Establish a formal scenario-planning governance process at Board level, with three defined scenarios (slow, moderate, fast transition) and pre-agreed capex adjustment protocols. Link executive compensation to returns on capital across scenarios, not just absolute production volumes.

---

### Recommendation 4: Reframe Stakeholder Engagement — From Litigation to Leadership
**Derived from:** SAF (acceptability dimension); Five Forces (investor/buyer power); governance analysis

**Rationale:** ExxonMobil's decision to sue activist shareholders, while legally defensible, is strategically counterproductive. The lawsuit received much scrutiny from company investors, many of whom pushed fellow investors to vote against its CEO and board slate. Litigation converts a governance disagreement into a public adversarial contest, amplifying reputational damage and entrenching opposition. The more effective strategic posture is to pre-empt shareholder proposals by demonstrating leadership — through the conditional Scope 3 pathway (Recommendation 1), through transparent scenario-linked capital discipline (Recommendation 3), and through proactive engagement with institutional investors on the CCS commercial pipeline.

**Action:** Establish a formal Investor Climate Dialogue Forum — a structured, twice-yearly engagement mechanism with the firm's top 20 institutional shareholders — to share progress on low-carbon investments, discuss scenario assumptions, and receive feedback before annual meeting season. This converts reactive litigation into proactive governance leadership.

---

### Recommendation 5: Prioritise Lithium as the Next VRIO-Building Investment, Contingent on Proof-of-Concept
**Derived from:** VRIO (transferable subsurface capabilities); Ansoff (diversification with capability leverage)

**Rationale:** By directly extracting lithium and refining it onsite, ExxonMobil eliminates much of the process currently dominated by China, building resilience in the US supply chain — a strategic framing that aligns with US industrial policy and creates a potential government-partnership moat. Direct lithium extraction is expected to have as low as approximately two-thirds less carbon intensity than hard rock mining, making it commercially attractive under carbon-pricing regimes. Critically, this venture leverages ExxonMobil's existing subsurface and brine-processing capabilities — a genuine VRIO extension rather than an unrelated diversification. However, the commercial scale has not yet been proven.

**Action:** Set a clear proof-of-concept milestone for the lithium venture by 2026 — defined by production cost per tonne relative to market price and carbon intensity versus hard rock mining. If the milestone is met, commit $3–5 billion in scaled capital; if not, divest or partner with a dedicated lithium producer to avoid capital dilution.

## 6. Conclusion

ExxonMobil's strategic situation is one of managed tension: between a hydrocarbon business that is currently generating exceptional returns and a transition environment that makes the long-run sustainability of that business uncertain. The analytical frameworks applied here — Porter's Five Forces, VRIO, Ansoff, and the SAF model — converge on the same conclusion: the firm's hybrid strategy (advantaged hydrocarbons plus selective low-carbon) is the most suitable, feasible, and — potentially — acceptable option available. But its implementation is incomplete. The Scope 3 credibility gap, the under-capitalisation of CCS relative to its VRIO strength, the concentration of capital in market penetration, and the adversarial governance posture all represent correctable execution failures rather than fundamental strategic errors.

The chain of reasoning is as follows: because the industry faces genuine demand uncertainty (Five Forces), ExxonMobil must be the lowest-cost producer to survive in a slow-demand scenario *and* must build transition assets to survive in a fast-demand scenario; because its strongest transition asset is CCS infrastructure (VRIO), capital should be reweighted toward it; because its capital allocation is currently skewed toward market penetration (Ansoff), a scenario-contingent discipline framework is needed to prevent overproduction into a potentially oversupplied market; and because its governance posture undermines the acceptability of its strategy (SAF), stakeholder engagement must shift from litigation to leadership. Together, these five recommendations do not ask ExxonMobil to abandon its identity as an energy company — they ask it to execute its stated dual-track strategy with the rigour and credibility that its financial strength makes entirely achievable.
