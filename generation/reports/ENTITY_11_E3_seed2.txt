# CASE STUDY: De Beers Group — Strategic Crisis and Renewal in the Global Diamond Industry (2022–2025)
### Anglo American Subsidiary | Strategic Management Analysis

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

De Beers Group, the world's largest diamond producer by value and an 85%-owned subsidiary of Anglo American plc, entered a period of acute strategic crisis between 2022 and 2025. A convergence of three structural forces — the commoditisation of lab-grown diamonds (LGDs), the collapse of Chinese luxury demand, and a persistent midstream inventory overhang — drove severe financial deterioration. This case study analyses De Beers' strategic situation using **Porter's Five Forces**, the **VRIO framework**, the **Ansoff Matrix**, and **dynamic capabilities theory**, and concludes with a prioritised, warranted set of strategic recommendations.

---

## 1. BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### 1.1 De Beers and Anglo American

De Beers Group is a vertically integrated diamond company spanning mining, sorting, trading, cutting, polishing, and retail. Anglo American plc holds an 85% stake; the Government of Botswana holds the remaining 15%. De Beers operates mines in Botswana (through the 50/50 joint venture Debswana), Namibia, South Africa, and Canada, and distributes rough diamonds through its proprietary "sightholder" system — a network of approved buyers who purchase at fixed prices in periodic "sights."

"A Diamond is Forever," launched in 1947 and coined by copywriter Frances Gerety, strategically positioned diamonds as the universal symbol of eternal love and commitment, transforming them from a semi-precious stone into a cultural necessity. At the time, De Beers controlled around 90% of the world's diamond supply through its mining operations and trading companies. This near-monopoly over both supply and cultural meaning was the foundation of De Beers' historical competitive advantage.

### 1.2 The Strategic Situation (2022–2025)

By 2024, De Beers' financial position had deteriorated sharply. Difficult trading conditions resulted in a year-on-year reduction in revenue of 23%, with total revenue of $3.3 billion (2023: $4.3 billion), primarily due to a 25% reduction in rough diamond sales to $2.7 billion (2023: $3.6 billion). Total rough diamond sales volumes decreased by 28% to 17.9 million carats. De Beers' underlying loss widened to $739 million from $288 million the previous year. Anglo American's group net loss widened 22% to $3.74 billion, mainly because of the De Beers impairment. This is the second year in a row Anglo has taken an impairment on De Beers; in 2024, it wrote down the company's value by $1.6 billion, to $7.6 billion — and then by a further $2.9 billion in 2025, bringing the cumulative write-down to approximately $4.5 billion.

In May 2024, Anglo American announced plans to separate from De Beers, either through a sale or demerger. The decision followed a takeover bid from BHP Billiton and formed part of a wider restructuring designed to streamline Anglo's portfolio. CEO Duncan Wanblad described these as "the most radical changes to Anglo American in decades," reflecting a fundamental portfolio rebalancing toward copper, premium iron ore, and crop nutrients.

This case study therefore analyses a subsidiary facing a triple strategic challenge: an existential competitive threat (LGD commoditisation), a demand-side structural shock (Chinese consumption collapse), and a corporate ownership transition that creates both urgency and uncertainty.

## 2. INDUSTRY ANALYSIS: PORTER'S FIVE FORCES

Porter's Five Forces framework (Porter, 1980) provides a systematic basis for evaluating the structural attractiveness of the diamond industry and the forces shaping De Beers' competitive position.

### Force 1: Threat of New Entrants — MODERATE (declining from HIGH historically)

Historically, De Beers' control of the supply chain created near-impenetrable barriers: capital-intensive mining, incumbency advantages, and the ability to stockpile and flood the market if a competitor threatened its position. These barriers have partially eroded, but entry into *natural* diamond mining remains capital-intensive and geographically constrained. The more consequential "new entrant" dynamic operates in the substitute space: LGD producers have entered the gem-quality diamond market at scale with dramatically lower capital requirements than underground mining.

**Analytical link:** The continued high barriers to entry in natural diamond mining provide De Beers with upstream protection. This justifies a strategy of doubling down on mining-based competitive advantages — explored in the VRIO section — even as the downstream market is disrupted.

### Force 2: Bargaining Power of Suppliers — LOW (with emerging political risk)

De Beers' principal "suppliers" are its own geological assets. The Botswana government signed a long-delayed diamond mining and sales agreement with De Beers. Under the terms, Botswana's share of the diamonds produced by Debswana will increase from 25% to 50%, and Botswana will receive 10 billion pula ($712 million) in development funding. The agreement also extends the mining licences for Debswana until 2054. Supplier power is therefore structurally low, but the Botswana government's growing assertiveness — including its stated desire for a controlling stake in any post-Anglo ownership structure — represents an emerging quasi-supplier power that must be managed diplomatically.

**Analytical link:** Low supplier power preserves De Beers' upstream margins, but Botswana's political leverage over the Debswana licences constitutes a non-commercial constraint on strategic freedom. Any acquirer must price this into the valuation, and De Beers' management must incorporate it into stakeholder strategy.

### Force 3: Threat of Substitutes — HIGH (and intensifying)

This is the most critical force reshaping the diamond industry. McKinsey & Co. described LGDs as "likely the biggest challenge facing diamond producers today," noting that adoption among millennials and Gen Z has run "well beyond what the mining industry had expected." The lab-grown diamond sector has crossed a historic threshold, with lab-grown stones now comprising more than half of all engagement rings purchased — the first time this milestone has been achieved. This represents a 40% surge since 2019.

The price dynamic is particularly damaging. LGDs sold at a 20% discount compared with natural counterparts in 2018, whereas prices today are at an 80% discount. This price collapse commoditised LGDs and simultaneously undermined natural diamond pricing: market data show that natural diamond prices are roughly 25–30% lower than in early 2022, while lab-grown stones have plunged about 70–80% in value since 2020.

De Beers announced the closure of Lightbox, its lab-grown diamond jewellery brand, in May 2025, marking a retreat from synthetic gems sold to consumers. Since 2018, De Beers had sold jewellery with diamonds made in a lab. Lightbox was not a lab-grown business; it was a defensive weapon — one that ultimately failed to arrest the LGD threat and inadvertently legitimised the category it was designed to contain.

**Analytical link:** The high and intensifying substitute threat is the primary driver of De Beers' revenue collapse and the core strategic problem to be solved. Any credible strategy must either differentiate natural diamonds from LGDs in the consumer's mind (a demand-side solution) or find new markets where LGD penetration is lower (a geographic solution) — or both. This directly motivates the "Origins" strategy and the India pivot analysed below.

### Force 4: Bargaining Power of Buyers — MODERATE to HIGH

De Beers sells rough diamonds to a relatively small, approved group of sightholders — historically giving De Beers significant price-setting power. However, the proliferation of LGD alternatives has increased sightholders' outside options. De Beers is cutting its sightholder list down to approximately 70–80 firms to improve supply chain efficiency, but a more concentrated network of buyers also means less competition at the top, which rarely results in lower retail prices. At the retail end, the massive success of lab-grown diamonds has reduced prices for natural stones well beyond what the mining industry had expected, driven largely by consumers who want more affordable options.

**Analytical link:** Rising buyer power at both the wholesale and retail levels constrains De Beers' ability to maintain rough diamond price premiums, reinforcing the need for a differentiation strategy based on provenance and emotional value rather than price competition.

### Force 5: Competitive Rivalry — MODERATE within natural diamonds

The most intense "rivalry" is now asymmetric: it is between the natural diamond *category* and the LGD category, rather than between natural diamond miners. Within the natural diamond segment, De Beers retains a dominant market position by value.

**Analytical link:** Because rivalry within natural diamonds is moderate, De Beers' strategic priority should be *category-level* competition — defending and growing the natural diamond category — rather than competing primarily against other natural diamond miners. This is the logic behind De Beers' investment in generic demand creation under the "Origins" strategy.

### Five Forces Summary

The structural analysis reveals that the threat of substitutes has fundamentally weakened the diamond industry's historical attractiveness. The combination of high substitute threat, rising buyer power, and demand-side structural shifts produces a compelling case for strategic transformation rather than incremental adjustment.

## 3. INTERNAL ANALYSIS: THE VRIO FRAMEWORK

The VRIO framework (Barney, 1991) evaluates whether a firm's resources and capabilities are **V**aluable, **R**are, costly to **I**mitate, and supported by the **O**rganisation — and thus whether they constitute sources of sustained competitive advantage.

### Resource 1: Mining Asset Portfolio — **Sustained Competitive Advantage**

De Beers has a highly competitive portfolio of mining assets that are among the best on the cost curve and have substantial production capability and reserves. With global natural diamond production forecast to decline over the coming decade, these assets become relatively more valuable.

- **Valuable:** Yes. High-grade ore bodies generate diamonds at lower unit cost than competitors.
- **Rare:** Yes. World-class kimberlite pipes are geologically scarce and non-replicable.
- **Inimitable:** Yes. Competitors cannot reproduce the geological endowment.
- **Organised:** Partially. De Beers production was lower in 2024, reflecting a proactive response to a prolonged period of lower demand and higher-than-normal levels of inventory in the midstream. However, De Beers is sitting on a $2 billion pile of diamonds as demand slumps, indicating imperfect demand-supply coordination.

**Analytical link:** The mining asset portfolio is the most defensible resource De Beers possesses. Its sustained competitive advantage in upstream production means that any viable long-term strategy must be anchored in these assets — supporting the "Origins" strategy's emphasis on "focusing upstream investments on the major projects that will deliver the highest returns."

### Resource 2: Brand Equity and Marketing Heritage — **Sustained Competitive Advantage (under threat)**

De Beers' iconic slogan "A Diamond is Forever" is one of the most successful and enduring marketing campaigns in history. Launched in 1948, the campaign strategically positioned diamonds as a symbol of everlasting love and commitment through massive advertising efforts over decades. De Beers' retail brands — De Beers Jewellers and Forevermark — carry significant premium positioning. De Beers Group has registered nearly three million diamonds on its Tracr blockchain platform since 2022. The system provides country-of-origin details and is expanding to include additional producers.

- **Valuable:** Yes. Brand equity enables premium pricing and consumer trust.
- **Rare:** Yes. No LGD producer possesses a comparable heritage brand.
- **Inimitable:** Yes. Decades of cultural embedding cannot be replicated quickly.
- **Organised:** Partially. The Lightbox closure and the refocusing of Forevermark on India suggest that brand management has been inconsistent, diluting the brand by simultaneously selling LGDs under the De Beers umbrella.

**Analytical link:** Brand equity is the key differentiator that separates natural diamonds from LGDs in the consumer's mind. Because LGDs are physically identical to natural diamonds, the only sustainable basis for a price premium is emotional and provenance-based differentiation — precisely what the De Beers brand and the Tracr platform are positioned to deliver. The strategic failure of Lightbox confirms this logic: by selling LGDs at low prices, De Beers implicitly signalled that diamonds could be commodities, undermining its own brand architecture.

### Resource 3: Sightholder Distribution System — **Temporary Competitive Advantage**

The sightholder system historically gave De Beers unparalleled control over the distribution channel and price stabilisation. De Beers tweaked its distribution in 2022 to provide more bespoke supply by classifying sightholders according to their business type: manufacturer, dealer, or retailer. The move was seen as an attempt to reduce the flipping of boxes on the secondary market and to bring more efficiency to the supply chain.

- **Valuable:** Yes. It provides pricing discipline and reduces market volatility.
- **Rare:** Moderately. No other diamond miner operates an equivalent system at scale.
- **Inimitable:** Moderately. The system depends on De Beers' market dominance, which has declined.
- **Organised:** Yes, following the 2022 restructuring.

**Analytical link:** The sightholder system's value depends on De Beers maintaining sufficient market share to make access to it commercially attractive. As LGD alternatives proliferate, sightholders' willingness to pay a premium for De Beers' rough diamonds depends entirely on whether natural diamonds retain a consumer premium. This creates a strategic interdependency: brand strategy and distribution strategy must be mutually reinforcing.

### Resource 4: Technology Capabilities (Tracr + Element Six) — **Emerging Sustained Competitive Advantage**

Element Six, a De Beers Group company, is spearheading technological advancements by leveraging synthetic diamonds to transform industries such as quantum technologies, semiconductors, and water purification. The company has entered strategic partnerships including a collaboration with Japanese manufacturer Orbray to develop the world's highest-quality wafer-scale single-crystal diamond. De Beers is also investing in Lightsynq, a quantum interconnect startup, to accelerate the development of faster and more robust quantum computing solutions.

- **Valuable:** Yes. Industrial synthetic diamonds are high-value, high-growth applications.
- **Rare:** Yes. Few competitors combine mining heritage with advanced synthetic diamond technology.
- **Inimitable:** Yes. The combination of IP, manufacturing know-how, and blockchain infrastructure is difficult to replicate.
- **Organised:** Yes, following the Lightbox closure, which refocused Element Six exclusively on industrial applications.

**Analytical link:** The pivot of Element Six away from consumer LGDs and toward industrial synthetic diamond technology is strategically coherent: it exploits a genuinely rare and inimitable capability in a growing market while removing the brand contradiction of competing with natural diamonds. This constitutes a **dynamic capability** — the ability to reconfigure assets in response to environmental change (Teece, Pisano & Shuen, 1997).

## 4. GROWTH STRATEGY ANALYSIS: THE ANSOFF MATRIX

The Ansoff Matrix (Ansoff, 1957) classifies growth strategies along two dimensions — existing vs. new products, and existing vs. new markets — producing four quadrants. De Beers' Origins strategy, unveiled in May 2024, aims to enhance value across the business from mining through to retail. The business will be streamlined and investments will be focused on high return projects in core upstream assets, midstream technologies, natural diamond retail, and synthetic diamond technology solutions. This strategy can be mapped onto the Ansoff Matrix to assess its internal coherence.

### Quadrant 1: Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets)

De Beers is pursuing market penetration in the United States through renewed generic marketing investment. McKinsey found that LGD adoption among millennials and Gen Z has run "well beyond what the mining industry had expected," with LGDs now comprising over 50% of US engagement ring purchases. Market penetration in the US is therefore a defensive necessity, not an offensive growth driver.

**Warrant:** Market penetration is appropriate where brand equity remains strong and where the competitive threat is primarily from substitutes. De Beers' brand heritage gives it the best platform in the industry for this strategy, but it requires sustained marketing investment that the current loss-making position makes difficult to fund — making it a necessary but insufficient strategy on its own.

### Quadrant 2: Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets) — **Highest Priority**

India has emerged as the world's second-largest market for natural diamonds. De Beers CEO Al Cook and Forevermark CEO Shweta Harit explained why the country now anchors the group's growth strategy, driven by demand, retail expansion and consumer confidence. "We plan to strengthen our retail by expanding Forevermark to 15 stores in 2025, and eventually growing to $100 million in revenue from 100 stores by 2030," said Amit Pratihari, Managing Director at De Beers India.

**Warrant:** Market development is strategically attractive in India for three reasons. First, LGD penetration in India is lower than in the US, giving natural diamonds a larger addressable premium market. Second, India's growing middle class and cultural attachment to precious stones provide a receptive consumer base. Third, India is already the world's leading diamond cutting and polishing hub, giving De Beers existing trade relationships that can be leveraged for retail expansion. The Ansoff framework classifies this as moderate risk (existing product, new geography), which is appropriate given De Beers' current financial constraints.

### Quadrant 3: Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets)

De Beers unveiled compelling new downstream propositions focused on driving desire for natural diamonds as a category and on differentiating polished diamonds sourced from De Beers Group, including its first "beacon" product at the JCK Las Vegas Show. De Beers Group is investing significantly in media and digital campaigns to drive consumer demand at the point of sale. The "ORIGIN – De Beers Group" polished diamond range carries verified provenance credentials from the Tracr platform. De Beers' Origins strategy also includes resetting the downstream by reinvigorating category marketing and evolving proprietary brands through development of De Beers Jewellers into a leading high jewellery maison business and refocusing Forevermark on the fast-growing Indian market.

**Warrant:** Product development through provenance-backed propositions is strategically coherent because it exploits the one attribute that natural diamonds possess and LGDs structurally cannot: a genuine, verifiable origin story. Tracr has begun providing country of origin information for all De Beers Group-sourced rough diamonds over one carat registered on the platform, and is undertaking both rough-to-rough and rough-to-polished objective verification. This transforms provenance from a marketing claim into a verifiable data asset — making it inimitable and directly responsive to consumer ESG concerns.

### Quadrant 4: Diversification (New Products, New Markets)

Element Six's pivot to industrial synthetic diamond technology represents a related diversification. Element Six CEO Siobhán Duffy said: "Synthetic diamond has the potential to shape our future — from the water we drink to the internet connections our societies rely on." The Oregon facility, once tied to Lightbox, will now produce synthetic diamonds exclusively for industrial uses such as quantum computing and semiconductors, helping to detach the industrial tech narrative from jewelry market volatility.

**Warrant:** Related diversification into industrial synthetic diamonds is justified by the VRIO analysis: Element Six possesses rare and inimitable capabilities in synthetic diamond production that are more valuable in industrial applications than in consumer jewellery. The closure of Lightbox and the reorientation of Element Six constitutes a dynamic capability deployment — reconfiguring assets from a declining market (consumer LGDs) to a growing one (industrial technology).

## 5. DYNAMIC CAPABILITIES ANALYSIS

Teece, Pisano & Shuen (1997) define dynamic capabilities as a firm's ability to integrate, build, and reconfigure internal and external competences in response to rapidly changing environments.

### 5.1 Sensing: Identifying Structural Shifts

De Beers demonstrated mixed sensing capability. It correctly identified the LGD threat early enough to launch Lightbox in 2018, but misread the speed and depth of LGD price commoditisation. De Beers built a lab-grown brand to prove lab-grown diamonds were cheap. Seven years later it closed the brand and proved the opposite. The evidence that LGDs were becoming commodity substitutes was available well before the 2025 closure decision.

**Analytical link:** The sensing failure is attributable in part to the conflict of interest embedded in the Lightbox strategy — De Beers was simultaneously trying to defend natural diamonds and profit from LGDs, creating an incentive to underestimate the substitution threat. This is a structural lesson: clarity of strategic intent reduces cognitive bias in environmental sensing.

### 5.2 Seizing: Committing Resources to New Opportunities

The "Origins" strategy and the India market development represent seizing actions. The agreement extends the Debswana mining licences to 2054 — a particularly significant seizing action that secures the upstream asset base for the long term. The refocusing of Element Six on industrial applications is a further seizing action that commits resources to the highest-return use of the synthetic diamond capability.

### 5.3 Reconfiguring: Transforming the Asset Base

Anglo American announced plans to separate from De Beers, either through a sale or demerger, as part of a wider restructuring designed to streamline Anglo's portfolio. For De Beers as an entity, the pending change of ownership is both a threat (uncertainty, management distraction) and an opportunity (a new owner with a longer-term horizon and potentially deeper pockets for marketing investment).

## 6. STAKEHOLDER ANALYSIS

Leaders from the governments of Botswana and Angola are expressing their interest in the future of De Beers Group as Anglo American looks to offload its majority stake. Botswana's president said his country wants a controlling share, while Angola envisions multiple countries holding minority stakes. Botswana seeks not only ownership, but also full control over the company's production, marketing, and value chain — a strategic shift reflecting both economic ambitions and frustration over Anglo American's management.

This stakeholder dynamic is not merely a financial negotiation: it is a strategic constraint. The Debswana joint venture is the foundation of De Beers' upstream production, and any sale structure that alienates the Botswana government risks jeopardising the licences that underpin the entire business. Anglo American aims to sell off its 85% stake in De Beers by the end of 2025 as it restructures and focuses on copper and other minerals that are more profitable than diamonds.

## 7. STRATEGIC RECOMMENDATIONS

### Recommendation 1: Execute a Provenance-Premium Strategy as the Core Competitive Positioning

**What:** Invest significantly in the Tracr blockchain platform and in marketing that communicates the verified provenance, ethical sourcing, and geological rarity of natural diamonds. Expand Tracr coverage to all diamonds over 0.5 carats and develop a consumer-facing digital "passport" for each stone.

**Why (warrant):** The VRIO analysis establishes that brand equity and provenance technology are De Beers' most defensible resources — valuable, rare, and inimitable. The Five Forces analysis (Force 3) confirms that the only sustainable basis for a price premium over LGDs is emotional and provenance-based differentiation, since physical properties are identical. Tracr has begun providing country of origin information for all De Beers Group-sourced rough diamonds over one carat registered on the platform. Expanding this to smaller stones and to the consumer level converts a B2B traceability tool into a B2C differentiation asset. This recommendation directly addresses the substitute threat identified in Force 3 and the buyer power identified in Force 4.

**Risk:** Provenance marketing is effective only if consumers value origin. Younger buyers cite environmental and ethical reasons in surveys, though industry analysts dispute how much weight those reasons actually carry. Pandora's CEO has stated publicly that price and design drive the purchase, with sustainability acting as a supporting factor rather than a primary one. Provenance strategy must therefore be combined with emotional storytelling — building on the "A Diamond is Forever" heritage — rather than relying on provenance alone.

---

### Recommendation 2: Accelerate the India Market Development Strategy

**What:** Accelerate the Forevermark expansion to 100 stores in India by 2028 (rather than 2030), supported by targeted digital marketing and partnerships with Indian jewellery retailers.

**Why (warrant):** The Ansoff Matrix analysis identifies India as the highest-return market development opportunity because LGD penetration is lower than in the US, the consumer base is growing rapidly, and De Beers already has trade relationships through India's cutting and polishing industry. India was chosen as the brand's incubation market due to strong legacy awareness, favourable economic tailwinds and deep consumer insight built over years of operating through shop-in-shop formats. "We plan to strengthen our retail by expanding Forevermark to 15 stores in 2025, and eventually growing to $100 million in revenue from 100 stores by 2030." Accelerating the timeline is warranted by the urgency of the financial situation: De Beers needs growth markets to offset US and China headwinds.

**Risk:** Retail expansion requires upfront capital investment that De Beers' current loss-making position makes challenging. The recommendation should be sequenced with the ownership transition: a new, well-capitalised owner would be better placed to fund the expansion than Anglo American, which is under its own capital constraints.

### Recommendation 3: Complete the Element Six Pivot to Industrial Synthetic Diamond Technology

**What:** Fully exit consumer LGD production (already underway with the Lightbox closure) and invest the freed capital into Element Six's industrial applications — particularly quantum computing and semiconductor-grade synthetic diamond.

**Why (warrant):** The VRIO analysis identifies Element Six's industrial synthetic diamond capabilities as an emerging sustained competitive advantage — rare, inimitable, and increasingly valuable as quantum computing and semiconductor applications scale. The Oregon facility, once tied to Lightbox, will now produce synthetic diamonds exclusively for industrial uses such as quantum computing and semiconductors. The closure of Lightbox removes the brand contradiction identified in the VRIO analysis (selling LGDs undermined the natural diamond brand) and refocuses Element Six on a market where De Beers' synthetic diamond expertise is not commoditised. The warrant is that the same physical asset (synthetic diamond production capability) generates higher and more defensible returns in industrial applications than in consumer jewellery.

**Risk:** Industrial synthetic diamond markets are early-stage and require patient capital. Revenue from Element Six will not compensate for De Beers' diamond revenue losses in the near term. This is a long-term strategic bet, not a short-term fix.

### Recommendation 4: Manage the Botswana Stakeholder Relationship as a Strategic Priority

**What:** Engage proactively with the Botswana government in the De Beers sale process, exploring a structure in which Botswana acquires a meaningful (potentially majority) stake, ideally in partnership with a strategic investor who brings marketing and capital capabilities.

**Why (warrant):** The stakeholder analysis establishes that Botswana's political leverage over Debswana's operating licences is a binding constraint on any sale process. The mining asset portfolio — identified in the VRIO analysis as De Beers' most defensible resource — is geographically concentrated in Botswana. Botswana seeks full control over the company's production, marketing, and value chain. A sale structure that alienates the Botswana government risks jeopardising the very asset that anchors De Beers' long-term value. The Five Forces analysis shows that supplier power (in the form of Botswana's control over mining licences) is the one force that could rapidly shift from low to high if the relationship deteriorates. Proactive engagement is therefore not a concession but a risk mitigation measure that protects the upstream foundation of the entire strategy.

**Risk:** Botswana's desire for a controlling stake may deter commercial bidders who require majority control to justify the investment. The recommendation requires creative deal structuring — for example, a governance arrangement that gives Botswana effective operational voice without requiring full majority ownership.

---

### Recommendation 5: Implement Disciplined Production Management

**What:** Maintain production restraint — aligned with De Beers' 2024 practice of proactive production cuts — until midstream inventory levels normalise, then resume production in line with a demand-led rather than supply-push model.

**Why (warrant):** The Five Forces analysis shows that De Beers retains significant control over natural diamond supply (low supplier power, moderate competitive rivalry within natural diamonds). De Beers production was lower, reflecting a proactive response to a prolonged period of lower demand and higher-than-normal levels of inventory in the midstream. The VRIO analysis confirms that the mining asset portfolio's value depends on price realisation, not volume. Disciplined production restraint protects price levels, which is particularly important during the period when the provenance-premium strategy (Recommendation 1) is building consumer demand. The Origins strategy focuses upstream investments on the major projects that will deliver the highest returns — consistent with a quality-over-volume approach.

**Risk:** Production restraint reduces short-term revenue, exacerbating the current loss position. It requires strong governance discipline to maintain in the face of pressure from shareholders and the Botswana government, which benefits from production volumes through its 50% Debswana share.

## 9. CONCLUSION

De Beers faces the most structurally challenging environment in its century-long history. The commoditisation of lab-grown diamonds has undermined the physical uniqueness that historically justified the natural diamond premium; the collapse of Chinese luxury demand has removed a key growth engine; and the Anglo American divestiture process has created ownership uncertainty at precisely the moment when strategic investment is most needed.

The analytical frameworks deployed in this case study converge on a coherent strategic logic: De Beers' path to recovery runs through its most defensible and inimitable assets — its geological endowment, its brand heritage, its provenance technology, and its synthetic diamond industrial capabilities — deployed in the markets where natural diamonds retain the strongest cultural resonance and where competitive disruption is least advanced. India is the clearest near-term market opportunity. Element Six is the clearest long-term technology opportunity. The Tracr platform is the clearest product differentiation opportunity. And the Botswana relationship is the clearest governance priority.

The Origins strategy, unveiled in May 2024, aims to enhance value across the business from mining through to retail, with investments focused on high return projects in core upstream assets, midstream technologies, natural diamond retail, and synthetic diamond technology solutions. This strategy is directionally correct. The principal risk is that the current loss-making position and the divestiture process will constrain the capital investment needed to execute it at the required speed and scale. A successful ownership transition — one that brings in a well-capitalised owner aligned with Botswana's interests — is therefore not merely a corporate finance event but a **strategic prerequisite** for De Beers' long-term survival.
