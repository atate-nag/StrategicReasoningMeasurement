# DIAGEO PLC: NAVIGATING THE PREMIUMISATION PARADOX
### A Strategic Case Study

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Diageo, the world's largest premium spirits company, finds itself at a critical strategic inflection point. Between FY2023 and FY2025 it suffered a sharp deterioration in financial performance — declining organic sales, an operational crisis in Latin America, leadership instability, and mounting macroeconomic headwinds including US tariffs and a structural moderation in alcohol consumption. Yet within the same portfolio, Don Julio tequila surged, Guinness delivered eight consecutive half-years of double-digit growth, and emerging markets showed genuine premiumisation momentum. This case study argues that Diageo's core strategic tension is a **premiumisation paradox**: its long-run competitive strategy depends on consumers trading up, yet cyclical and structural forces are simultaneously pushing consumers down. Resolving this paradox — not merely waiting for macro conditions to improve — is the central strategic task facing the incoming permanent CEO.

---

## PART I: CASE BACKGROUND AND SITUATION ANALYSIS

### 1.1 Company Overview

Diageo was formed in 1997 through the merger of Grand Metropolitan and Guinness and today operates in nearly 180 countries, stewarding a portfolio that spans Johnnie Walker, Guinness, Don Julio, Tanqueray, Smirnoff, Captain Morgan, Baileys, Crown Royal, Ketel One, and Casamigos. Premiumization and route-to-market scale anchor its position in the $1.1–1.2 trillion global beverage alcohol market. The company's stated ambition has been to increase its share of Total Beverage Alcohol (TBA) to 6% by 2030.

### 1.2 The Strategic Situation: Three Converging Crises

**Crisis 1 — Financial Deterioration.** In fiscal 2024, organic net sales were down 0.6%, with positive price/mix performance mostly mitigating a decline in volume. Organic operating profit declined 4.8%, as a result of the organic net sales decline, primarily due to LAC and North America. Diageo reported a 1.4% year-over-year decline in net sales, with pre-exceptional earnings falling 8.6%. FY2025 saw a partial recovery: organic net sales growth of 1.7% was driven by organic volume growth of 0.9% and positive price/mix of 0.8%. However, reported net sales of $20.2 billion declined 0.1% due to unfavourable foreign exchange of (0.6)% and acquisition and disposal adjustments of (1.1)%.

**Crisis 2 — Leadership Instability.** Diageo announced that Debra Crew stepped down as Chief Executive Officer and as a Board Director with immediate effect, by mutual agreement. The Board began a comprehensive formal search process, which will include consideration of internal and external candidates. Until a permanent appointment is made, Nik Jhangiani, Chief Financial Officer, assumed the role of Chief Executive Officer on an interim basis.

**Crisis 3 — Structural Headwinds.** Beyond the cyclical downturn, Diageo faces structural challenges that cannot be resolved by waiting for macro conditions to normalise: a sober-curious moderation movement, the emergence of GLP-1 weight-loss drugs that reduce alcohol consumption, and US tariffs creating significant cost pressure on imported products.

## PART II: EXTERNAL ENVIRONMENT ANALYSIS

### 2.1 PESTEL Analysis

**Political.** The imposition of US tariffs represents the most immediate political risk. Diageo's guidance for the expected impact of tariffs into the US from UK and European imports remains at approximately $200 million pre-mitigation on an annualised basis, assuming current tariffs remain at 10% on imports from the UK and 15% on imports from Europe, and that Mexican and Canadian spirits imports remain exempt under USMCA. Given the actions to date and before any pricing, Diageo expects to be able to mitigate around half of this impact on operating profit on an ongoing basis. The situation remains highly uncertain: CFO Nik Jhangiani noted that "the situation is still extremely fluid, particularly around the implementation and timing of any retaliatory tariffs and the impact."

**Economic.** The global cost-of-living crisis has compressed consumer discretionary spending and slowed premiumisation. Consumers are now having to reassess spending habits given the prolonged strain on disposable incomes, and rates of premiumisation are slowing. The need to economise is forcing some consumers to cut back on spirits purchases or even consider trading down for certain occasions, either through products or channels. This is directly evidenced in Diageo's own results: the LAC region experienced weakened demand for international premium spirits, the need to adjust inventory levels, and the challenge of lapping strong double-digit growth from the previous year.

**Social.** Two intersecting social trends pose structural (not merely cyclical) risk. First, the sober-curious movement: only half of 18- to 34-year-olds drank alcohol in 2025, down from nearly 60% two years earlier. Second, GLP-1 weight-loss drugs are reshaping consumption patterns: 44% of GLP-1 users drink less after starting treatment and 82% keep those habits even after stopping treatment, with younger, affluent consumers driving this evolution. These are precisely Diageo's target demographic for premiumisation. Low/no alcohol is booming, projected to grow at 13.6% CAGR (2024–29) in the US, compared to just 2.1% for traditional alcohol.

**Technological.** Diageo has invested in digital capabilities and sell-out analytics. Data systems: sell-out analytics and advertising algorithms improve ROI and reduce excess inventory risk in the distribution network. Diageo is fully utilising AI to organise data for better analytics, to help deliver efficiencies in a sustainable manner.

**Environmental.** Climate-driven yield variability affected agave and barley prices in 2024–2025; tequila production costs rose materially amid significant agave price swings. Diageo's Net Zero ambition by 2050 — with 2030 targets including a 50% reduction in Scope 1 and 2 emissions, renewable energy deployment at major distilleries, and water stewardship across 100+ sites — creates capital expenditure obligations that compete with growth investment.

**Legal.** Scotch Whisky Association's official statement encapsulated the sector's view: "The industry is disappointed that Scotch Whisky could be impacted by these tariffs." Regulatory pressure on alcohol advertising, minimum unit pricing, and health-warning labelling requirements are tightening globally.

**PESTEL Synthesis.** The PESTEL analysis reveals that Diageo faces a hostile external environment across multiple dimensions simultaneously. Critically, several forces — GLP-1 adoption, generational moderation, and structural US market saturation — are not cyclical. They will not reverse when interest rates fall or consumer confidence recovers. This distinction between cyclical and structural headwinds is the analytical foundation for the recommendations that follow: waiting for macro recovery is a necessary but insufficient response.

### 2.2 Porter's Five Forces Analysis

**Threat of New Entrants: Low–Moderate.** Barriers to entry into this industry remain high and stable, even for the production of white spirits — besides having to deal with difficult access to limited resources, distillers also have to cope with the complications imposed by the industry's capital intensity, legal requirements and distribution challenges. Diageo holds the world's largest inventory of maturing Scotch requiring decades to replicate, a structural barrier to entry. However, the tequila category demonstrates that brand equity can be built more rapidly: Flecha Azul, the upscale tequila label backed by Mark Wahlberg, leaped 57% to 94,000 cases in 2024, illustrating that celebrity-backed entrants can disrupt even premium categories.

**Bargaining Power of Buyers: Moderate–High.** Consumers are exercising their power through trading down. Further moves towards home socialising will be a common story given the attraction of lower prices in retail stores compared to bars and restaurants. In the off-trade, the consolidation of global retail concentrates buyer power, while large on-trade chains negotiate hard on listing fees and promotional support.

**Threat of Substitutes: Moderate and Rising.** The no/low-alcohol segment is growing at 13.6% CAGR in the US through 2029, driven by moderation movements and GLP-1 adoption. Across major global markets, no- and low-alcohol volumes are forecast to grow about 4% annually from 2024 to 2028, with the no-alcohol segment leading at +7%. This is the most strategically significant of the five forces because it interacts directly with the structural social trends identified in the PESTEL.

**Competitive Rivalry: Moderate–High.** The global premium spirits market is an oligopoly dominated by Diageo, Pernod Ricard, LVMH (Moët Hennessy), and Brown-Forman. Rivalry is intense at the brand level. Diageo's competitive edge rests on deep brand equity, unmatched Scotch maturing inventory and scale advantages that underpin production, distribution and M&A capacity.

**Five Forces Synthesis.** The rising threat of substitutes is the most strategically significant force because it interacts with demographic and pharmacological trends to structurally reduce the addressable market for traditional premium spirits. This is not merely a competitive risk — it is a market-definition risk that calls into question whether Diageo's 2030 TBA share targets are achievable on the current product portfolio.

## PART III: INTERNAL ANALYSIS

### 3.1 Resource-Based View (RBV) and VRIO Framework

The RBV holds that sustained competitive advantage derives from resources and capabilities that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Organised (Barney, 1991). Diageo's strategic assets can be assessed against this framework:

| Resource / Capability | V | R | I | O | Competitive Implication |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Maturing Scotch inventory (world's largest) | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | **Sustained advantage** |
| Brand equity (Johnnie Walker, Guinness, Don Julio) | ✓ | ✓ | Partial | ✓ | **Sustained advantage** |
| Global distribution (180+ countries) | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | Partial | **Advantage, currently underperforming** |
| Scale economies in production/marketing | ✓ | ✓ | Partial | ✓ | **Temporary advantage** |
| Digital/analytics capabilities | ✓ | ✗ | ✗ | Developing | **Parity, not advantage** |
| No/low-alcohol portfolio | ✓ | ✗ | ✗ | Developing | **Potential future advantage** |

Diageo's careful segmentation ensures that the firm's offerings enjoy high inventory turnover both on- and off-premises; in turn, by guaranteeing fast and predictable sales, this also makes Diageo a valued partner for vendors unwilling to bet on unproven products. This is the "Organised" dimension of the VRIO framework — but it is precisely this dimension that broke down in LAC and North America.

The November 2024 launch of the Diageo Luxury Group represents a deliberate attempt to deepen inimitability at the ultra-premium end. The key focus is on accelerating the growth of brands that retail at US$100 and above, and fine-tuning luxury strategy. Leading the initiative is managing director Julie Bramham, former global brand director of Johnnie Walker. This unit will devise luxury strategies to pace up the growth of brands that retail at more than $100, a fast-growing price tier in international spirits since 2020.

**Key RBV Insight:** Diageo's most durable advantages — maturing inventory and brand equity — are inherently backward-looking: they reflect decades of prior investment. The forward-looking question is whether the company is building the *next* generation of inimitable resources. The digital and route-to-market capabilities that will determine execution quality in the next decade are not yet differentiated, which is why the US route-to-market transformation is so strategically important.

### 3.2 Portfolio Analysis: BCG Growth-Share Matrix

Applying the BCG Growth-Share Matrix to Diageo's key brands reveals a portfolio under stress:

**Stars (High Growth, High Share):**
- *Don Julio:* Sales of the tequila brand soared by 28.2% between 2023 and 2024. Five years ago, Don Julio was selling less than 2 million cases annually; it is on track to sell nearly 5 million. After a decade of remarkable growth, Don Julio is now the largest spirits brand in the US market in retail value terms at $2.8 billion.
- *Guinness:* Guinness delivered double-digit growth for an eighth consecutive half, supported by brand-building expertise, innovation and growing global momentum. Great Britain net sales grew 9%, primarily driven by strong performance in Guinness, which gained share in both the on-trade and off-trade.

**Cash Cows (Low Growth, High Share):**
- *Johnnie Walker:* The world's best-selling Scotch whisky, generating substantial cash but facing tariff headwinds. Johnnie Walker gained share of the Scotch category in the first half of fiscal 2024. However, Scotch is disproportionately exposed to US tariffs given its UK origin.
- *Baileys, Smirnoff:* Mature, high-share brands in slower-growth categories. Smirnoff faces category pressure as US tequila value overtook US whiskey in 2023, indicating structural shifts in consumer preference.

**Question Marks (High Growth, Low Share):**
- *No/Low Alcohol Portfolio:* Diageo launched its first alcohol-free dark spirit, Captain Morgan Spiced Gold 0.0%, in fiscal 2024. Operating in a fast-growing segment but with nascent market positions.
- *RTD Cocktails:* Spirits-based cocktails net sales increased 33%, driven by the expansion of the cocktail collection including Ketel One Espresso Martini, Ketel One Cosmopolitan, and Tanqueray Negroni. In the US, the RTD category is expected to reach a 9% share of the TBA sector by 2029.

**Dogs (Low Growth, Low Share):**
- *Captain Morgan Original Spiced:* Captain Morgan net sales declined 2%, primarily due to Captain Morgan Original Spiced.
- *J&B Scotch:* Southern Europe net sales were 1% lower, due to declines in rum and J&B.

**BCG Synthesis.** The portfolio has a clear bifurcation: Don Julio and Guinness are performing brilliantly while a significant portion — particularly rum and some Scotch expressions — is in structural decline. This creates a strategic imperative to harvest cash from Cash Cows efficiently, invest aggressively behind Stars, and make decisive calls on the Question Marks (no/low, RTD) before competitors establish unassailable positions.

### 3.3 Value Chain Analysis

Diageo's value chain reveals where competitive advantage is created and where it is currently leaking:

**Operations:** The maturing stock inventory is a genuine moat. However, with nearly 40% of sales from emerging markets, Diageo is exposed to currency volatility and trade-route disruptions that can erode reported earnings. The $2 billion productivity target from FY25–27 (now increased to $625 million in cost savings over three years) indicates that operations are a source of value destruction as much as creation.

**Outbound Logistics & Marketing:** The US route-to-market transformation acknowledges that Diageo's distribution infrastructure had fallen behind. Diageo stepped up its route-to-market across several markets, including the most significant transformation in at least a decade in its US Spirits organisation. However, the LAC inventory crisis revealed a fundamental failure in demand-sensing: having conducted a review of inventory levels and monitored performance in the critical holiday season, Diageo took action and had further plans to reduce inventory to more appropriate levels for the current consumer environment in the region by the end of fiscal 2024.

**Value Chain Synthesis.** The primary leak in Diageo's value chain is the gap between brand investment (strong) and route-to-market execution (inconsistent). The LAC crisis was not primarily a demand problem — it was a channel management failure. Addressing this is not just about efficiency; it is about restoring trade partner trust and preventing future inventory cycles that destroy shareholder value.

## PART IV: THE PREMIUMISATION PARADOX — SYNTHESISING THE ANALYSIS

Drawing the PESTEL, Five Forces, and internal analyses together, the central strategic tension can be stated precisely.

**Diageo's competitive strategy is built on premiumisation.** TBA has grown at a 4.4% compound annual growth rate (CAGR) over the past decade, and international spirits has grown at 5.1% as measured by IWSR over the same period, while premium beer where Guinness competes has also grown ahead of TBA. Diageo is confident in the long-run trend of premiumisation, supported by demographic trends and rising incomes in developing markets.

**However, multiple forces are simultaneously compressing the premiumisation runway:**

1. *Cyclical:* The need to economise is forcing some consumers to cut back on spirits purchases or even consider trading down for certain occasions.
2. *Structural-demographic:* The Boomer generation, the key generation for drinking spirits, is aging out and drinking less.
3. *Structural-pharmacological:* 44% of GLP-1 users drink less after starting treatment and 82% keep those habits even after stopping treatment. These users are disproportionately the affluent consumers who would otherwise be Diageo's premium customers.
4. *Structural-competitive:* Low/no alcohol is booming, projected to grow at 13.6% CAGR (2024–29) in the US, compared to just 2.1% for traditional alcohol.

**The paradox is this:** Diageo cannot simply wait for the cycle to turn, because the structural forces mean the market it returns to will be smaller and different from the market it left. Yet over-rotating away from premiumisation would destroy the brand equity that is its primary competitive asset. The strategy must therefore be *adaptive premiumisation* — preserving and extending brand equity while simultaneously repositioning the portfolio to capture value from consumers who are moderating rather than abstaining.

### Ansoff Matrix: Evaluating Strategic Growth Options

The Ansoff Matrix (Ansoff, 1957) provides a structured framework for evaluating Diageo's growth options:

- **Market Penetration (existing products, existing markets):** The US route-to-market transformation and cost savings programme fall here. This is necessary but insufficient — it addresses execution gaps without resolving structural market headwinds. It is the right short-term priority but a dangerous long-term strategy if structural demand is declining.

- **Market Development (existing products, new markets):** In regions such as India, Diageo continued to benefit from consumer premiumisation, particularly in the prestige-and-above whisky segment, aided by smaller pack sizes and the potential tailwind of the UK-India Free Trade Agreement. Africa also delivered organic net sales growth across all markets, led by Ghana, while Latin America and the Caribbean saw improving conditions in Brazil and premiumisation-led growth in Mexico. These markets offer genuine structural growth because the premiumisation journey is still early-stage. This is the most compelling growth vector.

- **Product Development (new products, existing markets):** Premium and spirit-based RTDs have emerged as top performers in the category, and products that lean into premiumisation can drive wider growth. The no/low portfolio and RTD cocktails represent product development plays in existing markets that are urgent precisely because first-mover advantages are compounding.

- **Diversification (new products, new markets):** The Diageo Luxury Group brings together its finest assets, exceptional brands and talented individuals, to combine heritage with a forward-thinking drive, accelerating growth of brands retailing at US$100 and above — a fast-growing price tier in international spirits since 2020. This represents related diversification into the ultra-luxury experiences and collectibles market.

## PART V: RECOMMENDATIONS AND ACTION PLAN

The following five recommendations are sequenced by urgency and strategic priority, each grounded in the analytical chain developed above.

### Recommendation 1: Resolve the US Route-to-Market and Implement Inventory Guardrails

**The Problem:** The LAC crisis and North America underperformance both reflect the same root cause: Diageo's channel management and demand-sensing capabilities are insufficient for the volatility of the current environment. The LAC region, which accounts for 10% of Diageo's net sales, suffered net sales declining 15%, with organic net sales dropping 21% in fiscal 2024, driven by weakened demand for international premium spirits and the need to adjust inventory levels.

**The Logic:** The RBV analysis shows that Diageo's global distribution network is theoretically inimitable, but the VRIO "Organised" dimension is currently failing. Until this is fixed, the company's ability to execute any other strategic initiative is compromised: new product launches will fail if channel execution is poor; emerging-market expansion will replicate the LAC problem in new geographies.

**The Recommendation:** Complete the US route-to-market transformation with urgency. Invest in sell-out analytics (not just sell-in data) to create real-time visibility of consumer demand at point of sale. Establish hard inventory guardrails — maximum weeks-of-supply thresholds — for every major market, with automatic throttling of shipments when thresholds are breached. Apply lessons from LAC to prevent recurrence in India, Nigeria, and other high-growth markets where rapid volume build-up creates similar destocking risk.

**Metric:** Inventory days outstanding by region, tracked quarterly against pre-set guardrails. No region should exceed 12 weeks of forward cover without Board-level sign-off.

---

### Recommendation 2: Accelerate Emerging-Markets Expansion with a Differentiated Model

**The Problem:** North America and Europe face structural headwinds from moderation trends, tariffs, and generational shifts. Meanwhile, Africa, India, and parts of Latin America offer genuine structural growth.

**The Logic:** The Ansoff analysis identifies market development as the most strategically compelling growth vector. Diageo's fiscal 2025 results underscored the diverging fortunes of its portfolio across developed and emerging markets. While overall organic sales rose 1.7%, growth was disproportionately supported by emerging economies, wherein both volume and price/mix trends were positive. The PESTEL analysis confirms that the demographic and income trends driving premiumisation are still early-stage in Africa and India. The RBV analysis shows that Diageo's brand equity (particularly Johnnie Walker and Guinness) already has strong recognition in these markets, meaning the company can leverage existing assets rather than building new ones.

**The Recommendation:** Increase capital allocation to Africa and India as a proportion of total investment. In India, pursue the tailwinds from the UK-India Free Trade Agreement aggressively. In Africa, continue the localisation strategy while building aspirational brand positioning that creates the premiumisation ladder for rising middle-class consumers. Introduce smaller pack sizes (already successfully deployed in India) to lower the entry price point without diluting brand equity.

**Metric:** Emerging markets (Africa + India + Southeast Asia) as a percentage of total organic net sales growth, targeting 40%+ of incremental growth by FY2027.

### Recommendation 3: Build a Credible No/Low-Alcohol Position Before the Window Closes

**The Problem:** Low/no alcohol is booming, projected to grow at 13.6% CAGR (2024–29) in the US, compared to just 2.1% for traditional alcohol. Diageo has the brand assets to compete but lacks the scale and focused investment to establish category leadership. Specialist operators are building positions that will be difficult to displace once consumer habits form.

**The Logic:** The Five Forces analysis identifies the rising threat of substitutes as the most strategically significant force. If no/low-alcohol products are genuine substitutes for premium spirits, Diageo must own this substitution rather than cede it to competitors. The GLP-1 data is particularly instructive: 82% of GLP-1 users maintain reduced consumption even after stopping treatment — meaning these are not temporary abstainers but permanent moderators. Capturing their spending on no/low products is better than losing them entirely.

**The Recommendation:** Establish a dedicated No/Low Business Unit with its own P&L, marketing budget, and innovation pipeline — separate from the mainstream spirits organisation, which has structural incentives to protect its existing portfolio. Prioritise Guinness 0.0 (which already benefits from Guinness's broader brand surge) as the anchor brand. Invest in distribution specifically for no/low occasions (health food retailers, gyms, corporate events) not served by traditional on-trade and off-trade channels. Partner with GLP-1 programme providers and healthcare platforms to position Guinness 0.0 and Tanqueray 0.0 as the premium choice for moderating consumers.

**Metric:** No/low portfolio as a percentage of total net sales, targeting 5% by FY2028. Track brand awareness scores for Guinness 0.0 and Tanqueray 0.0 among 18–35-year-olds quarterly.

### Recommendation 4: Rationalise the Tail Portfolio and Redeploy Capital to Stars

**The Problem:** The BCG analysis reveals that a significant portion of Diageo's portfolio is in structural decline, consuming marketing investment, management attention, and distribution capacity that could be redeployed to Don Julio, Guinness, and the no/low portfolio.

**The Logic:** The RBV analysis shows that Diageo's competitive advantages are concentrated in its premium and ultra-premium brands. Investing in declining mainstream brands to defend market share is a value-destroying use of capital. The BCG framework is explicit: Dogs should be divested or managed for cash extraction, not reinvested. Don Julio is on track to sell nearly 5 million cases annually — the brand is an anomaly that requires dedicated investment to sustain its trajectory in a market rocked by headwinds. That investment must come from somewhere.

**The Recommendation:** Conduct a formal portfolio review with a clear divestiture mandate for brands that: (a) are declining in organic net sales for three consecutive years; (b) operate in categories where Diageo has no structural competitive advantage; and (c) would attract a premium valuation from a trade buyer. Captain Morgan Original Spiced and J&B are candidates for disposal or licensing. Proceeds should be redeployed into Don Julio capacity expansion, Guinness 0.0 investment, and emerging-markets route-to-market infrastructure.

**Metric:** Number of SKUs rationalised per year; proceeds from disposals reinvested in Stars and Question Marks; organic net sales growth rate of retained portfolio versus divested portfolio.

## CONCLUSION

Diageo is not a company in terminal decline. Its competitive edge rests on deep brand equity, unmatched Scotch maturing inventory and scale advantages that underpin production, distribution and M&A capacity. The fundamentals for global TBA, and particularly the spirits industry, remain strong.

But the company has spent two years managing crises — LAC destocking, North America softness, CEO departure, tariff uncertainty — rather than executing a coherent strategic adaptation. The danger is that management treats all of these as cyclical problems to be weathered, when several are structural problems to be solved.

The premiumisation paradox — that the strategy depends on consumers trading up, while multiple forces are simultaneously pushing them down — is the unifying strategic challenge. Resolving it requires not abandoning premiumisation (which would destroy the brand equity that is Diageo's primary moat) but *extending* it: into no/low-alcohol occasions, into emerging markets where the premiumisation journey is nascent, and into ultra-luxury experiences genuinely insulated from value-seeking behaviour.

Interim CEO Nik Jhangiani has stated: "We recognise the need to drive meaningful growth opportunities in an evolving TBA landscape." The frameworks applied in this analysis — PESTEL, Porter's Five Forces, RBV/VRIO, BCG Matrix, and Ansoff Matrix — converge on the same conclusion: the path forward is adaptive premiumisation, executed with urgency in no/low and emerging markets, and funded by disciplined rationalisation of a tail portfolio that is consuming capital without generating strategic returns.
