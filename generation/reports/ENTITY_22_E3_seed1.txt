# STRATEGIC CASE STUDY: REGENERON PHARMACEUTICALS (NASDAQ: REGN)
## Navigating the Dual Disruption of Biosimilar Erosion and Pricing Reform

---

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Regeneron Pharmaceuticals faces a strategic inflection point in 2024–2025. Its flagship ophthalmology franchise, EYLEA, confronts accelerating biosimilar competition following FDA approval of interchangeable biosimilars in May 2024, while the Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) introduces a structurally new pricing regime that threatens long-run revenue capture across the portfolio. Simultaneously, its growth engine, Dupixent, continues to expand—reaching its sixth FDA indication in September 2024—but analysts project that Dupixent will itself enter the IRA negotiation window in the early 2030s. This case analyses Regeneron's strategic situation using PESTEL, Porter's Five Forces, the Resource-Based View (RBV) with VRIO, and the Ansoff Matrix. The analysis leads to a three-horizon action plan centred on: (1) accelerating the Dupixent indication lifecycle, (2) deploying the Regeneron Genetics Center (RGC) and VelociSuite as a platform business, and (3) disciplined diversification into oncology and obesity to reduce portfolio concentration risk.

---

## 2. COMPANY OVERVIEW & STRATEGIC SITUATION

Regeneron Pharmaceuticals, founded in 1988 and headquartered in Tarrytown, New York, is a fully integrated biopharmaceutical company. Its commercial portfolio as of 2024 comprises EYLEA/EYLEA HD (retinal diseases), Dupixent (type-2 inflammatory diseases), and Libtayo (oncology). Full-year 2024 revenues increased 8% to $14.20 billion versus 2023; excluding the discontinued COVID antibody Ronapreve, revenues increased 10%.

**The Strategic Tension.** Regeneron's revenue base rests on two pillars moving in opposite directions. Dupixent global net sales grew 23% to $3.82 billion in Q3 2024 versus Q3 2023, sustaining a ~25% annual growth trajectory. EYLEA, however, faces an existential biosimilar threat: the FDA approved the first interchangeable biosimilars, Yesafili (aflibercept-jbvf) and Opuviz (aflibercept-yszy), in May 2024. Bayer's Eylea declined 24%, and Regeneron saw sales for the franchise plummet from $6 billion in 2024 to $4.4 billion in 2025. This tension—a high-growth asset funding a declining one—defines the core strategic problem.

## 3. ENVIRONMENTAL ANALYSIS: PESTEL

### 3.1 Political
The most consequential political development is the Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 (IRA). Under the IRA, biologics are spared from price negotiations for 13 years following approval, while the grace period for small molecules is only nine years—a disparity some stakeholders term the "pill penalty." This asymmetry directly shapes Regeneron's pipeline composition decisions, favouring continued investment in biologics. However, Executive Order 14273 directs HHS to work with Congress to align the treatment of small molecule prescription drugs with that of biological products, ending the so-called "pill penalty." If enacted, this would reduce the relative advantage of Regeneron's biologic-heavy portfolio.

**Warrant:** The IRA's negotiation mechanism creates a ceiling on long-run revenue for any drug with significant Medicare exposure. Because Dupixent's COPD indication targets an older population—disproportionately Medicare-covered—more patients will fall into Medicare coverage, making the IRA not merely a background risk but a direct constraint on the asset's revenue trajectory beyond its current exclusivity runway.

### 3.2 Economic
The average cost for a Big Pharma company to develop a drug in 2024 was $2.23 billion, up from $2.12 billion the year before. Half of all drug developers surveyed identified rising costs as the top challenge in 2024. This macro-economic pressure on R&D productivity raises the stakes of capital allocation decisions: each pipeline bet must clear a higher hurdle rate. Regeneron's Q1 2024 R&D spend alone was $863 million.

**Warrant:** Rising development costs compress the margin for error in pipeline prioritisation. A failed Phase 3 programme—such as Regeneron's CD20xCD3 bispecific antibody, which was rejected for accelerated approval because it failed to meet the criteria in its confirmatory trials—not only destroys sunk capital but delays revenue diversification, amplifying concentration risk in the existing portfolio.

### 3.3 Social
The obesity epidemic represents a major societal shift reshaping pharmaceutical demand. Despite all the enthusiasm surrounding GLP-1 agonists for obesity, it has been increasingly recognised that the profound weight loss is accompanied by substantial muscle loss, accounting for up to as much as 40% of the weight loss. Regeneron is developing muscle-preserving antibodies as differentiated complements to GLP-1 therapy, with interim results from the ongoing Phase 2 COURAGE trial confirming the potential to improve the quality of GLP-1-induced weight loss by preserving lean mass. This social trend creates a new addressable market that Regeneron can enter from a position of scientific differentiation rather than head-on GLP-1 competition.

### 3.4 Technological
VelociSuite platforms enable rapid, cost-effective discovery and development of fully human antibodies, giving Regeneron a material competitive edge, exemplified during the COVID-19 pandemic when they quickly produced potent antibody cocktails. The Regeneron Genetics Center (RGC), established in 2014, has developed one of the largest and richest human genetic databases globally. Since its inception and through a network of over 120 collaborators globally, RGC has developed landmark discoveries and led to multiple new therapeutic development programmes across a range of therapeutic modalities. Now capable of sequencing 200,000 exomes annually, RGC leverages its genomic data to inform drug development, with a stated goal of sequencing between 5 million and 10 million exomes over the next decade.

**Warrant:** Technological capability at the discovery stage is a leading indicator of future pipeline richness. Because the RGC's genetic insights have unlocked opportunities in precision medicine and enable precision identification of patient subpopulations most likely to respond to specific therapies, the platform functions as a compounding asset: each additional exome sequenced increases the probability of identifying the next validated drug target, creating a virtuous cycle that competitors without equivalent infrastructure cannot easily replicate.

### 3.5 Regulatory
In 2024, the FDA approved 38 new molecular entities for therapeutic use, a decline from 47 in the previous year. The FDA's increased support for accelerated approval pathways brought 24 accelerated approvals and label expansions in 2024. However, the Regeneron CD20xCD3 case illustrates the risk: accelerated approval requires confirmatory trial success, and failure can result in market withdrawal. The regulatory environment thus rewards companies that can generate robust evidence efficiently—a capability that Regeneron's integrated genetics and clinical platform is designed to provide.

## 4. INDUSTRY STRUCTURE: PORTER'S FIVE FORCES

### 4.1 Threat of New Entrants — LOW to MODERATE
Entry barriers in branded biopharmaceuticals are high: patents, regulatory approval requirements, manufacturing complexity, and large-scale clinical trial infrastructure create formidable obstacles. The development cost of $2.23 billion per asset and timelines of up to 15 years mean that de novo entry into Regeneron's therapeutic areas is practically impossible for new firms. However, the threat is moderate rather than low because large diversified pharmaceutical companies have the capital and capabilities to enter adjacent therapeutic areas—as evidenced by Roche launching its long-acting eye disease medicine Vabysmo in 2022, after which Bayer and Regeneron have seen the impact on sales of their rival treatment Eylea.

**Warrant:** Vabysmo's market entry demonstrates that the relevant threat is not start-up entry but lateral entry by established players with differentiated mechanisms. This shifts the competitive threat from a structural barrier question to a product-differentiation question: can Regeneron maintain clinical superiority?

### 4.2 Bargaining Power of Buyers — HIGH and RISING
Buyers in biopharma operate at two levels: institutional and individual. The six largest PBMs manage nearly ninety-five percent of all prescriptions filled in the United States. The IRA's Medicare negotiation programme amplifies government buyer power further: the Congressional Budget Office forecasts that the Medicare Drug Price Negotiation Program will reduce net prices for selected drugs by about 50%, on average.

**Warrant:** High buyer concentration (PBMs) combined with new statutory pricing authority (IRA) structurally compresses the price ceiling for any drug with significant Medicare exposure. This is not a cyclical risk but a permanent structural shift, warranting strategic responses that reduce Medicare exposure or compete on non-price dimensions such as superior dosing convenience and outcomes data.

### 4.3 Bargaining Power of Suppliers — LOW
Regeneron is vertically integrated in its key production activities, having completed the construction of its first fill/finish facility in Rensselaer, NY. Its proprietary VelociSuite manufacturing technologies reduce dependence on contract manufacturers for critical biological processes.

### 4.4 Threat of Substitutes — HIGH
The biosimilar threat to EYLEA is the most immediate substitute risk. The FDA approved the first interchangeable biosimilars, Yesafili and Opuviz, in May 2024. The timing of biosimilar launches will depend on pending litigation outcomes and commercial agreements, while Regeneron's strategy integrates legal defense and high-dose product roll-out to support market share retention.

**Warrant:** Interchangeable biosimilar status removes the prescriber as a gatekeeper to substitution, fundamentally changing the competitive dynamic from a physician-detailing contest to a price-and-access contest. This is why Regeneron's strategy of launching EYLEA HD is a rational but time-limited response: it differentiates on dosing convenience but does not address the fundamental price differential that biosimilars offer to payers.

### 4.5 Competitive Rivalry — HIGH
The markets in which Regeneron competes are characterised by intense rivalry. In oncology, Libtayo (cemiplimab) competes in the PD-1 inhibitor class against Merck's Keytruda and Bristol Myers Squibb's Opdivo. In type-2 inflammation, Dupixent currently has a first-mover advantage in COPD, but AstraZeneca and GSK are advancing rivals to Dupixent in COPD, with GSK reporting Phase III data on Nucala in COPD in September 2024.

## 5. INTERNAL ANALYSIS: RESOURCE-BASED VIEW (RBV) & VRIO

The Resource-Based View (Barney, 1991) holds that sustained competitive advantage derives from resources and capabilities that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Organised (VRIO). The following VRIO table applies this framework to Regeneron's four most strategically significant resources.

| Resource/Capability | Valuable? | Rare? | Inimitable? | Organised? | Implication |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| VelociSuite antibody platform | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ (proprietary, patent-protected) | ✓ | **Sustained competitive advantage** |
| Regeneron Genetics Center (RGC) | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ (scale, data network effects) | ✓ | **Sustained competitive advantage** |
| Dupixent indication breadth | ✓ | ✓ | Partially (mechanism replicable) | ✓ | **Temporary advantage; requires continuous expansion** |
| EYLEA franchise | ✓ | ✗ (biosimilar entry) | ✗ (molecule off-patent) | ✓ | **Competitive parity → erosion** |

### 5.1 VelociSuite
VelociSuite platforms enable rapid, cost-effective discovery and development of fully human antibodies, giving Regeneron a material competitive edge. Regeneron's founders built a culture and technology ecosystem designed for repeated innovation, combining deep scientific expertise with entrepreneurial discipline. The platform's inimitability derives from a combination of patent protection, tacit organisational knowledge accumulated over three decades, and the network of scientific talent built around it.

**Warrant:** Because VelociSuite reduces both time-to-candidate and attrition rates during clinical development, it functions as a cost advantage as well as a speed advantage. At an industry average development cost of $2.23 billion per asset, even a modest reduction in attrition probability translates into hundreds of millions of dollars of expected-value advantage per programme—a compounding benefit that grows with pipeline size.

### 5.2 Regeneron Genetics Center
VelociSuite, which enables rapid development of fully human antibodies and bispecifics, remains a cornerstone of the company's drug discovery strategy. Meanwhile, the RGC's genetic insights have unlocked opportunities in precision medicine. RGC has a stated goal of sequencing between 5 million and 10 million exomes over the next decade, with line-of-sight on a million in just a few years. Competitors cannot easily replicate this asset because it requires not just capital but a decade-long investment in patient partnerships and data infrastructure.

**Warrant:** The RGC provides Regeneron with a proprietary target-identification filter. The Regeneron Genetics Center provides competitive advantage in identifying patient subpopulations most likely to respond to specific therapies, enabling more targeted clinical development and commercial positioning. This is a direct response to the industry-wide problem of rising R&D costs and falling FDA approval rates.

### 5.3 Dupixent Indication Breadth
In September 2024, the FDA approved Dupixent for chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), marking its sixth indication and making it the first biologic approved for COPD. Dupixent is also approved for conditions including atopic dermatitis, asthma, chronic rhinosinusitis with nasal polyposis, eosinophilic esophagitis, and prurigo nodularis. However, this breadth is only a temporary advantage: the IL-4/IL-13 mechanism is now well-understood, making it theoretically replicable. The advantage is therefore maintained through continuous indication expansion rather than structural inimitability.

### 5.4 EYLEA Franchise — Erosion of Competitive Position
EYLEA's loss of exclusivity following biosimilar approvals in May 2024 represents a transition from "sustained competitive advantage" to "competitive parity" in VRIO terms. The molecule is no longer rare or inimitable. Biosimilar entry could lead to a 15–25% revenue decline over five years in key markets. EYLEA HD's differentiation on dosing convenience provides a temporary buffer, but the structural trajectory is one of revenue erosion.

## 7. STRATEGIC PROBLEM DEFINITION

The PESTEL, Five Forces, and RBV/VRIO analyses converge on a single strategic problem statement:

> **Regeneron faces a structural revenue replacement challenge: its highest-revenue legacy asset (EYLEA) is entering permanent biosimilar-driven decline, while its primary growth asset (Dupixent) faces a long-run pricing ceiling imposed by the IRA and eventual competitive entry. The company must leverage its proprietary platform capabilities (VelociSuite, RGC) to generate a third revenue pillar before Dupixent's growth rate decelerates—without destroying the scientific culture and R&D productivity that constitute its core competitive advantage.**

This framing is important because it identifies the problem as a *timing* problem as much as a *strategy* problem: Regeneron has the capabilities to solve it, but the window in which Dupixent's cash flows can fund the necessary investment is finite. Dupixent offers substantial runway with exclusivity extending to 2031, providing nearly six more years of market protection. This extended timeline allows Regeneron to maximise profitability while investing in next-generation therapies and pipeline development.
