# MANCHESTER UNITED: A STRATEGIC CASE STUDY
## *"The Fallen Giant" — Diagnosing Strategic Failure and Charting a Path to Recovery*

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Manchester United is one of the most commercially powerful football clubs in the world, yet it has entered a period of acute strategic crisis. Between 2022 and 2025, the club posted cumulative pre-tax losses exceeding £300 million, finished 15th in the Premier League in 2024/25 — its worst position in the modern era — and failed to qualify for any European competition in 2025/26. This case study identifies the central strategic problem as a **value-destroying misalignment** between the club's extraordinary commercial assets and its dysfunctional operating model — a dysfunction rooted in twenty years of governance failure, structural debt, and absent sporting strategy. Using **Porter's Five Forces**, the **Resource-Based View (RBV)**, a **SWOT analysis**, and **stakeholder mapping (Mendelow's Matrix)**, the case builds a coherent analytical chain from diagnosis to a concrete strategic action plan.

## 1. SITUATIONAL BACKGROUND

### 1.1 The Ownership Structure and Its Legacy

Manchester United's current difficulties cannot be understood without reference to the Glazer family's 2005 leveraged buyout (LBO). The root of Manchester United's financial troubles can be traced back to 2005, when the Glazer family acquired the club through a leveraged buyout, financing the £790 million purchase primarily through loans, with approximately £660 million of debt placed directly onto the club's balance sheet. This meant Manchester United — not the Glazers — became responsible for repaying the acquisition cost, a burden that has persisted for two decades.

As of 2025, Manchester United's debt stands at approximately £500 million to £600 million, a figure that includes a combination of short-term and long-term debts. The club's governance is further complicated by a **dual-class share structure**. Manchester United uses a two-class share structure: Class A (public) and Class B (super-voting). Class B shares are convertible into Class A on a one-for-one basis, but conversion is rare because it would dilute voting power. The Glazers hold around 48.9% of total shares and 67.9% of voting power, while Ratcliffe owns 28.9% and controls all football operations.

Manchester United now has one of the most unique ownership situations in world football, following the Glazer family selling 27.7 percent of the club to Sir Jim Ratcliffe's company INEOS. The deal saw Ratcliffe pay approximately £1.3 billion for an initial 26.2% stake in the club. This split creates a **principal-agent problem at the governance level**: INEOS can direct sporting decisions but cannot compel the Glazers to sell, recapitalise the club, or exit. Fan groups and institutional investors face near-zero ability to influence strategic direction.

### 1.2 Financial Performance: Record Revenue, Structural Loss

Manchester United's FY2025 results present a central analytical paradox. The Men's first team reached the final of the UEFA Europa League and finished the 2024/25 season in 15th position in the Premier League; the club achieved record fiscal 2025 total revenues of £666.5 million.

Yet despite record revenues, the club posted significant losses. The revenue breakdown reveals a structurally fragile model:

- Commercial revenue of $453 million represented 50% of United's 2024–25 revenue, up 10% over the prior year, as retail surged 16%, which the club attributed to the launch of a new e-commerce model. Sponsorships rose 6% with the 2024–25 season the first for new front-of-shirt partner, Qualcomm, via their Snapdragon brand.

- Broadcasting revenue sank 22% to $235 million, due to the 15th-place finish in the Premier League and competing in the less lucrative Europa League versus Champions League in 2023–24.

On the cost side, there was a substantial reduction in wages, which dropped £52 million (14%) from £365m to £313m, mainly due to lower performance bonuses and the impact of Sir Jim's restructuring, though player amortisation continued to rise.

The structural dependency on debt financing is acute. The injection of £80.0 million in equity proceeds from INEOS was mathematically insufficient to cover the capital shortfall. Consequently, the club was forced to execute a net drawdown of £130 million from its syndicated Revolving Credit Facility, highlighting a structural dependency on short-term bank debt to sustain core business operations.

Transfer payables have simultaneously risen. The current payables have risen to £323.4m from £309.5m. Inherited issues include Casemiro's wage increase clauses, as well as problems linked with Jadon Sancho, Antony, Andre Onana, and Rasmus Højlund.

**PSR Risk:** Manchester United said that they are "in danger" of breaching the Premier League's Profit and Sustainability Rules (PSR). United have reported losses before tax of £312.9 million over the last three years.

### 1.3 Sporting Performance

The arrival of Rúben Amorim at Manchester United in the middle of the 2024/25 campaign was met with cautious optimism. Yet the reality of his early tenure underscored the magnitude of the task he had inherited. Succeeding Erik ten Hag, Amorim stepped into a squad burdened with inconsistency, tactical disorganisation, and a loss of identity on the pitch. By the end of the season, United had slumped to 15th in the Premier League, their lowest finish in the modern era.

The sporting failure has direct financial consequences beyond broadcasting revenue. Due to performance during the 2024/25 season, the men's first team will not participate in any European competitions in the 2025/26 season. Furthermore, with respect to the Adidas contract, a £10 million deduction will be applied for 2025/26 due to lack of UCL participation.

There is, however, a meaningful recovery signal. Despite the cup competition disappointments in 2025/26, the season in regards to league form was a vast improvement compared to the previous season, with United finishing in third place, some twelve places higher than they managed during the previous season, securing a return to the UEFA Champions League.

## 2. ANALYTICAL FRAMEWORK 1 — PORTER'S FIVE FORCES

Porter's Five Forces (1979) is applied at the **industry level** (elite European football) to contextualise Manchester United's competitive environment and explain why structural industry dynamics are compounding the club's internal difficulties.

### Force 1: Rivalry Among Existing Competitors — HIGH ↑

The Premier League is intensely competitive, and the relevant competitive metric for Manchester United is not merely league position but **sustained access to Champions League revenue** — without which the financial model begins to unravel. The club's failure to maintain top-four finishes between 2022 and 2025 demonstrates that competitive intensity has exceeded United's current operational capacity to respond.

### Force 2: Bargaining Power of Suppliers (Players and Agents) — HIGH ↑

Elite footballers and their agents hold exceptional bargaining power in a global market. Manchester United's inherited wage commitments illustrate this asymmetry acutely. Manchester United spent £232.4m on new signings in the 2025 summer transfer window, while only recouping £61.7m in sales — their net spend was the third-worst of all Premier League clubs. The inability to offload high-earners without significant financial loss illustrates the asymmetric power agents hold once contracts are signed.

### Force 3: Bargaining Power of Buyers (Fans and Broadcasters) — MODERATE

Fans exhibit very low price elasticity — Old Trafford remains effectively sold out — but fan discontent is rising. Thousands of Manchester United fans joined the biggest protest demonstration against the club's ownership since Sir Jim Ratcliffe took a near 30% stake, ahead of the Premier League fixture against Arsenal in March 2025. The defiant scenes were reminiscent of those before a game against Liverpool in August 2022. Broadcasters pay clubs based on league position and European participation, making United's income highly sensitive to on-pitch results.

### Force 4: Threat of New Entrants — LOW (but Sovereign Wealth Funds shift the landscape)

Traditional barriers to entry in elite football are high. However, the effective "new entrants" are sovereign wealth fund-backed clubs — Manchester City post-2008, Newcastle post-2021, PSG post-2011 — that have permanently shifted the competitive frontier through near-unlimited capital injections.

### Force 5: Threat of Substitutes — MODERATE

The greater substitution threat is **commercial**: sponsors can redirect deals to better-performing clubs. The Snapdragon deal was secured on the basis of United's global brand heritage; sustained on-pitch failure risks future renewal negotiations occurring from a position of structural weakness.

**Porter synthesis:** The industry structure rewards clubs combining on-pitch success with financial sustainability. Manchester United's structural debt, governance fragmentation, and sporting decline have left it uniquely exposed to the compounding effects of Forces 1, 2, and 3 simultaneously.

## 3. ANALYTICAL FRAMEWORK 2 — RESOURCE-BASED VIEW (RBV)

The RBV (Barney, 1991) holds that sustainable competitive advantage derives from resources and capabilities that are **Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN)**. This framework explains why Manchester United has not yet collapsed commercially despite sporting failure — and identifies where the real strategic vulnerabilities lie.

### 3.1 VRIN Audit of Key Resources

| Resource | V | R | I | N | Strategic Assessment |
|---|:---:|:---:|:---:|:---:|---|
| Global brand & ~1.1bn fan base | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | **Core asset — currently being eroded** |
| Old Trafford / real estate | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | Underutilised; new stadium project critical |
| Commercial revenue infrastructure | ✓ | ~ | ~ | ~ | Record revenues but decoupling from performance |
| Youth academy | ✓ | ~ | ~ | ✓ | Underinvested relative to peers |
| Coaching capability | ✗ | — | — | — | Seven managers in ten years; no continuity |
| Data/analytics infrastructure | ✗ | — | — | — | Lagging behind Liverpool, City, Arsenal |
| Governance/ownership structure | ✗ | — | — | — | Value-destroying; principal-agent conflict |

### 3.2 The Brand-Performance Decoupling Problem

The most important RBV insight is that Manchester United's **brand** remains its most powerful VRIN resource — but it is not infinitely durable. Commercial revenue hit a record £333.3 million in FY2025, the first year of the club's 5-year front-of-shirt sponsorship agreement with Snapdragon. United's Chief Marketing Officer noted in an interview: "So, we chose Manchester United for several reasons. One is scale: United have 1.1 billion fans worldwide... They sell more than anybody."

This commercial resilience explains why the club has not yet entered financial freefall despite sporting collapse. However, brand value is a **lagging indicator**. Sponsorship deals are multi-year, negotiated on historical brand strength. As contracts come up for renewal, continued on-pitch failure will reduce negotiating leverage. The £10 million Adidas deduction for Champions League absence is an early signal of this dynamic. The RBV therefore predicts: **if sporting performance is not restored within 2–3 seasons, the commercial moat will begin to erode irreversibly.**

### 3.3 The Missing Dynamic Capabilities

RBV also directs attention to **dynamic capabilities** — the ability to reconfigure resources in response to environmental change. Manchester United has historically lacked:

- **Systematic talent identification and recruitment.** Since acquiring a minority stake, INEOS installed a new top team including CEO Omar Berrada, formerly of cross-city rival Manchester City, new sporting director Dan Ashworth, and technical director Jason Wilcox. However, Ashworth subsequently departed, signalling continued instability in the very leadership layer designed to provide stability.

- **Managerial continuity as an organisational routine.** Seven managers in ten years has destroyed the organisational routines that the RBV identifies as a source of competitive advantage. Clubs like Liverpool (under FSG) built systematic recruitment models based on statistical analysis; United's recruitment has been characterised by expensive, high-profile signings that failed to deliver value.

- **Cost discipline.** The strategic review that underpinned the INEOS overhaul cost the club up to £47.8 million in one-off payments, which the club referred to as "exceptional items" in its annual report. This is a significant one-off charge that reduced the financial headroom available for sporting investment.

## 6. COHERENT STRATEGIC DIAGNOSIS

Drawing the analytical threads together, the case identifies **one root cause and two compounding mechanisms**:

**Root cause:** The Glazer LBO created a structurally debt-laden club in which capital that should fund sporting investment is consumed by debt service. This is not merely a financial problem — it is a **governance problem** that no manager, director of football, or head coach can resolve from within the existing ownership structure.

**Compounding Mechanism 1 — The Performance-Revenue Spiral:**
> Debt-constrained investment → poor recruitment → sporting failure → loss of Champions League revenue → further constraint on investment → repeat

**Compounding Mechanism 2 — The Talent-Culture Erosion:**
> Managerial instability destroys organisational routines (RBV) → no coherent playing identity → cannot attract elite free agents → over-reliance on expensive external signings → when they fail, wage bill worsens → less money for reinvestment → repeat

The INEOS restructuring reduced full-time employee numbers from 1,140 in 2023/24 to 932 in 2024/25, an 18% year-on-year reduction, bringing the club back below pre-pandemic levels. This is a necessary but **insufficient** response. It addresses symptoms (cost base) without resolving the root cause (governance and ownership structure) or the second compounding mechanism (tactical and cultural instability). The analytical chain connecting governance → investment → performance → revenue → governance must be broken at its source.

## 7. STRATEGIC RECOMMENDATIONS AND ACTION PLAN

The following five recommendations are sequenced by urgency and causal logic — each addresses a specific link in the diagnostic chain identified above.

---

### RECOMMENDATION 1: RESOLVE THE OWNERSHIP IMPASSE
**Priority: Immediate (0–12 months) | Theoretical basis: Agency Theory**

No other recommendation can be fully implemented while the Glazers retain financial governance. The dual-class share structure means INEOS cannot compel the capital injection needed to clear debt and fund the stadium. The Glazer family is considering a sale at peak valuation, with the club estimated to be worth around $6.55 billion, making it one of the most valuable sports franchises in the world.

**Actions:**
- INEOS should formally table a full buyout offer for the Glazers' remaining stake, leveraging current valuations and the Glazers' apparent openness to exit
- If a full buyout is not immediately feasible, negotiate a **governance restructuring agreement** that converts Class B shares to Class A (one-share-one-vote) in exchange for a defined capital injection — removing the structural conflict between financial and operational governance
- Engage the UK Government as a facilitating stakeholder, given the stadium regeneration project's claimed £7.3bn annual economic impact

**Rationale:** Agency theory identifies the principal-agent conflict between financial owners (Glazers) and operational managers (INEOS) as the primary source of strategic value destruction. Resolution of this conflict is the prerequisite for all other reforms. Without it, every other recommendation operates under a binding constraint.

---

### RECOMMENDATION 2: ACHIEVE IMMEDIATE PSR COMPLIANCE THROUGH DISCIPLINED ASSET MANAGEMENT
**Priority: Immediate (0–18 months) | Theoretical basis: Porter's Five Forces (Regulatory Threat)**

A PSR points deduction would trigger the performance-revenue spiral in its most acute form — potential relegation, sponsor flight, and player exodus. The precarious financial position has so far prevented head coach Rúben Amorim from making any signings in the January transfer window. This is an existential threat that must be neutralised before any growth strategy can be pursued.

**Actions:**
- Execute a disciplined player sales programme targeting the highest-wage, lowest-contribution players — even at a loss — to reduce wage bill and generate transfer income
- Avoid speculative large-fee acquisitions until three-year rolling losses fall below PSR thresholds
- Maximise player amortisation efficiency by prioritising younger signings (longer amortisation periods, higher resale value)
- Engage Premier League compliance officers proactively to demonstrate a credible financial recovery trajectory

**Rationale:** The Premier League as regulator is a high-power, high-interest stakeholder (Mendelow's Matrix). Proactive compliance management converts a structural threat into a managed constraint. The RBV further supports this: protecting the financial base is a prerequisite for reinvesting in the capabilities (recruitment, coaching, analytics) that create competitive advantage.

---

### RECOMMENDATION 3: COMMIT TO MANAGERIAL AND SPORTING CONTINUITY
**Priority: Short-medium term (12–36 months) | Theoretical basis: RBV — Dynamic Capabilities**

The RBV identifies organisational routines as a source of competitive advantage. Seven managers in ten years has destroyed these routines. Amorim left the club just three points off the top four and a guaranteed place in the UEFA Champions League. Improved performances in 2025/26 did suggest the wide open spaces around the two-man midfield were diminishing — evidence that tactical continuity was beginning to yield results before his January 2026 departure, which appears strategically counterproductive.

**Actions:**
- Appoint a head coach on a **minimum 3-year contract** with explicit board commitment to the project timeline — communicated publicly to players, fans, and sponsors
- Implement a **data-driven recruitment philosophy**: prioritise players aged 20–26 with high statistical output relative to fee; avoid marquee signings based on reputation alone. Manchester United's transfer strategy should centre on sustainable squad building, blending young talent, high-impact signings, and smart exits to reshape a squad still recovering from years of underinvestment and inconsistent recruitment.
- Empower the Sporting Director role with genuine authority over transfer decisions, insulated from short-term managerial pressure
- Invest in a data analytics and performance science department comparable to Liverpool's model

**Rationale:** Dynamic capabilities — the ability to reconfigure resources in response to environmental change — require stable organisational routines as their foundation. Continuity is not a luxury; it is a strategic capability that must be institutionalised.

---

### RECOMMENDATION 4: PROTECT AND EXTEND THE COMMERCIAL MOAT
**Priority: Medium term (12–48 months) | Theoretical basis: RBV — Sustaining VRIN Resources**

Commercial revenue is Manchester United's most durable competitive advantage — but it is a lagging indicator of brand strength. The Snapdragon deal provides a 5-year runway; the club must use this window to deepen commercial relationships before renewal negotiations are weakened by further under-performance.

**Actions:**
- Accelerate **e-commerce and digital fan monetisation**: retail revenue's 16% growth in FY2025 demonstrates untapped potential among the 1.1 billion global fan base, particularly in Asia and North America
- Develop the **Women's team** as a distinct commercial asset with dedicated sponsorship packages, broadcast deals, and merchandise — a WSL 3rd-place finish and FA Cup final appearance are significantly underexploited
- Advance the **New Trafford stadium project** with clarity: Manchester United hope to move to the 100,000-seat stadium in time for the 2030–31 football season, which would represent a step-change in matchday revenue, naming rights income, non-matchday events (concerts, conferences), and the wider regeneration project's projected 1.8 million additional annual visitors

**Rationale:** The RBV shows the brand is VRIN but not self-sustaining. Commercial investment must run in parallel with on-pitch recovery — not as a substitute for it. The stadium project is particularly important because it converts a fixed, depreciating asset (Old Trafford) into a transformative revenue platform that partially decouples commercial income from short-term sporting performance.

---

### RECOMMENDATION 5: REBUILD THE SOCIAL CONTRACT WITH FANS
**Priority: Medium term (ongoing) | Theoretical basis: Stakeholder Theory (Freeman, 1984)**

Stakeholder mapping identifies active fan groups as high-interest stakeholders whose discontent, if sustained, risks reputational damage, reduced matchday atmosphere, and ultimately erosion of the brand's VRIN status. The fan-club social contract analysis reveals that fans consistently balance emotional attachment with rational evaluation. Hedge-fund ownership triggers scepticism, distrust, and high activist engagement.

**Actions:**
- Establish a **Fan Advisory Board with genuine decision-making input** on ticket pricing, stadium atmosphere, and community engagement — not merely consultative
- Commit to a **transparent financial reporting charter**: publish simplified annual financial summaries accessible to ordinary supporters, explaining PSR compliance status and investment plans
- Introduce a **supporter share scheme** (as used by clubs such as AFC Wimbledon and FC United of Manchester): even a small symbolic ownership stake builds community legitimacy
- **Freeze or reduce ticket prices** for a defined period (2 seasons) to signal good faith while the stadium project is developed

**Rationale:** Freeman's (1984) stakeholder theory holds that long-term value creation requires managing all stakeholder relationships, not merely shareholder returns. Fan alienation is not merely a reputational risk — it is a strategic risk that undermines the brand VRIN resource, the matchday revenue stream, and the political capital needed to advance the stadium planning process.

## 8. CONCLUSION: THE COHERENT CHAIN

The analysis yields a clear and connected strategic narrative:

> **Glazer LBO (2005)** → **Structural debt placed on the club** → **Capital diverted from investment to debt service** → **Under-investment in recruitment infrastructure and coaching continuity** → **Managerial instability destroys organisational routines** → **Sporting decline (15th place, 2024/25)** → **Loss of Champions League revenue** → **Worsening PSR position** → **Transfer market constraint** → **Further sporting decline**

Manchester United's commercial assets — its global brand, 1.1 billion fans, and record £333.3m commercial revenue — provide a **temporary buffer**. But the RBV analysis shows that brand value is not self-sustaining: it requires on-pitch success to remain VRIN. The Five Forces analysis shows that competitive intensity has permanently increased, with sovereign wealth fund-backed rivals rewriting the rules of the player market. The SWOT shows that the club's strengths are real but time-limited; the threats are structural and accelerating.

The recommended action plan is sequenced to address root causes before symptoms: **resolve governance first** (Recommendation 1), **achieve PSR compliance second** (Recommendation 2), **establish sporting continuity third** (Recommendation 3), and then **invest in commercial and community relationships** (Recommendations 4 and 5). Without the first step, the remaining four cannot be fully executed. With it, Manchester United possesses the brand heritage, global reach, and infrastructure to reclaim its position among Europe's elite clubs — but the window for action is narrowing with each passing season.
