# Strategic Case Study: Trustpilot and the Trust–Monetisation Paradox

## Navigating Platform Integrity in a Two-Sided Review Market

---

## 1. Introduction and Case Overview

Trustpilot occupies a peculiar strategic position: its entire commercial value depends on a resource — **consumer trust** — that its own revenue model is accused of undermining. Founded in Copenhagen in 2007, Trustpilot operates as a global online consumer review platform, connecting consumers who write reviews with businesses that use those reviews to build reputation and drive sales. Its platform links verified feedback with business discovery and performance marketing, aggregating verified reviews, boosting visibility in search, and selling subscription tools that help companies capture, analyse, and act on customer sentiment.

The strategic situation this case examines is what we term the **Trust–Monetisation Paradox**: Trustpilot's subscription revenue model — the source of its financial growth — creates structural incentives that systematically threaten the integrity of the very reviews that make the platform valuable to consumers in the first place. This is not merely a reputational problem; it is a platform-architecture problem with competitive, regulatory, and existential dimensions.

This case analyses that paradox using four interconnected frameworks: (1) the **Two-Sided Platform / Network Effects** model, to understand the structural logic of Trustpilot's value creation; (2) **Porter's Five Forces**, to map competitive pressures shaping the paradox; (3) a **SWOT Analysis**, to audit internal capabilities and external conditions; and (4) **Porter's Generic Strategies**, to evaluate the strategic options available. The analysis concludes with a prioritised action plan.

## 2. Context and Evidence Base

### 2.1 Financial Performance

Trustpilot's financial trajectory is strong on the surface. Revenue grew 18% in constant currency to $210.7 million (2023: $176.4 million), and the company delivered a record adjusted EBITDA of $24.1 million (2023: $15.5 million), with profit before tax of $5.2 million compared to a $1.9 million loss in the prior year. Annual recurring revenue reached $250 million in 2024, with bookings growing 21% in constant currency. Net Dollar Retention improved from 99% to 101% year-on-year, underpinned by a series of AI-driven product releases.

### 2.2 Platform Scale

Trustpilot's vision is to be the "universal symbol of trust." By the end of 2024, the platform hosted 301 million total active reviews — a 23% year-on-year increase — with 61 million reviews written in 2024 alone. In 2023, 54 million reviews were posted, and over one million businesses were reviewed, one-fifth of them for the first time.

### 2.3 The Fake Review Problem

Despite this scale, platform integrity is under severe and documented stress. In 2024, Trustpilot removed 4.5 million fake reviews — 7% of the total number of reviews posted that year — with 90% caught automatically by AI using machine learning, neural networks, and generative AI. In 2024, Trustpilot added automated guideline violation software using generative AI to support content moderation, and increased efforts to target review sellers by introducing new detection software.

### 2.4 The Monetisation Controversy

The structural tension is most sharply articulated by a 2022 short-seller report. Short seller Grizzly Research alleged that Trustpilot creates unsolicited review profiles for businesses with the intention to attract hyper-negative reviews and force those businesses into paying subscription deals to "more actively manage" their presence. Grizzly Research argued that "Trustpilot has traded the integrity of reviews for revenues," predicting a rapid depreciation of the Trustpilot brand and its fundamental value proposition.

A separate critical analysis reinforces this concern from the business side: businesses paying $599 monthly can selectively display only favourable reviews on their websites while suppressing negative ones — a pay-to-filter model that, combined with Trustpilot's open system allowing anyone to review any business, creates a structurally compromised information environment.

### 2.5 Regulatory Environment

The regulatory context is tightening. The EU Digital Services Act became fully operational on 17 February 2024, and systematic abuse of its Article 16 reporting mechanisms has emerged, with businesses using false defamation claims to remove unfavourable reviews — causing platforms like Trustpilot to err on caution to avoid legal liability and thereby inflating ratings artificially. The European Parliament has raised questions about how to ensure that fake reviews are banned and removed, noting that around one in two consumers reported being unable to find information on whether a company ensures reviews were submitted by real customers.

## 3. Framework 1 — Two-Sided Platform and Network Effects

### 3.1 The Structural Logic

Trustpilot is a canonical **two-sided platform**: it simultaneously serves consumers (who read and write reviews) and businesses (who pay for subscription tools to manage, respond to, and showcase those reviews). The value of the platform to each side depends on the participation of the other — the defining characteristic of a two-sided market (Rochet & Tirole, 2003; Parker & Van Alstyne, 2005).

Trustpilot monetises through a subscription model with a dual-sided platform that leverages network effects and provides businesses with tools to invite reviews, respond to feedback, showcase content, and benchmark performance. As a dual-sided platform, the more authentic reviews that are shared, the richer the insights provided to businesses, which in turn boosts their ability to earn trust.

This creates a **positive feedback flywheel**: more authentic reviews → more consumer utility → more consumer traffic → more business demand for subscription tools → more revenue to fund integrity systems → more authentic reviews.

### 3.2 The Network Effect as Both Asset and Liability

Trustpilot's competitive edge derives from its large review corpus, strong integrity systems that detect and remove fraudulent reviews, and network effects that increase search ranking and merchant retention. This is the asset side of network effects: scale creates defensibility.

However, the same network effects create a liability. Because consumer trust is the platform's core product, any erosion of review integrity produces a **negative feedback loop**: fake or manipulated reviews → declining consumer trust → reduced review quality → reduced business value → subscription churn → reduced revenue to fund integrity → more fake reviews. The two-sided platform model means that a failure on the consumer side (trust erosion) directly destroys value on the business side (subscription demand), and vice versa.

### 3.3 The Monetisation Tension

The monetisation model introduces a structural misalignment. Trustpilot charges businesses for tools to manage their reviews. This creates an asymmetry: businesses that invest in Trustpilot's premium services can benefit from features such as review flagging and enhanced visibility, creating an environment where the wealthiest businesses can shape the ratings landscape. The consumer side — which provides the raw material (reviews) that makes the platform valuable — receives no direct payment and may not know that the businesses they are reviewing are paying to curate what appears on those businesses' own websites.

**The inferential step is critical**: if consumers perceive that reviews on Trustpilot are curated by paying businesses rather than reflecting unfiltered experience, the informational value of the platform collapses. This is not a marginal risk — it is the central strategic threat, because 89% of global consumers check online reviews as part of their online buying journey, and 49% consider positive reviews one of their top three purchase influences. The moment consumers doubt the authenticity of those reviews, the entire value proposition — for both sides — disappears.

## 4. Framework 2 — Porter's Five Forces

Porter's Five Forces (Porter, 1979) maps the competitive structure of an industry. Applied to Trustpilot's strategic environment, the analysis reveals a business under pressure from multiple directions simultaneously.

### 4.1 Competitive Rivalry — HIGH

Google Reviews captures a large share of local feedback via search and Maps, impacting Trustpilot organic traffic. Yelp holds strong North American local-market presence, especially in hospitality. Bazaarvoice leads retail review syndication. G2 and Capterra dominate software review niches with technical buyer intent.

The rivalry is structurally intensified because the dominant competitor — Google — offers its review product for free. It is hard to justify Trustpilot's price point when Google reviews are entirely free, and there are many review management solutions that provide more, at a much lower cost. This price asymmetry means Trustpilot must continuously justify its premium through superior data, analytics, and integrity — precisely the capabilities threatened by the Trust–Monetisation Paradox.

### 4.2 Threat of Substitutes — HIGH

Google Reviews feeds directly into the local search algorithm, making it the dominant choice for local businesses, restaurants, clinics, gyms, and contractors whose acquisition funnels run through "near me" searches. For e-commerce and SaaS, Trustpilot retains relevance, but the substitution risk is real and growing as Google integrates AI-generated summaries of reviews directly into search results, potentially reducing the need for consumers to visit Trustpilot at all.

### 4.3 Bargaining Power of Buyers (Businesses) — MODERATE AND RISING

Businesses paying for Trustpilot subscriptions have increasing alternatives. The tiered pricing structure — with a Starter plan at $99/month for small businesses under $5M revenue, a Plus plan at $319/month, and a Premium plan at $799/month per domain — means that SMEs face meaningful price points relative to free alternatives. As the competitive landscape expands, buyer power rises, creating churn risk. This is evidenced by the fact that improving gross retention from 85% to 87% was a key management priority in FY2025.

### 4.4 Bargaining Power of Suppliers — LOW

Trustpilot's primary "suppliers" are consumers who write reviews. They are individually powerless and unorganised. However, collectively, their willingness to write authentic reviews is the platform's most critical input. If consumer confidence in the platform's integrity declines, the supply of authentic reviews contracts — a form of collective supplier power that operates through trust rather than negotiation.

### 4.5 Threat of New Entrants — MODERATE

The ratings and reviews platform market is large and growing: the global ratings and reviews platform market stood at $8.1 billion in 2024, with a projected CAGR of 15.2% from 2025 to 2033, reaching $27.6 billion. This growth attracts entrants, but Trustpilot's 301 million active reviews represent a significant data moat. The key barrier to entry is not technology (AI moderation tools are increasingly commoditised) but the accumulated corpus of consumer-written reviews and the brand recognition associated with the Trustpilot badge. However, that moat is only as durable as the perceived integrity of those reviews.

**Five Forces Summary**: The overall competitive environment is unfavourable. High rivalry and high substitution threat compress Trustpilot's pricing power, while rising buyer power increases churn risk. The threat of new entrants is mitigated by data scale, but that scale advantage is contingent on maintaining review integrity — the very thing threatened by the monetisation model.

## 5. Framework 3 — SWOT Analysis

The SWOT analysis synthesises the internal and external dimensions of Trustpilot's strategic position, drawing on the preceding frameworks to ensure each element connects to the analysis rather than standing alone.

### 5.1 Strengths

**S1 — Data Scale and Network Effects**: With 301 million total active reviews and 61 million written in 2024 alone, Trustpilot's corpus is unmatched among independent review platforms. This creates a structural barrier to imitation and provides the raw material for AI-driven analytics products.

**S2 — AI-Powered Integrity Infrastructure**: In 2024, Trustpilot expanded its AI capabilities by deploying generative AI to help enforce platform policies — including impersonation, abuse, and misinformation — enabling faster, more consistent detection of policy violations at scale.

**S3 — Subscription Revenue Model with Improving Retention**: Gross retention improved to 87% (from 85%), and the LTM Net Dollar Retention rate reached 103% in FY2024, underpinned by AI-driven product releases. This indicates that existing customers are expanding their usage, a positive signal for the unit economics.

**S4 — Regulatory Positioning**: Trustpilot's open, transparent model and investment in fake review detection position it as a natural compliance partner under the EU Digital Services Act — a potential first-mover advantage as regulation tightens across the sector.

### 5.2 Weaknesses

**W1 — The Structural Trust–Monetisation Conflict**: As documented above, the public perception that "Trustpilot has traded the integrity of reviews for revenues" represents an existential threat to the brand. This is a structural weakness, not merely a communications problem, because it is embedded in the architecture of the business model.

**W2 — Slow and Opaque Dispute Resolution**: Users often report slow dispute resolution and limited control over unfair or fake reviews without verified proof. This creates friction for both businesses (who cannot quickly remove demonstrably false reviews) and consumers (who may find their authentic reviews suppressed by automated systems).

**W3 — SME Price Sensitivity**: The comparison with free alternatives (Google Reviews) creates structural churn pressure among smaller subscribers, who represent the largest segment by count but the lowest revenue per customer.

### 5.3 Opportunities

**O1 — AI-Driven Product Expansion**: With more than 58% of consumers now turning to Generative AI for recommendations, Trustpilot's high domain authority and structured data drove a 246% surge in ChatGPT citations between June and August 2025, making it the 5th most cited platform in AI-generated recommendations. This represents a new distribution channel that amplifies the value of Trustpilot's review corpus without requiring additional consumer acquisition spend.

**O2 — Regulatory Tailwinds**: Tightening fake review regulation in the EU and US creates demand for platforms that can credibly demonstrate review integrity. Trustpilot's investment in AI moderation positions it to benefit from regulatory pressure that disadvantages less rigorous competitors.

**O3 — Enterprise Upsell**: Trustpilot's 2023 strategy to integrate directly with platforms like Shopify Plus and Stripe fuelled a 22% year-on-year surge in enterprise revenue by Q4 2024. Deepening integration with commerce infrastructure creates switching costs and expands the addressable market for analytics and insights products.

**O4 — Market Growth**: The sector is witnessing a CAGR of 15.2%, with projections indicating the market will reach $27.6 billion by 2033. Even maintaining current market share would deliver substantial absolute revenue growth.

### 5.4 Threats

**T1 — Google's Structural Advantage in Local Search**: Google Reviews feeds directly into the local search algorithm, making it the complete solution for local businesses where the entire acquisition funnel runs through Maps visibility. This is a threat that cannot be neutralised by product improvement alone — it is a distribution advantage built into the world's dominant search engine.

**T2 — DSA Abuse and Regulatory Complexity**: The systematic abuse of DSA Article 16 reporting mechanisms by businesses using false defamation claims to remove unfavourable reviews has emerged as an industrial-scale manipulation threat, causing platforms to err on caution and thereby artificially inflate ratings. This creates a regulatory compliance cost and a reputational risk simultaneously.

**T3 — Coordinated Fake Review Campaigns**: Coordinated fake-review campaigns and sophisticated manipulation risk eroding consumer trust; detection lag could reduce partner demand. As AI-generated fake reviews become harder to detect, the arms race between fraud and detection intensifies.

**T4 — AI Search Disintermediation**: As generative AI search tools (ChatGPT, Google AI Overviews, Perplexity) synthesise review data directly into answers, consumers may bypass Trustpilot's website entirely, reducing direct traffic and weakening the consumer side of the platform.

## 6. Framework 4 — Porter's Generic Strategies

Porter (1980) argues that sustainable competitive advantage requires a clear strategic commitment to one of three generic positions: **Cost Leadership**, **Differentiation**, or **Focus**. Firms "stuck in the middle" — attempting to pursue multiple strategies without clear commitment — tend to underperform.

Trustpilot's current position risks exactly this failure mode. Its open, free-to-consumers model is not cost leadership (it cannot undercut Google, which is free). Its premium subscription pricing signals differentiation, but the integrity controversy undermines the quality signal that differentiation requires. And it is not focused on a single segment — it serves SMEs, enterprises, consumers, and regulators simultaneously.

**The strategic imperative is clear**: Trustpilot must commit to a **Differentiation** strategy, with review integrity as the differentiating dimension. The reasoning is as follows:

1. Cost leadership is foreclosed by Google's free product. Trustpilot cannot win a price war.
2. Focus on a single segment (e.g., enterprise only) would sacrifice the network effects that make the platform valuable — fewer reviews means less consumer utility means less business demand.
3. Differentiation on integrity is the only strategy that simultaneously addresses the Trust–Monetisation Paradox, creates a sustainable competitive moat, and aligns with regulatory tailwinds.

Trustpilot is most relevant for e-commerce brands, SaaS companies, and service businesses without a strong local search component — companies running Google Ads that want seller ratings and operating in competitive spaces where brand trust is a major purchase driver. This segment is precisely where integrity-based differentiation commands a premium, because the cost of a trust failure (a consumer buying from a fraudulent business based on fake reviews) is high and reputationally consequential.

The challenge is that genuine differentiation on integrity requires Trustpilot to make choices that reduce short-term revenue — specifically, reducing the ability of paying businesses to suppress or filter negative reviews. This is the strategic tension that the action plan must resolve.

## 7. Synthesis: The Coherent Chain of Analysis

The four frameworks, taken together, produce a single coherent diagnosis:

- **Two-Sided Platform analysis** establishes that Trustpilot's value is entirely dependent on the authenticity of consumer reviews. Anything that compromises that authenticity destroys value on both sides of the platform.
- **Porter's Five Forces** demonstrates that Trustpilot operates in a structurally unfavourable competitive environment where its primary differentiation advantage (trusted reviews) is also its primary vulnerability.
- **SWOT analysis** shows that Trustpilot's strengths (data scale, AI infrastructure, improving retention) are all conditional on maintaining review integrity, while its most significant weakness (the trust–monetisation conflict) directly threatens those strengths.
- **Porter's Generic Strategies** concludes that the only viable path to sustainable competitive advantage is a committed differentiation strategy anchored in integrity — and that this requires resolving the structural conflict between monetisation and trust.

The connecting logic is: **Trustpilot's revenue model currently creates incentives that undermine the asset (authentic reviews) on which all revenue ultimately depends. Resolving this requires restructuring the monetisation model to align business incentives with consumer interests, rather than against them.**

## 8. Recommendations and Action Plan

The following recommendations flow directly from the analysis. Each is justified by the evidence and connected to the strategic frameworks above.

---

### Recommendation 1: Restructure the Subscription Model to Decouple Payment from Review Curation

**The Problem**: The current model allows paying businesses to filter which reviews appear on their own websites and to flag reviews for removal. Businesses paying $599 monthly can selectively display only favourable reviews on their websites while suppressing negative ones. This directly undermines the two-sided platform's value proposition by making the consumer-facing review corpus a function of business spending rather than authentic experience.

**The Recommendation**: Trustpilot should redesign its subscription tiers so that payment unlocks **analytics, insights, and response tools** — but never the ability to suppress or selectively display reviews. Review visibility on Trustpilot's own platform must be non-negotiable and non-purchasable. The TrustBox widget (which businesses embed on their websites) should display a representative sample of reviews, not a curated selection.

**Why This Works**: This resolves the structural conflict identified in the Two-Sided Platform analysis. It eliminates the perception that reviews are for sale, which is the primary driver of consumer trust erosion. It also aligns with Porter's Differentiation strategy: by making integrity non-negotiable, Trustpilot creates a credible quality signal that Google (which has no equivalent moderation infrastructure) cannot replicate. The short-term revenue cost of removing curation features is real but bounded; the long-term cost of consumer trust collapse is existential.

---

### Recommendation 2: Invest in a "Verified Purchase" Closed-Loop Integration Programme

**The Problem**: 4.5 million fake reviews were removed in 2024 — 7% of total reviews posted. The open platform model, which allows any consumer to review any business, is the structural source of this vulnerability. Closed platforms that only accept reviews from verified purchasers report that verified reviews convert at 2–3x the rate of unverified reviews.

**The Recommendation**: Trustpilot should expand its commerce integrations — already demonstrated by the Shopify Plus and Stripe integrations that fuelled a 22% enterprise revenue surge — to create a tiered verification system. Reviews from verified purchasers (confirmed via commerce platform data) should be visually distinguished from open reviews. This creates a "Verified Review" tier that commands a premium and directly addresses the fake review problem at its structural root.

**Why This Works**: This addresses Threat T3 (coordinated fake review campaigns) from the SWOT analysis and Strength S2 (AI infrastructure) by using transaction data to pre-screen review authenticity. It also creates a new upsell opportunity for enterprise clients — verified review collection as a premium service — which supports the revenue model without compromising integrity. The network effects logic supports this: verified reviews are more valuable to consumers, which increases consumer utility, which increases traffic, which increases business demand.

---

### Recommendation 3: Establish an Independent Review Integrity Board

**The Problem**: The Trust–Monetisation Paradox is partly a governance problem. Trustpilot is simultaneously judge and jury on review integrity disputes, and its financial incentives create a structural conflict of interest. Questions have been raised about whether platforms are over-relying on algorithms to decide what is genuine, with authentic voices being silenced in the name of "content integrity."

**The Recommendation**: Trustpilot should establish an independent Review Integrity Board — comprising consumer advocates, academic researchers, and regulatory experts — with the authority to audit moderation decisions, review appeals, and publish quarterly transparency reports. This body should be structurally separate from the commercial organisation, with its own budget and reporting line to the Board.

**Why This Works**: This recommendation directly addresses Weakness W2 (slow and opaque dispute resolution) and Threat T2 (DSA compliance risk). It also creates a powerful differentiation signal: no competitor — including Google — has an equivalent governance structure. The Porter's Differentiation logic applies: if Trustpilot can credibly claim third-party validated integrity, it commands a premium that cost-based competitors cannot match. The regulatory tailwind (Opportunity O2) means this investment will yield compliance dividends as the DSA and equivalent regulations tighten.

---

### Recommendation 4: Reposition the Consumer Value Proposition as a Strategic Asset

**The Problem**: Trustpilot currently treats consumers as a free input — they provide reviews without payment and without explicit value exchange. This is a missed opportunity and a strategic vulnerability: if consumers do not feel that Trustpilot serves their interests, they will not write reviews, and the platform collapses.

**The Recommendation**: Trustpilot should develop a consumer-facing value proposition that explicitly rewards authentic reviewing behaviour. This could include: a reviewer reputation score (publicly visible, gamified), priority access to new product reviews, and direct notification when businesses respond to their reviews. The goal is to make the act of writing an authentic review on Trustpilot intrinsically valuable to consumers — not just instrumentally useful to businesses.

**Why This Works**: Positive star ratings and reviews on the homepage were the trust signals most likely to drive customers to make a purchase (86%), and positive star ratings on a product page influenced 85% of customers. The consumer side of the platform is the source of this value. Investing in the consumer experience strengthens the supply of authentic reviews, which is the foundation of every other element of the strategy. This directly addresses the Two-Sided Platform logic: strengthening one side of the platform (consumers) creates positive network effects on the other side (businesses), enabling premium pricing.

---

### Recommendation 5: Develop an AI-Integrity Differentiation Narrative for the Enterprise Market

**The Problem**: Trustpilot's AI investments in fake review detection are real and significant, but they are currently framed defensively — as a cost of doing business rather than as a source of competitive advantage.

**The Recommendation**: Trustpilot should reframe its AI integrity infrastructure as a **premium enterprise product feature**. Enterprise clients should receive real-time integrity dashboards showing the proportion of verified reviews, the volume of fake reviews detected and removed for their category, and benchmarking against competitors. This transforms a defensive capability into an offensive differentiator.

**Why This Works**: Trustpilot's high domain authority and structured data drove a 246% surge in ChatGPT citations between June and August 2025, making it the 5th most cited platform in AI-generated recommendations. This demonstrates that the integrity of Trustpilot's data is already being recognised by AI systems as a quality signal. Packaging this as an explicit enterprise value proposition — "your reviews are cited by AI because they are verified" — creates a differentiation narrative that Google Reviews (which has weaker moderation) cannot replicate. This connects to Porter's Differentiation strategy and addresses Opportunity O1 (AI-driven product expansion).

## 10. Conclusion

Trustpilot's strategic situation is a textbook case of a **platform integrity dilemma**: the very mechanism by which it generates revenue (charging businesses for review management) creates incentives that corrode the asset (authentic consumer reviews) on which all revenue ultimately depends. The financial results — revenue of $210.7 million, record EBITDA, and a return to profitability — suggest the business is performing well in the short term. But the structural analysis reveals a platform whose growth trajectory is built on a foundation that is simultaneously its greatest strength and its most critical vulnerability.

The resolution is not to abandon monetisation, but to redesign it. A committed **Differentiation strategy** anchored in verifiable, non-purchasable review integrity — supported by verified purchase integrations, independent governance, and an AI-integrity enterprise narrative — is the only path that resolves the paradox rather than managing it. The market context is favourable: a sector growing at 15.2% CAGR toward $27.6 billion by 2033 means that a credible integrity-first strategy can capture substantial value without requiring Trustpilot to win on price. The goal, as Trustpilot itself states, is to become the "universal symbol of trust" — but that vision is only achievable if the business model is structured to make trust genuinely non-negotiable.
