# Mercedes-Benz at the Crossroads: Strategic Case Study

## A Convergence of Crises and the Search for Structural Renewal (2023–2025)

---

## 1. Introduction and Case Overview

Mercedes-Benz Group AG — the world's oldest premium automobile manufacturer — faces one of the most complex strategic environments in its 138-year history. Between 2023 and 2025, the company confronted a simultaneous compression of profitability, a faltering electrification transition, the erosion of its most important single market (China), and the destabilising shock of US trade tariffs. This case examines how those pressures interact, what strategic frameworks reveal about their structural causes, and what a coherent path forward requires.

The strategic situation is not a single crisis but a **compound problem**: a demand-side shock in China, a supply-side cost structure misaligned with lower volumes, an EV product portfolio that has underperformed, and a geopolitical environment that has restructured the economics of global manufacturing. Each element reinforces the others. The analytical task is to disaggregate them, apply appropriate frameworks, and derive recommendations that address root causes rather than symptoms.

## 2. The Strategic Situation: Evidence of Distress

For 2024, Mercedes-Benz Group reported revenue of €145.6 billion, a 4.5% decline, while EBIT was €13.6 billion, reflecting a 30.8% decrease. This is not a marginal deterioration: the Mercedes-Benz Cars division reported an adjusted EBIT of €8.7 billion, a decline from €14.3 billion in 2023, primarily due to lower sales volumes, particularly in China, negative net pricing, and rising costs. The scale of the earnings collapse — nearly one-third of operating profit erased in a single year — signals structural misalignment, not cyclical softness.

The deterioration has continued into 2025. Mercedes-Benz reported a 10% decrease in revenue to €33.2 billion for Q2 2025, down from €36.7 billion in Q2 2024, accompanied by a sharp 68% drop in earnings per share to €0.95, reflecting significant profitability challenges. Mercedes-Benz said its net profit had plummeted from €6.1 billion to €2.7 billion in the first half of 2025, a 56% year-on-year drop.

Three distinct but interacting causes drive this distress: the China collapse, the EV transition misfire, and the US tariff shock. Each is examined in turn before the frameworks are applied.

## 3. Situational Analysis: The Three Fault Lines

### 3.1 The China Fault Line

In China, Mercedes's biggest single market, sales dropped seven percent in 2024. More granularly, China, as the company's largest single market, saw a 6.7% drop in sales to 714,000 vehicles. This is not merely a volume problem; it is a structural repositioning of Chinese consumer preference. BYD, now the world's largest electric vehicle manufacturer, is producing cars with comparable technology at price points Mercedes cannot match without destroying its brand positioning. Xiaomi's entry into the premium EV segment has added another front to a war that European carmakers are losing on home turf. The competitive threat from domestic Chinese brands is therefore not a price war that Mercedes can win by discounting — doing so would undermine the very brand premium that justifies its margins.

In Q2 2025, sales in China dropped 19% to 140,400 vehicles. For the full year 2025, China contributed just 15.9% of group revenue — a share that has been shrinking steadily as domestic competitors have surged. This matters strategically because China was not just a large market; it was historically Mercedes's most *profitable* market, underpinning the group's superior return on sales relative to peers.

### 3.2 The EV Transition Misfire

Mercedes's electrification strategy has been characterised by ambition repeatedly revised downward. The company had initially targeted 50% electrified vehicle sales by 2025; the group's overall 2024 sales fell four percent, hit by a drop of 23% in sales of electric vehicles. The flagship EQ models have been a particular disappointment. Mercedes-Benz EQS sales disappointed, never surpassing 30,000 units annually, and the EQS will likely be discontinued before decade's end. Shipments of both the EQS and EQE SUVs have already fallen by about a third in the first half of 2025, and Mercedes has slashed prices on the vehicles, with the 2026 EQS SUV more than $15,000 cheaper than the 2025 model.

These EV failures are not simply a matter of consumer preference for ICE vehicles. They reflect a product architecture problem: the EQ range was built on adapted ICE platforms rather than purpose-built EV architectures, limiting range, efficiency, and the software integration that premium EV buyers now expect. This is a *resource gap*, not merely a market timing issue — a distinction that the RBV framework below will illuminate.

### 3.3 The US Tariff Shock

Mercedes-Benz Group AG reported a 9% decline in global vehicle sales during Q2 2025, as President Donald Trump's tariffs on imported vehicles and parts, along with retaliatory duties from China, began to impact the luxury automaker's key markets. Sales in the U.S. decreased 12% to 74,600 units. The German carmaking powerhouse predicted that US tariffs would knock €360 million off its bottom line. Mercedes-Benz has made the decision to absorb some of the impact of the tariffs to protect the MSRP of all Model Year 2025 vehicles until further notice — a margin-destroying response that illustrates the bind: raising prices risks volume loss in a brand-sensitive market; absorbing costs compresses margins further.

## 4. Framework Analysis

### 4.1 PESTEL Analysis: Mapping the Macro Environment

The PESTEL framework identifies the macro-environmental forces shaping Mercedes's strategic space. The analysis below focuses on the most materially significant dimensions.

**Political:** The reimposition of US automotive tariffs at 25% represents the most immediate political shock. The consequences were starkly visible in the company's annual report: group earnings collapsed by approximately 49% in 2025 to €5.3 billion. By boosting local production, management aims to mitigate this cost pressure over the medium term. The political dimension also includes EU–China trade friction, which has triggered retaliatory duties affecting German carmakers in China.

**Economic:** All three German luxury carmakers — BMW, Mercedes-Benz, and Audi — have lost ground in the once-lucrative Chinese market, where German carmakers have increasingly faced stiff competition from rising Chinese rivals, and seen sales in Germany suffer amid an economic slowdown. The macro context is therefore one of simultaneous demand compression in two of Mercedes's three most important markets (China and Germany), partially offset by strength in North America — itself now threatened by tariffs.

**Social/Demographic:** The luxury car market size was valued at USD 593.8 billion in 2025 and is expected to grow at a CAGR of 6.3% between 2026 and 2035, driven by the increasing consumer preference for premium comfort and features. This is the structural tailwind that makes the luxury segment attractive over the long run: rising global wealth supports demand for premium goods. However, the social shift among younger Chinese consumers — away from European heritage brands toward domestic technology-forward brands — represents a countervailing demographic threat in the world's largest auto market.

**Technological:** The technology dimension is the most strategically consequential. EV battery costs are falling, software-defined vehicles are becoming the new competitive frontier, and Chinese manufacturers are deploying AI-driven features at scale. MB.OS is designed and developed in-house to retain full control over the customer relationship, ensure data privacy, and to leverage the unique integration of all car functions, based on a purpose-built chip-to-cloud architecture. This in-house OS development represents Mercedes's attempt to close the software capability gap — but it is a multi-year investment with uncertain returns.

**Environmental:** A core element of Mercedes's sustainable business strategy is Ambition 2039, with which the company set the course in 2019 for a holistic approach to climate protection, with the ambition to make its entire fleet of new vehicles net carbon-neutral. Regulatory pressure from EU CO₂ fleet targets creates a structural obligation to accelerate electrification regardless of short-term demand signals — a tension at the heart of Mercedes's current strategic dilemma.

**Legal:** EU fleet emission regulations impose binding CO₂ reduction targets, creating a legal imperative for EV adoption that cannot be indefinitely deferred. Non-compliance carries substantial financial penalties, adding a regulatory cost dimension to the EV transition that is separate from market demand.

**PESTEL Synthesis:** The PESTEL analysis reveals that Mercedes faces *aligned headwinds across multiple macro dimensions simultaneously*. Political (tariffs), economic (China slowdown), technological (Chinese EV competition), and environmental (regulatory mandates) pressures all point in the same direction: the existing business model — high-margin ICE vehicles sold predominantly in China and the US — is structurally under pressure. This is the macro context that gives urgency to the strategic choices analysed below.

### 4.2 Porter's Five Forces: Industry Structure Analysis

Porter's Five Forces framework analyses the structural attractiveness of the luxury automotive industry and identifies where competitive pressure is most acute for Mercedes-Benz.

**Threat of New Entrants: Moderate–High (and Rising)**

Historically, the luxury automotive industry was protected by enormous capital requirements, brand heritage, and dealer network complexity — barriers that kept new entrants at bay. These barriers remain significant for traditional ICE vehicles. However, the shift to EVs has partially reset them: battery-electric vehicles have fewer mechanical components, reducing manufacturing complexity, and software-defined features can be developed outside the traditional automotive supply chain. This is precisely how BYD and Xiaomi have entered the premium segment in China without decades of automotive heritage. The threat of new entrants is therefore *rising* specifically in the EV segment, even as it remains moderate for ICE luxury vehicles.

**Bargaining Power of Suppliers: Moderate–High**

The shift to EVs has increased supplier power in critical areas. Battery cells, rare earth materials for electric motors, and semiconductor chips are concentrated among a small number of suppliers with limited substitutability. Mercedes-Benz Group AG's extensive global presence, with operations in over 100 countries and more than 30 production sites, supports robust sales volumes, with approximately 2.2 million vehicles sold globally in 2024. This scale provides some countervailing buyer power, but the concentration of battery supply chains in Asia — particularly China — represents a structural vulnerability. Mercedes's decision to develop its own MMA platform and MB.OS operating system is partly a response to this: by controlling more of the technology stack in-house, it reduces dependence on external suppliers for value-creating components.

**Bargaining Power of Buyers: Moderate (but Shifting)**

Luxury car buyers have traditionally exhibited moderate bargaining power: they are numerous enough that no individual buyer has leverage, and brand loyalty dampens switching. However, several forces are shifting this balance. The proliferation of EV options — including competitively priced alternatives from Tesla, BYD, and Xiaomi — has increased the credible outside options available to buyers, particularly in China. One of the notable weaknesses is Mercedes-Benz's high pricing strategy, which can limit its accessibility to the masses and potentially hinder market share growth. More critically, the price cuts Mercedes has been forced to make on EV models demonstrate that buyer power is increasing: Mercedes has slashed prices on the vehicles, with the 2026 EQS SUV more than $15,000 cheaper than the 2025 model. Price concessions of this magnitude signal that buyers now have credible alternatives — the defining condition for elevated bargaining power.

**Threat of Substitutes: Low–Moderate**

For the ultra-luxury segment (Maybach, AMG, G-Class), substitutes are limited — no other product category replicates the social signalling, performance, and exclusivity of a flagship Mercedes. For the core and entry luxury segments, however, substitutes are more credible: premium public transport in dense urban markets, ride-hailing services, and — increasingly — premium Chinese EVs that offer comparable technology at lower prices. The threat of substitutes is therefore segment-dependent, which has strategic implications for where Mercedes should concentrate its resources (addressed in the recommendations).

**Competitive Rivalry: High**

BMW retains the crown as the best-selling premium brand in 2024. Mercedes passenger car division sales fell 3% to 1,983,400 units. BMW's volume leadership, combined with a stronger EV product pipeline and a more diversified geographic exposure, means Mercedes faces intensified rivalry from its closest traditional peer. Beyond BMW and Audi, the emergence of Chinese luxury EV brands has added a new competitive dimension that did not exist five years ago. Rivalry is therefore high and intensifying.

**Five Forces Synthesis:** The industry structure analysis reveals that the luxury automotive sector is becoming *structurally less attractive* — at least for the mid-range luxury segments where Mercedes has historically derived volume. Supplier power is rising (batteries, chips), buyer power is increasing (EV alternatives), rivalry is intensifying (BMW, Chinese OEMs), and new entrant threats are materialising in the EV segment. The strategic implication is clear: Mercedes must migrate toward the segment where Five Forces are most favourable — ultra-luxury — where brand moats remain deep, buyer alternatives are scarce, and new entrants face the highest barriers. This conclusion, derived from industry structure analysis, directly supports the top-end luxury strategic pillar examined below.

### 4.3 Resource-Based View (RBV): Internal Capability Analysis

The RBV framework (Barney, 1991) holds that sustainable competitive advantage derives from resources and capabilities that are *valuable, rare, inimitable, and non-substitutable* (VRIN). Applying this lens to Mercedes identifies both the sources of its durable competitive position and the capability gaps that explain its current difficulties.

**VRIN Resources — Where Mercedes Has Advantage:**

*Brand Heritage and Reputation:* Mercedes-Benz boasts a rich history of more than a century, rooted in technological innovation and luxury. The brand's visual identity, symbolised by the iconic three-pointed star, is synonymous with high-quality engineering and premium vehicles. This heritage is genuinely VRIN: it cannot be purchased, replicated quickly, or substituted. It is the foundation of Mercedes's pricing power and the reason the ultra-luxury sub-brands (Maybach, AMG, G-Class) command significant premiums. The strategic implication is that brand dilution through aggressive discounting on EV models — which Mercedes has been forced into — is deeply harmful precisely because it erodes this non-substitutable resource.

*Global Manufacturing and Distribution Network:* Mercedes-Benz Group AG's extensive global presence, with operations in over 100 countries and more than 30 production sites, supports robust sales volumes. This network is valuable and difficult to replicate quickly, providing geographic diversification and production flexibility that new entrants lack.

*Engineering Capability:* The development of the MMA platform and MB.OS operating system demonstrates that Mercedes retains significant in-house engineering capability. Built on the brand's new MMA architecture, the CLA can be produced as a full EV or a mild hybrid with near-identical hardware and design — letting Mercedes flex production based on real-world demand. This platform flexibility is a genuinely valuable capability: it allows Mercedes to hedge between EV and hybrid demand without committing to a single technology trajectory — directly addressing the demand uncertainty that has punished single-technology bets.

**Capability Gaps — Where Mercedes Lacks VRIN Resources:**

*Software and Digital Experience:* The EQ range's underperformance reflects a software capability gap. Chinese EV buyers, in particular, now expect seamless digital integration, over-the-air updates, and AI-powered features as standard. Mercedes's in-house MB.OS development addresses this gap, but it is a lagging investment. MB.OS is designed and developed in-house to retain full control over the customer relationship, ensure data privacy, and to leverage the unique integration of all car functions. Until MB.OS is deployed at scale — beginning with the new MMA-platform CLA — this remains a capability gap relative to Tesla and leading Chinese OEMs.

*Cost Structure:* Mercedes's cost base was calibrated for higher volumes and richer pricing. The 30.8% EBIT decline in 2024 despite only a 4.5% revenue decline reveals significant operating leverage — meaning fixed costs are too high relative to the new volume reality. This is not a VRIN resource; it is a structural liability.

**RBV Synthesis:** The RBV analysis identifies a strategic asymmetry: Mercedes's most durable competitive advantages (brand, heritage, engineering) are concentrated in the ultra-luxury and top-end segments, while its capability gaps (software, cost structure) are most exposed in the volume-dependent core and entry luxury segments. This asymmetry provides a clear strategic direction: double down on the segments where VRIN resources are strongest, and use the cost-restructuring programme to right-size the cost base for a smaller, more profitable volume base.

### 4.4 Generic Strategy and the Stuck-in-the-Middle Risk

Porter's generic strategy framework (1980) posits that firms must choose between cost leadership, differentiation, or focus — and that firms that fail to make a clear choice risk being "stuck in the middle," achieving neither the scale economies of cost leaders nor the premium pricing of differentiators.

Mercedes's strategic history has been one of differentiation: the brand justifies premium prices through engineering quality, heritage, and status signalling. However, the expansion into entry luxury (A-Class, B-Class, CLA) over the past decade represented a partial move toward volume — a strategy that diluted brand exclusivity without achieving the cost economics of mass-market producers. The current strategic situation reflects the consequences: the entry-luxury models generate lower margins, are most exposed to Chinese competition, and have contributed to the brand dilution that weakens Mercedes's position even in core luxury segments.

Mercedes-Benz has set out long-term ambitions as the world's most valuable luxury car brand, with the share of Top-End vehicle sales projected to rise by around 60% by 2026, and a product offensive from Maybach, AMG, and G-Class with dedicated EV characteristics and greater individualisation. This strategic commitment to top-end luxury represents a deliberate move *away* from the stuck-in-the-middle position and toward focused differentiation — the generic strategy most consistent with Mercedes's VRIN resources. The strategic logic is sound: by concentrating on the segments where its brand moat is deepest, Mercedes can restore pricing power, reduce volume sensitivity, and defend against Chinese competition, which is most competitive in the value-for-money space rather than the ultra-luxury space.

However, the generic strategy analysis also reveals a tension: the cost-cutting programme required to restore profitability must not undermine the quality investments (engineering, materials, craftsmanship) that sustain differentiation. Cutting costs in ways that are visible to customers — reduced material quality, fewer standard features, slower model refreshes — would erode the very resource that justifies the premium price. This tension between cost discipline and differentiation investment is the central strategic challenge in executing the "Next Level Performance" plan.

### 4.5 The Value Chain: Identifying Where Value Is Created and Destroyed

Porter's value chain framework (1985) disaggregates the firm's activities into primary activities (inbound logistics, operations, outbound logistics, marketing and sales, service) and support activities (infrastructure, HR, technology development, procurement), identifying where value is created and where costs are incurred.

**Primary Activities:**

*Operations:* Mercedes's manufacturing operations are currently over-scaled for the reduced volume reality. Mercedes has proposed up to 20,000 job cuts worldwide, potentially rising to 33,000 — 20% of its global workforce — if market conditions worsen. These cuts include slashing profit-sharing bonuses, halving salary increases, and outsourcing non-core functions. The value chain implication is that the operations activity is generating cost without commensurate value at current volumes — a structural misalignment that the "Next Level Performance" plan is designed to address.

*Marketing and Sales:* The decision to pause US deliveries of EQ models and slash EQS prices represents a value chain failure in the marketing and sales activity: the product was positioned and priced for a demand level that did not materialise, requiring destructive repricing. Mercedes-Benz reported it will pause US deliveries of its EQ EV lineup and reduce prices on several models due to slowing demand and excess inventory at dealerships. This signals a misalignment between the marketing activity (which positioned EQ as a premium EV competitor) and the market reality (where Tesla and Chinese OEMs had established stronger positions).

*Service and After-Sales:* The development of MB.OS creates a new value chain activity — software services and over-the-air updates — that generates recurring revenue streams beyond the initial vehicle sale. This is a value chain *extension* that, if executed well, can create a new source of competitive advantage and reduce dependence on vehicle volume for profitability.

**Support Activities:**

*Technology Development:* Research and Development costs, focused on investments into future architectures and technologies, particularly for MB.OS, represent the most strategically significant support activity. The MMA platform and MB.OS are the technology investments that will determine whether Mercedes can close its software capability gap and build a defensible position in the EV era.

*Procurement:* The localisation strategy — manufacturing closer to end markets — directly addresses procurement and supply chain vulnerabilities. Mercedes-Benz has committed over USD 10 billion to production, infrastructure, R&D, design, and the dealer network, including USD 7+ billion in its Tuscaloosa, Alabama site. By producing vehicles locally in the US, Mercedes reduces its exposure to import tariffs — a direct value chain response to the political macro-environment identified in the PESTEL analysis.

**Value Chain Synthesis:** The value chain analysis reveals that value destruction is occurring primarily in two places: operations (over-scaled manufacturing) and marketing/sales (EV mispricing and inventory build-up). Value creation is concentrated in technology development (MMA, MB.OS) and brand/marketing for top-end luxury. The strategic recommendation that follows from this analysis is to *redirect investment from value-destroying activities to value-creating ones* — specifically, from volume-dependent manufacturing to technology and brand.

## 5. Strategic Synthesis: The Compound Problem and Its Logic

The four frameworks converge on a consistent diagnosis. The PESTEL analysis establishes that macro forces are permanently restructuring the competitive environment — there is no return to the pre-2022 status quo of high-volume, high-margin ICE sales in China. The Five Forces analysis shows that industry structure is deteriorating in volume luxury segments while remaining attractive in ultra-luxury. The RBV analysis identifies that Mercedes's durable competitive advantages are concentrated in brand, heritage, and engineering — all of which are most powerful in the ultra-luxury segment. The value chain analysis shows that value destruction is occurring in operations and EV sales, while value creation is concentrated in technology and top-end brand activities.

The compound conclusion is this: **Mercedes's current financial distress is the result of a strategic position that is misaligned with both its internal capabilities and the external competitive environment.** The company built a volume base in China and entry luxury that was calibrated for a world of unchallenged German premium dominance. That world no longer exists. The strategic response is not to defend the old position but to execute a deliberate migration toward the position where Mercedes's VRIN resources are most powerful: ultra-luxury, technology-led differentiation, with a right-sized cost base.

This is the logic behind the "Next Level Performance" plan — but the plan as currently articulated has execution risks that the frameworks help identify.

**Corollary:** The entry luxury segment (A-Class, B-Class) should be reviewed for strategic exit or significant repositioning. These models generate the lowest margins, are most exposed to Chinese competition, and dilute the brand positioning that sustains premium pricing. This pipeline should be treated as the primary vehicle for EV market re-entry — not the legacy EQ models, which have been permanently price-compromised.

**Warrant:** The value chain analysis shows that value destruction is concentrated in over-scaled operations. The RBV analysis shows that Mercedes's brand heritage is still valued by Chinese ultra-luxury buyers — but not by value-conscious buyers who can now access comparable technology from domestic brands at lower prices.

### Recommendation 6: Institutionalise Scenario Planning to Manage Strategic Uncertainty

**Warrant:** The PESTEL analysis reveals that Mercedes is operating in an environment of high macro-uncertainty — tariff regimes can change with election cycles, EV demand trajectories are uncertain, and Chinese competitive dynamics are evolving rapidly. The company's EV strategy reversals (from "electric-only by 2030" to a more flexible approach) suggest that its planning processes have been insufficiently robust to macro-uncertainty. The MMA platform's flexibility (EV and hybrid from the same architecture) is a product-level response to this uncertainty; the same logic should be institutionalised at the strategic planning level.

**Action:** Mercedes should adopt formal scenario planning — developing at least three distinct macro scenarios (accelerated EV transition, prolonged ICE resilience, geopolitical fragmentation) and testing its strategic commitments against each. Investments that are robust across all three scenarios (brand investment, MMA platform flexibility, US manufacturing localisation) should be prioritised. Investments that are only valuable in a single scenario (e.g., a fully electric-only product line in a world of uncertain EV demand) should be structured as options rather than commitments.

## 8. Conclusion

Mercedes-Benz's strategic situation is severe but not terminal. The company possesses genuine VRIN resources — brand heritage, engineering capability, and a global network — that remain the foundation of a defensible competitive position. The crisis is not one of irreversible decline but of strategic misalignment: a business model built for a world of unchallenged German premium dominance in China and a slow ICE-to-EV transition. Both assumptions have been invalidated.

The path forward requires a coherent strategic migration: from volume-dependent luxury toward focused ultra-luxury differentiation; from adapted-platform EVs toward purpose-built MMA architecture; from a globally uniform cost structure toward a localised manufacturing footprint that hedges geopolitical risk; and from a planning process that bets on single scenarios toward one that builds robustness across multiple futures.
