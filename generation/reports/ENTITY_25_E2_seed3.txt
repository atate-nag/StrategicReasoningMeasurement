# MTN Group in Africa: Navigating the Strategic Trilemma of Growth, Currency Risk, and Platform Transformation

## A Strategic Case Study

---

## 1. Introduction and Case Framing

This case study examines MTN Group Limited — Africa's largest mobile network operator by subscribers — as it confronts a strategic trilemma: how to sustain continental growth, absorb severe macroeconomic shocks concentrated in its most important market (Nigeria), and successfully execute a platform-diversification pivot from pure-play connectivity into fintech and digital infrastructure. The case is analytically useful because it sits at the intersection of industry structure analysis, resource-based competitive advantage, and strategic growth choice — the core frameworks of competitive strategy.

The central strategic question is: **Given that MTN's connectivity revenues are being eroded by currency devaluation and regulatory inertia, while new platform businesses (MoMo fintech; Bayobab infrastructure) offer growth but require capital and organisational transformation, which strategic posture should MTN adopt to sustain competitive advantage across its 16-market African footprint?**

The analysis proceeds in four stages: (i) macro-environmental scan via PESTLE; (ii) industry structure analysis via Porter's Five Forces; (iii) internal capability audit via the VRIO framework; (iv) strategic growth options mapped via the Ansoff Matrix. These frameworks are applied sequentially and linked — each output feeds into the next — before converging on a set of prioritised recommendations.

---

## 2. Company and Market Background

MTN Group has 291 million customers across 16 markets and is driven by the intent to "lead digital solutions for Africa's progress." This scale makes it the continent's dominant mobile operator, but scale alone does not insulate it from structural pressures.

Africa's telecommunications industry is changing rapidly, with the market worth approximately $63.17 billion in 2024 and projected to reach more than $82.34 billion by 2029, growing at a rate of 5.44% annually. This headline growth figure, however, conceals enormous heterogeneity: some markets are currency-stable and data-hungry; others are geopolitically volatile and tariff-frozen.

In 2024, mobile technologies and services generated 7.7% of Africa's GDP, amounting to $220 billion in economic value, and the mobile industry's economic contribution is projected to reach $270 billion by 2030, driven by the expansion of digital technologies including 4G, 5G and AI. This macro tailwind is real — but it does not automatically translate into profitability for any single operator.

## 3. PESTLE Analysis: The Macro-Environment

A PESTLE analysis identifies the external forces shaping the strategic landscape before any firm-level or industry-level analysis begins. The purpose is to establish *which* environmental factors are most consequential, so that subsequent frameworks can be applied to the conditions that actually exist.

### 3.1 Political

African political environments vary sharply across MTN's 16 markets, and political instability directly impairs operational performance. In Sudan, the ongoing conflict in the country negatively impacted MTN's operational and financial performance. This is not merely an anecdote: it demonstrates that a geographically diversified portfolio does not automatically hedge political risk if conflict markets are large enough to drag group-level results.

Decisions on spectrum bands, license duration, and allocation models can either accelerate or slow the rollout of high-speed mobile networks, which have become critical for sectors such as healthcare, agriculture, fintech, and education. Governments that treat spectrum as a revenue tool rather than an infrastructure enabler impose an implicit tax on operators and, through them, on consumers.

### 3.2 Economic

The economic dimension is the most immediately damaging force on MTN's financials. MTN Nigeria Communications Plc recorded a N400.4 billion loss after tax in the full year of 2024 despite a 35.9% growth in revenue to N3.3 trillion, with financial performance continuing to be impacted by record-high inflation and the naira's devaluation. This paradox — rising revenues, catastrophic losses — is the defining economic pathology of the case. It arises because MTN's capital and debt obligations are denominated in hard currency (USD), while its revenues are earned in naira. MTN Nigeria recorded ₦925.4 billion in foreign exchange losses in 2024, rising from ₦740 billion in 2023.

The group-level consequence was severe. In the year to 31 December 2024, service revenue of R178 billion was down by some 15% in reported terms, and reported EBITDA (before once-off items) of R60 billion was a third lower than it was in 2023, with adjusted headline earnings per share impacted mainly by the sharp devaluation in the naira.

**Analytical inference:** The economic environment reveals that MTN's single-market concentration risk is structurally dangerous. Nigeria alone represented 22.9% of group service revenue in 2024 (per the group results), meaning that one country's macroeconomic dysfunction can overwhelm group-level performance regardless of operational excellence elsewhere. This inference directly motivates the diversification recommendations in Section 7.

### 3.3 Social

While 416 million people are now using mobile internet in Africa, almost 75% of the population remain unconnected, with 960 million people in Africa — 64% of the population — not using mobile internet despite living in an area where coverage is available. This usage gap is simultaneously a social challenge and a commercial opportunity: the "unconnected" represent MTN's largest available market.

A substantial usage gap of 60% remains, affecting those who live within coverage areas but face barriers to get online, such as unaffordable devices and limited digital skills. The implication is that coverage expansion alone is insufficient; demand-side barriers (device cost, digital literacy) require complementary interventions if subscriber growth is to materialise.

### 3.4 Technological

Spectrum allocation remains a critical challenge: as of mid-2025, fewer than 30% of African countries — just 16 — had assigned 5G spectrum, a stark contrast to the over 90% in Europe. This creates a paradox: operators cannot build 5G networks without spectrum, but governments are slow to allocate it. The technological opportunity of 5G is therefore politically gated.

AI-driven customer support, network optimisation, and fraud detection can improve operational performance, and AI is expected to contribute up to $1.5 trillion to Africa's economy by 2030. For MTN, AI represents both a cost-reduction lever (network optimisation) and a revenue-generation tool (personalised digital services).

### 3.5 Legal/Regulatory

Despite soaring inflation and the devaluation of the Naira, the Nigerian telecom sector had kept tariffs unchanged for 11 years. This regulatory freeze was a compounding injury: costs rose with inflation and currency devaluation, but prices were locked. The eventual resolution — the 50% tariff hike approved by the Nigerian Communications Commission (NCC) on January 24, 2025 — was necessary but belated, and its partial reversal of losses illustrates how regulatory risk is asymmetric: regulators can freeze prices quickly but release them slowly.

African nations are becoming more assertive about data sovereignty, and compliance with data protection laws, spectrum allocation policies, and licensing requirements is essential for ensuring operational efficiency and regulatory compliance.

### 3.6 Environmental

Infrastructure expansion into rural Africa raises environmental considerations around energy consumption and e-waste. These are noted here as emerging constraints that will shape the cost of network rollout, particularly for off-grid tower sites.

**PESTLE Summary:** The dominant macro-forces are: (i) currency/macroeconomic volatility (economic); (ii) regulatory price-setting power (legal); (iii) demand-side barriers to digital adoption (social); (iv) slow spectrum allocation (technological); and (v) geopolitical instability in sub-markets (political). These forces define the *threat environment* into which Porter's Five Forces analysis must be embedded.

## 4. Porter's Five Forces: Industry Structure Analysis

Porter (1980) argues that the profitability of an industry is determined by five structural forces: threat of new entrants, bargaining power of buyers, bargaining power of suppliers, threat of substitutes, and competitive rivalry. Five competitive forces act on an industry: (1) threat of new entrants, (2) intensity of rivalry among existing firms, (3) threat of substitute products or services, (4) bargaining power of buyers, and (5) bargaining power of suppliers — and looking at industry structure provides a way to consider how value is created and divided among existing and potential participants. The purpose here is not merely to describe each force but to explain *why* the force has the magnitude it does and *what strategic implication* follows.

### 4.1 Threat of New Entrants — *Moderate-Low*

Mobile telecommunications requires spectrum licences, tower infrastructure, and substantial capital expenditure. The African digital infrastructure boom presents a $100 billion investment gap, with 80% of required funding needed for core infrastructure such as 4G/5G towers and fibre networks. This capital intensity deters pure-play new entrants. However, the threat comes not from traditional telcos but from **over-the-top (OTT) players** (WhatsApp, Meta) that use existing networks to deliver communication services, and from **fintech disruptors** that bypass the operator layer entirely.

**Inference:** The threat of entry is low for traditional connectivity but moderate-to-high in the fintech and digital services adjacencies that MTN is trying to enter. This means MTN's diversification strategy faces competitive pressure from well-capitalised technology firms even as its core business is protected.

### 4.2 Bargaining Power of Buyers — *Low-to-Moderate*

Individual consumers have limited switching power in markets with two dominant operators, but collective consumer pressure — mediated through regulators — can force price controls. The 11-year tariff freeze in Nigeria is an example of regulatory amplification of buyer power. Following the tariff hike, Nigeria's active mobile subscriptions dropped by 942,000 in June 2025, with MTN Nigeria leading the decline by losing over 1 million subscribers in that month. This demonstrates that buyer power, once exercised through the exit option, is real even in an oligopoly.

**Inference:** Buyer power is structurally low (few alternatives) but regulatorily amplified. MTN must manage the regulatory relationship as a proxy for managing buyer power — the regulator is the effective buyer representative in price negotiations.

### 4.3 Bargaining Power of Suppliers — *Moderate-High*

MTN's key suppliers are: (i) equipment vendors (Ericsson, Huawei, Nokia) who supply network infrastructure; (ii) tower companies; and (iii) currency markets (for USD-denominated debt and equipment). Further financial risks result from regulatory complexities, including high taxation and spectrum licensing fees, as well as fluctuating foreign exchange rates. The foreign exchange market is an involuntary supplier with effectively infinite bargaining power — MTN cannot negotiate the exchange rate.

**Inference:** Supplier power is high specifically in the FX dimension, and this is the channel through which macroeconomic risk (identified in PESTLE) enters the income statement. Reducing this exposure requires either local-currency debt financing or natural hedging through local-currency revenue growth — both of which feature in the recommendations.

### 4.4 Threat of Substitutes — *Moderate and Rising*

With approximately 835 million registered mobile money accounts in SSA in 2023, telecom operators are leveraging their infrastructure to offer financial services, increasing financial inclusion and creating new revenue streams. This is a double-edged dynamic: mobile money is both a new revenue stream for MTN and a potential substitute for voice/SMS revenue if standalone fintech platforms (M-Pesa, Wave, Moniepoint) erode the telco's share of financial services.

**Inference:** Substitution is most acute in the financial services layer. MTN's strategic response — building MoMo as a platform — is the correct countermove, but it requires MoMo to achieve network effects before standalone fintechs capture the market. Speed of execution is therefore a source of competitive advantage, not merely a management preference.

### 4.5 Competitive Rivalry — *High*

MTN led Nigeria's telecom market with 84.6 million subscribers representing a 51.4% market share and approximately ₦3.94 trillion ($2.63 billion) in revenue, while Airtel followed with 56.6 million subscribers (34.4%) and ₦2.64 trillion ($1.76 billion). This duopoly structure intensifies rivalry: each operator has enough scale to retaliate against competitive moves, creating a dynamic of mutual deterrence punctuated by aggressive competition for data subscribers.

Airtel and MTN are two of the largest telecommunications companies in Africa, and their rivalry is fierce — the two companies are constantly competing for market share, customers, and investment, rivalling at the peak of Africa's telecom space.

**Inference:** High rivalry compresses margins in the connectivity segment, reinforcing the strategic logic of platform diversification. However, rivalry also extends into fintech (Airtel Money vs. MoMo), meaning that diversification does not escape competition — it relocates it to a new battleground.

**Five Forces Summary:** The industry structure is moderately attractive for incumbents in connectivity (high barriers, regulated oligopoly) but deteriorating in fintech and digital services (lower barriers, technology-enabled substitution, intensifying rivalry). This structural reading directly motivates the VRIO analysis: if industry-level profitability is under pressure, the source of *above-average* returns must lie in firm-specific, hard-to-imitate resources.

## 5. VRIO Analysis: Internal Capability Audit

The Resource-Based View (RBV), articulated by Barney (1991), holds that sustained competitive advantage derives from the resources and capabilities a firm controls that are valuable, rare, imperfectly imitable, and not substitutable. The VRIO framework operationalises this by asking, for each resource: is it **V**aluable, **R**are, **I**nimitability-protected, and supported by **O**rganisational capability to exploit it?

The VRIO tool can be used to determine if resources or capabilities are valuable, rare, difficult-to-imitate, and organised to capture value, and thereby understand what type of competitive advantage they offer to a firm.

### Resource 1: Pan-African Subscriber Base and Brand

**Valuable?** Yes. MTN's MoMo mobile money platform, now serving 100 million users across 16 African nations, has emerged as a cornerstone of its digital strategy. A 291-million-subscriber base generates network effects: each additional subscriber increases the value of the network to all others, particularly for mobile money (where utility rises with the number of counterparties who can receive payments).

**Rare?** Yes. No other operator has comparable pan-African reach across 16 markets simultaneously. Airtel Africa operates in 14 markets but with a smaller subscriber base.

**Inimitable?** Moderately. Building a subscriber base of this scale took decades and required spectrum licences in each market — a process that cannot be replicated quickly. However, the brand can be damaged by service quality failures or regulatory disputes.

**Organised?** Partially. MTN's "Ambition 2025" strategy signals organisational intent to leverage this base for platform services. MTN Group's 'Ambition 2025' strategy is a blueprint for significant expansion, focusing on five key growth areas: connectivity, fintech, digital infrastructure, Ayoba, and enterprise services.

**VRIO Verdict:** Sustained competitive advantage — but only if the organisational capability to monetise the base beyond voice/SMS is developed.

### Resource 2: Bayobab Digital Infrastructure (Fibre and Subsea)

**Valuable?** Yes. Bayobab brings world-class infrastructure capability and the reach of a network serving over 300 million subscribers across 16 African markets. Bayobab now aims to position itself as a world-class, open-access, Africa-centric digital infrastructure platform, serving not only the MTN Group but also third-party clients.

**Rare?** Yes. Bayobab claims over 108,000 kilometres of fibre and intends to increase to 135,000 kilometres by 2025. No competitor has comparable owned fibre infrastructure across the continent.

**Inimitable?** High. Laying submarine cables and terrestrial fibre requires multi-year construction timelines, rights-of-way negotiations with multiple governments, and capital investment that rivals cannot quickly replicate.

**Organised?** Yes. The strategic separation of Bayobab from MTN's core telecom operations — rebranded from MTN GlobalConnect in May 2023 — demonstrates deliberate organisational design to unlock the asset's value as a third-party platform.

**VRIO Verdict:** Sustained competitive advantage. Bayobab is MTN's most strategically distinctive asset precisely because it serves both internal needs and generates external revenue from competitors.

### Resource 3: MoMo Mobile Money Platform

**Valuable?** Yes. In Ghana alone, active MoMo users increased by 16.2% to 16.5 million, with revenue from MoMo growing by 44.8%. The platform generates fee income that is less exposed to currency devaluation than infrastructure-heavy connectivity revenue.

**Rare?** Moderately. M-Pesa (Safaricom/Vodacom) is a comparable platform in East Africa. However, MoMo's 16-market footprint is broader than any single competitor.

**Inimitable?** Moderate. The platform itself can be replicated technologically, but the trust relationships, agent networks, and regulatory licences in each market are slow to build. The switch from a digital-first methodology of acquiring customers to an agent-led, "boots on the ground" strategy is better suited to MoMo's target market. This agent network is a tacit, relationship-based asset that is difficult to imitate quickly.

**Organised?** Partially. MTN Group is pushing through the final stages of separating its fintech operations from its core telecom, creating a standalone entity attractive enough for outside capital, starting with a Mastercard investment worth up to $200 million, with the restructuring covering MTN's mobile money platform MoMo, which serves 60 million active wallets. The Mastercard partnership validates the platform's value and provides capital for acceleration.

**VRIO Verdict:** Temporary-to-sustained competitive advantage, contingent on execution speed. The advantage is not yet fully locked in because standalone fintech competitors are scaling rapidly.

### Resource 4: Financial Capital and Balance Sheet

This is a **competitive disadvantage** in the current period. Basic earnings per share swung by 758 cents in 2024 to a loss of 531 cents, with adjusted headline earnings per share decreasing by 32% to 816 cents. The naira-denominated losses have weakened the group's capacity to fund strategic investments. This is not a VRIO advantage — it is a constraint that bounds the set of feasible strategic options.

**VRIO Summary Table:**

| Resource | Valuable | Rare | Inimitable | Organised | Competitive Position |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Pan-African subscriber base | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | Partial | Sustained (if monetised) |
| Bayobab fibre/subsea infrastructure | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | Sustained |
| MoMo platform + agent network | ✓ | Partial | Partial | Partial | Temporary → Sustained |
| Financial capital | ✗ | — | — | — | Competitive disadvantage |

The VRIO analysis reveals a critical strategic tension: MTN's most durable advantages (Bayobab infrastructure, subscriber base) are in assets that require capital to maintain and develop, but its capital position has been degraded by the very macroeconomic shocks identified in PESTLE. This tension defines the strategic imperative: MTN must find ways to generate returns from existing advantages quickly enough to fund the development of emerging ones.

## 6. Ansoff Matrix: Mapping Strategic Growth Options

The Ansoff Matrix, developed by Igor Ansoff (1957), provides a framework for classifying growth strategies along two dimensions: whether the market is existing or new, and whether the product/service is existing or new. By examining the four distinct quadrants — Market Penetration, Market Development, Product Development, and Diversification — decision-makers can gain valuable insights into the potential risks and rewards associated with each option, enabling them to make more informed choices and allocate resources effectively.

MTN's strategic options can be mapped as follows:

### Quadrant 1: Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets)

This is the lowest-risk strategy — deepening share in markets where MTN already operates with existing connectivity services. The tariff increase in Nigeria is an example of market penetration via ARPU (average revenue per user) growth rather than subscriber growth.

Voice revenue growth of 42.1% demonstrates that consumption volumes absorbed the pricing adjustments without significant subscriber churn, and the company's medium-term guidance acknowledges this structural pricing shift. This is an important signal: demand for connectivity is sufficiently inelastic that price increases can restore revenue without catastrophic volume loss. However, MTN Nigeria led the overall mobile subscription decline, losing over 1 million subscribers in June 2025, with Nigeria's active mobile subscriptions dropping by 942,000 in that month. The trade-off between ARPU and subscriber volume is real and must be managed carefully.

**Risk assessment:** Low strategic risk but limited upside. Market penetration cannot address the structural currency exposure problem.

### Quadrant 2: Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets)

MTN already operates in 16 markets, limiting geographic expansion options. However, the GSMA predicts that the number of unique mobile subscribers in sub-Saharan Africa will grow to 751 million by 2030, with a compound annual growth rate of 4.5% between 2023 and 2030. This growth will come partly from new geographic markets (e.g., expansion into currently unserved countries) and partly from new customer segments within existing markets (rural populations, the digitally excluded).

A significant coverage gap remains, with 13% of the population still unreached, and a 60% usage gap affecting those who live within coverage areas but face barriers to get online, such as unaffordable devices and limited digital skills. Addressing this usage gap within existing markets is a form of market development — it brings new customers into the connectivity ecosystem.

**Risk assessment:** Moderate risk. Rural expansion requires capital expenditure with long payback periods, but it deepens the subscriber base that underpins MoMo's network effects.

### Quadrant 3: Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets)

This is where MTN's "Ambition 2025" strategy is most explicitly focused. MTN's strategy streamlines execution into three principal platforms: Connectivity (short-term), Fintech (medium-term), and Digital Infrastructure (long-term), enhancing the ability to leverage the strengths of established businesses while rapidly developing and scaling emerging ones.

MoMo, Bayobab's third-party infrastructure services, enterprise cloud services, and the Ayoba messaging super-app all represent new products offered to MTN's existing customer base. The Mastercard partnership will empower MoMo merchants to seamlessly accept card payments, enhancing the platform's instant cross-border money remittance services and capabilities. This expands MoMo from a peer-to-peer transfer tool into a merchant payments and remittance platform — a significant product extension.

**Risk assessment:** Moderate-high risk (new product development requires organisational capability building) but high strategic importance because it reduces dependence on currency-exposed connectivity revenues.

### Quadrant 4: Diversification (New Products, New Markets)

Bayobab's strategy of serving third-party operators — including competitors — represents related diversification. Bayobab now aims to position itself as a world-class, open-access, Africa-centric digital infrastructure platform, serving not only the MTN Group but also third-party clients. By selling infrastructure capacity to other operators, MTN generates hard-currency revenues from a USD-denominated asset base, partially hedging its naira exposure.

**Risk assessment:** Moderate (related diversification leverages existing assets) with significant upside in terms of currency diversification.

**Ansoff Summary:** MTN should pursue a *portfolio of strategies* weighted towards Quadrant 3 (product development via MoMo and enterprise services) and Quadrant 4 (Bayobab third-party diversification), while using Quadrant 1 (tariff normalisation) to stabilise near-term cash flows. Quadrant 2 (rural market development) is strategically important for long-run network effects but must be capital-efficient.

## 7. Synthesis: The Strategic Coherence Chain

The four frameworks, applied sequentially, produce a coherent analytical chain:

1. **PESTLE** establishes that currency devaluation (economic), regulatory price-freezes (legal), and slow spectrum allocation (technological) are the dominant environmental threats, while the unconnected population (social) and AI/5G (technological) are the dominant opportunities.

2. **Porter's Five Forces** shows that the connectivity segment is structurally oligopolistic (protecting incumbents) but that fintech substitution and OTT rivalry are eroding the value of that protection. The industry-level conclusion is that connectivity alone will not sustain above-average returns.

3. **VRIO** identifies that MTN's durable advantages lie in its pan-African subscriber base, Bayobab infrastructure, and (conditionally) MoMo — not in its financial capital, which has been degraded. The firm-level conclusion is that MTN must monetise its non-financial resources to rebuild financial resilience.

4. **Ansoff** maps the growth options: product development (MoMo, enterprise) and related diversification (Bayobab third-party) are the highest-value strategic directions, because they leverage VRIO-identified advantages to address the structural weaknesses identified by Porter and the threats identified by PESTLE.

The chain is coherent: each framework's output provides the input for the next, and all four converge on the same strategic direction — accelerate platform diversification while stabilising the core connectivity business.

## 8. Recommendations and Action Plan

The following recommendations are organised by time horizon and directly traceable to the analytical chain above.

---

### Recommendation 1: Accelerate Natural FX Hedging Through Local-Currency Revenue Diversification *(Immediate: 0–12 months)*

**The problem (from PESTLE + Porter):** MTN Nigeria's losses skyrocketed by 192% compared to the previous year, illustrating a critical challenge: substantial revenue growth cannot offset the devastating impact of currency devaluation on foreign-currency obligations. The core issue is a currency mismatch: hard-currency debt, local-currency revenues.

**The solution (from VRIO + Ansoff):** MTN should aggressively refinance USD-denominated debt into local-currency instruments wherever possible, and accelerate the growth of MoMo, which generates fee income in local currencies but with lower capital intensity than network infrastructure. In 2024, MTN and Airtel Africa took decisive steps to slash their foreign debt, repaying a combined $1.2 billion to alleviate the pressures of foreign exchange fluctuations. This deleveraging should be continued and deepened.

**Why this is warranted:** The evidence shows that revenue growth (35.9% in Nigeria) is being entirely consumed by FX losses (₦925.4 billion). No operational improvement can overcome a structural balance sheet mismatch of this magnitude. Debt restructuring is therefore a prerequisite for all other strategic investments, not an alternative to them.

---

### Recommendation 2: Leverage the NCC Tariff Increase to Fund Network Reinvestment, Not Shareholder Returns *(Immediate: 0–18 months)*

**The evidence:** The Nigerian telecom sector had kept tariffs unchanged for 11 years despite soaring inflation and naira devaluation. The NCC's approval of the 50% tariff hike is seen as necessary to ensure the sustainability of the telecom sector, with the industry unable to continue at the existing tariff levels.

**The action:** The incremental revenue from the tariff increase should be ring-fenced for network quality investment and 4G densification, not extracted as dividends. This serves two purposes: (i) it reduces subscriber churn by improving service quality, addressing the 39% of customers who rated MTN service as "Excellent" or "Very Good" while many rated it "Fair" or "Poor," indicating dissatisfaction post-tariff hike; (ii) it builds the network quality that is a prerequisite for MoMo adoption (mobile money requires reliable connectivity).

**Why this is warranted:** The tariff increase is a one-time regulatory relief. If it is not converted into service quality improvement, subscriber churn will accelerate, and the relief will be temporary. The Five Forces analysis shows buyer power is exercised through exit — retaining subscribers requires delivering value commensurate with the new price.

---

### Recommendation 3: Scale MoMo as a Platform Business with Urgency, Leveraging the Mastercard Partnership *(Medium-term: 12–36 months)*

**The evidence:** MTN Group is pushing through the final stages of separating its fintech operations from its core telecom, creating a standalone entity attractive enough for outside capital, starting with a Mastercard investment worth up to $200 million, covering MTN's mobile money platform MoMo, which serves 60 million active wallets. In Sub-Saharan Africa, mobile money transaction volumes increased by 20% in 2024, while transaction values grew by 16%.

**The action:** MTN should: (a) complete the organisational separation of MoMo fintech from the connectivity business to unlock external capital and management focus; (b) deploy the Mastercard partnership to enable merchant payments and cross-border remittances, which generate higher margins than peer-to-peer transfers; (c) shift to the agent-led acquisition model identified as more effective for the target market.

**Why this is warranted:** The VRIO analysis identifies MoMo as a *temporary* competitive advantage that can become *sustained* only through execution speed. The fintech market in Africa is not a slow-moving oligopoly — it is a contested space where new entrants are introducing alternative models to cater to Africa's growing demand for affordable digital financial services, with FinTech companies experimenting with lower fees and innovative service offerings. If MTN delays, the window for establishing MoMo as the dominant platform will close.

---

### Recommendation 4: Expand Bayobab's Third-Party Revenue Model to Generate Hard-Currency Infrastructure Income *(Medium-term: 12–36 months)*

**The evidence:** Bayobab claims over 108,000 kilometres of fibre and intends to increase to 135,000 kilometres by 2025, with the explicit goal of serving third-party operators. A study analysing 107 tower-sharing agreements across 28 low-income countries found a USD 1.60 drop in the mobile price index from a baseline of USD 3.16, and a USD 1.00 reduction in the average cost of one gigabyte of data from USD 3. Infrastructure sharing benefits the entire ecosystem, not just the sharing parties.

**The action:** MTN should actively market Bayobab's fibre and subsea capacity to third-party operators (including Airtel, Orange, and smaller national operators), pricing in USD where contractually possible. This converts a cost-centre asset into a revenue-generating platform and provides natural hard-currency income that offsets naira-denominated losses.

**Why this is warranted:** The Ansoff analysis identifies this as *related diversification* — it leverages an existing VRIO-identified asset (Bayobab infrastructure) to generate revenues from new customers (third-party operators) in a new business model (open-access infrastructure). The risk is moderate because the asset already exists; the incremental investment is in sales capability and contract management, not in physical infrastructure.

---

### Recommendation 5: Deepen the MTN-Airtel Infrastructure Sharing Agreement and Extend to Additional Markets *(Medium-term: 12–36 months)*

**The evidence:** MTN Group and Airtel Africa have entered into agreements to share network infrastructure in Uganda and Nigeria, targeting improved network cost efficiencies, expanded coverage and the provision of enhanced mobile services to millions of customers.

**The action:** MTN should extend this model to additional high-cost, low-density markets across its 16-market footprint — particularly in Francophone West Africa and Central Africa, where tower economics are most challenging. Infrastructure remains a challenge for African telcos, with profitability limited by high costs and low levels of infrastructure sharing, unlike global counterparts that have embraced cost-sharing agreements to improve efficiency and reduce costs.

**Why this is warranted:** The Five Forces analysis shows that supplier power (particularly in tower leasing and equipment) is a margin-compressing force. Infrastructure sharing directly reduces this exposure by spreading fixed costs across multiple operators. Critically, sharing with Airtel does not surrender competitive advantage in connectivity (which derives from brand, service quality, and MoMo ecosystem) — it merely reduces the cost of delivering that connectivity.

---

### Recommendation 6: Address the Digital Divide as a Strategic Investment, Not Corporate Social Responsibility *(Long-term: 36+ months)*

**The evidence:** A significant coverage gap remains, with 13% of the population still unreached, and a 60% usage gap affecting those who live within coverage areas but face barriers to get online, such as unaffordable devices and limited digital skills. Unlike mature markets weighed down by legacy infrastructure, African operators hold a strategic advantage: a mobile-first customer base, a rising digital economy, and the opportunity to lead in adjacent services like fintech, eHealth, and EdTech.

**The action:** MTN should develop affordable device financing programmes (leveraging MoMo for instalment payments), digital literacy partnerships with governments and NGOs, and subsidised data bundles for first-time internet users. Each new internet user is a potential MoMo customer, data subscriber, and Ayoba user — the network effects of the platform business compound with scale.

**Why this is warranted:** The Ansoff analysis identifies this as market development — bringing new customers into existing products. The VRIO analysis shows that subscriber scale is a rare and valuable resource; growing it deepens the competitive moat. The social dimension (PESTLE) means that governments and development finance institutions may co-fund this investment, reducing MTN's capital burden.

## 9. Conclusion

This case study has applied PESTLE, Porter's Five Forces, VRIO, and the Ansoff Matrix to MTN Group's strategic situation as Africa's dominant pan-continental telecoms operator. The analytical chain is internally consistent: the macro-environment (PESTLE) identifies currency risk and regulatory constraints as the primary threats; industry structure (Porter) shows that connectivity alone cannot sustain above-average returns; internal capabilities (VRIO) reveal that MTN's durable advantages lie in infrastructure and platform assets, not financial capital; and growth strategy (Ansoff) points towards product development and related diversification as the highest-value paths forward.

The six recommendations form a coherent action plan: stabilise the balance sheet through FX deleveraging (Rec. 1); reinvest tariff relief into service quality (Rec. 2); accelerate MoMo as a standalone fintech platform (Rec. 3); monetise Bayobab infrastructure with third parties (Rec. 4); extend infrastructure sharing to reduce costs (Rec. 5); and invest in digital inclusion as a long-run subscriber acquisition strategy (Rec. 6).

The strategic logic is clear: MTN cannot solve a structural currency problem with operational improvements alone. It must transform its business model from a connectivity utility — whose revenues are entirely local-currency denominated and whose costs are partially hard-currency denominated — into a diversified digital platform business that generates hard-currency infrastructure revenues (Bayobab), fee-based fintech revenues (MoMo), and enterprise services revenues, while using its unrivalled subscriber scale as the network-effect foundation for all three. The evidence, the frameworks, and the inferential chain all point in the same direction.
