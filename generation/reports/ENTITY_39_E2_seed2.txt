# Strategic Case Study: Trustpilot and the Credibility Paradox
### *A Platform Caught Between Monetisation and Mission*

---

## Executive Summary

Trustpilot occupies a structurally unusual competitive position: its core product is *trust*, yet its business model creates systematic incentives that undermine it. This case study examines the strategic situation crystallised by the December 2025 Grizzly Research short-seller attack, which wiped 32% of Trustpilot's market value in a single session. Using **Porter's Five Forces**, a **two-sided platform lens**, a **SWOT analysis**, and the concept of the **Credibility Trap**, the analysis argues that Trustpilot's dominant strategic challenge is not operational (fake reviews) but structural (a monetisation model that makes the fake-review problem appear self-serving). The case concludes with a prioritised, phased action plan.

---

## 1. Company Background and the Strategic Situation

Trustpilot's strategy comprises five pillars, with trust at the heart of everything it does, as active use of the platform by consumers and businesses generates a strong growth flywheel. Founded in Copenhagen in 2007 and listed on the London Stock Exchange in 2021, the platform today hosts more than 300 million reviews, serves 64 million monthly active users across the globe, and generates 140 billion annual TrustBox impressions.

Financially, the trajectory is strong. Revenue grew 18% in constant currency to $210.7 million (2023: $176.4 million), and the company delivered a record adjusted EBITDA of $24.1 million (2023: $15.5 million), with profit before tax of $5.2 million against a prior-year loss of $1.9 million. Bookings grew 21% in constant currency, setting the group up for a good 2025.

The platform's commercial model is freemium. The freemium model allows businesses to claim their profile for free, with targeted upsell funnels promoting paid subscription tiers that offer advanced tools for online reviews management. The digital self-serve channel is the core of the Trustpilot marketing strategy for SMBs, accounting for 65% of all new customer acquisitions in 2024.

**The triggering event.** On 4 December 2025, Grizzly Research disclosed a short position and accused the global review platform of creating fake profiles that gave negative reviews and then pressuring companies to pay for subscriptions. The short seller accused the review platform of running a "mafia-style extortion" scheme, claiming businesses with paid Trustpilot subscriptions saw their review scores jump from under 2 out of 5 stars to over 4 out of 5 stars. The stock closed down 32%, sending it to the lowest level since December 2023 and cutting the firm's market value by about £243 million ($324 million) to about £517 million — its steepest ever one-day decline.

Trustpilot immediately denied the claims, calling the report "selective, misleading, and framed to support a predetermined narrative." Trustpilot published data comparing reviews removed for paying and free business profiles, which showed free business profiles had 14% negative reviews and paid business profiles had 14% negative reviews — virtually identical proportions. Analyst sentiment remained overwhelmingly positive, with 13 buy ratings and a consensus price target ranging from 229p to 402p. Nevertheless, as the analysis below demonstrates, the event exposed a structural vulnerability that financial performance alone cannot resolve.

## 2. Framework 1 — Porter's Five Forces

Porter's Five Forces provide the industry-level context for understanding *why* Trustpilot is exposed to the credibility attack, and *where* its competitive position is most fragile.

### 2.1 Competitive Rivalry — **HIGH**

Market dynamics, channel advantages, and vertical specialisation shape Trustpilot's competitive landscape: Google Reviews captures a large share of local feedback via Search and Maps, impacting Trustpilot organic traffic. TikTok and Instagram are shifting discovery and reputation toward social proof formats, forcing Trustpilot to adapt how review data is surfaced outside its domain.

**Why this matters for the analysis:** The rivalry is not merely price-based; it is *credibility-based*. Rivals can exploit the Grizzly allegations as a switching narrative, meaning competitive rivalry and the credibility problem are mutually reinforcing.

### 2.2 Threat of Substitutes — **HIGH**

For independent business reviews, Google Reviews is considered the most trusted because it is tied to real Google accounts and has massive visibility. Paid platforms like Feefo offer verified-purchase reviews, providing a more trusted dataset than open platforms.

**Why this matters:** The substitution logic is not merely cost-driven — it is *trust-driven*. Businesses and consumers can migrate to platforms that are structurally free of the paid-provider conflict of interest. This is the direct competitive expression of the Credibility Trap.

### 2.3 Threat of New Entrants — **MODERATE**

Trustpilot Group benefits from a powerful network effect: as more consumers use the platform, its value to businesses increases, and vice versa. Conversely, as more businesses join and respond to reviews, consumers find the platform more useful for decision-making. This creates a meaningful scale barrier. However, AI-generated content makes fake reviews much harder to spot by language alone, as a ChatGPT-written review about a shipping experience can pass the eye test. Generative AI therefore lowers the cost of simulating scale, partially eroding the network-effect moat for potential entrants.

### 2.4 Bargaining Power of Buyers (Businesses) — **MODERATE-HIGH**

Business subscribers face switching costs (lost review history, TrustBox embeds, API integrations), but the Grizzly event amplifies buyer power by providing a legitimising narrative for switching. Anybody can add a business profile to Trustpilot, and once added it cannot be removed — businesses can "claim" their profile but cannot remove their company or information from the platform. This structural lock-in is a source of buyer resentment that elevates churn risk when credibility is questioned.

### 2.5 Bargaining Power of Suppliers — **LOW, but with a regulatory caveat**

Trustpilot's primary "suppliers" are consumer reviewers who write for free. However, obligations under the EU Digital Services Act, GDPR/CCPA privacy enforcement, and emerging AI governance raise compliance costs and operational complexity. Regulation therefore functions as a structural cost supplier, raising the floor on operating expenditure.

## 3. Framework 2 — The Two-Sided Platform and the Credibility Trap

### 3.1 The Structural Architecture

Trustpilot is an open, trusted customer feedback platform that monetises as a high-margin SaaS business with network effects. This positions it as a canonical two-sided platform, intermediating between consumers (who seek trustworthy information) and businesses (who seek reputation management and conversion tools). The value of the platform depends on cross-side network effects: more consumer reviews increase value to businesses; more business participation increases value to consumers.

### 3.2 The Credibility Trap

The two sides have structurally opposed interests with respect to review *content*:
- **Consumers** benefit from unfiltered, honest reviews — including negative ones.
- **Businesses** benefit from positive reviews and the ability to manage or suppress negative ones.

Trustpilot monetises the *business* side. This creates what this analysis terms the **Credibility Trap**: the platform's revenue depends on satisfying the side whose interests are partially antagonistic to the quality of the product that attracts the other side. The academic literature on two-sided markets (Rochet & Tirole, 2003) establishes that platforms must carefully price both sides to maintain participation equilibrium. Trustpilot's version is unusually acute because the product being sold *is* credibility itself — any perception that the business side can purchase a better credibility outcome directly destroys the consumer-side value proposition.

### 3.3 The Institutional Moral Hazard

Institutions that broadcast reputations in a marketplace confront two intertwined moral hazards: the classic hazard between buyers and sellers, and an institutional-level hazard where the platform itself may compromise the accuracy of the reputation system to encourage transactions that boost its own revenue. Grizzly Research's allegations are precisely a claim that this institutional moral hazard has materialised. Trustpilot's counter-data (equal negative review proportions) addresses the *outcome* of the hazard but not its *structural existence* — and the perception of structural conflict is itself damaging to the platform's core asset.

### 3.4 The Open-Platform Double Bind

How Trustpilot works: nobody can pay to delete or downrank criticism. If any business — whether they pay or not — believes a review is not real, they can flag it for review. This openness is the foundation of consumer trust. Yet the open model creates a double bind: businesses are listed without consent and cannot remove their profiles, facing a binary choice between paying for tools to manage the situation or remaining exposed. This dynamic is structurally coercive in appearance regardless of intent, and it is the precise mechanism Grizzly Research exploited.

## 4. Framework 3 — SWOT Analysis

### Strengths

**Scale and network effects.** 301 million total active reviews as of end of December 2024 — a 23% year-on-year increase — with 61 million reviews written in 2024 alone (a 15% year-on-year increase). This data moat is very difficult for any competitor to replicate.

**AI-powered integrity infrastructure.** In 2024, of the 4.5 million detected fake reviews removed from the platform, 90% were automatically detected by fake review detection models using machine learning, neural networks, and generative AI. This represents 53% more reviews being removed automatically than in 2023.

**Financial momentum.** Revenue grew 18% in constant currency to $210.7 million with a record adjusted EBITDA of $24.1 million.

**Brand recognition.** The "Trustpilot Star" is one of the most recognised trust signals on the web, with Trustpilot reviews appearing in the top 1% of web impressions.

### Weaknesses

**Structural conflict of interest.** The monetisation model creates a perception — and potentially a reality — of bias toward paying customers. This is the foundational weakness exploited by Grizzly Research and is not resolvable by data alone.

**Open-profile liability.** Businesses are listed without consent and cannot remove their profiles, creating a coercive dynamic that generates reputational and legal risk for Trustpilot.

**One-sided monetisation.** Trustpilot currently generates revenue almost exclusively from the business side, creating the structural incentive imbalance identified in the two-sided platform analysis.

### Opportunities

**AI-augmented product innovation.** In 2024, Trustpilot expanded its AI capabilities by deploying generative AI to help enforce platform policies such as impersonation, abuse, and misinformation. This infrastructure can be extended into premium analytical products.

**Regulatory tailwinds.** The EU Digital Services Act became applicable across EU member states in February 2024 and covers all digital platforms. Trustpilot's existing integrity infrastructure positions it as a compliance partner rather than a compliance target.

**Enterprise integration.** Trustpilot's 2023 strategy to integrate directly with platforms like Shopify Plus and Stripe fuelled a 22% year-on-year surge in enterprise revenue by Q4 2024.

**Agentic AI search.** As agentic search rapidly becomes more relevant, trust matters more than ever, and Trustpilot occupies a unique position in the online review ecosystem with no single direct global competitor offering the same combination of openness, cross-industry coverage, and consumer reach.

### Threats

**AI-generated fake reviews.** AI-generated content makes fake reviews much harder to spot by language alone — a ChatGPT-written review can pass the eye test. The arms race between synthetic content generation and detection is accelerating.

**DSA exploitation.** German businesses have been found to systematically exploit DSA Article 16 notification mechanisms to remove unfavourable business reviews through false defamation claims, creating artificial rating inflation. This undermines platform integrity from the business side.

**Short-seller vulnerability.** The Grizzly Research event demonstrated that Trustpilot's share price is acutely vulnerable to credibility narratives, with a 32% single-day decline wiping £243 million in market value.

## 5. The Core Strategic Problem: Connecting the Analysis

The three frameworks converge on a single diagnosis. **Porter's Five Forces** identify that Trustpilot's competitive position is most threatened not by operational rivals but by the substitution of its credibility asset — both sides can migrate to platforms free of the paid-provider conflict. **The two-sided platform analysis** explains *why* this threat is endogenous: the monetisation model is architecturally in tension with the quality of the product. **The SWOT** confirms that all of Trustpilot's Strengths (scale, AI, brand) are contingent on maintaining consumer trust — which is precisely what the Weaknesses and Threats erode.

The strategic situation can therefore be stated precisely: **Trustpilot's growth model requires it to sell reputation management to businesses, but its core value proposition to consumers requires that business payments do not influence review outcomes. These two requirements are in structural tension, and the perception of that tension — whether or not it reflects reality — is itself a competitive liability.**

This is not merely a communications problem. Trustpilot's own data showing equal negative review proportions across paid and free profiles is credible evidence against the most extreme version of Grizzly's allegations. But despite Trustpilot's denials, the share price fell by over 30%, wiping around £240 million from its value, and the long-term damage to Trustpilot's reputation is harder to quantify. The market's response is a signal that self-reported data is not sufficient to resolve a structural credibility question. The data addresses *outcomes*; the market is concerned about *structure*.

## 6. Strategic Options

Three broad options follow from the analysis:

- **Option A — Incremental (Deepen the current model):** Continue AI investment, expand enterprise subscriptions, manage attacks through communications. Viable short-term given financial momentum, but does not resolve the structural conflict of interest and leaves the platform exposed to repeated credibility attacks.
- **Option B — Transformational (Structural separation of integrity and monetisation):** Establish formally independent governance of content moderation, creating a firewall between subscription revenue decisions and review outcomes. Addresses the root cause.
- **Option C — Diversification (Rebalance two-sided monetisation):** Develop consumer-facing premium products that generate revenue from the consumer side, reducing structural dependence on business-side revenue and the conflict of interest it creates.

The analysis supports a **combined Option B + C strategy**. Option A alone is insufficient because it does not address the structural credibility trap. Options B and C together resolve the root cause while opening new revenue streams.

## 7. Recommendations and Action Plan

### Phase 1: Structural Credibility Repair (Months 0–6)

**Recommendation 1.1 — Establish an Independent Review Integrity Board.**

*What:* An external board of consumer protection experts, academics, and former regulators, with published quarterly audits of review removal decisions across paid and free profiles.

*Why the evidence warrants this:* Trustpilot's own data shows equal negative review proportions for paid and free profiles — strong evidence against differential treatment. Yet the stock still closed down 32% on the day of the Grizzly report. This demonstrates that the market does not treat self-reported data as sufficient. An independent board transforms self-reported data into *audited* data, which commands a different credibility premium. The institutional moral hazard identified in the two-sided platform analysis requires an *institutional* response, not merely a data response.

**Recommendation 1.2 — Publish a Structural Transparency Charter.**

*What:* A binding public commitment that: (a) content moderation is structurally insulated from sales and account management; (b) flagging tools available to paid subscribers are identical in effect to those available to free profiles; (c) removal rates by subscription tier are published quarterly.

*Why the evidence warrants this:* Grizzly Research alleged that Trustpilot creates "unsolicited review profiles for all kinds of businesses with the intention to attract hyper-negative reviews and force these businesses into paying subscription deals to 'more actively manage' the reviews." Trustpilot's response addressed *outcomes* (equal removal rates) but not *structure* (the architectural relationship between subscription revenue and moderation decisions). The Charter closes this gap by making the governance architecture publicly verifiable.

---

### Phase 2: Monetisation Rebalancing (Months 6–18)

**Recommendation 2.1 — Introduce Consumer-Side Premium Products.**

*What:* AI-curated review summaries, personalised trust scores, verified-reviewer identity badges, and scam-alert notifications offered to consumers at a modest subscription price.

*Why the evidence warrants this:* The two-sided platform analysis establishes that Trustpilot currently monetises only one side (businesses), creating a structural incentive imbalance. The platform has 64 million monthly active users who currently derive value at zero cost. Generating revenue from the consumer side rebalances the model and reduces the structural conflict of interest — because the side whose interests are aligned with review quality (consumers) becomes a revenue contributor, not merely a resource to be monetised indirectly. The freemium literature supports this: network effects and freemium strategies are complementary for market leaders, and Trustpilot's scale makes it well-placed to convert a small fraction of its consumer base.

**Recommendation 2.2 — Reframe the Freemium-to-Paid Upsell Narrative.**

*What:* Redesign the paid subscription value proposition around offensive value creation (analytics, customer insight, conversion optimisation) rather than defensive risk mitigation (managing negative reviews).

*Why the evidence warrants this:* Grizzly Research's core claim was that "Trustpilot's growth relies on extorting businesses while eroding trust in the brand." This allegation has traction because the defensive framing of the upsell — "claim your profile and manage your reviews" — is structurally consistent with the extortion narrative. The 22% year-on-year surge in enterprise revenue following Shopify Plus and Stripe integrations demonstrates that Trustpilot can grow rapidly when positioned as a growth enabler. Extending this framing to the SMB tier removes the structural basis for the extortion narrative.

---

### Phase 3: Competitive Repositioning (Months 18–36)

**Recommendation 3.1 — Develop a DSA Compliance-as-a-Service Offering.**

*What:* Package Trustpilot's AI-based review integrity infrastructure as a licensed compliance service for platforms that need DSA-compliant review integrity but lack the scale to build it internally.

*Why the evidence warrants this:* The EU Digital Services Act became applicable across EU member states in February 2024 and creates mandatory review integrity obligations. Trustpilot's AI infrastructure removed 4.5 million fake reviews in 2024 with 90% automated accuracy. Smaller platforms cannot easily replicate this. Converting a defensive investment (integrity infrastructure) into an offensive revenue stream (B2B SaaS licensing) diversifies Trustpilot's revenue away from the business-subscription model that creates the structural conflict, while reinforcing its regulatory positioning.

**Recommendation 3.2 — Accelerate Infrastructure Embedding.**

*What:* Expand the Shopify Plus / Stripe integration model to additional commerce, fintech, and SaaS platforms, positioning Trustpilot as a trust infrastructure layer rather than a standalone review destination.

*Why the evidence warrants this:* Porter's Five Forces analysis identifies that Google Reviews is the most trusted for independent business reviews because it is tied to real Google accounts and has massive visibility. This substitution threat applies to Trustpilot-as-destination but not to Trustpilot-as-infrastructure. The Shopify Plus and Stripe integrations already demonstrated a 22% enterprise revenue surge. When Trustpilot is embedded within commerce flows, switching costs become technical and contractual rather than merely reputational, creating a more durable competitive moat.

## 9. Conclusion

Trustpilot's strategic situation is not primarily a product problem or an operational problem. It is a **structural problem** rooted in the architecture of its two-sided platform: the side it monetises (businesses) has interests that are partially antagonistic to the quality of the product that attracts the other side (consumers). The Grizzly Research attack wiped around £240 million from Trustpilot's value and left businesses that collect online reviews with fundamental questions about the integrity of the platform. It did not create the underlying problem; it exposed it.

The recommended strategy — structural governance reform combined with monetisation rebalancing — addresses the root cause rather than the symptom. It is grounded in the analytical finding that Trustpilot's competitive moat (scale, network effects, AI infrastructure) is real and defensible, but only if the credibility asset that underpins it is structurally protected.

The chain of reasoning is explicit: **credibility is the moat → the monetisation model structurally threatens credibility → governance reform protects the moat → a rebalanced monetisation model sustains it over the long term.** Each step is warranted by the evidence presented; together they constitute a coherent, source-grounded, and actionable strategic response.
