# COMAC AND THE C919: STRATEGIC ANALYSIS OF CHINA'S STATE-OWNED AIRCRAFT MAKER

## A Case Study in Industrial Strategy, Geopolitical Risk, and Competitive Positioning

---

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Commercial Aircraft Corporation of China (COMAC), a state-owned enterprise (SOE) founded in 2008, represents China's most ambitious bid to break the Boeing–Airbus duopoly in commercial aviation. Its flagship narrow-body aircraft, the C919, entered commercial service in May 2023 after years of delays. Despite receiving between $49 billion and $72 billion in state-related support through 2020, and a further CNY 44 billion ($6.2 billion) capital injection from eight state-owned shareholders, COMAC delivered only approximately 13 C919s in 2024 and approximately 16 in 2025, missing even its revised target by nearly half. The core strategic tension is this: COMAC is simultaneously a national champion pursuing industrial-policy goals and a commercial aircraft manufacturer that must achieve scale, certification, and supply-chain independence to survive geopolitical disruption. This case study analyses that tension through PESTEL, Porter's Five Forces, SWOT, and the Resource-Based View (VRIO), and concludes with a prioritised action plan.

---

## 2. CASE BACKGROUND

### 2.1 Corporate Profile

The Commercial Aircraft Corporation of China (COMAC) was established on 11 May 2008 in Shanghai. It was established jointly by the Aviation Industry Corporation of China (AVIC), the Aluminium Corporation of China, Baosteel Group Corporation, Sinochem Group, Shanghai Guosheng Corporation Limited, and the State-owned Assets Supervision and Administration Commission (SASAC). The latest round of fundraising for COMAC was led by SASAC, whose ownership stake increased to 53.08% after the infusion.

COMAC's mandate was explicit from the outset. Beijing marked aerospace and aviation equipment as one of ten strategic industries targeted in its Made in China 2025 initiative, aiming to move China's economic development away from cheap, low-value-added exports.

### 2.2 The C919 Programme

The Comac C919 is a narrow-body airliner developed by the Chinese state-owned aircraft manufacturer. The development programme was launched in 2008 as a competitor to the Airbus A320 and Boeing 737. Production began in December 2011, with the first prototype being rolled out on 2 November 2015; the maiden flight took place on 5 May 2017. On 29 September 2022, the C919 received its CAAC type certificate.

The first C919 airframe intended for commercial service was delivered to launch customer China Eastern Airlines on 9 December 2022. As of December 2024, all 16 operational C919s had carried more than one million revenue passengers. The order book stands at over 1,000 aircraft, with major Chinese airlines like Air China, China Eastern Airlines, and China Southern Airlines each ordering 100 aircraft.

### 2.3 The Triggering Crisis

In late May 2025, the US Department of Commerce suspended export licences allowing American companies to supply aircraft engines and related technologies to COMAC. The restrictions covered Electronic Computer-Aided Design (ECAD) software, chemicals for semiconductor manufacturing, butane and ethane, machine tools, and aerospace equipment. Affected manufacturers include Cadence, Synopsys, and Siemens EDA, as well as engine supplier GE Aerospace, and key avionics component suppliers Honeywell and Collins Aerospace.

The core issue lies in COMAC's reliance on foreign suppliers for 50–60% of the C919's components. The aircraft depends on Western technology, including CFM International's LEAP-1C turbofan engines, produced by a GE Aerospace and Safran joint venture. In June and July 2025, escalating US–China trade tensions led to a temporary US export halt on these engines, stalling production. Although exports resumed in August, the disruption created a lasting backlog. Consequently, COMAC slashed its 2025 production target from 75 to 25 C919 aircraft.

## 3. THE STRATEGIC SITUATION

### 3.1 The Opportunity

Global demand for narrow-body aircraft is enormous. The world's passenger and freighter fleet is expected to nearly double, growing from 24,730 aircraft at the end of 2024 to 49,210 aircraft by the end of 2044. Airbus plans to increase A320-family output to 75 units per month by 2027, citing a backlog exceeding 7,000 frames. Production delays at Airbus and Boeing have created openings for new players, and some industry leaders in the Gulf and Asia are watching closely. China is projected to become the world's largest passenger aviation market, and Chinese airlines are expected to purchase $1.1 trillion in new aircraft by 2036.

### 3.2 The Problem

Despite these tailwinds, COMAC faces a multi-dimensional strategic crisis spanning supply-chain dependency, production underperformance, geopolitical exposure, certification gaps, and reputational damage — each of which is examined in the analysis below.

## 4. ANALYTICAL FRAMEWORK

### 4.1 PESTEL Analysis

**Political**

COMAC operates at the intersection of Chinese industrial policy and US–China geopolitical competition. On one side, COMAC benefits from protectionist policies and state-directed procurement, ensuring a growing share of the Chinese aviation market. The Chinese government deployed multiple policy instruments to develop its domestic commercial aviation manufacturing industry through supply-side policies, including merging multiple aviation industry corporations to form COMAC.

On the other side, in January 2021, the United States government named COMAC as a company "owned or controlled" by the People's Liberation Army (PLA) and thereby prohibited any American company or individual from investing in it. In January 2025, COMAC was added to a US Department of Defense list of companies that allegedly work with the PLA. In May 2025, the US tightened export controls on certain parts previously shipped to COMAC.

The political inference is direct: the state-ownership structure that provides unlimited capital and captive customers simultaneously triggers Western sanctions and certification barriers. Political advantage domestically is the source of political liability internationally.

**Economic**

COMAC received between $49 billion and $72 billion in state-related support through 2020. More recently, COMAC received a CNY 44 billion ($6.2 billion) capital injection from eight state-owned shareholders, nearly doubling its registered capital, to support mass production of the C919. COMAC aims to raise annual production capacity to 150 aircraft by 2027 and 200 by 2029. Additionally, COMAC intends to invest CNY 10 billion annually in R&D and a further CNY 10 billion annually in fixed asset investments in Shanghai over three years.

This level of state financial support insulates COMAC from commercial pressures that would bankrupt a privately held firm — but it also creates moral hazard, reducing the incentive to achieve genuine efficiency benchmarks. The economic inference is that COMAC's financial model is sustainable only as long as state support continues and domestic demand remains captive.

**Technological**

The C919's technological profile is paradoxical. CrowdStrike said it was highly likely that COMAC and AVIC "benefited significantly from the cyber efforts of the MSS [Ministry of State Security], knocking several years and potentially billions of dollars off its development time." A mix of CrowdStrike's intelligence and US government reporting showed that Beijing used a "multi-faceted system of forced technology transfer, joint ventures" and cyber operations to accelerate the programme. This raises fundamental questions about the depth of indigenous technological capability.

The domestic engine programme is the most critical technology gap. To reduce its dependence on the West, China is developing its own CJ-1000A jet engine. Analysts predict mass production won't occur until around 2030. According to the South China Morning Post, certification and initial deliveries for the CJ-1000A engine are more likely to happen in 2027 or 2028. Until then, every C919 produced requires a Western engine controlled by a US-French consortium.

**Environmental**

The European Union's ReFuelEU regulation stipulates a 2% sustainable aviation fuel (SAF) blend in 2025, rising to 70% by 2050. COMAC's EASA certification pursuit is therefore complicated not only by airworthiness standards but by the need to demonstrate compliance with evolving environmental regulations, adding another layer to an already complex certification challenge.

**Legal**

The EASA certification process is estimated to take three to six years, with no delays or needing to change the aircraft characteristics. The FAA might take longer. This can delay the C919's short-term market potential outside China. Without European or FAA validation, many airlines and leasing companies cannot operate the C919 in their home countries.

### 4.2 Porter's Five Forces

**Threat of New Entrants: LOW (for incumbents)**

Commercial aircraft manufacturing has the highest barriers to entry of any manufacturing sector. Since World War II, with the exception of Embraer of Brazil, no company or country has successfully entered the civil jet transport manufacturing sector. And Embraer has yet to enter what can be termed the large jet market; all of its products seat fewer than 120 passengers. COMAC has overcome the capital barrier through state funding, but has not yet overcome the certification and MRO network barriers. The barriers that protect Boeing and Airbus from other new entrants also protect them from COMAC in non-Chinese markets.

**Bargaining Power of Suppliers: HIGH and Rising**

This is COMAC's most acute competitive force. Cutting off the supply of jet engines to China would significantly slow production of the C919, which Beijing sees as a competitor to the Airbus A320 and Boeing 737. With the LEAP-1C as the sole engine option for the C919, any disruption in engine deliveries would leave newly built aircraft grounded, effectively stalling China's plans to ramp up production.

As a smaller customer than Airbus or Boeing, COMAC has lower priority for industry suppliers, as they sell fewer components. The Russian precedent is instructive: since sanctions were imposed on Russia's aviation sector in 2022, no commercial aircraft have been delivered. Supplier power is therefore existential, not merely commercial.

**Bargaining Power of Buyers: MODERATE (domestic) / HIGH (international)**

Domestically, COMAC's buyers — China's state-owned mega-carriers — face strong political direction. Boeing's Chinese customers are refusing delivery of new airplanes built for them because of US tariffs, channelling demand towards COMAC. However, this is not a genuine market signal of competitive quality. Internationally, buyer power is high: Ryanair CEO Michael O'Leary stated in May 2025 that he would consider COMAC aircraft if prices were 10 to 20 per cent cheaper — a remark widely interpreted as leverage against Boeing rather than genuine purchase intent. Even interest is conditional on steep price concessions.

**Threat of Substitutes: LOW**

There is no near-term substitute for the narrow-body jet in short-to-medium-haul aviation. Electric or hydrogen aircraft remain decades from commercial viability at this scale. This force does not constrain COMAC in any meaningful near-term sense.

**Competitive Rivalry: INTENSE**

The future success of COMAC to challenge Airbus and Boeing hinges on many factors, including Airbus and Boeing's abilities to meet projected demand, the support of the Chinese state, geopolitical relations between China and the United States, and whether the C919 receives EASA and FAA certificates. Currently, COMAC's in-service fleet reached 182 aircraft by August 2025, concentrated among China Southern, Air China, and China Eastern. COMAC is a domestic monopolist in a captive market, not yet a global competitor.

### 4.4 Resource-Based View: VRIO Analysis

The Resource-Based View (RBV), developed by Barney (1991), holds that sustained competitive advantage derives from resources that are **Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Organisationally exploited**. The resource-based model of competitive advantage suggests that competitive advantage may be sustained by harnessing resources that are valuable, rare, imperfectly imitable, and non-substitutable.

| Captive domestic market | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ (short-term) | ✓ | **Sustained advantage** — within China only |
| CAAC type certificate | ✓ | ✗ | ✗ | ✓ | Competitive parity — necessary but insufficient |

| EASA/FAA certification | ✓ | ✗ | ✗ | ✗ (absent) | **Critical gap** |
| Global MRO network | ✓ | ✗ | ✗ | ✗ (absent) | **Critical gap** for internationalisation |

The VRIO inference is decisive: COMAC's only truly sustained competitive advantages are its captive domestic market and state financial backing — both political rather than commercial in origin. Its technological resources are either externally dependent (LEAP-1C), built on questionable foundations, or not yet realised (CJ-1000A). COMAC currently lacks the VRIO resources necessary to sustain competitive advantage in international markets. Its strategic priority must therefore be to *build* inimitable resources — principally the domestic engine and indigenous supply chain — rather than expand internationally before those foundations are secure.

## 5. SYNTHESIS: THE CORE STRATEGIC TENSION

The analyses above converge on a single coherent strategic tension: **COMAC is being asked to pursue two objectives simultaneously that are, in the near term, in conflict.**

**Objective 1 (Political)**: Achieve rapid production scale and international market presence to validate China's Made in China 2025 industrial policy and demonstrate technological sovereignty.

**Objective 2 (Commercial)**: Build genuine engineering capabilities, supply-chain independence, certification credentials, and MRO infrastructure necessary to compete sustainably in the global commercial aircraft market.

These objectives are in tension because pursuing Objective 1 rapidly — ramping production before supply-chain independence is achieved, seeking international orders before EASA certification is secured — exposes COMAC to the geopolitical vulnerabilities that have already materialised. The production shortfalls of 2024–2025 are not primarily a manufacturing problem; they are the symptom of a strategic misalignment between political ambition and commercial readiness. As evidence: in 2025, production and deliveries were curtailed by supply chain disruptions, regulatory hurdles, and escalating trade frictions, underscoring the fragility of globally integrated aerospace manufacturing.

COMAC's SOE governance structure, which provides its financial lifeline, simultaneously generates the geopolitical exposure that threatens its supply chain and the governance constraints that slow its commercial learning. This is the core paradox that any strategic recommendation must address.

## 6. STRATEGIC OPTIONS

Three broad options present themselves:

**Option A — Accelerated International Expansion**: Pursue EASA certification aggressively, seek international orders in Southeast Asia and BRI markets, and use political leverage to accelerate foreign airline adoption.
*Assessment*: Premature. Without supply-chain independence, every international delivery is hostage to US export controls. A single sanctions episode would strand foreign operators' fleets, destroying COMAC's international reputation irreparably.

**Option B — Domestic Consolidation and Indigenisation First**: Prioritise CJ-1000A engine certification, accelerate domestic supply-chain substitution, stabilise production at achievable rates, and defer international expansion until supply-chain independence is substantially achieved.
*Assessment*: Strategically sound but politically difficult. It requires Beijing to accept slower progress against Made in China 2025 targets in exchange for long-term resilience.

**Option C — Dual-Track Strategy with Sequenced Milestones** *(Recommended)*: Pursue domestic consolidation and indigenisation as the primary near-term priority, while simultaneously building the certification, MRO, and partnership infrastructure for international expansion — with a clear sequenced trigger: international commercial launch contingent on CJ-1000A certification and EASA type certificate. This resolves the core tension by making international expansion conditional on, rather than concurrent with, supply-chain independence.

## 7. RECOMMENDATIONS AND ACTION PLAN

### 7.1 Immediate Priority (0–2 Years): Stabilise and Indigenise

**Recommendation 1: Treat CJ-1000A certification as the programme's single most critical milestone.**

*Evidence*: China is developing its own CJ-1000A jet engine, which is close to completing certification, but analysts predict mass production won't occur until around 2030.

*Reasoning*: The VRIO analysis shows that the LEAP-1C engine is neither rare nor inimitable and is controlled by a US-French consortium subject to US export controls. The CJ-1000A, if certified, would be a genuinely rare and partially inimitable resource. COMAC is continuing to move forward in one direction to produce key elements of the aircraft domestically rather than to rely on western suppliers. For the C919, the largest western component is the engine. Redirecting a disproportionate share of COMAC's CNY 10 billion annual R&D budget to accelerate the CJ-1000A timeline is therefore the highest-return strategic investment available.

**Recommendation 2: Establish a strategic engine inventory buffer and European sourcing alternative.**

*Evidence*: In June and July 2025, escalating US–China trade tensions led to a temporary US export halt on LEAP-1C engines, stalling production. Although exports resumed in August, the disruption created a lasting backlog.

*Reasoning*: Until the CJ-1000A is certified, COMAC remains hostage to US export policy. COMAC should negotiate with CFM International and its European parent Safran for a multi-year, pre-paid engine inventory held in a third-country bonded warehouse outside direct US export-control jurisdiction. The geopolitical calculus is clear: Europe's neutrality in the US–China feud makes it a trusted partner. Simultaneously, COMAC should explore whether a European-only engine alternative — a Safran-led consortium without GE participation — could be developed, leveraging Europe's stated interest in maintaining strategic autonomy.

**Recommendation 3: Reset production targets to credible, achievable levels.**

*Evidence*: COMAC's production ramp-up has proven slower than initially projected. In 2024, the company delivered approximately 13 C919s, falling well short of earlier targets. For 2025, COMAC announced a target of 75 aircraft but subsequently slashed this to 25.

*Reasoning*: The Porter's Five Forces analysis shows that domestic buyer power is currently suppressed by political direction — but this suppression is not permanent. If Chinese carriers lose confidence in COMAC's delivery reliability, they will seek political cover to diversify back to Airbus. COMAC should announce a revised, conservative production trajectory (e.g., 30 per year by 2026, 60 by 2028, 100 by 2030) and then consistently exceed it, rebuilding credibility through demonstrated performance rather than political announcement.

**Recommendation 4: Establish a transparent, independent technical review mechanism.**

*Reasoning*: The SOE governance structure creates a principal-agent problem: COMAC's management is accountable to political principals (SASAC, the State Council) whose objectives (industrial policy milestones, national prestige) diverge from commercial objectives (quality, reliability, cost efficiency). This is a root cause of the chronic target-setting and missing dynamic. COMAC should establish an independent technical review board — including retired engineers from Airbus and Embraer, Chinese aviation academics, and CAAC representatives — with a mandate to provide honest assessments of programme readiness before targets are publicly announced.

### 7.2 Medium-Term Priority (2–5 Years): Certify and Selectively Expand

**Recommendation 5: Prioritise EASA certification above FAA certification.**

*Evidence*: The geopolitical dimension complicates matters: the US has blocked FAA certification for the C919, a decision tied to broader tensions over China's industrial policies. By contrast, COMAC is pursuing certification from EASA as early as 2025, a critical step for broader global acceptance.

*Reasoning*: EASA certification would unlock not only European markets but, crucially, the globally integrated leasing and financing market — most major aircraft lessors are headquartered in Ireland or the US and require EASA or FAA certification for their portfolios. Without lessor participation, COMAC cannot finance international sales at competitive rates. The strategic group map shows that international reach requires supply-chain independence AND certification; EASA is the more achievable certification target.

*Caution*: China delays Airbus delivery approvals in a push for COMAC certification — a coercive tactic that signals bad faith to a safety regulator. COMAC should advocate for China to abandon this approach and instead demonstrate good-faith technical compliance with EASA's requirements.

**Recommendation 6: Launch a structured Southeast Asia market-entry programme, contingent on CJ-1000A certification.**

*Evidence*: AirAsia has confirmed it is in active discussions with COMAC over the C919 narrowbody jet, a move that could make it the type's first foreign operator. If airlines in Malaysia and Thailand move forward with C919 acquisitions, Southeast Asia could become the first overseas market for the aircraft, further anchoring China's role in shaping regional aviation.

*Reasoning*: The Five Forces analysis shows that international buyer power is high: foreign airlines will not commit to an aircraft whose production can be halted by US sanctions. The trigger for Southeast Asia market entry should therefore be CJ-1000A certification, which eliminates the engine-supply vulnerability. At that point, COMAC can credibly offer a narrowbody jet with no US-controlled components in its critical systems, making it genuinely sanction-resistant.

**Recommendation 7: Invest in a dedicated international MRO and parts-support network ahead of sales.**

*Evidence*: After-sales support, international certification, and global spares networks remain key concerns for potential international operators.

*Reasoning*: The RBV analysis identifies the absence of a global MRO network as a critical gap. Foreign airlines considering the C919 face not only the question of whether the aircraft performs well, but whether they can maintain it when it needs repair. COMAC should establish regional MRO hubs in Singapore, Dubai, and Nairobi — markets aligned with BRI partnerships — staffed initially by COMAC engineers and progressively trained local technicians. This investment must precede, not follow, international sales commitments.

### 7.3 Long-Term Priority (5–10 Years): Scale and Diversify

**Recommendation 8: Design the C929 wide-body with a fully indigenous supply chain from the outset.**

*Reasoning*: The lesson of the C919 is that a programme built on Western components is perpetually vulnerable to geopolitical disruption. In 2024, a report stated that COMAC was working on a plan to become more self-sufficient in terms of suppliers, with a long-term goal of replacing all US-export-controlled components, because American export restrictions were affecting the ability to produce aircraft. The C929 should be designed from its inception with Chinese-sourced components wherever technically feasible, with Western components sourced only from countries (e.g., France, Germany) with lower geopolitical risk than the US.

**Recommendation 9: Pursue selective technology partnerships with non-US Western firms.**

*Evidence*: Investors should watch Thales and Liebherr, which provide avionics and hydraulic systems now in demand as COMAC cuts ties with US suppliers.

*Reasoning*: The espionage allegations have poisoned COMAC's relationships with US aerospace companies. However, European firms — Safran, Thales, Liebherr, MTU — have a different risk calculus. Europe's stated interest in maintaining strategic autonomy from both the US and China creates space for genuine commercial partnerships. COMAC should pursue legitimate joint-development agreements with European firms, offering market access in exchange for technology transfer, structured transparently to avoid the forced-transfer allegations that have damaged its reputation.

**Recommendation 10: Reframe the national-champion narrative around commercial excellence.**

*Evidence*: Since World War II, with the exception of Embraer of Brazil, no company or country has successfully entered the civil jet transport manufacturing sector. Embraer succeeded by building genuine engineering excellence and competing on commercial terms — including accepting international investors.

*Reasoning*: The social analysis shows the C919 is deeply embedded in a national-pride narrative — a double-edged sword that sustains political support but means any serious failure would be catastrophically damaging. Over the long term, COMAC should consider partial privatisation or the introduction of international institutional investors to import commercial discipline and reduce the SOE governance distortions identified throughout this analysis.

## 8. CONCLUSION

COMAC's strategic situation is one of the most complex in contemporary business: a state-owned enterprise pursuing national industrial-policy objectives in a market characterised by extreme barriers to entry, intense geopolitical competition, and existential supply-chain vulnerability. The analytical frameworks applied in this case study converge on a coherent diagnosis and a sequenced prescription.

**The diagnosis**: COMAC's competitive advantages are currently political (captive market, unlimited state funding) rather than commercial (genuine engineering capability, supply-chain independence, international certification). Political advantages are inherently fragile because they can be neutralised by the political actions of adversaries — as US export controls have twice demonstrated. These pressures have forced COMAC to recalibrate production targets, temper growth expectations, and reassess its long-term industrial strategy.

**The prescription**: COMAC must urgently convert its political advantages into commercial ones. The sequenced action plan — indigenise the supply chain (CJ-1000A first), stabilise production at credible rates, pursue EASA certification, then expand internationally — reflects the logical chain of the analysis. Attempting to shortcut this sequence by expanding internationally before supply-chain independence is achieved risks the catastrophic outcome illustrated by the Russian precedent.

The ultimate measure of COMAC's strategic success will not be whether it has delivered aircraft to Chinese state carriers — that outcome is guaranteed by political direction. It will be whether, by 2035, a foreign airline has placed an unsolicited order for the C919 or its successor on purely commercial grounds. That outcome requires not a national champion, but a genuine commercial competitor.
