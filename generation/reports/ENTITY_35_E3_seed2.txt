# Strategic Case Study: Shell plc and the Energy Transition Dilemma

## The "Value-or-Velocity" Problem, 2021–2024

---

## 1. Introduction and Strategic Situation

Shell plc faces one of the most complex strategic dilemmas in contemporary corporate history: how to remain competitive and financially credible to capital markets in the short-to-medium term while executing a credible transition to a net-zero emissions business by 2050. The tension crystallised publicly between 2021 and 2024 as the company retreated from several renewable commitments it had made under its "Powering Progress" strategy, while simultaneously doubling down on liquefied natural gas (LNG) and cost discipline. This case analyses that strategic pivot, examines its internal logic and external legitimacy risks, and concludes with a set of recommendations.

The central strategic question is: **Does Shell's "value over volume" reorientation represent a coherent and defensible long-term competitive position, or does it sacrifice transition credibility in ways that will ultimately destroy shareholder value?**

---

## 2. Background and Context

Shell's updated energy transition strategy aims to transform the company into a net-zero emissions business by 2050 while still providing secure energy supplies during the transition. This goal was articulated in the "Powering Progress" strategy launched in 2021 and updated at a Capital Markets Day in June 2023. At the Capital Markets Day, Shell updated investors on its strategy to create more value with less emissions and deliver increased shareholder returns through a balanced energy transition.

However, the strategy underwent a significant reorientation under CEO Wael Sawan, who took over in 2023. Shell extended job cuts beyond previously announced reductions in its low-carbon division as Sawan sought to trim costs and be more competitive with US rivals. In October, Shell said 200 positions in its Low Carbon Solutions unit would be cut in 2024, about 15% of the total. More structurally, Shell stepped back from new offshore wind investments and split its power division following an extensive review of the business that was once seen as a key driver of the company's energy transition strategy. The changes were part of a company-wide review launched in 2023 aimed at reducing costs as CEO Wael Sawan focused on activities with the highest returns. In many cases that meant reducing spending on low-carbon and renewable businesses and increasing the focus on oil, gas and biofuels.

The financial context matters: Shell posted adjusted earnings of $23.72 billion for the full-year 2024, compared to annual profit of $28.25 billion a year earlier. This earnings decline, combined with a persistent valuation gap relative to US rivals, intensified pressure on management to prioritise returns. As of June 2024, ExxonMobil's $490 billion market cap dwarfed Shell's $220 billion, with Chevron ($279 billion) and TotalEnergies ($156 billion) trailing further behind.

## 3. External Analysis: PESTEL and Porter's Five Forces

### 3.1 PESTEL Analysis

**Political/Regulatory.** The European Union's Emissions Trading System (EU ETS) set a carbon price at €80.66 per ton of CO₂ in January 2024. This rising carbon cost structurally disadvantages high-emission operations and increases the long-run cost of Shell's fossil fuel portfolio. At the same time, on 12 November 2024, a Dutch appeals court ruled that Shell does not have to reduce its CO₂ emissions by 45% by 2030 compared to 2019 levels, as previously ordered by the Hague District Court in 2021. Shell now has the right to adjust its own emissions reductions targets as it sees fit. However, the appeals court maintained that there exists a private law duty of care which requires, through corporate policy, that companies contribute to the mitigation of dangerous climate change by reducing their emissions. The regulatory implication is that while Shell gained tactical flexibility on specific targets, the underlying legal obligation to act on climate remains—and may intensify as the EU's Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CS3D) takes hold.

**Economic.** Oil price volatility remains a systemic risk. Shell's 2024 earnings decline was partly attributable to lower crude prices. The valuation gap between US and European majors reflects a stark divide in how investors perceive growth potential: US majors are seen as cash-generative, low-risk assets in a volatile sector, while European peers are viewed as transitional. This perception gap creates a structural cost-of-capital disadvantage for Shell.

**Social.** Consumer and societal expectations are shifting. A 2024 survey indicated that over 60% of consumers in Europe are more likely to choose energy providers with strong renewable commitments. This signals an emerging demand-side risk: if Shell's brand becomes associated with fossil-fuel entrenchment, it may face customer attrition in retail energy markets—a dynamic already visible in Shell's withdrawal from household energy supply in Europe.

**Technological.** LNG technology and CCS are core to Shell's transition pathway. Shell's Quest CCS project has maintained steady performance, consistently capturing around 79% of emissions and storing 9 million tonnes by May 2024, with plans for a 75% capacity expansion. This demonstrates operational CCS capability, though the technology remains expensive and unproven at the scale needed for net-zero ambitions.

**Environmental.** Shell's core beliefs hold that LNG will play a critical role in the energy transition, including replacing coal in industry, and that oil will have a continued role in transport, with growth in demand slowing over time. This belief system drives strategy but is contested by climate scientists who argue that any continued expansion of fossil fuel infrastructure is incompatible with 1.5°C pathways.

**Legal.** Beyond the Milieudefensie case, Shell's 'intensity' pledges for 2030 and 2035 are not the same as commitments to achieve near-term absolute reductions in fossil fuel business in line with climate science. Instead, the gaps in Shell's pledges permit continued fossil fuel sales for the next 14 years. This legal and reputational exposure—characterised by NGOs as greenwashing—constitutes a material risk to Shell's social licence to operate.

### 3.2 Porter's Five Forces

**Competitive Rivalry (High).** Shell remains the primary LNG rival to TotalEnergies, with the largest global fleet and higher trading volumes. BP competes strongly in European EV charging and offshore wind auctions. Rivalry is intensifying as US majors, emboldened by shale production economics, compete for capital and talent. TotalEnergies is diversifying its portfolio, aiming for renewables to constitute 40% of its energy mix by 2025. Its net margin of 9.14% recently surpassed Shell's 5.27%. This margin compression signals that Shell's competitive position is under genuine pressure.

**Threat of New Entrants (Moderate–Low in upstream; Moderate–High in renewables).** The capital intensity of upstream oil and gas creates high barriers to entry. However, in renewable energy—which Shell has partially vacated—technology companies, utilities, and sovereign wealth funds are entering at scale, with lower costs of capital and fewer legacy asset burdens. Shell's retreat from offshore wind therefore cedes ground to entrants that face fewer institutional constraints.

**Bargaining Power of Suppliers (Moderate).** Capital expenditures for specialized oil and gas equipment include offshore drilling rigs costing $650 million to $1.2 billion per unit. The concentration of specialist suppliers gives them some leverage, though Shell's scale enables volume-based negotiating power.

**Bargaining Power of Buyers (Moderate–High in retail; Lower in LNG).** Individual consumers exert influence through price sensitivity and low switching costs, as seen in fluctuating gasoline prices during 2024. Large industrial clients, however, hold substantial sway due to their massive procurement volumes. In LNG, long-term contract structures reduce buyer power, but spot market growth is increasing buyer optionality.

**Threat of Substitutes (Growing).** Renewable electricity, battery storage, and green hydrogen represent long-run substitutes for oil and gas. The pace at which these substitutes erode Shell's core markets depends on policy, technology cost curves, and infrastructure build-out—all of which are accelerating. Shell's LNG-centric strategy implicitly bets that the substitution timeline is slower than climate advocates project.

## 4. Internal Analysis: Resource-Based View and Value Chain

### 4.1 VRIN Analysis of Core Capabilities

The Resource-Based View (Barney, 1991) holds that sustainable competitive advantage derives from resources that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN). Shell's strategic pivot makes most sense when evaluated through this lens.

**LNG Trading and Optimisation Capabilities.** Shell has a world-leading Integrated Gas and LNG business, meeting around 16% of global demand in 2025. Its flexible, cost-competitive portfolio is underpinned by diverse global supply, strong customer reach in key growth markets, and one of the world's largest LNG shipping fleets. This capability is *valuable* (it generates consistent cash flows), *rare* (few competitors have equivalent global reach across the full LNG value chain), *inimitable* (it took decades of relationship-building, infrastructure investment, and proprietary trading knowledge to construct), and *non-substitutable* in the near term. It therefore meets the VRIN threshold for a source of sustainable advantage.

Shell's ability to integrate LNG trading and marketing enables it to react rapidly to price movements due to changes in commodity arbitrage, fluctuations in demand from weather events, or supply disruptions elsewhere. That ability is particularly important during times of high market volatility since response time directly impacts both the effectiveness and profitability of Shell's overall LNG portfolio. This dynamic responsiveness is a form of what Teece et al. (1997) term a "dynamic capability"—the capacity to sense and seize opportunities in volatile markets.

**Scale and Financial Strength.** In 2024 Shell reported adjusted earnings of about $36 billion and free cash flow of roughly $21 billion, underscoring continued cash generation that funds dividends, buybacks and transition investments. This financial firepower is a resource that enables strategic optionality—the ability to invest in multiple transition pathways simultaneously.

**Weaknesses: Capital Allocation History and Renewables Returns.** Shell is prioritising competitive advantages in deep-water oil production, LNG leadership, and trading capabilities while requiring 10–15% IRR hurdles for new projects to improve capital allocation after historically poor performance. The company previously struggled, with approximately $45 billion in capital employed generating poor or negative returns (broken down as $25 billion in chemicals and $20 billion in "Low-Carbon" solutions). This historical weakness in capital discipline explains—and in management's view, justifies—the retreat from renewables that did not meet return thresholds. However, it also reflects a failure of strategic learning: Shell entered renewable markets without the capabilities needed to compete profitably, then exited rather than building those capabilities.

### 4.2 Value Chain Analysis

Shell's value chain is deeply integrated across upstream production, LNG liquefaction, shipping, trading, and downstream retail. Shell wants to become the world's leading integrated gas and LNG business and the most customer-focused energy marketer and trader, while sustaining a material level of liquids production. The strategic logic is to concentrate investment in the segments of the value chain where Shell has genuine VRIN advantages (trading, LNG logistics, deepwater exploration) and divest or reduce exposure where it does not (offshore wind generation, retail household energy, carbon offsets).

This is consistent with a focused differentiation strategy in Porter's (1980) generic strategy framework: Shell is not attempting to be the lowest-cost producer (that role belongs to national oil companies such as Saudi Aramco) nor a broad renewables leader. Instead, it is differentiating on the basis of trading sophistication, LNG flexibility, and integrated supply-chain management.

## 5. The Strategic Dilemma: Coherence vs. Legitimacy

The analysis above reveals a coherent internal logic to Shell's pivot: concentrate on VRIN-based advantages, improve capital discipline, and use LNG as a "bridge fuel" while the energy system transitions. However, this logic faces a serious external legitimacy challenge.

### 5.1 The Transition Credibility Gap

Shell scrapped its 2035 target of a 45% reduction in net carbon intensity, citing "uncertainty in the pace of change in the energy transition." Follow This, an investor group, noted that Shell had completely scrapped its emissions reduction target of 45% by 2035. Shell's CEO responded that attempts to be 'forensic' about Shell's emissions in 2035 are 'perilous'. This retreat is strategically significant: it signals to investors, regulators, and counterparties that Shell's long-term commitments are revisable under financial pressure—which itself undermines the credibility of the 2050 net-zero target.

AXA IM expressed concern about the lack of absolute Scope 3 emissions reduction targets and what it described as an excessive reliance on nature-based offsets. In June 2023, Shell announced a scale-down of its low-carbon investments, from 33% of total investments in 2022 to 15–20% over 2023–25. This reduction in low-carbon investment share is not merely a reallocation of capital—it is a signal about where Shell believes it can generate competitive returns, and that signal is read by markets, regulators, and civil society alike.

### 5.2 The LNG Demand Bet

Shell's strategy is predicated on sustained LNG demand growth. Asian LNG imports are projected to approach 400 million tons by 2030, representing a 36% increase from the 280 million tons in 2024. The IEA reports that about 300 billion cubic metres per year of LNG export capacity—a record—is set to be added by 2030, primarily from the United States and Qatar. This supply surge creates a risk: if LNG supply outpaces demand growth, prices will fall, compressing the margins on which Shell's strategy depends. The IEA further notes that growth in global gas demand is forecast to slow from the pace seen in recent years.

### 5.3 The Valuation Paradox

Shell's strategic pivot is explicitly designed to close the valuation gap with US majors. Yet the valuation gap between Exxon ($490B) and Shell ($220B) reflects divergent investor perceptions of growth stability and risk profiles. The paradox is that Shell's retreat from renewables—intended to improve near-term returns—may reinforce the perception that it is a declining-asset business, which would widen rather than narrow the valuation gap over a longer horizon. The strategic question is therefore not only "what generates returns today?" but "what narrative of future value creation is credible to capital markets?"

## 6. Ansoff Matrix: Where Is Shell Growing?

Using Ansoff's (1957) growth matrix to map Shell's current strategic choices:

- **Market Penetration (existing products, existing markets):** Shell is deepening its LNG position in existing Asian markets—a low-risk, capability-aligned strategy. Shell aims to become the world's leading integrated gas and LNG business.
- **Market Development (existing products, new markets):** Shell is expanding LNG bunkering into maritime shipping. In 2024, Shell delivered a record-breaking 1.1 million tonnes of marine LNG to vessels across the globe, more than double the amount delivered in 2023, reinforcing its dominant position in the global LNG bunkering market.
- **Product Development (new products, existing markets):** Shell is investing in CCS and hydrogen as new low-carbon products for industrial customers. Shell's Polaris CCS project in Canada is designed to capture approximately 650,000 tonnes of CO₂ annually from the Scotford refinery and chemicals complex.
- **Diversification (new products, new markets):** Shell has *retreated* from diversification into offshore wind and retail household energy—reversing the strategic direction of its predecessor strategy. This retreat is the most contested element of the current strategy.

The Ansoff analysis shows that Shell is concentrating on the lower-risk quadrants (penetration and market development) while pulling back from the higher-risk diversification quadrant. This is rational from a short-term risk management perspective but leaves Shell with limited exposure to the fastest-growing segments of the future energy system.

## 7. Stakeholder Analysis

A power/interest matrix reveals conflicting stakeholder demands that underpin the strategic dilemma:

- **High power, high interest:** Institutional shareholders (e.g., BlackRock, AXA IM) demand competitive returns but also increasingly require credible transition plans. Shell raised its structural cost reduction target from $2–3 billion by end-2025 to a cumulative $5–7 billion by end-2028, and enhanced shareholder distributions from 30–40% to 40–50% of cash flow from operations. These moves are directed squarely at this stakeholder group.
- **High power, high interest:** Regulators and courts. The Milieudefensie ruling confirmed that while the court did not impose specific reduction targets on Shell, it affirmed that companies have a duty of care to align their business models with the goals of the Paris Agreement. This is a latent constraint that could become binding as climate litigation evolves.
- **Moderate power, high interest:** Climate NGOs and activist investors (e.g., Follow This), who have successfully used AGM votes and litigation to put transition credibility on the board agenda.
- **High power, moderate interest:** Host governments in LNG-producing and consuming countries, whose energy security interests align with Shell's LNG expansion but whose climate commitments may diverge from it over time.

## 8. Synthesis: Strategic Assessment

The foregoing analysis supports the following synthesis:

**Shell's pivot is internally coherent but externally fragile.** The VRIN analysis confirms that LNG trading and optimisation represent genuine, hard-to-replicate competitive advantages. The Ansoff analysis confirms that Shell is concentrating investment in areas where it has proven capabilities. The financial analysis confirms that this is generating strong cash flows in the near term.

However, the PESTEL and stakeholder analyses reveal three structural vulnerabilities:

1. **Regulatory escalation risk:** Rising carbon prices (EU ETS at €80.66/tonne in 2024 and trending upward) and the CS3D will progressively increase the cost of Shell's fossil fuel operations. The Milieudefensie ruling, while tactically favourable, confirmed the existence of a duty of care that could be operationalised by future courts or legislation.

2. **Demand-side transition risk:** The LNG demand bet is plausible over a 10-year horizon but becomes increasingly uncertain beyond 2035, precisely as Shell's current investment cycle matures. After 2030, gas demand in Europe is anticipated to enter a phase of gradual decline as the energy mix shifts further toward renewables.

3. **Legitimacy and talent risk:** The scrapping of the 2035 emissions target and the reduction of low-carbon investment share from 33% to 15–20% erode Shell's credibility as a transition leader. This matters not only for regulatory relations but for talent attraction: the engineers and data scientists who will build the next generation of energy systems are choosing employers partly on the basis of mission alignment.

## 9. Recommendations

The following recommendations are derived directly from the analytical findings above. Each recommendation addresses a specific gap identified in the analysis.

### Recommendation 1: Establish a Credible, Absolute Scope 3 Emissions Pathway

*Rationale:* The legitimacy analysis shows that Shell's intensity-based targets are widely perceived as insufficiently ambitious and create legal and reputational exposure. Carbon intensity metrics technically allow companies to mask their total emissions growth, whereas absolute emissions metrics are more transparent and provide a straightforward measure of total emissions emitted. Shell should commit to absolute Scope 3 reduction milestones—not merely intensity improvements—tied to its LNG portfolio's carbon content. This would directly address the credibility gap identified by AXA IM and Follow This, and reduce the risk of future litigation under the duty-of-care standard affirmed by the Dutch Court of Appeal.

### Recommendation 2: Invest in Transition-Enabling Capabilities Rather Than Transition-Replacing Assets

*Rationale:* The VRIN analysis shows that Shell's competitive advantage lies in trading, optimisation, and complex logistics—not in owning generation assets. Rather than owning offshore wind farms (where it lacks competitive advantage), Shell should position itself as the *trading and optimisation layer* of the renewable energy system: managing intermittency, providing storage, and connecting renewable supply to industrial demand. Shell's "more value, less emissions" strategy requires competitive advantages and reasonable returns for low-carbon investments, moving away from direct renewable generation toward intermediary roles in power trading and storage. This is the correct strategic direction but needs to be pursued more aggressively and communicated more clearly as a long-term growth narrative.

### Recommendation 3: Scale CCS Investment as a Differentiating Capability

*Rationale:* CCS is one area where Shell has demonstrated operational capability (Quest project, Polaris FID) and where its industrial customer base creates genuine demand. Shell's investments in carbon capture and storage, hydrogen and renewable energy will help it produce LNG with lower carbon intensity in the future. Scaling CCS from a compliance tool into a commercial service offered to third-party industrial emitters would create a new revenue stream aligned with Shell's existing engineering capabilities and consistent with the Paris Agreement's recognition that carbon removal will be necessary. This transforms CCS from a cost centre into a value driver.

### Recommendation 4: Reduce LNG Supply Concentration Risk Through Portfolio Diversification

*Rationale:* The LNG demand analysis shows a coming supply surge. About 300 billion cubic metres per year of LNG export capacity is set to be added by 2030, primarily from the United States and Qatar. If supply outpaces demand, Shell's LNG margins will compress. Shell should use its trading capabilities to build optionality across LNG, green hydrogen, and ammonia—molecules that share infrastructure and logistics characteristics but serve different decarbonisation pathways. This hedges the LNG demand bet without requiring Shell to abandon its core competence.

### Recommendation 5: Close the Valuation Gap Through Narrative, Not Only Buybacks

*Rationale:* Shell's current approach to the valuation gap is primarily financial (buybacks, dividend growth). Shell raised shareholder distributions from 30–40% to 40–50% of cash flow from operations, continuing to prioritise share buybacks. While this improves near-term shareholder returns, it does not address the underlying perception that Shell is a declining-asset business. Shell needs to articulate a compelling 2035–2050 growth narrative—centred on LNG-to-hydrogen transition, CCS-as-a-service, and power trading—that gives long-term investors a reason to re-rate the stock on future earnings potential, not just current cash return.

## 10. Conclusion

Shell's strategic situation is one of genuine complexity, not mere indecision. The company has correctly identified its VRIN-based competitive advantages and is concentrating capital accordingly. The "value over volume" logic is financially coherent in the near term. However, the analysis reveals that the strategy's external legitimacy—with regulators, climate-oriented investors, and civil society—is being eroded faster than internal returns are improving. The scrapping of the 2035 target, the reduction in low-carbon investment, and the retreat from offshore wind have created a credibility deficit that the current buyback programme cannot repair.

The recommendations above do not require Shell to abandon LNG or accept below-threshold returns on low-carbon investments. They require Shell to use its genuine competitive advantages—trading, optimisation, CCS capability, and balance-sheet strength—to position itself as an *enabler* of the energy transition rather than a reluctant participant in it. That repositioning is both strategically coherent and commercially necessary for long-term value creation.
