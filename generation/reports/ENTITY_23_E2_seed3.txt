# Manchester United: A Strategic Case Study in Institutional Decline and Structural Renewal

## Abstract

This case study examines the strategic situation facing Manchester United Football Club between 2013 and 2025 — a period characterised by ownership-induced financial engineering, chronic managerial instability, on-pitch deterioration, and a governance structure misaligned with sporting success. The analysis applies Johnson, Scholes & Whittington's Strategic Drift model, Porter's Five Forces, the Resource-Based View (RBV), and McKinsey's 7-S Framework to diagnose the root causes of decline. It concludes with a sequenced action plan grounded in the evidence.

---

## 1. Introduction and Case Context

Manchester United is one of the world's most commercially powerful sporting institutions. Forbes valued Manchester United at approximately $6.5 billion in 2024, reflecting unmatched recognition and sponsor appeal. Yet this commercial prestige now exists in jarring contradiction with sporting reality. Manchester United finished 15th in the Premier League in 2024–25 — their worst league placement in Premier League history — breaking their previous all-time low of eighth place set the season before. The 2024–25 season saw United set records for fewest wins (10), fewest goals (42), fewest points (39), and most defeats (17) in a single Premier League campaign.

This is not merely a bad season. It is the culmination of over a decade of strategic drift: a misalignment between ownership priorities, governance structures, resource allocation, and sporting ambition. The strategic question this case addresses is: **Why has one of football's most resource-rich clubs experienced sustained competitive decline, and what must change structurally to reverse it?**

## 2. Background: The Glazer Legacy and the Debt Architecture

To understand Manchester United's current predicament, one must begin with the ownership structure imposed in 2005. The leveraged buyout (LBO) represents one of the most controversial ownership approaches, in which acquisition debt is placed on the purchased asset rather than remaining with the purchasing entity. The financing structure included both senior debt secured against club assets and payment-in-kind (PIK) loans with interest rates of 14.25% per annum, creating an immediate annual interest burden of £62 million for a previously debt-free institution.

By relying upon debt, the Glazers avoided risking too much of their personal wealth, shifting the financial burden of the leveraged buyout to the club itself. The consequence has compounded over two decades. The club's total debt has climbed to a record £1.29 billion ($1.72 billion), driven by fresh borrowing and the long-standing legacy of leveraged ownership. The long-term Glazer debt remains at $650 million, short-term borrowing has risen to £295.7 million, and more than £500 million is owed on transfers.

This debt architecture is strategically significant because it has diverted cash flows away from reinvestment in playing resources and infrastructure. Supporters have long criticised the leveraged takeover that saddled the club with significant debt, arguing it has constrained investment in infrastructure and contributed to uneven sporting performance since the retirement of Sir Alex Ferguson. The Old Trafford stadium — once the "Theatre of Dreams" — was left off the list for hosting UEFA Euro 2028 matches due to its deteriorating condition, and has suffered embarrassments including a roof leak during a match against Arsenal.

## 3. Analytical Framework 1: Strategic Drift (Johnson, Scholes & Whittington)

Johnson, Scholes and Whittington's concept of **Strategic Drift** describes how organisations incrementally adjust their strategy in response to environmental changes but fail to make the transformative shifts required when the environment changes fundamentally. The result is a widening gap between strategy and environment, eventually culminating in crisis or transformation.

Manchester United's trajectory maps precisely onto this model across four phases:

**Phase 1 — Fit (1986–2013):** Under Sir Alex Ferguson's 27-year reign, United's strategy — built on youth development (the "Class of '92"), aggressive reinvestment, and a clear footballing identity — was tightly aligned with the competitive environment of English football. This produced 13 Premier League titles and two Champions League trophies.

**Phase 2 — Incremental Drift (2013–2020):** From David Moyes to Ruben Amorim, United have gone through 10 permanent and interim managers in just 13 years, a stark contrast to the consistency and dominance under Ferguson's remarkable 27-year reign. Each managerial appointment represented a reactive, incremental adjustment rather than a coherent long-term strategy. For most of the post-Ferguson period, executive vice-chairman Ed Woodward led football operations without a sporting director, creating a disconnect between the manager's short-term needs and a long-term squad-building strategy. The club's strategy drifted: it continued to spend heavily on transfers (reactive to each manager's preferences) without a coherent sporting vision to anchor those decisions.

**Phase 3 — Flux (2020–2024):** The contradictions became impossible to ignore. Boardroom changes, ownership controversies, and recruitment inefficiencies muddled Manchester United's strategic vision. The club lacked a clear footballing identity that transcended managerial spells. The appointment and sacking of Ole Gunnar Solskjaer, the brief interim tenure of Ralf Rangnick, and the protracted failure of Erik ten Hag's project each reflected a club unable to commit to a direction.

**Phase 4 — Transformative Change Attempt (2024–present):** Sir Jim Ratcliffe's INEOS completed a partial acquisition in February 2024. INEOS accepted a request by the Board to be delegated responsibility for the management of the Club's football operations, including all aspects of the men's and women's football operations and Academies, alongside two seats on the Manchester United PLC board. This represents the structural intervention that Johnson et al. identify as necessary to escape drift — but as the analysis below shows, the transformation remains incomplete and internally contradictory.

**The inferential link:** Strategic Drift theory predicts that organisations that fail to make transformative change eventually face a crisis sufficiently severe to force it. United's 15th-place finish in 2024–25 constitutes that crisis moment. The question is whether INEOS's restructuring is genuinely transformative or merely another incremental adjustment dressed as revolution.

## 4. Analytical Framework 2: Porter's Five Forces

Porter's Five Forces framework maps the competitive pressures shaping an industry's attractiveness and a firm's strategic position within it. Applied to elite European football, it reveals why United's position has structurally weakened.

### 4.1 Rivalry Among Existing Competitors (HIGH and Intensifying)

The Premier League's competitive intensity has increased dramatically since the 2000s, driven by sovereign wealth investment (Manchester City, Newcastle), American ownership models (Arsenal, Liverpool), and a new generation of analytically sophisticated clubs. Manchester United faces significant competitive pressure from clubs like Manchester City, Liverpool, and other top European teams. Crucially, the financial gap between Champions League participants and non-participants is enormous and widening. The new expanded Champions League format means English clubs have each made between £73 million and £86 million from just playing in the league phase. United's absence from the Champions League in 2024–25 — a consequence of their 15th-place finish — therefore had a direct and material impact on their competitive capacity to recruit and retain elite players.

### 4.2 Threat of New Entrants (MEDIUM-HIGH)

In the football industry, "new entrants" take the form of state-backed or billionaire-funded clubs that can rapidly acquire the resources — players, coaches, infrastructure — previously only available to established elite clubs. Manchester City's transformation under Sheikh Mansour and Newcastle United's acquisition by the Saudi Public Investment Fund demonstrate how quickly competitive positions can be disrupted. The implication for United is that their historical brand advantage is no longer a sufficient moat: rivals can now match or exceed their commercial attractiveness to elite players through superior wages and the promise of Champions League football.

### 4.3 Bargaining Power of Suppliers (HIGH)

In football, "suppliers" are elite players and their agents. The post-Bosman era, combined with the explosion of broadcast revenues, has shifted bargaining power decisively towards players. Manchester United's wage bill, tallying a reported $420 million (£313 million), is the fourth-highest in the Premier League. Yet this expenditure has not delivered proportionate on-pitch returns, suggesting that United has been a price-taker rather than a price-setter in the transfer market — paying premium wages for players whose value was inflated by the club's desperation and lack of coherent recruitment strategy. Sir Jim Ratcliffe himself has acknowledged this, bemoaning the inherited contracts of players like Casemiro, Jadon Sancho, and Antony as a structural drag on the club's finances.

### 4.4 Bargaining Power of Buyers (MEDIUM)

Manchester United's "buyers" are its fans, broadcasters, and commercial partners. Fan loyalty is historically very high — there are over 220 officially recognised Manchester United Supporters' Clubs in more than 60 countries. However, sustained poor performance is beginning to erode this. Around 10 years ago, Manchester United were considered the biggest club on the planet with the most fans across the globe. While they once claimed 600 million fans worldwide, these numbers have dipped recently as the club's success on the pitch has dwindled. This matters commercially: brand equity in football is not static but is partly maintained by on-pitch relevance. If United's sporting decline continues, commercial partners will eventually reassess the value of association with a mid-table club.

### 4.5 Threat of Substitutes (MEDIUM)

The threat of substitutes — other entertainment options competing for fan time and money — is growing in the digital era. However, Manchester United's global brand and deep cultural embeddedness in communities across Asia, Africa, and North America provide a degree of insulation. The more pressing substitute threat is *intra-football*: fans in key markets (China, the US, Southeast Asia) may increasingly shift their allegiance to clubs that are winning trophies and playing Champions League football. This makes on-pitch recovery not merely a sporting priority but a commercial one.

**The inferential link from Five Forces:** The analysis reveals that United's competitive position is being eroded from multiple directions simultaneously. Rivalry is intensifying; supplier power (player wages) is consuming revenues; buyer power (fan engagement) is weakening; and new entrants threaten to permanently displace United from the elite tier. This is not a cyclical downturn but a structural deterioration that requires strategic, not merely tactical, responses.

## 5. Analytical Framework 3: The Resource-Based View (RBV)

The Resource-Based View (Barney, 1991; Wernerfelt, 1984) holds that sustainable competitive advantage derives from resources and capabilities that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN). Applying this lens to Manchester United reveals a club whose most valuable resources are being eroded, while its attempts to build new capabilities have been incoherent.

### 5.1 Valuable and Rare Resources (Strengths)

**Brand equity:** Manchester United has converted 146 years of history into a global marketing powerhouse, turning cultural relevance into commercial scale. The club combines on-pitch drama with a sophisticated sponsorship engine that monetises reach, heritage, and storytelling across continents. This is reflected in commercial revenues that remain the club's largest single revenue stream: Manchester United's commercial revenue increased year-on-year by £30.4 million (10.0%) to £333.3 million, reflecting a £10.6 million (6.1%) sponsorship segment increase and £19.8 million (15.8%) retail and merchandising segment increase. Commercial revenue remained the club's largest segment.

**Stadium and matchday potential:** Old Trafford's 74,310 capacity and the planned new stadium represent a valuable physical asset. Manchester United is building the UK's largest football stadium with a 100,000-seat capacity on land adjacent to Old Trafford, designed by Foster + Partners as part of a £2 billion regeneration project set to complete by 2030–31.

**Academy infrastructure:** The academy at Carrington has historically been a source of elite talent (Beckham, Scholes, Giggs, Rashford). This pipeline represents an inimitable resource built over decades — though recent cost-cutting has threatened it.

### 5.2 Resources Under Threat (Weaknesses)

**Managerial and coaching capability:** The capacity to attract, retain, and develop elite coaching talent has collapsed. Ruben Amorim's numbers were beginning to rise — 31 points from 20 matches in 2025/26 (1.67 per game) is much better than the 27 points from 27 matches in 2024/25 (1.0 per game) — but the 2024–25 season ended with United breaking the record for fewest wins (10), fewest goals (42), fewest points (39), and most defeats (17).

**Squad coherence:** The RBV emphasises that resources must be *deployable* — they must work together as an organisational system. United's squad has been assembled reactively over multiple managerial regimes, resulting in players whose profiles are misaligned with any single tactical system. Analysts say Amorim's 3-4-3 system is the least suited to United's current squad, with the coaching transition underestimated and questions raised about whether there is the time and patience for it to succeed. This is a classic RBV failure: individual player resources may be valuable in isolation, but they do not constitute a coherent capability when assembled without strategic logic.

**Governance and organisational capability:** Dan Ashworth left his role as Manchester United sporting director after just five months, having officially started on 1 July after a lengthy period of gardening leave from Newcastle. The inability to retain the sporting director — the very role designed to provide strategic continuity in recruitment and player development — illustrates the depth of the organisational capability gap.

**The inferential link from RBV:** United's brand equity (its most VRIN resource) is currently subsidising a football operation that is destroying value. The commercial machine is generating record revenues — Manchester United achieved record revenues for 2024/25 of £666.5 million, despite not having Champions League football and finishing 15th in the Premier League — their lowest top-flight finish in 51 years. But this commercial resilience is not indefinitely sustainable if on-pitch performance continues to erode the brand's association with winning. The RBV therefore predicts that without urgent investment in the *organisational capabilities* that convert financial resources into sporting performance, United's brand equity will depreciate.

## 6. Analytical Framework 4: McKinsey's 7-S Framework

The 7-S Framework (Peters & Waterman, 1982) examines organisational effectiveness through seven interdependent elements: Strategy, Structure, Systems, Style, Staff, Skills, and Shared Values. It is particularly useful for diagnosing *why* a strategy is failing to translate into results — which is precisely United's problem. The framework reveals that even where individual elements have been addressed (e.g., new Strategy under INEOS), misalignment across the other six elements prevents effective execution.

| Element | Current State | Assessment |
|---|---|---|
| **Strategy** | Transition to data-driven recruitment, cost discipline, youth integration, new stadium | Partially defined but inconsistently executed |
| **Structure** | New executive layer (Berrada as CEO, INEOS oversight) but sporting director role already vacated | Structurally incomplete; accountability gaps remain |
| **Systems** | Redundancy programme, new e-commerce model, Qualcomm shirt sponsorship | Commercial systems improving; football systems (recruitment, scouting) still maturing |
| **Style** | Ratcliffe's industrial cost-cutting ethos clashing with football's cultural norms | Cultural mismatch; youth coaches made redundant, morale damaged |
| **Staff** | High-wage, tactically mismatched squad; key positions (sporting director) unstable | Significant misalignment between staff capabilities and strategic needs |
| **Skills** | Commercial skills strong; tactical/coaching skills in transition | Asymmetric capability profile |
| **Shared Values** | Tension between Glazer financial extraction model and INEOS sporting ambition | Values not yet unified; creates internal incoherence |

The 7-S analysis reveals that the **Strategy** element has been partially updated under INEOS, but the other six elements — particularly Structure, Staff, and Shared Values — remain misaligned. This explains why the strategy is not yet translating into results. Sir Jim Ratcliffe's brutal Manchester United job cuts reportedly "left children in tears" as youth coaches paid the price for INEOS' cost-cutting, with United announcing plans to cut an additional 200 jobs following the 250 job cuts made previously. Cutting youth coaches while simultaneously claiming to prioritise youth development is a textbook 7-S misalignment: the *Style* (cost-cutting) contradicts the *Strategy* (youth integration).

**The inferential link from 7-S:** The 7-S framework predicts that strategies fail not because they are wrong in principle but because the organisation's other elements are not reconfigured to support them. INEOS's strategy is broadly correct — cost discipline, data-driven recruitment, youth integration — but the implementation is incoherent. Until all seven elements are aligned, the strategy will continue to underperform.

## 7. The Financial Paradox: Record Revenue, Record Loss, Record Debt

A critical strategic tension at Manchester United is the simultaneous achievement of record commercial revenues and record debt. This paradox requires explicit explanation because it shapes the constraints within which any strategic recommendations must operate.

Manchester United achieved record revenues of £666.5 million for 2024–25, with total operating expenses down 4.5 per cent and employee expenses down 14.1 per cent (£51.5 million), but still posted net losses of £33 million — down from £113 million in 2024. The improvement in losses is real but insufficient. More concerning is the debt trajectory: Manchester United's net debt has spiralled past $1 billion (£749 million) for the first time ever after an extravagant summer transfer window, with United borrowing £105 million to finance transfer instalments.

The revenue breakdown illuminates the structural vulnerability. Broadcasting revenue sank 22% to $235 million due to the 15th-place finish in the Premier League and competing in the less lucrative Europa League versus the Champions League. This is not a marginal difference: the new expanded Champions League format means English clubs have each made between £73 million and £86 million from just playing in the league phase. United's absence from the Champions League in 2024–25 therefore cost the club somewhere between £37 million and £50 million in direct broadcast income alone — a figure that dwarfs the savings from the redundancy programme.

The new Premier League financial rules — Squad Cost Ratio (SCR) and Sustainability and Systemic Resilience (SSR) — will replace the existing Profitability and Sustainability Rules (PSR) from the 2026/27 season. This regulatory shift adds urgency: United must achieve financial sustainability not merely to avoid PSR sanctions but to comply with an incoming regime that ties squad investment directly to revenue generation. A club with £1.29 billion in debt and a wage bill of £313 million cannot indefinitely sustain a squad capable of winning trophies without Champions League revenue.

**The inferential link:** The financial analysis demonstrates that on-pitch performance and financial sustainability are not separate problems — they are the same problem. Every year United fails to qualify for the Champions League costs them approximately £75–100 million in combined broadcast income and commercial premium, while simultaneously making it harder to attract the elite players needed to return to the Champions League. This is a negative reinforcing loop: poor performance → reduced revenue → reduced squad quality → poor performance. Breaking this loop is the central strategic imperative.

## 8. The Governance Failure: A Decade Without Strategic Continuity

Underlying all of the above is a governance failure that has been both cause and consequence of United's decline. The club has lacked the institutional architecture to make and sustain long-term strategic decisions about football.

For most of the post-Ferguson period, Ed Woodward led football operations without a sporting director, creating a disconnect between the manager's short-term needs and a long-term squad-building strategy. Woodward was a commercial specialist — he was rightly credited with dramatically growing United's sponsorship revenues from £48.7 million in 2005 to £117.6 million by 2012 — but he had no footballing background. The consequence was a transfer strategy driven by commercial considerations (signing marketable players) rather than sporting ones.

INEOS's restructuring was meant to address this. The appointment of Omar Berrada as CEO, Dan Ashworth as Sporting Director, and Jason Wilcox as Technical Director represented an attempt to professionalise the football operations structure. But Dan Ashworth left his role as Manchester United sporting director after just five months, having officially started on 1 July. It was CEO Omar Berrada who ran point on identifying Ruben Amorim as the club's new head coach, undertaking negotiations with Sporting and making the pitch to the Portuguese manager. The fact that the CEO — not the sporting director — led the manager appointment process suggests that the new structure has not yet produced the clear lines of authority it was designed to create.

## 9. Recommendations and Action Plan

The following recommendations are sequenced according to urgency and interdependency. They are grounded in the analytical frameworks above and the specific evidence presented throughout this case.

### Recommendation 1: Stabilise the Governance Architecture (Immediate — 0–6 months)

**What:** Appoint a permanent, experienced Sporting Director with a clear mandate and a minimum five-year tenure commitment. Define explicit boundaries between the CEO's commercial responsibilities and the Sporting Director's football responsibilities.

**Why the evidence warrants this:** The 7-S analysis shows that structural misalignment is the primary barrier to strategy execution. The RBV shows that organisational capability — specifically the capacity to make coherent, sustained recruitment decisions — is United's most critical missing resource. The Strategic Drift model predicts that transformative change requires institutional commitment, not just personnel changes. The revolving door of sporting directors (Ashworth lasted five months) perpetuates exactly the instability that has characterised the post-Ferguson era. Until governance is stabilised, no football strategy can be executed effectively.

**How:** Model the structure on clubs with proven governance frameworks — Arsenal's use of Edu Gaspar as Sporting Director (2019–2024), or Liverpool's use of Michael Edwards — where the sporting director provides multi-year strategic continuity across managerial changes.

---

### Recommendation 2: Commit to a Defined Playing Philosophy and Align Squad Investment Accordingly (6–18 months)

**What:** Formally adopt Amorim's 3-4-3 system as the club's playing identity for a minimum three-year period, and use the next two transfer windows exclusively to acquire players whose profiles match this system's requirements. Simultaneously, accelerate the exit of players whose profiles are permanently misaligned with this system.

**Why the evidence warrants this:** The RBV shows that individual player resources only create competitive advantage when they constitute a coherent *capability* — a system in which the whole is greater than the sum of its parts. Analysts say Amorim's 3-4-3 system is the least suited to United's current squad, and that the coaching transition has been underestimated. This is a solvable problem — but only if the club commits to the system long enough to recruit for it. The post-Ferguson era's central failure has been changing tactical philosophy with each managerial appointment, leaving the squad perpetually misaligned. The 2024 investment by INEOS and the subsequent appointment of a new football executive team may represent the most significant structural change since Ferguson's retirement. The challenge now is to implement a unified, multi-year sporting project, disciplined negotiation, and a recruitment model that integrates with the manager's philosophy while respecting financial sustainability.

**How:** The Sporting Director (Recommendation 1) should work with Amorim to produce a three-year squad profile document specifying positional requirements, age profiles, and budget envelopes. All transfer decisions should be evaluated against this document.

### Recommendation 3: Resolve the Debt Architecture Through Stadium Revenue (12–36 months)

**What:** Accelerate the new stadium project and secure long-term financing that converts the stadium investment into a revenue-generating asset, thereby breaking the negative reinforcing loop between debt servicing and squad investment.

**Why the evidence warrants this:** The Five Forces analysis shows that United's matchday revenue capacity is a structural competitive disadvantage relative to clubs with modern, larger stadiums. The new stadium plan addresses this directly. Oxford Economics projects that the stadium and wider regeneration project have the potential to deliver an additional £7.3 billion per year to the UK economy, creating 92,000 new jobs and attracting 1.8 million additional visitors yearly to Greater Manchester. Manchester United's Chief Operating Officer Collette Roche says the club will pay £2 billion to build a new Old Trafford, but the UK government will be asked to support funding for local infrastructure and area regeneration. Securing government co-investment in infrastructure (roads, transport, public realm) reduces the club's net capital outlay and improves the project's financial viability.

**How:** Pursue a naming rights deal (estimated to generate £20–30 million per year based on comparable deals) and explore infrastructure bonds or a public-private partnership structure for the regeneration elements. The additional matchday revenue from a 100,000-seat stadium (versus 74,310 currently) would, at current ticket pricing, generate an additional £30–50 million per season — partially offsetting the debt servicing costs.

### Recommendation 4: Protect and Reinvest in the Academy as a Financial and Sporting Asset (Immediate — ongoing)

**What:** Reverse the cuts to academy coaching staff and increase investment in youth development infrastructure, framing this as both a sporting and financial strategy under the new PSR/SCR regime.

**Why the evidence warrants this:** Under the incoming Squad Cost Ratio rules, clubs' squad costs will be limited to a proportion of their revenues, incentivising the development and use of homegrown players. Academy graduates count favourably in these calculations — they represent high-value, low-cost squad members. The RBV identifies the academy as an inimitable resource built over decades: it cannot be replicated quickly by rivals. Manchester United's academy continues to produce high-quality prospects, many of whom are expected to become key figures in coming years. The integration of youth players into the first team has become a priority, with several academy graduates earning regular minutes. Cutting academy coaches — as INEOS did in 2024 — destroys this resource for short-term cost savings that are dwarfed by the long-term value of a single academy graduate. Selling Alejandro Garnacho (an academy product) generated approximately £40–46 million in pure profit under PSR accounting — a figure that illustrates the financial return on academy investment.

### Recommendation 5: Restore Champions League Qualification as the Non-Negotiable Sporting KPI (Ongoing)

**What:** Define Champions League qualification (top four or five in the Premier League) as the minimum acceptable sporting outcome, and structure all football decisions — squad investment, managerial tenure, financial planning — around this objective.

**Why the evidence warrants this:** The financial analysis demonstrates that Champions League participation is not merely a prestige matter but a financial imperative. United's absence from Europe would be a financial disaster for most clubs, but United's finances are just about holding up — despite overall debt rising to £1.29 billion with no new stadium for that vast borrowing. The new Champions League format means that participation generates £73–86 million in broadcast income for English clubs before a ball is kicked. This revenue directly enables squad investment, which in turn enables Champions League qualification — a positive reinforcing loop that is the mirror image of the negative loop United currently occupies. Manchester United qualified for the Champions League for 2026–27 by finishing third in the Premier League in 2025–26 with 71 points. This return to European football is an encouraging sign that the loop can be broken — but sustaining it requires the structural changes outlined in Recommendations 1–4.

## 10. Conclusion: From Drift to Direction

Manchester United's strategic crisis is not, at its core, a football problem. It is an organisational problem — a decade-long failure to build the institutional architecture capable of translating exceptional commercial resources into sustained sporting excellence. The Strategic Drift model explains how this happened: incremental adjustments to a fundamentally misaligned strategy, repeated until crisis forced a reckoning. The Five Forces analysis shows that the competitive environment has become structurally more demanding, reducing the margin for error. The RBV reveals that United's most valuable resource — its brand equity — is being eroded by the very sporting failures that the brand's commercial revenues are subsidising. And the 7-S Framework demonstrates that INEOS's strategic intentions, however sound in principle, are being undermined by misalignment across structure, staff, style, and shared values.

The recommendations presented here are not individually novel. What is novel — and what the frameworks collectively demand — is their *sequencing and integration*. Governance stability (Recommendation 1) is the precondition for strategic commitment (Recommendation 2), which is the precondition for financial sustainability (Recommendations 3 and 4), which is the precondition for sporting success (Recommendation 5). Each step is necessary but not sufficient without the others. This is the coherent chain that Manchester United's leadership must now construct — not as separate initiatives, but as a single, unified transformation programme.

The evidence suggests that the ingredients for recovery exist: record commercial revenues, a new stadium project, a return to Champions League football, and a manager whose system, given time and the right players, has been shown to work. What has been missing is the strategic architecture to connect these ingredients. Building that architecture is the work of the next three years.
