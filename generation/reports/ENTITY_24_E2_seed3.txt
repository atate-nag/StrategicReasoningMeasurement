# Mercedes-Benz at the Crossroads: Strategic Analysis of the Electrification Dilemma and the Path to Profitable Luxury Leadership

---

## 1. Executive Summary

Mercedes-Benz Group AG faces one of the most consequential strategic inflection points in its 138-year history. Having staked its future on an aggressive all-electric transition, the company has been forced into a public and costly reversal: the automaker had planned for EVs to account for half its sales by 2025 but reversed course amid slowing demand. Simultaneously, for 2024, the Mercedes-Benz Group reported revenue of €145.6 billion (down 4.5% year-on-year), EBIT of €13.6 billion (down 30.8% year-on-year), and net profit of €10.4 billion (down 28.4% year-on-year). This case study diagnoses the strategic situation using Porter's Five Forces, the Resource-Based View (RBV), Porter's Generic Strategies, and the Ansoff Matrix. The analysis reveals a coherent chain of causation: an over-committed electrification timeline, a misaligned EV product portfolio, over-dependence on a weakening Chinese market, and a brand identity under siege from digitally-native Chinese rivals. The case concludes with a structured action plan to restore profitability while managing the long-run transition.

---

## 2. Company Background and Strategic Context

Mercedes-Benz Group AG, headquartered in Stuttgart, Germany, is one of the world's foremost luxury automotive manufacturers. The brand with the star stands for "Desire for Iconic Luxury," combining its strong roots as a luxury automobile manufacturer that has created numerous style-defining icons with a pioneering spirit. By 2024, the Mercedes-Benz brand was valued at an impressive $58.8 billion, underscoring its significant global influence and market standing.

In 2021, the company underwent a strategic realignment, separating from Daimler Truck to become a pure-play luxury car and van manufacturer, and simultaneously announced an ambition to go fully electric by 2030. In 2021, the firm promised to go all-electric by 2030, where market conditions allow, and set an interim target of 50 percent electrified vehicle sales by the middle of the decade. This was a bold strategic bet—and one that has since unravelled under the pressure of market realities.

## 3. The Strategic Situation: Problem Identification

### 3.1 The Electrification Reversal

The most visible manifestation of Mercedes-Benz's strategic crisis is its EV performance. While the brand launched 25 new models in 2023–2025, including the all-electric E-Class, its early EVs—like the EQE and EQS—struggled with high prices, limited charging infrastructure, and underwhelming sales (a 23% decline in BEV sales in 2024). The scale of the EV collapse is striking: EQB sales were down 36% for the year, while the EQE lineup fell 39%. The range-topping EQS lineup was even harder hit as sales plummeted 52% to a disappointing 6,963 units.

The reasons for EV failure are not merely cyclical. The EQS is the perfect storm of electric, luxury and lackluster desire from its target audience. That has made the depreciation curve very steep on these cars to the point where a used 2023 can be had for as cheap as $33,000. The product itself—styled as a radical departure from Mercedes convention—alienated the core luxury buyer. This matters strategically: it demonstrates that the company's first-generation EV strategy suffered from a product-market misalignment, not simply an external demand shortfall.

### 3.2 The China Dependency Problem

The 3% decline to 1.98 million vehicles was led by a slump in deliveries in China, its largest market, where consumers are shunning models like the S-Class and Maybach in favour of local brands. Sales in the country dropped 7% to 683,600 cars from a year earlier. This is not a temporary correction. Mercedes-Benz, BMW, and Audi still command the legacy internal-combustion space, but NIO, Li Auto, XPeng, and Huawei-backed AITO carve out leadership in the premium BEV arena. The premium Chinese brands' share of the premium segment roughly tripled in just two years, to over a fifth of the Chinese market by mid-2024, representing a structural competitive shift rather than a cyclical dip.

### 3.3 The Financial Deterioration

The Mercedes-Benz Cars division reported an adjusted EBIT of €8.7bn, a decline from €14.3bn in 2023, primarily due to lower sales volumes, particularly in China, negative net pricing, and an unfavourable model mix. The phrase "negative net pricing" is analytically significant: it reveals that Mercedes has been forced to discount, which directly erodes the brand's luxury positioning—the core of its competitive advantage. Looking ahead, Mercedes-Benz anticipates group revenue in 2025 to be slightly below the prior-year level, with group EBIT expected to decline significantly.

## 4. External Environment Analysis: Porter's Five Forces

Porter's (1980) Five Forces framework examines the structural attractiveness of the industry and the competitive pressures acting upon a firm. Applied to Mercedes-Benz's luxury automotive context, it reveals why the current environment is particularly hostile.

### Force 1: Competitive Rivalry — HIGH

The luxury automotive segment is intensely contested. BMW sold more luxury EVs than the combined tallies of Mercedes-Benz and Audi in 2024, a payoff from early modular-platform bets and thermal-management patents. In the US market, Mercedes-Benz moved a record 88,259 passenger cars in Q4 2024, but the company behind the three-pointed star couldn't catch rival BMW, which sold 371,346 cars last year versus Mercedes' 324,528. In China, the rivalry is compounded by domestic insurgents: Li Auto delivered 47,774 vehicles in June 2024 alone, up 46.7% year-over-year, illustrating the velocity at which Chinese premium brands are scaling. The inference is clear: Mercedes faces a two-front war—against established German peers in Western markets and against fast-moving Chinese OEMs in its largest growth market.

### Force 2: Threat of New Entrants — MODERATE TO HIGH

Historically, the capital intensity of automotive manufacturing created formidable entry barriers. However, the shift to electric vehicles has lowered these barriers by reducing powertrain complexity. Chinese EV entrants like NIO, Li Auto, and Huawei-backed AITO have demonstrated that technology-first, software-defined vehicles can credibly compete in the premium segment without decades of heritage. The launch of the Tesla Model S in 2012 challenged European luxury automakers by shifting consumer perceptions of automotive excellence toward digital features. The likes of BYD and NIO are following that playbook by touting their lead in software and battery technology. This represents a paradigm shift in what constitutes a luxury car, and it weakens the traditional moat that heritage brands like Mercedes relied upon.

### Force 3: Bargaining Power of Buyers — HIGH

Customers in the premium and luxury automotive segment, where Mercedes-Benz operates, wield considerable bargaining power. This is largely due to the wide array of high-quality alternatives available from rivals such as BMW, Audi, and Lexus. The EV transition has intensified this power further: in early 2024, adjustments in EV pricing by major manufacturers demonstrated how this dynamic amplifies customer leverage. Concerns about charging infrastructure and battery costs in 2023 also contributed to heightened price sensitivity among even luxury buyers. The consequence is the "negative net pricing" that depressed Mercedes' 2024 margins—buyers extracted discounts that the company felt compelled to offer.

### Force 4: Bargaining Power of Suppliers — MODERATE

Mercedes-Benz has established a very large network of approximately 4,000 direct suppliers in Asia, Europe, and North America. This diversification reduces dependence on any single supplier. However, the transition to EVs introduces new dependencies on battery cell manufacturers and semiconductor suppliers, where concentration is high and Mercedes lacks the vertical integration of Tesla or BYD. This is a growing structural risk.

### Force 5: Threat of Substitutes — LOW TO MODERATE

Urban transit, congestion pricing and 56% urbanisation in 2024 reduce private-car need, yet luxury demand persists as status and convenience resist full substitution. In the luxury segment specifically, the car is a status symbol as much as a transport tool, which insulates Mercedes from complete substitution. However, high-quality used Mercedes vehicles, particularly as EV depreciation accelerates, present a credible substitute for new sales.

**Five Forces Summary:** The overall industry structure is unfavourable and deteriorating for Mercedes. The combination of intensifying rivalry, rising buyer power, and the lowered entry barriers created by electrification creates a structural profit squeeze. This explains *why* Mercedes' financial performance has deteriorated—the industry forces have shifted against it.

## 5. Internal Analysis: Resource-Based View (RBV) and VRIO

The Resource-Based View (Barney, 1991) holds that sustainable competitive advantage derives from resources that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Organised (VRIO). This analysis identifies where Mercedes' true competitive moat lies—and where it has been investing in the wrong resources.

### 5.1 Brand Heritage and Luxury Positioning — Sustained Competitive Advantage

Mercedes-Benz's brand is its most powerful strategic asset. The "Best Global Brands 2023" list showcases Mercedes-Benz's relentless pursuit of excellence. The brand has seen a remarkable 9% increase in its value compared to the previous year, now standing at an impressive $61.4 billion. A brand valued at over $61 billion, built over 138 years, is rare, inimitable, and deeply organised into every customer touchpoint—from dealer experience to after-sales service. This resource satisfies all VRIO criteria and constitutes a sustained competitive advantage.

Critically, the brand's value is *differentiated* from pure volume. Top-End vehicle mix, including AMG, Maybach, G-Class, S-Class, and EQS, reached an estimated 22 percent of 2024 sales, improving margins. Where Mercedes has leaned into its brand heritage—AMG performance, Maybach ultra-luxury, G-Class iconic design—it has maintained pricing power. Top-End vehicle sales grew by 34% quarter-on-quarter to 82,800 units driven by Mercedes-AMG, S-Class and G-Class sales underpinned by solid demand in the United States in particular. This is the most important insight from the internal analysis: the brand's strength is concentrated in its top tier, and the financial evidence confirms this is where value is being created.

### 5.2 Engineering and R&D Capability — Potential Competitive Advantage

In 2024, Mercedes-Benz spent around €10.5 billion on R&D, focusing on autonomous driving, electrification, and digital ecosystems. This is a substantial resource commitment. Mercedes-Benz signed off on an investment plan for the years 2022 to 2026 of more than €60 billion for the transformation into a software-driven and emissions-free future. However, R&D investment is only a competitive advantage if it translates into products that customers value. The EQS failure demonstrates that engineering excellence alone is insufficient—the product must be *positioned* in alignment with the brand's luxury identity. The new CLA, built on the MMA platform with 800V architecture and MB.OS, represents a more strategically coherent application of these R&D resources. Powered by an advanced 800-volt electric architecture, the new CLA maximises performance, efficiency and can significantly reduce charging times. With DC fast charging up to 320 kW, it is possible to add 100 miles of range in just five minutes.

### 5.3 MB.OS Digital Platform — Emerging Competitive Advantage

The proprietary Mercedes-Benz Operating System (MB.OS) will help the German automaker control customer data, develop targeted offerings, and strengthen brand identity. The automaker has also mentioned that they plan on generating as much as €1 billion in EBIT profits from its digital services as early as 2025. Software-defined vehicles create recurring revenue streams through over-the-air updates and feature subscriptions—a fundamentally different business model from the one-time hardware sale. If MB.OS achieves genuine differentiation, it could become a rare and inimitable resource. However, it remains nascent and unproven at scale.

### 5.4 Manufacturing Network — Competitive Parity

The Mercedes-Benz Group operates over 30 manufacturing facilities worldwide. This global footprint provides operational flexibility but does not constitute a distinctive advantage in itself—BMW and Audi possess comparable networks. The RBV analysis therefore concludes that manufacturing is a threshold resource, not a source of competitive advantage.

**RBV Summary:** Mercedes' sustainable competitive advantage resides in its brand heritage and luxury positioning, particularly in the Top-End segment. Its engineering capability is valuable but must be strategically directed. The MB.OS platform is a potential future advantage. The implication is that strategy should double down on what is genuinely rare and inimitable—the brand—rather than competing on technology commodities where Chinese rivals have structural cost advantages.

## 6. Competitive Strategy Analysis: Porter's Generic Strategies

Porter (1985) identifies three generic competitive strategies: Cost Leadership, Differentiation, and Focus. A firm that fails to commit to one risks becoming "stuck in the middle," achieving neither cost leadership nor differentiation.

Mercedes-Benz has historically pursued a **Focused Differentiation** strategy—competing in the luxury automotive segment (focus) by offering superior quality, engineering, and brand prestige (differentiation). Mercedes-Benz has employed focused market generic differentiation strategy, making their cars appear more luxurious than the models of rivals in the same range. The focused differentiation strategy is intended to aid the organisation's strategic goal of achieving superior customer experience.

The strategic crisis of 2023–2024 can be interpreted as a *temporary strategic drift* away from this position. The first-generation EQ vehicles (EQS, EQE) attempted to compete in a new technology category but did so with products that were neither the most technologically advanced (Tesla held range and software advantages) nor the most brand-coherent (the radical styling alienated traditional Mercedes buyers). This is a textbook "stuck in the middle" outcome—the EQ lineup was too expensive to compete on value, yet insufficiently differentiated to command premium pricing on brand alone.

The evidence supports this interpretation: Mercedes-Benz's electrification strategy has been a mixed bag. While the brand launched 25 new models in 2023–2025, including the all-electric E-Class, its early EVs—like the EQE and EQS—struggled with high prices, limited charging infrastructure, and underwhelming sales. The company's response—the "Next Level Performance" strategy—represents a return to focused differentiation. As part of its sharpened strategy, Mercedes-Benz aims to grow the sales share of its Top-End vehicles by around 60% by 2026 versus 2019 and intends to achieve higher quality growth and a further significant increase in profitability and resilience. This is a strategically coherent move: retreat from volume competition and concentrate resources on the segment where the brand's differentiation is most potent and margins are highest.

## 7. Growth Strategy Analysis: Ansoff Matrix

The Ansoff (1957) Matrix maps growth strategies across two dimensions: products (existing vs. new) and markets (existing vs. new). It provides a framework for evaluating Mercedes' options for restoring revenue growth.

### Quadrant 1: Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets)

In the US market, Mercedes has demonstrated that market penetration with its existing top-end portfolio is viable. The G-Class achieved its best-ever sales quarter after new model launches in 2024, including the all-new electric G-Class. Furthermore, Mercedes-AMG sales in Q4 resulted in a best-ever quarter, thanks to further improved product availability and customer demand especially for the Mercedes-AMG GT and Mercedes-AMG GLC models. The US market thus offers a penetration opportunity: by improving product availability and leveraging dealer network strength, Mercedes can extract more value from an already-receptive market.

### Quadrant 2: Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets)

India represents a compelling market development opportunity. Mercedes-Benz achieved a record sales milestone of 19,565 cars in India in 2024, highlighting strong consumer demand in the region. Strategic investments include inaugurating new showrooms in Faridabad and other tier-2 cities. The company also intends to expand its retail presence with 20 new luxury outlets across emerging markets such as Agra, Kanpur, and Jammu. Franchise partners are expected to invest over INR 450 crore in these initiatives. India's growing upper-middle class, aspirational luxury culture, and relatively low penetration of premium vehicles make it a structurally attractive market development target. The risk is lower here than in China, because Indian consumers are not yet served by sophisticated domestic luxury EV brands.

### Quadrant 3: Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets)

The new CLA on the MMA platform is the centrepiece of Mercedes' product development strategy. The all-new CLA is the first in a completely new family of Mercedes-Benz vehicles and the most intelligent model from the brand to date. The compact sedan sets new standards with intuitive technologies, striking design. Crucially, this car is built on Mercedes' Modular Architecture (MMA) platform, meaning it was designed to be an EV first, and a hybrid vehicle second. This is a fundamental departure from the EQS/EQE approach, where electric powertrains were grafted onto architectures conceived for internal combustion. A purpose-built EV architecture allows genuine engineering differentiation rather than a compromised retrofit.

### Quadrant 4: Diversification (New Products, New Markets)

The MB.OS digital ecosystem represents a diversification play—a new product (recurring digital services and subscriptions) sold to a new customer relationship (post-purchase digital engagement). The new MB.OS architecture underpins powerful 3D graphics and expanded in-car app portfolio. Mercedes-Benz's new OS will give the company new prospects to increase revenues; for example, they can offer subscriptions to virtual features that can be turned on or off by the owner after they purchase the vehicle. This is the highest-risk quadrant in the Ansoff matrix, but also potentially the highest reward: if successful, it transforms Mercedes from a capital-intensive hardware manufacturer into a partially software-driven business with higher-margin recurring revenues.

## 8. Strategic Gap Analysis: Connecting the Frameworks

The three frameworks together form a coherent diagnostic chain:

**Porter's Five Forces** establishes that the external environment has structurally deteriorated: buyer power has risen, Chinese rivals have lowered entry barriers, and competitive rivalry has intensified—particularly in the EV segment. This explains the *direction* of the financial decline.

**The RBV** establishes that Mercedes' sustainable competitive advantage resides in its brand heritage and Top-End vehicle positioning—not in EV technology per se. This explains *where* the company is still generating value (AMG, Maybach, G-Class record sales) versus where it is destroying it (EQ lineup discounting).

**Porter's Generic Strategies** establishes that the first-generation EQ strategy represented a drift toward "stuck in the middle"—neither the technology leader nor the brand-differentiated option. The return to Focused Differentiation via the Top-End strategy is strategically correct.

**The Ansoff Matrix** identifies the specific growth levers available: US market penetration, India market development, CLA product development, and MB.OS diversification.

The **strategic gap** is therefore: Mercedes has the right long-run direction (Focused Differentiation, luxury-first electrification) but faces a near-term profitability crisis caused by the legacy EQ lineup's failure, China exposure, and the cost base of a company still sized for higher volumes.

## 9. Recommendations and Action Plan

The following recommendations are sequenced by time horizon and derive directly from the analytical chain above.

---

### Recommendation 1: Accelerate the Top-End Pivot and Defend Pricing Integrity (Immediate: 0–12 months)

**Rationale:** The RBV analysis identifies the Top-End brand as the sole source of sustained competitive advantage. The Five Forces analysis confirms that buyer power is highest in the volume luxury segment. Porter's Generic Strategies prescribes that Mercedes must deepen its Focused Differentiation rather than compete on volume. The evidence demonstrates that this strategy is already working: it was a record quarter for Mercedes-AMG high-performance models, totalling 16,169 units and representing a 50% increase from the same quarter last year. The G-Class group sales of 10,987 in 2024 exceeded 10,000 units for the first time ever, representing a 12% year-over-year increase.

**Action:** Eliminate all volume-driven discounting on core luxury models. Redirect marketing investment toward Maybach, AMG, and G-Class. Reduce the entry-level model count as planned: in the Entry Luxury segment, Mercedes-Benz will reduce the number of model variants from seven to four while significantly elevating the technological substance of these products. This reduction is not a retreat—it is a strategic concentration of brand equity.

---

### Recommendation 2: Execute the CLA-Led EV Relaunch with Brand Coherence as the Primary Design Principle (12–24 months)

**Rationale:** The product development quadrant of the Ansoff Matrix identifies the new CLA as the critical growth lever. The failure of the EQS demonstrates that EV products must be *brand-coherent* to succeed under the Mercedes badge—they must look, feel, and perform like Mercedes cars, not like technology experiments. The new CLA corrects this: DC fast charging up to 320 kW enables up to 202 miles of range to be added in as little as ten minutes. New generation battery with high energy density and smaller carbon footprint. Combined with conventional Mercedes design language, this product addresses the dual failure of the first-generation EQ lineup: insufficient range/charging performance and insufficient brand coherence.

**Action:** Position the CLA as the entry point to Mercedes luxury electrification, not as a standalone technology product. Use the MB.OS integration as a differentiator that reinforces, rather than replaces, the luxury experience. Ensure the forthcoming electric GLC and C-Class follow the same MMA-platform, brand-first design philosophy.

---

### Recommendation 3: Reduce China Concentration Through Accelerated India and US Investment (12–36 months)

**Rationale:** The Five Forces analysis identifies China as a structurally deteriorating market due to the rise of domestic premium EV brands. The Ansoff Matrix identifies India (market development) and the US (market penetration) as lower-risk growth alternatives. Data indicates that the U.S. could (or should) be a major focal point for Mercedes in the years to come as a means of diversifying out of China.

**Action:** Reduce China's share of total revenue from approximately 34% toward 25% over three years through differential investment allocation. Accelerate the India expansion programme: Mercedes-Benz is aggressively expanding its presence in India's emerging markets, targeting tier 2 and 3 cities with new luxury facilities. Simultaneously, invest in US dealer network capacity and localise production for the US market to hedge against tariff risk: to achieve these savings, the automaker will localise more production in China and the United States to shield itself from geopolitical trade risks, including potential U.S. tariffs on vehicle imports.

---

### Recommendation 4: Execute Structural Cost Reduction to Restore Margin Without Impairing the Brand (Immediate and Ongoing)

**Rationale:** The financial analysis shows EBIT fell 30.8% in 2024. The Five Forces analysis confirms that competitive rivalry and buyer power will continue to pressure margins. The RBV analysis confirms that the brand—not volume—is the source of advantage, meaning Mercedes can afford to be leaner on headcount and model complexity without strategic damage.

**Action:** Implement the announced restructuring programme: by 2025, the company anticipates €2.5 billion in savings from workforce adjustments including voluntary buyouts, reducing hiring, and freezing executive salaries to cut fixed costs. Target the projected €5 billion in annual savings by 2027. Critically, ring-fence R&D spending on the CLA platform and MB.OS—these are the two investments that directly support future competitive advantage. Cost reduction must not become brand destruction.

---

### Recommendation 5: Monetise MB.OS as a Long-Run Revenue Diversification Strategy (24–60 months)

**Rationale:** The Ansoff diversification quadrant identifies MB.OS as the highest-risk but highest-reward strategic option. The RBV analysis identifies it as an *emerging* competitive advantage—not yet proven at scale but potentially rare and inimitable if the proprietary architecture creates genuine lock-in. The target of generating as much as €1 billion in EBIT profits from digital services as early as 2025 is ambitious but directionally correct.

**Action:** Develop a tiered subscription model for MB.OS features—driving assistance, entertainment, performance upgrades—that mirrors the luxury brand's pricing architecture. Partner with premium content providers (as demonstrated at CES 2024 with AI-powered MBUX) to create exclusive in-car experiences unavailable in competitor vehicles. This builds a recurring revenue stream that partially offsets the cyclicality of vehicle sales and deepens customer loyalty.

---

### Recommendation 6: Engage Strategically with China's Premium EV Ecosystem Rather Than Competing Head-On (24–48 months)

**Rationale:** The Five Forces analysis confirms that direct competition with NIO, Li Auto, and AITO in China's premium EV segment is structurally disadvantaged for Mercedes: domestic brands have software superiority, government support, and local consumer preference. The precedent of the IONCHI charging joint venture—SERES Group's Huawei-powered AITO brand is joining IONCHI, the premium charging joint venture between BMW and Mercedes-Benz in China, as an equal 33.3% shareholder—suggests that selective collaboration is already being pursued. This is strategically sound.

**Action:** Extend the collaborative model: pursue technology licensing, platform-sharing, or joint venture arrangements with Chinese premium EV brands for the Chinese domestic market, while maintaining full brand independence in export markets. This allows Mercedes to participate in China's EV growth without the capital intensity of competing directly on software and battery technology where Chinese firms have structural advantages.

## 10. Conclusion

Mercedes-Benz's strategic situation in 2024–2025 is serious but not irreversible. The company's fundamental competitive advantage—a brand valued at over $61 billion, built on 138 years of engineering heritage and luxury positioning—remains intact and is demonstrably generating value in its Top-End segment. The crisis is the product of three compounding strategic errors: an over-ambitious electrification timeline that forced premature, brand-incoherent EV products to market; excessive revenue concentration in a structurally deteriorating Chinese market; and a cost base sized for volumes that the market can no longer sustain.

The analytical chain is clear. Porter's Five Forces explains *why* the environment has become hostile—rising buyer power, Chinese entrants, and intensifying rivalry. The RBV explains *where* Mercedes' real advantage lies—brand heritage and Top-End positioning, not EV technology commodities. Porter's Generic Strategies prescribes *how* to compete—Focused Differentiation, not volume-driven compromise. The Ansoff Matrix identifies *where* to grow—US penetration, India development, CLA product development, and MB.OS diversification.

The six recommendations form a coherent programme: defend and deepen the brand's luxury positioning, relaunch electrification on brand-coherent terms via the new CLA and MMA platform, diversify geographically away from China, restructure costs without impairing brand investment, monetise MB.OS as a recurring revenue stream, and engage collaboratively with China's EV ecosystem rather than competing head-on. Executed in sequence and with strategic discipline, this programme offers Mercedes-Benz a credible path to restoring double-digit Return on Sales margins while positioning the brand for the software-defined, electrified automotive era on terms that leverage—rather than abandon—its most powerful and irreplaceable asset.
