# GE HealthCare Technologies: Navigating the Transition from Industrial Subsidiary to Precision-Care Platform

## A Strategic Case Study

---

## 1. Introduction and Strategic Context

GE HealthCare Technologies Inc. (Nasdaq: GEHC) completed its spin-off from General Electric and began trading on the Nasdaq exchange on January 4, 2023, positioning itself as a leader in precision care, focused on creating a world where healthcare has no limits. This separation was not merely a corporate restructuring exercise; it was a fundamental strategic reorientation. For decades, GE Healthcare operated as a division within one of the world's largest industrial conglomerates, benefiting from GE's capital, brand, and cross-divisional synergies, but also constrained by the parent's priorities and capital allocation logic. Independence brought both freedom and exposure.

For 2024, the company reported nearly $20 billion in total revenue split between about $13 billion in products and about $6.6 billion in services, growing by 2% from the prior year. While these are substantial numbers, the growth rate tells a more complex story. GE HealthCare's annual revenue growth of 4.4% over the last four years was below standards for the healthcare sector, and organic sales performance over the past two years indicates the company may need to make strategic adjustments or rely on M&A to catalyse faster growth.

The **central strategic situation** this case study examines is the following: *GE HealthCare finds itself at a critical inflection point — possessing world-class assets in imaging hardware, a growing AI portfolio, and a global installed base, yet facing structural growth headwinds from China market deterioration, a hardware-centric legacy business model, intensifying competition from software-native rivals, and macro-level tariff disruption. The company must determine how to accelerate its transition from a capital-equipment supplier to a recurring-revenue, AI-enabled precision-care platform before competitors close the gap.*

This analysis proceeds through four linked analytical layers — PESTEL (macro environment), Porter's Five Forces (industry structure), SWOT/VRIN (internal resources), and Ansoff's Growth Matrix (strategic options) — before concluding with an integrated set of recommendations.

## 2. PESTEL Analysis: Mapping the Macro Environment

### 2.1 Political

The geopolitical environment is GE HealthCare's most immediate political risk. GE Healthcare has faced challenges in China due to weakened sales and tariff impacts, with revenue from the region declining by about 15% in 2024. This is not merely a cyclical demand issue; it reflects a structural decoupling between Western medtech companies and the Chinese healthcare procurement system, accelerated by China's anti-corruption campaign targeting hospital procurement officials. GE HealthCare had expected to see positive sales growth in the second half of the year, but the prolonged rollout of the stimulus was "impacting the timing of order and sales."

On the US side, GE Healthcare expects steep tariff costs — driven largely by U.S.-China trade tensions — to slash earnings in 2025, prompting a lowered profit forecast despite strong Q1 results. Arduini estimated the impact of tariffs is expected to be about $1.75 per GE share, but tariff mitigation efforts hopefully will bring this figure down to about $0.85 per share of net, incremental impact.

### 2.2 Economic

The International Monetary Fund (IMF) projected global growth to be 3.2% in 2024, a slight slowdown from 2023, but still indicative of continued demand, with a healthy economic environment typically translating into higher government and private healthcare spending. However, inflationary pressures are squeezing both customers and suppliers simultaneously. Inflationary pressures are increasing raw material and labour costs, with raw material expenses seeing a 15–20% rise in 2024. This dual pressure — customers facing budget constraints while input costs rise — compresses the pricing power that GE HealthCare has historically relied upon.

### 2.3 Social

Demographic tailwinds are unambiguously positive for the medical imaging industry. The world's population aged 65 and older is projected to reach 1.6 billion by 2050, nearly doubling from current levels. An ageing population drives higher demand for diagnostic imaging, patient monitoring, and pharmaceutical diagnostics — GE HealthCare's core segments.

At the same time, the healthcare workforce is under severe strain. According to a 2024 retention and staffing report from Nursing Solutions Inc., nearly half of clinicians report high levels of burnout, and hospitals average a 100% staff turnover approximately every five years. By 2027, the U.S. anticipates a 10% nursing shortage. This social pressure simultaneously creates a problem (constrained operational capacity for hospital customers) and a market opportunity: AI-powered workflow automation tools that reduce clinician burden become a compelling value proposition.

### 2.4 Technological

The AI in healthcare market represents the sector's most consequential technological shift. In July 2024, GE Healthcare teamed up with AWS to develop AI-driven healthcare models to streamline diagnostics and workflows, using AWS's cloud and AI technology to accelerate healthcare app development, enhance diagnostic accuracy, and improve patient care. GE HealthCare's Edison platform is central to this strategy — Edison is described as a vendor-agnostic hosting and data aggregation platform with an integrated AI engine.

Cloud-based enterprise imaging and command-centre software are shifting CAPEX to OPEX, enabling subscription models and recurring revenue; enterprise imaging spend growth outpaces capital equipment in several health systems in 2024–25. This structural shift from hardware transactions to software subscriptions is the defining technological transition GE HealthCare must navigate.

### 2.5 Environmental

Sustainability is an emerging procurement criterion in healthcare. GE HealthCare's 2024 Investor Day highlighted resilient, sustainable practices and products, and a commitment to growing access to care as pillars of strategy. While not yet a primary competitive differentiator, environmental compliance is becoming a licence-to-operate requirement across the 160 countries in which GE HealthCare operates.

### 2.6 Legal

GE HealthCare operates in a highly regulated industry where evolving compliance standards and potential policy shifts present ongoing threats. FDA clearance timelines for AI-enabled medical devices, GDPR and health data privacy regulations across jurisdictions, and the evolving reimbursement landscape for AI-assisted diagnostics all create both barriers to entry (protecting incumbents) and compliance costs that disproportionately burden smaller competitors.

## 3. Porter's Five Forces: Industry Structure Analysis

Porter's (1980) Five Forces framework allows us to assess the structural attractiveness of the medical technology industry and GE HealthCare's position within it.

### 3.1 Threat of New Entrants — **Moderate but Rising**

Traditional barriers to entry in medical imaging are high: regulatory clearance cycles are long, capital requirements are enormous, and clinical relationships are deeply embedded. However, the shift toward software-defined healthcare is lowering barriers in adjacent layers of the value chain. AI startups can develop FDA-cleared algorithms without manufacturing a single piece of hardware, potentially commoditising the analytical layer that sits atop GE HealthCare's equipment. These advantages create barriers to entry and defend GE HealthCare's market share against industry rivals, while competitive pressures arise from open-platform software, cloud-native entrants, and lower-cost Chinese manufacturers.

### 3.2 Bargaining Power of Suppliers — **Moderate**

GE HealthCare's global manufacturing footprint spans 43 manufacturing, assembly, and pharmaceutical production sites across 17 countries. This diversification provides some resilience, but the company remains exposed to concentrated supply chains for specialist components (e.g., rare earth magnets for MRI systems, semiconductor chips for CT detectors). The company's global supply chain is susceptible to disruptions beyond trade tariffs, including natural disasters, pandemics, and logistical bottlenecks.

### 3.3 Bargaining Power of Buyers — **Increasing**

Hospital systems are consolidating rapidly, giving large health networks greater negotiating leverage. However, GE HealthCare is proactively countering this by converting transactional relationships into long-term enterprise partnerships. The strategic partnership with Sutter Health could generate $1 billion in revenue over its seven-year term. In 2024, GE HealthCare introduced approximately 40 new innovations and secured over 50 strategic enterprise deals globally. These multi-year, multi-product agreements reduce buyer power by increasing switching costs and embedding GE HealthCare deeper into hospital operations.

### 3.4 Threat of Substitutes — **Low to Moderate**

For high-acuity diagnostic imaging (MRI, CT, PET/CT), there are no near-term technological substitutes. However, at the margin, point-of-care ultrasound devices — a segment GE HealthCare participates in via its Vscan Air — are substituting for more expensive hospital-based imaging in lower-acuity settings. Point-of-care ultrasound proliferation and the ambulatory shift are restructuring where imaging occurs. This is both a threat to traditional imaging revenue and an opportunity for GE HealthCare's portable device portfolio.

### 3.5 Competitive Rivalry — **High and Intensifying**

GE HealthCare is among the top three global players in diagnostic imaging, with Imaging contributing roughly 54% of 2024 revenue and a 20–25% share in high-end MRI and CT modalities. Its primary rivals are formidable: Siemens Healthineers holds €23 billion in imaging revenue, and its Varian acquisition strengthens oncology workflows; Philips leads in clinical informatics and patient monitoring with a strategic emphasis on connected care; Canon and Fujifilm are aggressive in lower-cost segments.

Alliances and M&A (e.g., Siemens-Varian integration, Fujifilm acquisitions) connect diagnostics to therapy and IT, altering competitive edges. The competitive battlefield is therefore shifting from hardware specifications to integrated clinical workflow solutions — a shift that both threatens GE HealthCare's hardware-centric legacy and opens space for its AI and digital platform investments.

## 4. SWOT Analysis: Internal Capability Assessment

### 4.1 Strengths

**Global scale and installed base:** High-margin recurring revenue from installed base and service contracts represents approximately 50% of revenue. This installed base creates a durable, captive revenue stream and a data flywheel for AI model training that new entrants cannot easily replicate.

**AI leadership and FDA clearances:** GE HealthCare holds a leading number of FDA AI clearances, exceeding 80 as of early 2025. The AIR Recon DL technology exemplifies this — AIR Recon DL is a deep learning-based image reconstruction technology for MRI that enables better image quality and shorter scan times, and has already benefited more than 10 million patients.

**Pharmaceutical Diagnostics vertical integration:** GE HealthCare's unique PDx vertical integration — hardware plus contrast agents — creates a distinctive competitive moat. This integration of imaging hardware with proprietary radiopharmaceuticals positions the company uniquely in the emerging theranostics (therapy + diagnostics) market.

**R&D investment:** A substantial R&D investment of over $1.3 billion annually in 2024 fuels a robust innovation pipeline, with over 25 new product launches planned through 2028.

### 4.2 Weaknesses

**Hardware-centric culture and integration complexity:** Internal weaknesses like integration complexity and a hardware-centric culture risk ceding ground to more agile, software-focused competitors. The organisational DNA of a company that has built MRI machines for a century is not naturally aligned with the rapid iteration cycles of software development.

**Patient Care Solutions underperformance:** The Patient Care Solutions segment posted a 7% organic revenue decline in Q3 2025, with EBIT margin compressing to just 3.7% (down from 10.5% in Q3 2024). This segment — which includes monitoring solutions, anaesthesia delivery systems, and respiratory care devices — is a significant drag on overall performance.

**China dependency:** The medical device maker's revenue from the China market, which constitutes nearly 13% of its total revenue, dropped more than 11% in the quarter ended March 31, 2024. A 13% revenue concentration in a single market undergoing structural procurement reform represents material strategic risk.

### 4.3 Opportunities

**Enterprise partnership model:** The shift from transactional equipment sales to long-term enterprise partnerships represents a structural revenue quality improvement. In 2024, GE HealthCare introduced approximately 40 innovations and closed more than 50 strategic enterprise deals globally, laying a solid foundation for future growth, including the Sutter Health deal in the U.S. and Nuffield Health in the UK.

**Radiopharmaceuticals and precision oncology:** New PET/CT scanners and cyclotron technologies launched by 2025 target radiopharmaceutical applications in prostate cancer and neurology, aligning with GE HealthCare's future prospects in precision care. The acquisition of Nihon Medi-Physics reinforces this: the closing of the acquisition of Nihon Medi-Physics is expected to increase global access to next-generation radiopharmaceuticals.

**AI-driven workflow solutions:** In 2024, health systems' expenses rose 6% year-over-year, including a 5% jump in staff expenses and a 9% increase in supply expenses. This cost pressure makes AI-driven efficiency tools — GE HealthCare's fastest-growing product category — increasingly attractive to hospital CFOs.

### 4.4 Threats

**Tariff escalation:** Bilateral tariffs have already cost the company $375 million in 2025, with impacts escalating to $200 million per quarter by year-end.

**Talent shortages:** A lack of available skilled professionals could hinder innovation and slow down the development and deployment of new technologies. Competition for AI and data science talent from technology giants (Google, Microsoft, Amazon) is particularly acute.

**Competitor innovation velocity:** Failure to quickly adopt and commercialise disruptive technologies could see GE HealthCare lose market share, as competitors are not just innovating but also focusing on faster go-to-market strategies.

## 5. Resource-Based View (VRIN Analysis): Identifying Sustainable Competitive Advantage

Barney's (1991) VRIN framework — assessing whether resources are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable — helps identify which of GE HealthCare's assets constitute genuine sources of sustainable competitive advantage, rather than mere table stakes.

| Resource | Valuable? | Rare? | Inimitable? | Non-substitutable? | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Global installed base (~4M+ devices) | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ (scale + history) | ✅ | **Sustained advantage** |
| FDA AI clearances (80+) | ✅ | ✅ | Partially (replicable over time) | ✅ | **Temporary advantage** |
| PDx vertical integration | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ (capital + regulatory) | ✅ | **Sustained advantage** |
| Edison AI platform | ✅ | Partially | Partially | Partially | **Contested advantage** |
| Brand and clinical trust | ✅ | Partially | ✅ (decades of relationships) | Partially | **Sustained advantage** |
| R&D capability ($1.3B+/year) | ✅ | Partially | Partially | Partially | **Competitive parity** |

The analysis reveals a clear pattern: GE HealthCare's most durable advantages are **physical and relational** — the installed base, the PDx vertical, and clinical brand trust. Its **digital and AI advantages are contested** and require continuous investment to maintain. This distinction is critical for strategy formulation: the company must use its durable physical advantages to fund and protect its contested digital ones.

Core strengths combine scale, regulatory-approved AI, PDx integration, and service reach — each translating into measurable financial and operational benefits.

## 6. Ansoff Matrix: Strategic Growth Options

Ansoff's (1957) Growth Matrix provides a structured framework for evaluating GE HealthCare's strategic growth options across two dimensions: markets (existing vs. new) and products (existing vs. new).

### 6.1 Market Penetration (Existing Products × Existing Markets) — **Near-Term Priority**

The enterprise partnership model is GE HealthCare's primary market penetration vehicle. Rather than competing on unit price for individual equipment sales, the company is deepening its share-of-wallet with existing hospital customers through multi-year, multi-product agreements. The Sutter Health partnership, described as one of GE HealthCare's largest-ever enterprise strategic partnerships and an evolution of a 20-year relationship, aims to increase access to innovative imaging services and create a more seamless and coordinated experience for clinicians and patients.

This strategy is sound: it converts lumpy capital equipment cycles into predictable recurring revenue, increases switching costs, and generates longitudinal data that feeds AI model improvement. The risk is over-concentration — if a small number of mega-deals generate a disproportionate share of revenue, customer concentration risk increases.

### 6.2 Product Development (New Products × Existing Markets) — **Medium-Term Growth Engine**

GE HealthCare's AI innovation pipeline is its most important product development initiative. GE HealthCare has invested approximately $2.2 billion since 2022 in research and development to create a pipeline of innovation that includes cloud-based applications, enhancements to its product portfolio, and new, AI-enabled solutions to address healthcare challenges.

The AIR Recon DL technology exemplifies successful product development: AIR Recon DL covers nearly all MRI clinical procedures and anatomies, enabling better image quality and shorter scan times. This is not a new market — it targets existing radiology customers — but it is a genuinely new product category (AI-enhanced imaging) that commands premium pricing and creates upgrade cycles within the installed base.

Radiopharmaceuticals represent a particularly high-value product development opportunity. GE HealthCare pairs high-sensitivity PET/CT scanners with proprietary radiopharmaceuticals to enable joint diagnostic-and-treatment workflows, supporting precision oncology and molecular-targeted care.

### 6.3 Market Development (Existing Products × New Markets) — **Selective Expansion**

GE HealthCare's China challenge illustrates the risks of over-dependence on a single large market. The strategic response should be a deliberate rebalancing toward other high-growth geographies. GE HealthCare is strengthening its global presence, with a particular focus on emerging markets, complemented by robust performance in the U.S. market, which experienced strong order growth in Q1 2025.

India and the Middle East represent the most compelling near-term market development opportunities: large populations, growing healthcare infrastructure investment, and government mandates for domestic healthcare capacity expansion.

### 6.4 Diversification (New Products × New Markets) — **Long-Term Option**

The theranostics (therapy + diagnostics) market represents GE HealthCare's most ambitious diversification opportunity. GE HealthCare builds on a century of innovation to shape the future of radiology, with strategic collaborations and acquisitions that accelerate innovation, including MIM Software (advanced imaging analytics), Spectronic Medical (radiation oncology planning), and icometrix (AI-enabled precision imaging for neurological diagnosis). These acquisitions collectively signal a move toward integrated oncology workflows — a new market that combines GE HealthCare's imaging hardware with therapeutic planning and delivery software.

## 7. The Core Strategic Problem: A Coherent Diagnosis

Synthesising the four analytical layers above, the core strategic problem can be stated precisely:

**GE HealthCare is caught in a transitional value-chain position.** Its hardware business is mature and geographically concentrated (China risk); its service business is stable but not high-growth; and its digital/AI business is growing rapidly but not yet large enough to offset headwinds elsewhere. The company must accelerate the revenue mix shift toward recurring, software-enabled revenue — but faces three structural impediments:

1. **Organisational inertia**: A hardware-centric culture that optimises for equipment sales cycles, not software subscription metrics.
2. **Tariff-driven margin compression**: A drop in free cash flow for 2025 was attributed exclusively to tariff payments, moving the previously projected floor of $1.75 billion down to at least $1.2 billion. Reduced free cash flow constrains the investment capacity needed to fund the digital transition.
3. **Competitive urgency**: Siemens Healthineers, Philips, and software-native entrants are simultaneously pursuing the same recurring-revenue model, meaning the window for GE HealthCare to establish platform dominance is not indefinite.

The strategic logic is clear: GE HealthCare must use the cash-generative durability of its installed base and service business to fund an accelerated pivot to platform-based, recurring-revenue digital solutions — while simultaneously de-risking its geographic concentration and restructuring its underperforming Patient Care Solutions segment.

## 8. Recommendations and Action Plan

The following recommendations are sequenced by time horizon and connected by a coherent strategic logic: **protect the cash engine → invest in the growth engine → restructure the drag → de-risk the geography**.

---

### Recommendation 1: Accelerate the Enterprise Partnership Model as the Primary Commercial Strategy
**Time horizon: Immediate (0–12 months)**

The enterprise partnership model — exemplified by Sutter Health and UCSF Health — is the single most important commercial innovation GE HealthCare has developed since independence. These enterprise partnerships feature creative and collaborative problem-solving for health systems that builds on GE HealthCare's innovative suite of medical technology and care solutions, with long-term relationships customised for the unique needs and vision of the healthcare system.

**Action steps:**
- Establish a dedicated Enterprise Partnership unit with P&L accountability, separate from the traditional capital equipment sales force, to avoid channel conflict and misaligned incentives.
- Develop a standardised "Care Alliance" framework (as piloted with Sutter Health and UCSF) that can be templated and scaled across the top 200 US health systems and equivalent networks in Europe.
- Shift sales force incentive structures from equipment unit revenue to total contract value and recurring revenue metrics, to align commercial behaviour with the new business model.

**Rationale:** Cloud-based enterprise imaging and command-centre software are shifting CAPEX to OPEX, enabling subscription models and recurring revenue. GE HealthCare must lead this transition rather than resist it.

---

### Recommendation 2: Establish Edison as the Dominant Open Healthcare AI Platform
**Time horizon: 12–24 months**

The Edison platform is GE HealthCare's most strategically important digital asset, but it currently risks being perceived as a proprietary GE tool rather than a true industry platform. GE Healthcare officials described Edison as a vendor-agnostic hosting and data aggregation platform with an integrated AI engine. This vendor-agnostic positioning must be operationalised, not merely marketed.

**Action steps:**
- Open Edison's API ecosystem to third-party AI developers, creating a marketplace model analogous to Apple's App Store — positioning GE HealthCare as the platform owner, not just an app developer.
- Invest in clinical data partnerships with major academic medical centres to generate proprietary training datasets that competitors cannot easily replicate, reinforcing the installed-base data flywheel.
- With hospital systems currently harnessing only about 3% of their available data, GE HealthCare should market Edison explicitly as the infrastructure that unlocks this 97% latent value — a compelling CFO and CIO proposition.
- Partner with NVIDIA (building on existing relationships) to co-develop foundation models for medical imaging, establishing a technical moat through exclusive or preferential access to specialised compute infrastructure.

**Rationale:** Platform economics create winner-take-most dynamics. The company that establishes the dominant AI infrastructure layer in medical imaging will capture disproportionate value as the industry digitises. GE HealthCare's installed base gives it a unique starting position — but only if it moves decisively.

---

### Recommendation 3: Restructure or Divest the Patient Care Solutions Segment
**Time horizon: 12–24 months**

The Patient Care Solutions segment posted a 7% organic revenue decline in Q3 2025, with EBIT margin compressing to just 3.7% (down from 10.5% in Q3 2024). While the company launched the Carestation 850 anaesthesia delivery system at ANESTHESIOLOGY 2025, reversing the segment's trajectory will require sustained innovation and improved commercial execution.

A segment with 3.7% EBIT margins in a company targeting high-teens to low-twenties adjusted EBIT margins is a strategic misfit. Capital and management attention devoted to Patient Care Solutions has a high opportunity cost when the Pharmaceutical Diagnostics and AI segments offer structurally superior returns.

**Action steps:**
- Commission a strategic review of Patient Care Solutions with three explicit options: (a) turnaround through product refresh and commercial restructuring; (b) carve-out as a standalone entity; (c) divestiture to a strategic buyer (e.g., a monitoring-focused company such as Masimo or Nihon Kohden).
- If retained, ring-fence Patient Care Solutions investment and set a 24-month performance threshold: achieve EBIT margins above 8% or initiate divestiture proceedings.
- Redeploy capital freed from Patient Care Solutions into Pharmaceutical Diagnostics and AI — the two segments with the strongest structural growth dynamics.

**Rationale:** Portfolio discipline is a core requirement of standalone strategic clarity. GE HealthCare's independence from GE was justified partly by the ability to make sharper capital allocation decisions than a conglomerate could. Retaining a chronically underperforming segment undermines that logic.

---

### Recommendation 4: Aggressively Pursue Geographic De-risking and Supply Chain Regionalisation
**Time horizon: 12–36 months**

GE Healthcare is planning several mitigation strategies to reduce the financial burden of tariffs in 2026, including moving more manufacturing closer to where products are sold and diversifying its supplier base. This is strategically correct but must be accelerated.

**Action steps:**
- Accelerate the build-out of regional manufacturing hubs in India, Mexico, and Eastern Europe to serve local markets, reducing both tariff exposure and geopolitical concentration risk.
- In China specifically, GE Healthcare has faced challenges in China due to weakened sales and tariff impacts and is reportedly weighing a stake sale in its China unit. A joint venture with a local Chinese partner — transferring manufacturing and some commercial rights in exchange for regulatory access and market share protection — represents a more capital-efficient approach than full ownership in a market where Western companies face structural headwinds.
- Accelerate market development in India, where the government's Ayushman Bharat Digital Mission is creating large-scale healthcare infrastructure demand, and in the Middle East, where sovereign wealth fund-backed hospital expansion programmes represent multi-year procurement opportunities.

**Rationale:** A 13% revenue concentration in China — a market facing structural anti-corruption procurement reform and escalating tariff risk — is an unacceptable level of geographic concentration for a standalone company. Geographic diversification is not optional; it is a strategic imperative.

---

### Recommendation 5: Double Down on Radiopharmaceuticals as the Long-Term Growth Platform
**Time horizon: 24–60 months**

GE HealthCare is focused on delivering on its precision care strategy, accelerating growth, and further optimising its business while creating value for customers, patients and providers. Within this strategy, Pharmaceutical Diagnostics is the segment with the clearest path to structural outperformance.

GE HealthCare has built on a century of innovation to shape the future of radiology, with strategic collaborations and acquisitions including MIM Software (advanced imaging analytics), Spectronic Medical (radiation oncology planning), and icometrix (AI-enabled precision imaging for neurological diagnosis). These acquisitions, combined with the Nihon Medi-Physics deal and the launch of Flyrcado (a PET cardiac imaging agent), collectively constitute the building blocks of an integrated theranostics platform.

**Action steps:**
- Increase PDx R&D allocation to develop a pipeline of proprietary radiopharmaceutical molecules across oncology (prostate, lung, breast), neurology (Alzheimer's, Parkinson's), and cardiology — disease areas with large unmet diagnostic need and premium reimbursement.
- Establish cyclotron manufacturing capacity in key geographies (US, Europe, Japan, India) to ensure radiopharmaceutical supply chain reliability — a critical operational requirement given the short half-lives of PET tracers.
- Pursue targeted acquisitions of clinical-stage radiopharmaceutical companies to accelerate pipeline depth, funded by cash flows from the imaging installed base.

**Rationale:** The VRIN analysis identified PDx vertical integration as one of GE HealthCare's two sources of sustained competitive advantage. Competitors cannot easily replicate the combination of imaging hardware, proprietary molecules, and cyclotron manufacturing expertise. This is the segment where GE HealthCare can build a genuinely unassailable position.

## 10. Conclusion

GE HealthCare's strategic situation is one of managed transition under competitive pressure. The company possesses genuinely rare and inimitable assets — a global installed base, a vertically integrated pharmaceutical diagnostics business, and a growing portfolio of FDA-cleared AI tools — but these advantages are not self-sustaining. They must be actively leveraged to fund and accelerate the transition to a recurring-revenue, platform-based business model before software-native competitors and newly integrated rivals (Siemens-Varian, Philips-connected care) close the gap.

The primary opportunity lies in transforming from a hardware-centric supplier to an integrated precision care platform — and the formidable strengths of a vast installed base and trusted brand provide the stable foundation from which to do so.

The five recommendations above form a coherent chain: enterprise partnerships generate recurring revenue and customer data; Edison monetises that data through AI; divesting Patient Care Solutions frees capital for higher-return investments; geographic diversification reduces the China and tariff risk that currently constrains investment capacity; and the radiopharmaceuticals platform builds the long-term, structurally defensible growth engine. Each recommendation reinforces the others — and each is grounded in GE HealthCare's existing strengths rather than requiring capabilities it does not possess.

The strategic imperative is clear: GE HealthCare must move with urgency. The window to establish platform leadership in AI-enabled medical imaging is open now — but it will not remain open indefinitely.
