# **JARIR BOOKSTORE: THE MARGIN-MIX DILEMMA — A STRATEGIC CASE STUDY**

---

## 1. INTRODUCTION AND STRATEGIC SITUATION

Jarir Bookstore (Jarir Marketing Company, TADAWUL: 4190), established in Riyadh in 1974 as a small bookstore, has grown into a Saudi joint-stock company and one of the most prominent retail entities in the Kingdom, solidifying its position as the market leader across consumer IT products, electronics, office supplies, and books. The company operates through the following segments: Retail Outlets, Wholesale, and E-Commerce. The Retail Outlets segment sells office supplies, school supplies, books, and other products; the Wholesale segment serves other resellers; and the E-Commerce segment represents the sale of goods from its online platform, Jarir.com and the Jarir Bookstore App.

The strategic situation confronting Jarir as of 2024–2025 is what this case study terms the **Margin-Mix Dilemma**: revenue is growing, but profitability is being eroded because the product categories driving that growth are precisely those with the lowest margins. The high-margin schools and office supplies segment (10.2% of total sales) saw a 10.3% year-on-year decline in sales in 2024, while smartphones and electronics and other IT and digital products segments saw only nominal increases of 3.7% and 4.2% year-on-year respectively. Channel-wise, retail outlet sales dropped by 2.4% year-on-year to SAR 7.6 billion in 2024, as customers continued their move towards online purchases, where Jarir saw a healthy 25.1% year-on-year increase in revenues.

This situation is strategically significant because it is not merely a cyclical fluctuation: it reflects structural changes in the Saudi retail environment — digital channel migration, intensifying platform competition, and a generational consumer shift — that, if unaddressed, will compress Jarir's profitability even as its top line expands.

## 2. CONTEXT: THE EXTERNAL ENVIRONMENT

### 2.1 The Saudi Digital Retail Boom

Saudi Arabia is in the midst of a digital transformation that is redefining its economic landscape. This transformation is an integral part of Vision 2030, the Kingdom's strategic framework aimed at reducing its dependency on oil and diversifying its economy. The Saudi e-commerce market was valued at $6.1 billion in 2024, representing 6.4 percent of the Kingdom's $95 billion retail market, and the number of e-commerce users is expected to reach 34.5 million by 2025.

This macro-environment is simultaneously an opportunity and a threat for Jarir. It is an opportunity because Jarir's own online channel is growing rapidly. It is a threat because the same environment is attracting and empowering formidable competitors. The top e-commerce companies in Saudi Arabia include Amazon, eBay, Emaar Properties Namshi, Noon, Alibaba, Jarir Marketing (Bookstore), and Carrefour — all competing for the same consumer base.

### 2.2 Consumer Behaviour Shift

The Saudi consumer landscape is increasingly defined by a young, dynamic, and empowered population, with an openness to innovation and keen to embrace new opportunities, driving a uniquely Saudi consumer ethos. Insights from PwC Middle East's Voice of the Consumer 2024 survey echo the sentiments of over 755 Saudi consumers, who have indicated the importance of trust, sustainability, and digital innovation, with data privacy remaining a critical concern. This consumer profile — young, digital-first, and value-conscious — is precisely the demographic that gravitates toward electronics and online purchasing, the categories that are growing for Jarir but at lower margins.

### 2.3 Digital Payments Infrastructure

Electronic payments accounted for 79% of all retail transactions in Saudi Arabia in 2024, up from 70% in 2023, marking a substantial increase in cashless transactions. This infrastructure shift reduces friction for e-commerce purchases, further accelerating the channel migration away from physical stores that is already compressing Jarir's per-store revenue productivity.

## 3. INTERNAL ANALYSIS: RESOURCES AND CAPABILITIES

### 3.1 Resource-Based View (RBV) / VRIN Analysis

The RBV framework (Barney, 1991) holds that sustainable competitive advantage derives from resources that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable. Applying this to Jarir:

**Brand and Trust (Valuable, Rare, partially Inimitable).** Jarir's supply chain is supported by a centralized, fully automated warehouse in Riyadh spanning 800,500 square feet, integrated with an enterprise resource planning (ERP) system for efficient inventory management and distribution to over 80 stores across Saudi Arabia and the GCC. This physical infrastructure, built over decades, represents a rare and costly-to-replicate asset. Jarir has solidified its position as market leader in the Middle East for consumer IT products, electronics, office supplies, and books — a brand position that functions as a trust signal for consumers in a market where counterfeiting and grey-market goods remain concerns.

**Authorized Brand Partnerships (Valuable, Rare, Inimitable).** Jarir's supply chain is complemented by strategic partnerships with global brands such as Apple, Jarir's first direct retail partner for iPhone distribution, and Samsung, through authorized sales of Galaxy devices, Care+ services, and trade-in programs. Local procurement accounts for 78% of spending. These exclusive or preferred-partner agreements are not easily replicated by smaller or newer entrants, representing a genuine VRIN resource.

**Loyalty Programme (Valuable, but increasingly imitable).** Jarir Discount Card is a loyalty programme that provides a 10% instant discount on almost all school and office supplies, arts and crafts, books, and computer bags, alongside special exclusive offers to card holders, usable in showrooms and on Jarir.com. While this builds switching costs, it is most potent in the high-margin categories (stationery, books) that are now declining — reducing its strategic impact precisely when it is most needed.

**Financial Strength (Valuable, Rare, Non-substitutable).** Jarir has a debt-free balance sheet, a low capital-intensive business (capex averaged at SAR 0.05 per share in the last five years), and robust free cash flow generation (2024 operating cash flow SAR 0.93 per share). This financial position is a critical strategic resource: it gives Jarir the capacity to invest in digital transformation and new capabilities without the leverage risk that constrains competitors.

**E-Commerce Platform (Valuable, growing).** Jarir generated revenue of US$754 million on its biggest online store jarir.com in 2024, with projections indicating a 10–15% increase in 2025. The platform is growing rapidly, but as the analysis below will show, it is cannibalising physical store revenue without yet compensating for margin compression.

## 4. STRATEGIC ANALYSIS

### 4.1 Porter's Five Forces Analysis

**Force 1 — Rivalry (HIGH).** The dominant players competing in the Saudi e-commerce and retail space include Amazon, Noon, Alibaba, and Carrefour, all of which possess significant financial resources, logistics networks, and brand recognition. This rivalry is intensifying: previously announced price competition appeared to weigh on margins in Q4 2024, contracting over 300 basis points quarter-on-quarter, despite slightly higher margins on smartphone products. The causal mechanism is clear — to compete with Amazon and Noon on electronics, Jarir must price aggressively, which directly erodes the gross margin on its fastest-growing segment.

**Force 2 — Threat of Substitutes (HIGH).** For books and educational materials — Jarir's heritage categories — digital substitutes (e-books, online learning platforms) represent a structural threat. For electronics, the substitute is not a different product but a different channel: purchasing direct from brand websites or global marketplaces. This is why the high-margin book and stationery segment is declining; the product itself is being substituted digitally.

**Force 3 — Buyer Power (MODERATE to HIGH).** The Saudi e-commerce market is characterised by a high per capita GDP and high individual net income, with rising purchasing power predicted to fuel demand, but also driving consumers to look for ease and variety, making e-commerce platforms a desirable choice because of their affordability, wide selection, and competitive pricing. Empowered, price-sensitive consumers who can compare prices across platforms in seconds have elevated buyer power, particularly in the electronics category.

**Force 4 — Supplier Power (MODERATE).** Jarir's authorised partnerships with Apple and Samsung create a degree of mutual dependency — Jarir needs these brands to attract footfall, and the brands need Jarir's distribution network. However, as Apple expands its own direct retail presence globally, the risk that suppliers bypass Jarir increases over time, making these partnerships strategically important to maintain and deepen.

**Force 5 — Threat of New Entrants (MODERATE).** The combination of Vision 2030 investment in digital infrastructure and relatively low regulatory barriers to e-commerce means new entrants can emerge quickly. As e-commerce evolves, many companies and startups are expanding their market presence by gaining new contracts and entering new areas. However, Jarir's physical store network, brand equity, and authorised supplier relationships represent substantial entry barriers that protect its core position.

**Five Forces Summary:** The combined force analysis reveals that Jarir's competitive position is most exposed in the electronics segment — where rivalry is highest, buyer power is greatest, and margin pressure is most acute — and most defensible in its heritage categories (books, stationery), where its brand, loyalty programme, and physical experience create differentiation. The strategic implication is that Jarir must find a way to either defend its high-margin categories or build new high-margin capabilities in electronics (e.g., services, protection plans, B2B supply).

**Key SWOT Insight:** The most dangerous strategic pattern visible in the SWOT is a *strengths-threats collision*: Jarir's strongest revenue growth is occurring in the category (electronics) most exposed to competitive threats, while its strongest margin protection exists in the category (books/stationery) most exposed to digital substitution. This is the structural core of the Margin-Mix Dilemma.

### 4.3 Ansoff Matrix: Growth Strategy Options

The Ansoff Matrix (Ansoff, 1957) provides a structured framework for evaluating Jarir's growth options across four quadrants:

**Quadrant 1 — Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets):** Jarir can intensify its e-commerce push within Saudi Arabia. Retail outlet sales dropped by 2.4% year-on-year in 2024 as customers moved towards online purchases, where Jarir saw a 25.1% year-on-year increase in revenues, boosted by the launch of a new version of the Jarir Bookstore app. This growth trajectory warrants continued investment in the digital channel as a penetration tool — reaching existing Saudi customers more efficiently. However, as the Five Forces analysis shows, penetration in electronics will intensify price competition and further compress margins. Therefore, penetration strategy must be *selectively applied* to high-margin categories (stationery, books, arts and crafts) through the digital channel, not electronics.

**Quadrant 2 — Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets):** As of the end of 2024, Jarir operates a total of 11 branches across four GCC countries outside Saudi Arabia, emphasising localised retail strategies such as product assortments. With 59 stores in Saudi Arabia and only 11 internationally, there is substantial headroom for GCC expansion. Jarir plans to spend SAR 120–150 million per year on expansion, with the chairman noting "It's low-hanging fruit... We are growing in Saudi and in the Gulf, and we want to see that we populate the GCC." This is a relatively low-risk growth path that leverages existing product capabilities and brand recognition in culturally similar markets.

**Quadrant 3 — Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets):** The most immediately actionable product development opportunity is the expansion of **after-sales services and protection plans** — a high-margin category that leverages Jarir's existing electronics customer base. Jarir offers a protection service for Apple devices through its Jarir Protection service, providing global coverage for accidental damage, including unlimited incident coverage. Extending this model across more brands and product categories would create a recurring, high-margin revenue stream that does not depend on selling more hardware.

**Quadrant 4 — Diversification (New Products, New Markets):** The highest-risk quadrant. The most credible diversification opportunity is B2B institutional supply — targeting the government, education, and corporate sectors that are expanding under Vision 2030. Jarir's wholesale division already serves resellers; extending this to direct institutional contracts would be a related diversification that builds on existing logistics and product capabilities.

## 5. THE CORE STRATEGIC PROBLEM: MARGIN-MIX EROSION

Synthesising the above analyses, the strategic problem can be stated precisely:

**Jarir's revenue growth is being driven by the wrong products in the wrong channels.** Electronics and smartphones are growing, but they carry structurally lower gross margins and face intense price competition from Amazon and Noon. Stationery, books, and arts and crafts — the categories that carry the highest margins and where Jarir's loyalty programme and brand differentiation are most effective — are declining. The decline in H1 2024 profitability was attributed to a 1.1% year-on-year decrease in gross profits, mainly due to a shift in the sales mix towards the relatively less profitable sections, with selling and marketing costs and general and administrative expenses also increasing.

This is not simply a matter of selling more — it is a matter of *what* is being sold and *at what margin*. Jarir's top-line increased by 2% year-on-year to SAR 2.9 billion for Q4 2024, mostly driven by higher sales in electronics, computers, and smartphone products, yet gross profits decreased 15% sequentially. This data point is the clearest evidence of the dilemma: revenue growth and profit growth are moving in opposite directions.

The RBV analysis explains *why* this is happening: Jarir's most inimitable resources (brand trust, loyalty programme, in-store experience) are most effective in the declining categories, while the growing categories are commodity-like, where Jarir's advantages are weakest and rivals' advantages (logistics, scale, price) are strongest.

## 6. RECOMMENDATIONS AND ACTION PLAN

The following recommendations follow directly from the analytical chain: the Five Forces reveal where competitive pressure is highest; the RBV identifies where Jarir's advantages are strongest; the SWOT maps the collision between the two; and the Ansoff Matrix structures the growth options. The recommendations are therefore designed to redirect Jarir's competitive energy toward positions where its VRIN resources are most potent.

---

### Recommendation 1: Rebuild the High-Margin Category Engine via Digital

**What:** Invest in making the digital channel the primary driver of growth for Jarir's high-margin categories — stationery, books, arts and crafts, and educational supplies — rather than using it primarily to compete in electronics.

**Why the evidence warrants this:** The high-margin schools and office supplies segment saw a 10.3% year-on-year decline in 2024, yet this is the category where Jarir's loyalty card, brand differentiation, and private-label products (Roco) create the most durable competitive advantage. The decline is partly structural (digital substitution) but also partly a failure to make these categories as convenient to purchase digitally as electronics. The loyalty card offers 10% instant discount on school and office supplies, arts and crafts, and books — a meaningful incentive that should be promoted more aggressively through the app. Jarir's digital strategy, led by its powerful app, complements its physical presence across the Middle East and should be leveraged to drive repeat, subscription-style purchasing of consumable stationery and school supplies.

**How:** Introduce a subscription replenishment model for stationery and school supplies (analogous to Amazon Subscribe & Save), promoted exclusively through the Jarir app, with enhanced loyalty card discounts for subscribers. This would convert one-off, price-sensitive purchases into recurring, loyalty-anchored transactions.

---

### Recommendation 2: Expand the Services and Protection Revenue Stream

**What:** Systematically scale after-sales services — protection plans, device trade-in, repair, and setup services — across all electronics categories, not just Apple.

**Why the evidence warrants this:** Services carry structurally higher margins than hardware. Gross margins contracted over 300 basis points in Q4 2024 due to price competition, despite slightly higher margins on smartphone products. This shows that even within electronics, margin variation exists — services and protection are the high-margin sub-category. Jarir already has the infrastructure: strategic partnerships with Apple for iPhone distribution and Samsung through authorized sales of Galaxy devices, Care+ services, and trade-in programs provide the template. Extending this model to laptops, tablets, and smart home devices would create a recurring, high-margin revenue stream that is also difficult for pure-play e-commerce rivals (Amazon, Noon) to replicate, because it requires physical service centres.

**How:** Launch a "Jarir Care+" programme covering all major electronics brands sold in-store, with tiered annual protection plans. Use the existing store network as service hubs. This leverages the physical store's comparative advantage over online-only competitors and rebuilds the margin contribution of the electronics segment.

---

### Recommendation 3: Accelerate GCC Market Development

**What:** Accelerate the opening of new stores in GCC markets, prioritising cities with high concentrations of young, digitally active consumers and limited existing competition.

**Why the evidence warrants this:** Jarir operates only 11 branches across four GCC countries outside Saudi Arabia, against 59 stores in Saudi Arabia — a stark imbalance given the cultural and commercial similarities across the GCC. Average revenue per store declined by 1.3% year-on-year to SAR 150.4 million in 2024, and analysts expect revenue growth of 4.3–5.3% in 2025–2026, driven by store additions rather than per-store productivity gains. This analyst consensus confirms that geographic expansion is the most reliable near-term growth lever. Jarir's debt-free balance sheet and low capex needs mean this expansion can be self-funded without financial risk. The GCC e-commerce market is growing rapidly, and establishing physical presence early creates the omnichannel advantage that Jarir has built in Saudi Arabia.

**How:** Commit to opening a minimum of five new GCC stores annually over the next three years, prioritising Kuwait City, Dubai, and Doha, where the young professional demographic aligns with Jarir's product mix. Pair each new store opening with a localised digital marketing campaign to drive app downloads and online purchases in the catchment area.

---

### Recommendation 4: Build a B2B Institutional Channel

**What:** Develop a dedicated B2B sales unit targeting schools, universities, government ministries, and large corporates for bulk supply of stationery, technology, and educational materials.

**Why the evidence warrants this:** Saudi Arabia's Vision 2030 is driving massive investment in education, healthcare, and infrastructure, creating large institutional buyers of exactly the products Jarir sells. Vision 2030 is aimed at diversifying the economy and developing key sectors including health, education, and infrastructure — all of which are intensive consumers of office supplies, technology hardware, and educational materials. B2B sales typically carry higher average order values and more predictable demand than B2C retail, which would help stabilise Jarir's revenue mix and reduce dependence on margin-compressing consumer electronics. Jarir's wholesale division already serves resellers; extending this capability to direct institutional contracts is a natural adjacency that requires minimal new capability development.

**How:** Establish a dedicated B2B portal on Jarir.com with volume pricing, invoicing capabilities, and account management. Assign a dedicated B2B sales team to target the 500 largest government and educational institutions in Saudi Arabia and the GCC. Use the existing wholesale infrastructure for fulfilment.
