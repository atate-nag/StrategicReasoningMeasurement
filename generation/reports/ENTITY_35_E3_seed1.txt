# Shell plc: The Strategic Dilemma of the Energy Transition — A Case Study

## Executive Summary

Shell plc occupies one of the most contested strategic positions in global business: it must simultaneously satisfy investors demanding near-term returns, regulators and activists demanding accelerated decarbonisation, and customers requiring secure and affordable energy. Between 2021 and 2024, Shell oscillated between two poles — aggressive expansion into renewables under its "Powering Progress" strategy, and a sharp retrenchment toward fossil-fuel profitability under CEO Wael Sawan's "more value, less emissions" pivot. This case study analyses that strategic situation using Porter's Five Forces, the Resource-Based View (RBV) / Dynamic Capabilities framework, and Ansoff's Growth Matrix, leading to a set of integrated recommendations.

---

## 1. The Strategic Situation

### 1.1 Background and Context

In the face of accelerating climate change and a global imperative for environmental stewardship, Shell plc has embarked on an ambitious journey toward a sustainable future with its Energy Transition Strategy 2024 — a bold declaration of Shell's intent to transform into a net-zero emissions energy business by 2050, aligning with the Paris Agreement's goal of limiting global warming to 1.5°C above pre-industrial levels.

Yet this stated ambition sits in direct tension with a series of operational retreats. Shell announced it will scale back its involvement in new offshore wind investments, in a move that marks a significant departure from its previous commitments to renewable energy. The integrated oil and gas company also revealed plans to restructure its power division, stemming from a comprehensive review of its operations launched in 2023. This extensive review, led by CEO Wael Sawan, has set the stage for a new era at Shell — one that is refocusing on higher-return ventures while cutting back on expenditures in low-carbon and renewable energy businesses.

The strategic situation is therefore one of **competing logics**: a decarbonisation logic imposed by climate science, regulation, and stakeholder pressure; and a financial logic imposed by capital markets, the cost of capital, and competitive rivalry. The central strategic question is: *how should Shell allocate capital across its portfolio to survive the energy transition without destroying shareholder value in the near term?*

### 1.2 The Pivot in Detail

At the June 2023 Capital Markets Day, Shell updated investors on its strategy to create more value with less emissions and deliver increased shareholder returns through a balanced energy transition. CEO Wael Sawan stated: "We are investing to provide the secure energy customers need today and for a long time to come, while transforming Shell to win in a low-carbon future. Performance, discipline, and simplification will be our guiding principles as we allocate capital to enhance shareholder distributions, while enabling the energy transition."

The financial logic behind this pivot is explicit. Shell enhanced its focus on performance and stronger capital and cost discipline, underpinning higher shareholder distributions of 30–40% of cash flow from operations (CFFO) through the cycle, compared with 20–30% previously, through a combination of dividends and share buybacks.

The operational consequences were significant. In many cases, the company-wide review has meant reducing spending on low-carbon and renewable businesses and increasing the focus on oil, gas and biofuels. The decision to pull back from offshore wind development came at a time when renewable energy projects, particularly in the offshore wind sector, were facing growing economic challenges. Rising costs, supply-chain disruptions and an overall uncertain investment climate led Shell to reassess its approach.

Simultaneously, Shell weakened its near-term climate targets. Shell has abandoned a key climate target for 2035 and weakened another goal for 2030. The oil major updated its target to cut the total "net carbon intensity" of all the energy products it sells to customers by 20% between 2016 and 2030 — the reduction is now set at between 15–20%. Within Shell's strategy, CEO Wael Sawan wrote that this change reflects "a strategic shift" to focus less on selling electricity, including renewable power. Instead, the company says investment in oil and gas "will be needed" due to sustained demand for fossil fuels.

## 2. Environmental Analysis: Porter's Five Forces

Porter's Five Forces framework (Porter, 1979) provides a structured lens for understanding the industry-level pressures that constrain Shell's strategic choices.

### 2.1 Threat of New Entrants — **Low to Moderate**

The upstream oil and gas sector retains formidable barriers to entry: the capital requirements for deepwater drilling, LNG liquefaction trains, and global distribution networks are prohibitive for most new players. Shell is one of the leading LNG suppliers globally, being involved in every stage of the value chain. This end-to-end integration is not easily replicated. However, in the renewable power segment — where Shell previously sought growth — barriers to entry are far lower, with technology costs declining and project finance widely available, making that segment structurally less attractive for incumbents seeking differentiated returns.

### 2.2 Bargaining Power of Suppliers — **Moderate**

Oilfield services companies (Schlumberger, Halliburton, Baker Hughes) and specialised equipment manufacturers exert moderate supplier power. Supply chain inflation was a direct driver of Shell's retreat from offshore wind: the decision to pull back from offshore wind development came at a time when the sector was facing growing economic challenges, with rising costs, supply-chain disruptions and an overall uncertain investment climate leading Shell to reassess its approach. This illustrates that supplier power in capital-intensive infrastructure projects can erode project returns to the point where strategic withdrawal is rational.

### 2.3 Bargaining Power of Buyers — **Moderate to High**

Industrial and government buyers of LNG — particularly in Asia — hold significant negotiating leverage given the proliferation of supply sources and long-term contract structures. Chinese gas demand grew 8% despite a modest economic recovery, with China overtaking Japan to become the world's largest LNG importer again. This growing demand provides Shell pricing support in the near term, but it also means that Asian state-owned buyers (CNOOC, JERA) are large, sophisticated counterparties capable of extracting favourable contract terms.

### 2.4 Threat of Substitutes — **Rising**

Global investment in clean energy has increased by 60% since 2015, when the world signed the Paris Agreement. Investment flows to clean energy projects are approaching USD 2 trillion each year, almost double the combined amount spent on new oil, gas and coal supply. This structural shift means that the long-run threat of substitution — from renewable electricity replacing fossil fuels in transport and industry — is real and accelerating, even if the near-term pace remains slower than some forecasts suggest. While energy demand is rising, clean energy growth means that demand for each fossil fuel — coal, gas and oil — will all peak by 2030 according to the IEA's current policies scenario. This rising substitution threat is the foundational reason Shell *cannot* simply remain a pure-play fossil fuel company indefinitely, even as it retreats from renewables in the short run.

### 2.5 Competitive Rivalry — **High**

Major oil and gas companies are relatively equal in size, power and capabilities. This increases the intensity of rivalry, which can manifest itself in a price war if a competitor tries to influence prices. Shell faces rivalry from ExxonMobil and Chevron (which trade at a valuation premium), TotalEnergies (which has maintained a stronger renewable commitment), and national oil companies (Saudi Aramco, QatarEnergy) with structural cost advantages. This cautious stance on renewables highlights a broader trend within the energy industry, as major players like BP and Equinor have also scaled back their investments in renewables in response to rising interest rates, increased capital costs and narrowing profit margins within the sector. The convergence of rivals around the same strategic pivot — retrenchment toward fossil fuels — intensifies rivalry in Shell's core markets and reduces the scope for differentiation.

**Porter's Five Forces synthesis:** The industry structure rewards incumbents with integrated, large-scale positions in fossil fuels (low entry threat, moderate supplier power, growing buyer demand), but the rising substitution threat means this advantage is time-limited. The rational near-term response — extracting cash from fossil fuels while selectively investing in transition technologies — is exactly what Shell's Sawan strategy prescribes. The risk is that this rational short-term logic becomes a structural trap if the transition accelerates faster than anticipated.

## 3. Internal Analysis: Resource-Based View and Dynamic Capabilities

The Resource-Based View (Barney, 1991) holds that sustainable competitive advantage arises from resources that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN). Teece, Pisano, and Shuen (1997) extend this to *dynamic capabilities* — the capacity to sense, seize, and reconfigure resources as environments change.

### 3.1 Shell's VRIN Resources

**LNG trading and integration:** A core element of Shell's strategy is the optimization of its LNG portfolio, where it is not just enhancing capacity but also integrating renewable energy sources and carbon capture technologies to decrease the carbon footprint. This dual approach secures energy supply and caters to increasing demand, especially from Asia, while supporting global decarbonisation efforts. Shell's LNG capability — spanning liquefaction, shipping, regasification, and trading — is rare, inimitable in the short run (it took decades to build), and valuable. In 2024, Shell achieved a significant milestone in the maritime fuel sector by delivering a record-breaking 1.1 million tonnes of marine LNG to vessels across the globe — more than double the amount delivered in 2023 — reinforcing Shell's dominant position in the global LNG bunkering market.

**Trading and optimisation capability:** Shell's integrated gas and power trading desk — one of the largest in the world — constitutes a genuine dynamic capability. The strategy connects low-carbon energy supply and demand through Shell's trading business. This capability is valuable regardless of whether the underlying commodity is natural gas, renewable electricity, or carbon credits, giving Shell optionality that pure-play renewables companies lack.

**Brand, retail network, and customer relationships:** Shell aims to take the lead in the energy transition where it can combine competitive strengths with strong customer demand and support from governments, like in the transport sector — including public charge points for electric vehicles at Shell forecourts, on streets and at locations such as supermarkets. This was up from 27,000 in 2022. The retail network is a distribution asset that is genuinely difficult for new entrants to replicate.

### 3.2 Dynamic Capabilities: The Reconfiguration Challenge

Shell's strategic challenge is fundamentally a dynamic capabilities problem: can it reconfigure assets built for a fossil-fuel world to compete in a low-carbon one? The evidence is mixed.

On the *sensing* dimension, Shell correctly identified that offshore wind was becoming structurally unattractive — rising costs, supply-chain disruptions and an overall uncertain investment climate led Shell to reassess its approach, with the company's new direction representing a shift in its energy transition goals. This is sensible capital discipline.

On the *seizing* dimension, Shell has made selective bets. Shell planned to invest $10–15 billion across 2023 to 2025 to support the development of low-carbon energy solutions including biofuels, hydrogen, electric vehicle charging and CCS. However, the *reconfiguration* of deep organisational capabilities — from hydrocarbon extraction to, say, large-scale green hydrogen production — remains unproven.

The weakening of climate targets raises a structural concern: if Shell is reconfiguring away from low-carbon investments, it may be eroding the very capabilities it will need when the energy transition accelerates. The weakening of climate goals from Shell comes after BP scaled back its ambitions last year. The new medium-term target will contribute even less to significant reductions in global emissions this decade. From an RBV perspective, divesting or underinvesting in nascent capabilities (renewables, hydrogen, CCS) before they achieve scale creates a *capability gap* that will be costly to close later.

## 4. Portfolio Analysis: Ansoff's Growth Matrix

Ansoff's matrix (1957) maps strategic options along two axes: existing versus new markets, and existing versus new products/technologies.

**Market Penetration (existing products, existing markets):** Shell's primary near-term strategy is market penetration in LNG and upstream oil and gas. As Shell grows its world-leading LNG business by an expected 20–30% by 2030, it will continue to reduce the carbon intensity of its operations. LNG produces around 50% less carbon emissions than coal when used to generate electricity (IEA). This is the lowest-risk quadrant and the source of Shell's current cash generation.

**Market Development (existing products, new markets):** The Shell–Equinor North Sea joint venture exemplifies market development through consolidation. Equinor and Shell are to combine their UK offshore oil and gas assets and expertise to form a new company which will be the UK North Sea's biggest independent producer. The incorporated joint venture will be set up to sustain domestic oil and gas production and security of energy supply in the UK. The combined company should deliver more than 140,000 barrels of oil equivalent per day in 2025, with Equinor currently producing about 38,000 boe/d and Shell UK producing more than 100,000 boe/d. The rationale is cost-sharing in a maturing basin, not growth — a rational late-cycle play.

**Product Development (new products, existing markets):** Shell is pursuing hydrogen, biofuels, and CCS as new products for existing industrial customers. The strategy focuses on growing liquefied natural gas, investing in carbon capture and storage, hydrogen, and renewable energy. However, the biofuels programme has stumbled: Shell announced it was pausing construction at its 820,000-tonne-per-year biofuels facility at Rotterdam "to address project delivery and ensure future competitiveness given current market conditions."

**Diversification (new products, new markets):** Shell's earlier push into offshore wind was a diversification play — a new technology in a new market. Its retreat signals that the diversification premium (the returns required to justify entering an unfamiliar market) was not being met. The company-wide review, led by CEO Wael Sawan, has set the stage for a new era at Shell, one that is refocusing on higher-return ventures while cutting back on expenditures in low-carbon and renewable energy businesses.

**Ansoff synthesis:** Shell is concentrating capital in the bottom-left quadrant (market penetration) and selectively pursuing product development (CCS, hydrogen) in adjacent markets. The retreat from diversification is financially rational in the short run but strategically risky if the energy transition accelerates, because Shell will have foregone the learning-curve benefits of early-mover investment in new technologies.

## 5. Stakeholder and Legitimacy Tensions

A complete strategic analysis must account for the legitimacy dimension — the degree to which Shell's strategy is accepted as valid by key stakeholder groups (Suchman, 1995).

**Regulatory legitimacy:** On 12 November 2024, the Court of Appeal in The Hague handed down its ruling in the appeal proceedings between Shell plc and Milieudefensie. Shell appealed a district court ruling ordering the company to reduce its global CO2 emissions by 45% by 2030 compared to 2019 levels. The Court of Appeal ruled that, while Shell is indeed obligated to reduce its CO2 emissions, a civil court cannot require it to meet specific reduction targets. This ruling reduces Shell's immediate legal exposure but does not eliminate the broader regulatory risk: future legislation, carbon pricing mechanisms, or international frameworks could impose binding obligations.

**Investor legitimacy:** Oil major Shell is looking at "all options" including switching its listing to New York from London, according to comments made by CEO Wael Sawan. "If we work through the sprint, and we are doing what we are doing, and we still don't see that the gap is closing, we have to look at all options," Sawan said. The persistent valuation discount relative to US peers (ExxonMobil, Chevron) — partly attributable to Shell's London listing and partly to its perceived strategic ambiguity — is a material constraint on Shell's ability to finance the energy transition through equity.

**Civil society legitimacy:** Shell is counting discredited carbon credits towards its climate goals, drawing accusations of "bad faith" and "malintent." Shell used rice farming carbon credits to offset a chunk of its annual emissions, claiming to reduce the "carbon intensity" of its fossil fuel products. But experts have long argued that the sellers of these offsets are over-counting their emissions reductions and using accounting tricks to evade checks. This reputational risk is not merely ethical — it creates material business risk through regulatory scrutiny, talent attraction difficulties, and customer preference shifts.

## 6. Financial Performance: Connecting Strategy to Outcomes

Shell posted adjusted earnings of $23.72 billion for the full-year 2024, compared to annual profit of $28.25 billion a year earlier. This 16% decline in adjusted earnings reflects both lower oil prices and the structural drag of Shell's transition investments. However, Shell CEO Wael Sawan described 2024 as a "very strong year," one which gave the company a platform "to do everything we said we were going to do."

The cash generation story is more favourable. The company achieved $3.1 billion in pre-tax structural cost reductions through 2024 compared to 2022 levels. Cash flow from operating activities remained stable at $54,684 million. This robust cash flow has enabled sustained shareholder distributions: Shell announced a 4% increase in dividend per share and launched another share buyback programme of $3.5 billion, expected to be completed over the next three months.

The strategic logic is internally consistent: by cutting costs, retreating from low-return renewables, and concentrating on high-return LNG and upstream assets, Shell is generating cash to return to shareholders — which in turn supports the valuation needed to finance future capital allocation. The risk is that this logic is self-reinforcing in the wrong direction: cutting transition investments reduces future optionality, which reduces long-term value, which increases the valuation discount, which creates further pressure to cut transition investments.

## 7. Recommendations

The following recommendations follow directly from the analytical chain above. Each is grounded in the strategic tensions identified, and each is designed to resolve a specific identified weakness.

### Recommendation 1: Maintain LNG Leadership as the Transition Bridge — but Attach Carbon Conditions

**Warrant:** The Five Forces analysis shows that LNG is Shell's most defensible competitive position. The RBV analysis confirms that Shell's LNG trading and integration capability is genuinely VRIN. The Ansoff analysis shows that market penetration in LNG is the lowest-risk near-term strategy. However, Shell's legitimacy analysis shows that unqualified LNG expansion without credible decarbonisation pathways exposes it to regulatory and reputational risk.

**Action:** Shell should continue its planned 20–30% LNG growth by 2030, as it grows its world-leading LNG business and continues to reduce the carbon intensity of its operations, but should attach explicit carbon-intensity reduction commitments to each new LNG project — for example, by requiring that new liquefaction trains be powered by renewable electricity and equipped with CCS, as envisaged in the LNG Canada joint venture. Shell's LNG joint venture in Canada will use natural gas and renewable electricity to reduce emissions from the plant by more than one-third compared with the world's best performing facilities. This template should become the standard, not the exception.

### Recommendation 2: Concentrate Low-Carbon Investment in CCS and Hydrogen — Divest Carbon Credits

**Warrant:** The Ansoff product development quadrant shows that CCS and hydrogen are the most credible adjacencies to Shell's core competencies (large-scale industrial processes, trading, and customer relationships). The legitimacy analysis shows that Shell's current use of carbon credits is a reputational liability that undermines the credibility of its net-zero commitments. Shell is counting discredited carbon credits towards its climate goals, drawing accusations of "bad faith" and "malintent." The RBV analysis suggests that CCS is a capability where Shell's engineering expertise and industrial relationships create genuine competitive advantage.

**Action:** Shell should phase out reliance on nature-based carbon offsets for meeting its net carbon intensity targets — replacing them with verifiable operational emissions reductions — and redirect the capital saved toward CCS hubs and green hydrogen projects where Shell has demonstrable technical capability. Shell's Energy Transition Strategy 2024 includes investments in carbon capture and storage technology to reduce emissions from its own operations such as refineries and LNG plants, and, in the longer term, to help industrial customers reduce their emissions too. This is the right direction; it should be accelerated.

### Recommendation 3: Resolve the Valuation Discount Through Strategic Clarity, Not Listing Migration

**Warrant:** The investor legitimacy analysis shows that Shell trades at a persistent discount to US peers. Shell is considering "all options" including switching its listing to New York, with Sawan saying "if we still don't see that the gap is closing, we have to look at all options." However, the root cause of the discount is strategic ambiguity — Shell is perceived as neither a credible fossil-fuel compounder nor a credible energy transition company. Relocating the listing addresses the symptom, not the cause.

**Action:** Shell should resolve strategic ambiguity by explicitly communicating a *sequenced* capital allocation framework: (a) 2024–2027: cash extraction from LNG and upstream, with strict cost discipline; (b) 2027–2032: scaled deployment of CCS and hydrogen as these technologies reach commercial viability; (c) 2032+: portfolio rebalancing toward low-carbon as the energy transition accelerates. This sequencing is consistent with Shell's stated beliefs — that LNG will play a critical role in the energy transition, oil will have a continued role in transport with growth in demand slowing over time, and low-carbon molecules and renewable power will underpin the future energy system — but it makes the sequencing explicit to investors, reducing the ambiguity premium embedded in the discount.

### Recommendation 4: Use the North Sea JV as a Template for Managed Decline — Not Growth

**Warrant:** The Ansoff analysis identifies the Shell–Equinor North Sea joint venture as a market development play in a maturing basin. With the once-prolific basin now maturing and production naturally declining, the combination of portfolios and expertise will allow continued economic recovery of this vital UK resource. This is rational capital discipline — consolidating to extract remaining value at lower per-barrel cost. However, it should not be confused with a growth strategy.

**Action:** Shell should apply the same managed-decline joint-venture template to other mature upstream assets globally — pooling operations with partners to reduce per-unit costs and extend economic field life — while redirecting freed capital toward the CCS and hydrogen investments identified in Recommendation 2. The North Sea JV's stated goal of being "more agile, focused, cost-competitive and strategically well positioned to maximise the value of its combined portfolios" is the right framework, but the freed capital must be explicitly earmarked for transition investments, not simply returned to shareholders.

### Recommendation 5: Restore Credible Near-Term Climate Targets

**Warrant:** The legitimacy analysis shows that Shell's decision to weaken its 2030 target and scrap its 2035 target has damaged its credibility with regulators, institutional investors, and civil society. The weakening of climate goals from Shell comes after BP scaled back its ambitions last year. The new medium-term target will contribute even less to significant reductions in global emissions this decade. From a strategic risk perspective, this creates regulatory exposure: if governments introduce mandatory sectoral emissions pathways — as is increasingly likely — Shell will be further from compliance than peers who maintained their targets.

**Action:** Shell should reinstate a credible 2035 net carbon intensity target — not the scrapped 45% figure, which was arguably too aggressive, but a clearly-defined, science-based intermediate target — and tie executive remuneration explicitly to its delivery. Shell has already introduced metrics in the annual bonus scorecard to reflect its role in the energy transition; these should be strengthened and made more specific, with clear links to the sequenced capital allocation framework above. This restores legitimacy without sacrificing financial discipline.
