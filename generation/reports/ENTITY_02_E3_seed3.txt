# **ABBVIE INC.: NAVIGATING THE POST-HUMIRA INFLECTION POINT**
## A Strategic Case Study

---

## 1. INTRODUCTION AND CASE OVERVIEW

AbbVie Inc. is a research-based global biopharmaceutical company headquartered in North Chicago, Illinois. Spun off from Abbott Laboratories in January 2013, the company built its early identity almost entirely around a single blockbuster drug: Humira (adalimumab), a TNF-inhibitor biologic used across multiple autoimmune conditions. For over a decade, Humira was the world's best-selling drug, generating revenues that made AbbVie one of the most profitable pharmaceutical firms on the planet. That singular dependence, however, created a structural vulnerability that now defines the central strategic challenge this case study examines.

AbbVie filed 247 patent applications for Humira in the U.S., 89% of which were filed after the drug was first approved and on the market, and similarly filed 165 patent applications for Imbruvica, 55% of which were filed after approval. This aggressive patent strategy, described by critics as "evergreening," delayed biosimilar entry but could not prevent it indefinitely. AbbVie's Humira successfully delayed U.S. biosimilar competition for years after its primary patent expired, allowing it to generate over $114 billion in revenue for AbbVie since the end of 2016. When the loss of exclusivity (LOE) arrived on 31 January 2023, the revenue cliff was steep and immediate.

The strategic situation this case examines is therefore: *how does AbbVie successfully transition from a single-product dependency to a sustainable, diversified multi-franchise business, and what further actions are required to secure long-term competitive position?* The analysis applies Porter's Five Forces, the Resource-Based View (RBV) and Dynamic Capabilities framework, Ansoff's Growth Matrix, and a SWOT synthesis to diagnose AbbVie's position and generate a coherent set of strategic recommendations.

## 2. SITUATIONAL ANALYSIS: THE HUMIRA CLIFF AND ITS AFTERMATH

### 2.1 The Revenue Shock

AbbVie delivered full-year net revenues of $54.318 billion in 2023, a decrease of 6.4 percent on a reported basis and 5.9 percent on an operational basis, with full-year global net revenues from the immunology portfolio decreasing 9.6 percent due to Humira biosimilar competition; global Humira net revenues were $14.404 billion. This decline illustrates the direct financial consequence of LOE. By 2024, the erosion had deepened: in the United States, Humira sales decreased by 40% for the three months ended March 31, 2024, primarily driven by direct biosimilar competition following the loss of exclusivity on January 31, 2023. The warrant connecting this evidence to the strategic problem is straightforward: a 40% quarterly revenue decline on a product that had constituted the majority of company revenues represents an existential-level disruption that forces a fundamental strategic reorientation.

Yet by full-year 2024, the headline picture had partially recovered. AbbVie delivered full-year net revenues of $56.334 billion, an increase of 3.7 percent on a reported basis and 4.6 percent on an operational basis, with full-year global Humira net revenues of $8.993 billion. That revenue recovery — achieved even as Humira fell from $14.4 billion to $8.993 billion in a single year — signals that the transition strategy was working, but also that it remains incomplete and fragile.

### 2.2 The Growth Platform: Skyrizi and Rinvoq

The engine of recovery has been two next-generation immunology assets. Full-year global net revenues from the immunology portfolio were $26.682 billion, an increase of 2.1 percent on a reported basis; global Skyrizi net revenues were $11.718 billion; global Rinvoq net revenues were $5.971 billion. These figures are strategically significant for two reasons. First, Skyrizi alone at $11.7 billion already approaches Humira's 2024 revenue level, suggesting the substitution is nearing completion in immunology. Second, the growth rates are exceptional: net revenues for Skyrizi increased 48% for the three months ended March 31, 2024, primarily driven by continued strong market share uptake and market growth across all indications; net revenues for Rinvoq increased 62% for the three months ended March 31, 2024, primarily driven by continued strong market share uptake as well as market growth. These rates of growth — at scale — reflect genuine demand expansion rather than mere substitution.

AbbVie has raised its forward guidance accordingly. AbbVie has increased its 2027 projected combined sales of Skyrizi and Rinvoq to a combined $31 billion, a $4 billion increase on its previous guidance for the immunology duo. The warrant here is that if this forecast is achieved, Skyrizi and Rinvoq together would exceed Humira's peak annual revenue, effectively completing the platform transition within immunology.

### 2.3 Portfolio Diversification: Beyond Immunology

AbbVie's strategy has not relied solely on immunology substitution. The company has built material positions in three additional franchises.

**Neuroscience:** Full-year global net revenues from the neuroscience portfolio were $8.999 billion, an increase of 16.6 percent on a reported basis, or 16.9 percent on an operational basis; global Botox Therapeutic net revenues were $3.283 billion; global Vraylar net revenues were $3.267 billion; combined global Ubrelvy and Qulipta net revenues were $1.664 billion.

**Oncology:** Full-year global net revenues from the oncology portfolio were $6.555 billion, an increase of 10.8 percent on a reported basis, or 12.0 percent on an operational basis; global Imbruvica net revenues were $3.347 billion; global Venclexta net revenues were $2.583 billion.

**Aesthetics:** Full-year global net revenues from the aesthetics portfolio were $5.176 billion, a decrease of 2.2 percent on a reported basis, or 0.6 percent on an operational basis; global Botox Cosmetic net revenues were $2.720 billion; global Juvederm net revenues were $1.177 billion.

This diversification across four franchises — immunology, neuroscience, oncology, and aesthetics — represents a structurally different AbbVie from the single-product entity of 2015. The strategic logic is that no single LOE event can again threaten total company survival.

## 3. PORTER'S FIVE FORCES ANALYSIS

Porter's Five Forces framework examines the structural attractiveness of an industry and the competitive pressures a firm faces. Applied to AbbVie's position, it reveals a complex mix of durable advantages and intensifying threats.

### 3.1 Competitive Rivalry (HIGH)

The immunology and oncology therapeutic areas in which AbbVie competes are characterised by intense rivalry among large, well-resourced incumbents. In immunology, AbbVie's stronghold, it faces intense competition; Johnson & Johnson's Stelara generated $10.4 billion in 2024, while Novartis's Cosentyx is a significant challenger. In oncology, AbbVie's oncology drugs, such as Imbruvica and Venclexta, compete with offerings from Roche, AstraZeneca, and Merck & Co.; Merck's Keytruda, with $29.5 billion in 2024 sales, represents a substantial competitive force.

The rivalry is not merely about existing products. Imbruvica illustrates the risk of being superseded by next-generation molecules: Imbruvica is a first-generation Bruton's tyrosine kinase (BTK) inhibitor that isn't nearly as targeted as next-generation BTK inhibitors from AstraZeneca and BeiGene; off-target effects make Imbruvica difficult to tolerate and it's already losing ground to new BTK-inhibitors with better safety profiles. This is the warrant for treating competitive rivalry as a high and escalating threat: even approved, revenue-generating drugs can be displaced by superior successor molecules, creating a continuous innovation treadmill.

### 3.2 Threat of New Entrants (MODERATE–HIGH, Specifically Biosimilars)

Traditional barriers to entry in branded pharmaceuticals — capital requirements, regulatory approval timelines, patent protection — are high. However, the biosimilar segment represents a structurally distinct threat. The U.S. market has seen increased competition due to the introduction of multiple Humira biosimilars, impacting its sales; this trend underscores the growing influence of biosimilar manufacturers in the market. The mechanism is that once a biologic loses exclusivity, biosimilar manufacturers can enter with lower-priced alternatives, rapidly eroding the originator's market share. The Humira experience — a 40% U.S. sales decline within one year of LOE — demonstrates the speed and severity of this threat, warranting its classification as high for any currently approved AbbVie biologic approaching patent expiry.

### 3.3 Bargaining Power of Buyers (HIGH)

In the U.S. pharmaceutical market, buyers are not individual patients but institutional intermediaries — pharmacy benefit managers (PBMs), hospital integrated delivery networks (IDNs), and government payers. Securing formulary inclusion within integrated delivery networks is paramount for AbbVie, as it directly impacts patient access and market penetration for its treatments; the ability of these institutions to dictate which drugs are stocked and prescribed means AbbVie must offer compelling value propositions, including competitive pricing and demonstrated clinical efficacy, to gain preferred status. This buyer concentration grants significant leverage, particularly when multiple competing biologics exist in the same therapeutic class.

The Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) of 2022 has amplified government buyer power. US drug pricing reform is creating $25 billion in government savings through 2031, with AbbVie, Bristol Myers, Gilead and AstraZeneca taking the biggest hits. The IRA's Medicare drug price negotiation provisions directly reduce the price AbbVie can charge for its highest-revenue products in the government payer channel, compressing future margins on existing blockbusters.

### 3.4 Bargaining Power of Suppliers (LOW–MODERATE)

AbbVie's key inputs are specialised biological manufacturing capabilities, active pharmaceutical ingredients, and scientific talent. While specialised contract manufacturers have some leverage, AbbVie's scale and vertical integration in biologics manufacturing moderate supplier power. This force is less strategically determinative than the others in AbbVie's specific context.

### 3.5 Threat of Substitutes (MODERATE–HIGH)

Within therapeutic classes, the threat of substitution is significant. In immunology, AbbVie has demonstrated a successful transition strategy, with Skyrizi and Rinvoq showing robust growth, helping to mitigate the impact of biosimilar competition on Humira; in the first quarter of 2024, Skyrizi revenue grew 47% year-over-year to $2.2 billion, and Rinvoq revenue increased 57% to $1.2 billion, indicating a strong pipeline offset. However, this also illustrates the substitution dynamic: Skyrizi and Rinvoq are themselves *substitutes* for Humira, meaning AbbVie has had to cannibalise its own legacy revenue. The broader threat is that competitors' next-generation molecules could similarly substitute for Rinvoq (which carries an FDA black-box warning on cardiovascular risks for JAK inhibitors) or Skyrizi.

**Five Forces Summary:** The overall industry structure is moderately attractive for incumbents with strong IP, but AbbVie faces high competitive rivalry, a specific high threat from biosimilar entry, and growing buyer power through regulatory reform. These structural forces warrant a strategy centred on continuous innovation, portfolio diversification, and pricing resilience — exactly the approach AbbVie is pursuing, though with execution risks that the following sections examine.

## 4. RESOURCE-BASED VIEW AND DYNAMIC CAPABILITIES

The Resource-Based View (Barney, 1991) holds that sustained competitive advantage derives from resources and capabilities that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN). The Dynamic Capabilities framework (Teece, Pisano & Shuen, 1997) extends this by examining a firm's ability to sense, seize, and reconfigure resources in response to environmental change — precisely the challenge AbbVie faces.

### 4.1 Key Resources and Capabilities

**R&D Capability (Valuable, Rare, Inimitable):** AbbVie significantly increased its R&D expenditure to some $12.8 billion U.S. dollars in 2024, compared to $7.7 billion dollars in the year before. This near-doubling of R&D investment signals a deliberate capability build. The warrant is that in a science-driven industry where competitive advantage is created through discovery, R&D capability is the foundational VRIN resource. AbbVie's ability to develop Skyrizi and Rinvoq as differentiated successors to Humira — rather than simply losing the market — demonstrates this capability is real and productive.

**Commercial and Market Access Capability (Valuable, Rare):** AbbVie's competitive advantages are built on a foundation of strong research and development capabilities, an extensive patent portfolio, and a diversified product pipeline; the company has demonstrated a significant ability to innovate and transition from its legacy blockbuster drug to next-generation therapies, a crucial aspect of its market analysis. The commercial machine that drove Humira to $20 billion in annual sales has been redeployed to accelerate Skyrizi and Rinvoq uptake, with Skyrizi now holding 40% of the total prescription share of the biologics psoriasis market. Market share of this magnitude reflects commercial execution that competitors cannot easily replicate.

**Acquisition and Integration Capability (Dynamic Capability):** AbbVie has demonstrated a pattern of large-scale acquisitions followed by integration: Pharmacyclics (2015, ~$21B, bringing Imbruvica), Allergan (2020, ~$63B, bringing aesthetics and neuroscience), ImmunoGen (2024, ~$10.1B), and Cerevel (2024, ~$8.7B). The acquisition of ImmunoGen demonstrates AbbVie's commitment to deliver on its long-term growth strategy and enables AbbVie to further diversify its oncology pipeline across solid tumors and hematologic malignancies. In the Cerevel deal, AbbVie acquires the company's neuroscience pipeline of multiple clinical-stage and preclinical candidates with potential across several diseases including schizophrenia, Parkinson's disease, mood disorders and dementia-related psychoses in Alzheimer's disease and Parkinson's disease. The ability to identify, price, close, and integrate acquisitions of this scale is a dynamic capability that allows AbbVie to rapidly enter new therapeutic areas without building from scratch — a critical advantage given the time horizons involved in organic drug development.

### 4.2 Resource Vulnerabilities

**Over-reliance on two immunology assets:** While Skyrizi and Rinvoq are performing exceptionally, they are both within immunology and both face eventual LOE. The same structural vulnerability that made Humira's cliff so severe could recur if the company does not build a third wave of assets in oncology and neuroscience before the immunology duo peaks.

**Pipeline execution risk:** The emraclidine failure illustrates the limits of the acquisition-as-R&D strategy. AbbVie's treatment, emraclidine, which it got through its $9 billion acquisition of Cerevel Therapeutics, failed to significantly help patients in two trials, a blow to the company. In Phase 2 studies, patients on different doses of the drug, called emraclidine, did not experience significant improvements on a test called the Positive and Negative Syndrome Scale (PANSS) compared with the placebo group. AbbVie's shares fell approximately 13% on the day of announcement. This demonstrates that acquisition-led pipeline building transfers clinical risk rather than eliminating it, and that the company's valuation remains sensitive to pipeline readouts — a vulnerability that must be managed through diversification across multiple programmes.

**Debt load from acquisitions:** In connection with the ImmunoGen and Cerevel acquisition agreements, AbbVie obtained a commitment for up to $9.0 billion of senior unsecured bridge term loans for the purpose of financing all or a portion of the consideration payable, repaying certain indebtedness of ImmunoGen and Cerevel, and otherwise paying related fees and expenses. Serial large acquisitions have elevated financial leverage, constraining future strategic flexibility and increasing vulnerability to revenue shortfalls.

## 5. ANSOFF MATRIX ANALYSIS

Ansoff's Growth Matrix classifies strategic growth options along two dimensions: existing/new markets and existing/new products. AbbVie's current strategy employs all four quadrants, but with varying degrees of execution success.

### 5.1 Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets)

AbbVie's most successful near-term strategy has been driving Skyrizi and Rinvoq deeper into their approved indications within established markets. Net revenues for Skyrizi increased 48% for the three months ended March 31, 2024, primarily driven by continued strong market share uptake and market growth across all indications. The warrant is that penetration — gaining share in already-approved indications — has been the primary driver of revenue recovery and is the lowest-risk growth vector because it leverages existing commercial infrastructure and physician relationships.

### 5.2 Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets)

AbbVie is investing heavily in new products for markets it already serves. In oncology, highlights included new safety and efficacy data from a Phase 1 study of ABBV-400, a next-generation, potential best-in-class c-Met directed ADC, in patients with metastatic colorectal cancer; data from a first-in-human study of ABBV-706, a potential best-in-class SEZ6 directed ADC, in small cell lung cancer, high-grade central nervous system tumors and high-grade neuroendocrine neoplasms. These ADC programmes represent next-generation oncology products targeting patient populations AbbVie already serves through Venclexta and Elahere. The strategic logic is sound: building on existing oncology commercial infrastructure and medical affairs relationships while extending into new tumour types.

### 5.3 Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets)

AbbVie is expanding approved indications for Skyrizi into new disease areas. The approval was supported by data from two pivotal Phase 3 trials in which Skyrizi achieved the primary endpoint of clinical remission as well as key secondary endpoints; this marketing authorization for Skyrizi marks its fourth approved indication in the European Union. Each new indication approval effectively constitutes market development — the same molecule entering a new therapeutic market — and extends the commercial life and revenue potential of an already-approved asset.

### 5.4 Diversification (New Products, New Markets)

The Cerevel acquisition represented a bold diversification into neuroscience indications (schizophrenia, Parkinson's disease) that were new to AbbVie's pipeline. AbbVie has not been shy about its interest in expanding its presence in neuroscience, and the Cerevel deal looks to fit squarely within its stated strategy, adding five clinical-stage candidates and two preclinical candidates across several diseases including schizophrenia, Parkinson's disease, and mood disorders, which should drive growth in the latter part of this decade and into the 2030s. However, the emraclidine failure demonstrates the risk inherent in diversification: new therapeutic areas carry higher scientific uncertainty, and the $8.7 billion acquisition price embedded an assumption of clinical success that did not materialise for the lead asset. This warrants a recalibration of diversification strategy — maintaining optionality in neuroscience through the remaining Cerevel pipeline, but with more rigorous clinical de-risking before committing full acquisition premiums.

## 7. THE CORE STRATEGIC PROBLEM

The analyses converge on a single, coherent strategic problem: **AbbVie has successfully managed the first phase of the post-Humira transition — replacing immunology revenue with Skyrizi and Rinvoq — but has not yet secured the third wave of growth that will be required when the immunology duo approaches its own peak and eventual LOE in the 2030s.** The emraclidine failure has partially disrupted the neuroscience diversification thesis, Imbruvica is in structural decline, and the aesthetics franchise is stagnating. The company's financial leverage limits the option to simply acquire its way into the next wave. The strategic challenge is therefore to allocate R&D and business development resources efficiently across oncology (ADCs), neuroscience (remaining Cerevel pipeline plus new targets), and aesthetics recovery, while managing the IRA pricing headwind and the Rinvoq safety perception issue.

## 8. STRATEGIC RECOMMENDATIONS

The following recommendations form a coherent action plan, each grounded in the preceding analysis.

### Recommendation 1: Accelerate the ADC Platform as the Oncology Third Wave

**Rationale:** The ImmunoGen acquisition brought not only Elahere but an ADC platform. A credible immunology base gives AbbVie room to place longer bets elsewhere — to buy platforms, take clinical risk in oncology or neuroscience, and absorb setbacks without every programme being framed as a make-or-break Humira replacement. AbbVie's ABBV-400 (c-Met ADC) and ABBV-706 (SEZ6 ADC) are in early clinical development across multiple solid tumour types. Given that Imbruvica is in structural decline and Venclexta will face generic competition post-2030, the ADC pipeline must be the primary oncology growth engine for the next decade.

**Action:** Prioritise Phase 2 acceleration for ABBV-400 in colorectal cancer and ABBV-706 in small cell lung cancer. Allocate a dedicated ADC commercial team ahead of anticipated approvals. Consider in-licensing complementary ADC payloads or linker technologies to build platform breadth, as platform superiority — not single-asset success — creates durable competitive advantage in ADCs.

### Recommendation 2: Pursue Selective, De-Risked Neuroscience Business Development

**Rationale:** The emraclidine failure demonstrates the danger of paying full acquisition premiums for unvalidated Phase 2 assets in novel neuroscience mechanisms. AbbVie acquired Cerevel Therapeutics in August 2024 for $8.7 billion, through which the company gained a neuroscience pipeline of multiple clinical-stage and preclinical candidates. The remaining Cerevel assets in Parkinson's disease and mood disorders retain value, but the company should not repeat the pattern of large upfront acquisitions for single assets in high-risk indications.

**Action:** Shift neuroscience business development towards structured option-to-acquire agreements and milestone-gated licensing deals that transfer clinical risk to the smaller partner until Phase 2 proof-of-concept is established. This preserves the upside of neuroscience diversification while protecting the balance sheet from further write-downs. Advance the remaining Cerevel Parkinson's and mood disorder candidates through Phase 2 before making further large commitments in the space.

### Recommendation 3: Extend Skyrizi's Lifecycle Through Indication Expansion

**Rationale:** Skyrizi is AbbVie's most valuable current asset, with $11.7 billion in 2024 revenues and a trajectory toward peak sales well above Humira's. Indication expansion — the Ansoff market development strategy — is the most capital-efficient way to extend its commercial life. Skyrizi is part of a collaboration between Boehringer Ingelheim and AbbVie, with AbbVie leading development and commercialization globally. Its mechanism (IL-23 inhibition) has demonstrated efficacy across dermatology and gastroenterology, with further indications plausible.

**Action:** Invest in Phase 3 programmes for Skyrizi in additional inflammatory conditions (e.g., hidradenitis suppurativa, where lutikizumab is also in development). Each new indication approval extends the effective LOE date and adds incremental revenue with limited additional manufacturing or commercial cost. This is the highest-return investment available to AbbVie in the near term.

### Recommendation 4: Rehabilitate the Aesthetics Franchise Through Operational Restructuring

**Rationale:** The aesthetics portfolio — acquired through the $63 billion Allergan deal — has underperformed. AbbVie launched a revamped version of its Allē loyalty programme, which ultimately was not adopted by providers; instead of boosting adherence to AbbVie's products, the company saw aesthetics product sales decline 4.4% year over year to $1.3 billion in the fourth quarter. Additionally, a decrease in China due to global economic headwinds particularly took a chunk out of the aesthetics earnings for the quarter. The Allergan acquisition was justified partly by aesthetics providing non-cyclical, non-reimbursed revenue that would be immune to drug pricing pressures — but the reality has been more volatile.

**Action:** Conduct a strategic review of the Allergan Aesthetics division. In the near term, simplify the Allē programme to maximise provider adoption. For China, develop a localised commercial strategy that addresses the specific dynamics of the Chinese aesthetics market rather than applying a global template. If the aesthetics franchise cannot achieve the projected high single-digit CAGR through 2029 within two years, AbbVie should evaluate whether a partial divestiture or partnership structure could unlock value while freeing capital for higher-return pharmaceutical R&D.

### Recommendation 5: Proactively Manage the IRA Pricing Headwind

**Rationale:** Analysts from Morningstar figure that the IRA's new measures will reduce industry revenue by 2% overall, but some companies will feel the pinch more than others — including AbbVie. The mechanism is that Medicare price negotiation will directly reduce net revenues on AbbVie's highest-volume products, compressing margins on the very assets that are funding the transition strategy.

**Action:** AbbVie should accelerate its engagement with the IRA negotiation process to secure the best achievable net prices, while simultaneously shifting its commercial contracting strategy toward value-based agreements with private payers that can partially offset Medicare price reductions. On the portfolio side, prioritise pipeline investments in therapeutic areas and patient populations with lower Medicare exposure — including paediatric indications, rare diseases, and aesthetics — which are either exempt from negotiation or less exposed to the IRA's pricing provisions.

### Recommendation 6: Reduce Financial Leverage to Restore Strategic Flexibility

**Rationale:** Serial large acquisitions have loaded AbbVie's balance sheet with debt. AbbVie obtained a commitment for up to $9.0 billion of senior unsecured bridge term loans for the purpose of financing the ImmunoGen and Cerevel acquisitions. High leverage constrains the ability to respond to future strategic opportunities — whether defensive (e.g., a competitor threatening Rinvoq's market share) or offensive (e.g., a transformative acquisition in a new therapeutic area).

**Action:** Establish a formal deleveraging target over the next three years, prioritising debt reduction over share buybacks until leverage ratios return to investment-grade norms. The strong cash generation from Skyrizi and Rinvoq provides the capacity to do this without sacrificing R&D investment. Restored balance sheet flexibility is not merely a financial objective — it is a strategic one, enabling AbbVie to act decisively when the next acquisition opportunity emerges.

The six recommendations form a coherent, sequenced plan:

The logic connecting these priorities is as follows: Recommendations 3 and 5 protect the current revenue base (Skyrizi/Rinvoq and margin management). Recommendations 1 and 2 build the next wave of growth (oncology ADCs and neuroscience). Recommendations 4 and 6 resolve structural weaknesses (aesthetics underperformance and leverage) that constrain strategic options. Together, they address the core problem — securing a third wave of growth before the immunology duo peaks — while managing the near-term risks of pricing pressure and pipeline concentration.

## 10. CONCLUSION

AbbVie's post-Humira transition is, by the evidence, succeeding in its first phase. "2024 was a year of significant progress for AbbVie; our growth platform delivered outstanding results, we advanced our pipeline with key regulatory approvals and promising data, and we strengthened our business through strategic transactions," said Robert A. Michael, chief executive officer, AbbVie. The numbers bear this out: total revenues grew 3.7% despite Humira falling by nearly $5.5 billion in a single year. However, the strategic challenge is not the transition already achieved but the one that must begin now — building the third wave of growth that will sustain the company through the 2030s when Skyrizi and Rinvoq themselves approach maturity.

The Porter Five Forces analysis shows that AbbVie operates in a structurally demanding environment where competitive rivalry is high, biosimilar threats are structural, and buyer power is being amplified by regulatory reform. The RBV analysis shows that AbbVie's R&D and commercial capabilities are genuine sources of competitive advantage, but that acquisition-led pipeline building transfers rather than eliminates clinical risk, as emraclidine demonstrated. The Ansoff analysis shows that market penetration (Skyrizi/Rinvoq) and market development (indication expansion) are the highest-return near-term strategies, while diversification (ADCs, neuroscience) requires more disciplined capital allocation than AbbVie has shown to date.

The recommendations — accelerate ADCs, de-risk neuroscience deals, extend Skyrizi, fix aesthetics, manage IRA, and deleverage — are not independent initiatives but a coherent response to a single strategic problem: how to sustain a top-quartile pharmaceutical franchise through not one but two successive product transitions. The evidence supports the conclusion that AbbVie has the capabilities to succeed, but only if it applies them with greater selectivity and financial discipline than the Cerevel experience suggests it has done.
