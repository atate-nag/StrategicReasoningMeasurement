# ExxonMobil: Navigating the Dual Imperative — Hydrocarbon Dominance and Low-Carbon Transformation

## A Strategic Case Study

---

## 1. Introduction and Case Overview

ExxonMobil Corporation (NYSE: XOM) stands at one of the most consequential strategic crossroads in its 140-year history. As the world's largest publicly traded integrated oil and gas company, it faces a situation that is simultaneously familiar — commodity price volatility, geopolitical risk, competitive rivalry — and structurally novel: a global energy system undergoing a long-run, non-linear transition away from fossil fuels. The strategic question at the heart of this case is not simply *whether* ExxonMobil should respond to the energy transition, but *how* — and whether its chosen approach, which diverges sharply from European peers, is coherent, defensible, and durable.

This case study analyses ExxonMobil's strategic situation using five interconnected frameworks: PESTEL analysis (macro-environment), Porter's Five Forces (industry structure), the Resource-Based View / VRIN framework (internal capabilities), SWOT analysis (synthesis), and the Ansoff Matrix (growth strategy options). The analysis culminates in a set of concrete strategic recommendations.

---

## 2. Background: The Company and the Situation

ExxonMobil holds a dominant position in the integrated oil and gas sector, underpinned by its comprehensive upstream, downstream, and chemical operations. The company's value proposition lies in its ability to manage the entire energy value chain, from resource extraction to product delivery.

For full-year 2024, the company reported earnings of $33.7 billion, or $7.84 per share assuming dilution, and delivered $55.0 billion in cash flow from operations — its third best year in a decade. Yet this financial strength coexists with mounting structural pressure. The energy products of oil and gas majors have contributed significantly to global greenhouse gas emissions and planetary warming over the past century. Decarbonizing the global economy by mid-century to avoid dangerous climate change cannot occur without a profound transformation of their fossil fuel-based business models.

The strategic situation is therefore defined by a fundamental tension: ExxonMobil's core business remains highly profitable, yet the long-run legitimacy, regulatory environment, and investor appetite for that business are all under pressure. The company has chosen a distinctive response — a "dual-track" strategy that doubles down on advantaged hydrocarbons while simultaneously building an industrial-scale Low Carbon Solutions (LCS) business — that sets it apart from both its American peers and its European rivals.

## 3. PESTEL Analysis: The Macro-Environment

### 3.1 Political

The political environment is bifurcated. In the United States, the Trump administration has been broadly favourable to oil and gas, reducing regulatory burden and supporting domestic energy production. However, ExxonMobil's global footprint means it is simultaneously exposed to contrasting political regimes. ExxonMobil operates in 46 countries worldwide, with significant exposure to politically sensitive regions. The company has also faced direct political confrontation over climate governance: at the end of January 2024, ExxonMobil filed a lawsuit against shareholders Follow This and Arjuna Capital in order to exclude their proposal from the company's 2024 proxy statement — a proposal to implement stricter Scope 3 emission targets. This unprecedented legal action signals a deliberate political-strategic choice to resist externally imposed climate governance.

### 3.2 Economic

The macroeconomic backdrop is characterised by oil price uncertainty. ExxonMobil's 2030 plan is stress-tested at a conservative $65/barrel Brent, and the company has built its strategy around capital discipline at lower price points. ExxonMobil's strategic focus on cost efficiency and asset optimization has resulted in leading returns on capital employed, with a notable 12.7% return for 2024. The company's structural cost savings programme is a key economic pillar: since 2019, ExxonMobil has delivered $13.5 billion in structural cost savings, more than all other IOCs combined.

### 3.3 Social

Social pressure on fossil fuel companies continues to intensify, particularly from institutional investors, NGOs, and younger consumers. Scope 3 emissions — indirect pollution from the end use of ExxonMobil's products — account for 90% of ExxonMobil's carbon footprint, according to activist investors Arjuna Capital and Follow This. This social and reputational dimension is not merely symbolic: it translates into real capital allocation decisions by large asset managers and sovereign wealth funds. Simultaneously, social demand for affordable, reliable energy — particularly in the developing world — provides a counter-narrative. ExxonMobil's Global Outlook shows an approximately 25% increase in energy use in developing countries versus 2024, driven by population growth and rising living standards.

### 3.4 Technological

Technology is both a threat and an opportunity. Renewable energy costs have fallen dramatically, but the pace of displacement is slower than many anticipated. Even with faster growth, solar and wind still only made up approximately 3% of the world's energy mix in 2024. Meanwhile, ExxonMobil is investing heavily in technologies that leverage its existing engineering competencies: carbon capture and storage (CCS), direct lithium extraction, low-carbon hydrogen, and advanced polymer materials. ExxonMobil's plan for lithium is to pump lithium-rich brines from far underground and chemically remove the metal using direct lithium extraction (DLE), a family of separation technologies that has not yet been deployed at commercial scale.

### 3.5 Environmental

Environmental regulation is tightening globally. As of 2024, ExxonMobil is involved in 17 active climate-related lawsuits with potential total litigation exposure estimated at $3.2 billion. The Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) in the United States has created both risk and opportunity: the IRA raised the 45Q tax credit for carbon capture to $85 per metric ton for permanently stored CO₂, up from $50 per metric ton previously. This policy shift materially improves the economics of ExxonMobil's CCS business, making the Low Carbon Solutions strategy more viable than it would have been under the prior regulatory regime.

### 3.6 Legal

The legal landscape is complex and evolving. Beyond the climate litigation noted above, ExxonMobil faces regulatory scrutiny across multiple jurisdictions. The company's decision to sue activist shareholders in 2024 represents a legal-strategic gambit: ExxonMobil filed a lawsuit in an unprecedented effort to block activist investors from putting forward an "extreme" proposal for stricter emissions reductions at its annual shareholder meeting. The outcome of such cases will shape the boundaries of shareholder rights and corporate climate governance for the entire industry.

## 4. Porter's Five Forces: Industry Structure Analysis

Porter's Five Forces framework (1979, 1980) analyses the structural attractiveness of an industry by examining five competitive pressures. Applied to ExxonMobil's position, the picture reveals a structurally challenging but not unwinnable environment.

### 4.1 Competitive Rivalry (HIGH)

ExxonMobil faces intense rivalry from global giants like Shell, Chevron, BP, and TotalEnergies, all operating across the full oil and gas spectrum. This competition spans exploration, production, refining, and marketing, creating a dynamic and fiercely contested landscape. Rivalry is intensified by the commodity nature of the core product (crude oil is effectively homogeneous), high fixed costs, and the fact that all majors are simultaneously competing for the same "advantaged" assets — low-cost, low-carbon-intensity reserves. National oil companies (NOCs) such as Saudi Aramco and QatarEnergy add a further competitive dimension, as they benefit from sovereign backing and preferential resource access. The energy transition is beginning to reshape rivalry: ExxonMobil's strategy is uniquely defined by its 'dual-track' approach of aggressively pursuing both advantaged hydrocarbon growth and building a new, industrial-scale Low Carbon Solutions business, which differs significantly from its peers.

### 4.2 Threat of New Entrants (LOW–MODERATE)

The upstream oil and gas industry has extraordinarily high barriers to entry: capital requirements in the tens of billions of dollars, decades-long project timelines, specialist geological and engineering expertise, and regulatory hurdles. However, the energy transition is creating new competitive spaces — CCS services, hydrogen, lithium — where barriers are lower and non-traditional entrants (technology firms, utilities, start-ups) are active. ExxonMobil's early-mover acquisitions in CCS infrastructure (Denbury) and lithium (Arkansas) are partly designed to erect barriers in these nascent markets before they become crowded.

### 4.3 Bargaining Power of Suppliers (LOW–MODERATE)

ExxonMobil's enormous scale generally moderates supplier power. The general supplier power is tempered by ExxonMobil's massive scale, with a market capitalisation of approximately $530 billion as of late 2024. However, in specialised technology domains — advanced drilling equipment, proprietary CCS technology, subsea engineering — suppliers of unique solutions can command higher prices. The shift toward low-carbon technologies also introduces new supplier dependencies in areas such as direct lithium extraction equipment and electrolyser manufacturing.

### 4.4 Bargaining Power of Buyers (MODERATE)

At the retail level, buyers have limited power — fuel is largely undifferentiated and consumers face few alternatives for liquid transport fuels in the short run. However, large industrial and sovereign buyers — refiners, petrochemical companies, governments — have considerable negotiating leverage. Downstream customers at the retail level face low switching costs in competitive fuel markets, but large industrial buyers can exert significant pressure on pricing and contract terms. As the energy transition progresses, some large industrial buyers are actively seeking to decarbonise their supply chains, creating demand for ExxonMobil's CCS services — a reversal of the traditional buyer-seller dynamic.

### 4.5 Threat of Substitutes (HIGH and RISING)

This is arguably the most strategically significant force. The energy transition is accelerating the threat of substitutes, with rivalry heating up against Shell and National Oil Companies for prime assets. The threat of substitutes, primarily renewables, is intensifying as their costs continue to decline. Electric vehicles threaten gasoline demand; heat pumps threaten heating oil; solar and wind threaten natural gas in power generation. ExxonMobil's strategic response — emphasising demand scenarios in which oil remains essential for industrial feedstocks, aviation, and shipping — is a deliberate counter-narrative to the substitution threat. Oil is projected to remain the largest source of primary energy, as it is essential for industrial manufacturing, including as a raw material, and commercial transportation.

**Porter's Five Forces Summary:** The industry is structurally challenging, with high rivalry, rising substitution threats, and evolving buyer dynamics. ExxonMobil's competitive position within this structure depends critically on its internal capabilities — which the Resource-Based View addresses.

## 5. Resource-Based View: Internal Capabilities and VRIN Analysis

The Resource-Based View (Barney, 1991; Wernerfelt, 1984) holds that sustained competitive advantage derives from resources and capabilities that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN). ExxonMobil possesses several such resources.

### 5.1 Scale and Integration (Valuable, Rare, Inimitable)

ExxonMobil's vast scale and global operations provide a significant competitive advantage. The company's extensive network of upstream, downstream, and chemical operations ensures a steady revenue stream from diverse sources. Vertical integration across the entire hydrocarbon value chain — from exploration to refining to chemicals to retail — creates synergies that pure-play competitors cannot replicate. ExxonMobil's competitive advantages are deeply rooted in its vast scale, technological prowess, integrated operational model, and robust financial health, enabling synergies across upstream, downstream, and chemical segments. In the first quarter of 2025, this scale translated into a 22.71% market share within the integrated oil and gas industry based on total revenues.

### 5.2 Technology and R&D Capability (Valuable, Rare, Inimitable)

ExxonMobil's proprietary technology portfolio — in reservoir engineering, refining catalysis, polymer chemistry, and increasingly in CCS and direct lithium extraction — constitutes a durable competitive advantage. ExxonMobil's industry-leading resource base, technology advantages, project-management discipline, and financial strength provide a sustainable competitive advantage to capitalise on the opportunities ahead. Crucially, these engineering competencies are being deliberately redirected toward low-carbon applications: ExxonMobil's Low Carbon strategy focuses on Carbon Capture and Storage and hydrogen, leveraging its core engineering competencies, whereas Chevron's approach is more 'pragmatic' with targeted investments in areas like renewable diesel and power for AI data centres.

### 5.3 Structural Cost Discipline (Valuable, Rare)

ExxonMobil's cost management is genuinely exceptional. Since 2019, ExxonMobil has delivered $13.5 billion in structural cost savings, more than all other IOCs combined, and its 2030 structural cost savings plan exceeds their cumulative cost savings targets. This capability allows ExxonMobil to remain profitable at lower oil prices than most competitors, providing strategic resilience across commodity cycles.

### 5.4 Advantaged Asset Portfolio (Valuable, Rare, Inimitable)

Two assets in particular stand out as VRIN resources:

**Permian Basin:** In 2024, ExxonMobil acquired Pioneer Natural Resources, more than doubling its Permian footprint, and is now operating as a single combined entity across the region. The Permian is the lowest-cost, highest-return shale basin in the world, and ExxonMobil's dominant position there — now the largest single operator — is effectively irreplicable.

**Guyana / Stabroek Block:** Exxon Guyana production exceeded 900,000 barrels per day in 2025, cementing the Stabroek Block as one of the most profitable offshore developments in the world. The efficiency of the Stabroek Block — with an estimated breakeven of US$35/bbl — explains the robust margins even under price pressure. This asset is genuinely rare: world-class deepwater discoveries of this magnitude are exceptionally uncommon.

### 5.5 CCS Infrastructure (Valuable, Rare, Potentially Inimitable)

The Denbury acquisition has created a new category of VRIN resource in the emerging CCS market. The acquisition of Denbury provides ExxonMobil with the largest owned and operated CO₂ pipeline network in the U.S. at 1,300 miles, including nearly 925 miles of CO₂ pipelines in Louisiana, Texas, and Mississippi — located within one of the largest industrial emitting regions in the country. This physical infrastructure — pipelines, injection wells, storage sites — is capital-intensive and geologically constrained, making it genuinely difficult for competitors to replicate quickly. ExxonMobil's recent acquisition of Denbury is set to make it the largest owner and operator of the CO₂ pipeline network in the United States.

**RBV Summary:** ExxonMobil's competitive advantage is grounded in a cluster of VRIN resources: scale-integrated operations, proprietary technology, cost discipline, and a portfolio of genuinely advantaged assets in both hydrocarbons and emerging CCS infrastructure. The strategic challenge is ensuring these resources remain relevant as the energy system evolves.

## 6. SWOT Analysis: Synthesis

The PESTEL and Five Forces analyses of the external environment, combined with the RBV assessment of internal capabilities, can now be synthesised into a SWOT framework.

### Strengths
- **Financial strength:** ExxonMobil's strategic focus on cost efficiency and asset optimisation resulted in a 12.7% return on capital employed for 2024, with robust cash flow allowing $16.7 billion in dividends and $19.3 billion in share repurchases.
- **Structural cost leadership:** $13.5 billion in cumulative structural cost savings since 2019, surpassing all other IOCs combined.
- **Advantaged asset base:** World-class positions in the Permian Basin and Guyana, plus the largest CO₂ pipeline network in the United States.
- **Integrated business model:** The company's integrated structure across upstream, downstream, and chemical operations creates economic synergies, enabling ExxonMobil to capture margins across the value chain.

### Weaknesses
- **Scope 3 exposure:** Scope 3 emissions — indirect pollution from the end use of ExxonMobil's products — account for 90% of its total carbon footprint, representing a significant and largely unaddressed reputational and regulatory liability.
- **Fossil fuel concentration:** Despite LCS investments, ExxonMobil's earnings remain overwhelmingly dependent on hydrocarbon prices. The company's refusal to commit to Scope 3 targets leaves it vulnerable to regulatory tightening and investor withdrawal.
- **Reputational risk:** ExxonMobil must address its shortcomings, particularly its reliance on fossil fuels and environmental liabilities, by embracing innovation and expanding into renewable energies to navigate the shifting tides.
- **Policy dependency of LCS:** Execution of low-emission opportunities is contingent on the right policy and regulation as well as continued technology and market development.

### Opportunities
- **CCS market leadership:** The IRA's enhanced 45Q tax credits — raised to $85/metric ton for permanently stored CO₂ — materially improve CCS project economics. ExxonMobil's existing infrastructure positions it to be the dominant provider of industrial decarbonisation services.
- **Emerging market energy demand:** Rising living standards will increase energy use by 25% in developing countries, with half the world's population currently lacking the energy needed to address basic human needs.
- **Lithium for the energy transition:** ExxonMobil hopes to start lithium production in Arkansas in 2027 and intends to eventually become a leading producer of the mineral — a market directly enabled by the electric vehicle transition that threatens its core business.
- **LNG growth:** New LNG projects in Papua New Guinea and Mozambique will extend ExxonMobil's gas portfolio into structurally growing markets in Asia and Africa.
- **High-value product growth:** High-value products, including new businesses such as Proxxima™ systems and carbon materials, are projected to contribute more than 40% of earnings potential by 2030, extending growth into high-margin, high-growth markets.

### Threats
- **Accelerating substitution:** The rising cost-competitiveness of renewables and EVs threatens long-run oil demand, particularly in road transport and power generation.
- **Regulatory and legal risk:** As of 2024, ExxonMobil is involved in 17 active climate-related lawsuits with potential total litigation exposure estimated at $3.2 billion.
- **Investor activism:** The 2024 shareholder lawsuit signals growing tension between management and ESG-oriented investors, with potential implications for capital costs and governance.
- **Geopolitical risk:** Operations in 46 countries expose ExxonMobil to political disruption, resource nationalism, and supply chain instability.
- **Policy reversal risk:** Execution of low-emission opportunities is contingent on the right policy and regulation, meaning that IRA rollback or changes in the 45Q regime would materially impair the LCS business case.

## 7. Ansoff Matrix: Growth Strategy Analysis

The Ansoff Matrix (1957) provides a framework for evaluating ExxonMobil's growth options across four quadrants: Market Penetration, Product Development, Market Development, and Diversification. ExxonMobil's current strategy spans all four, but with varying degrees of risk and coherence.

### 7.1 Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets)
ExxonMobil's Permian and Guyana expansions are the clearest examples of market penetration — producing more oil and gas for existing global energy markets. By 2030, at a 2024 dollar real Brent price of $65 per barrel, the company plans to deliver an additional $9 billion in Upstream annual earnings potential — more than 50% higher than in 2024. This is a low-risk strategy that leverages existing capabilities and assets, and is supported by ExxonMobil's demand scenario, which projects continued oil demand well into the 2040s. The Pioneer acquisition is the cornerstone of this quadrant.

### 7.2 Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets)
ExxonMobil is developing new products — performance chemicals, Proxxima™ polymer systems, battery anode materials — for its existing industrial and commercial customer base. ExxonMobil's unparalleled integration across the value chain, particularly its strategic push into high-value performance chemicals and materials such as Proxxima resins and battery anode materials, creates revenue streams and synergies that competitors cannot easily replicate. This quadrant is where ExxonMobil's chemical and downstream businesses are evolving from commodity to speciality, improving margins and reducing commodity price sensitivity.

### 7.3 Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets)
ExxonMobil's CCS business — selling CO₂ capture and storage services to third-party industrial emitters — represents market development: applying existing geological and engineering capabilities to a new customer segment (heavy industry seeking to decarbonise). ExxonMobil is pursuing up to $30 billion of low emission opportunities between 2025 and 2030, with almost 65% spent on reducing emissions for third-party customers. The Denbury pipeline network is the enabling infrastructure for this market development play.

### 7.4 Diversification (New Products, New Markets)
Lithium extraction represents ExxonMobil's boldest diversification move — entering the battery materials market with a product and customer base entirely distinct from its core oil and gas business. This is the highest-risk quadrant. ExxonMobil's plan to use direct lithium extraction is a family of separation technologies that has not yet been deployed at commercial scale. The logic is that ExxonMobil's drilling expertise and subsurface knowledge — genuine VRIN capabilities — transfer to brine extraction, reducing the technological risk of what would otherwise be a pure diversification bet.

**Ansoff Summary:** ExxonMobil's growth strategy is broadly coherent: it concentrates capital in the low-risk market penetration quadrant (Permian, Guyana) while making targeted investments in product development (speciality chemicals), market development (CCS services), and selective diversification (lithium). The risk is that the portfolio is too weighted toward hydrocarbon market penetration, leaving the company exposed if the energy transition accelerates faster than its demand scenarios suggest.

## 8. The Central Strategic Tension: The Dual-Track Dilemma

The analysis above reveals a coherent but precarious strategic logic. ExxonMobil's "dual-track" approach — maximising hydrocarbon returns while building LCS capabilities — rests on a specific set of assumptions about the pace and direction of the energy transition. If those assumptions are correct, ExxonMobil will have captured the best of both worlds: cash flows from advantaged hydrocarbons funding the patient development of new businesses. If they are wrong — if the transition accelerates, policy tightens, or demand peaks earlier than projected — ExxonMobil risks being caught between two incomplete strategies.

The peer comparison illuminates the stakes. Only the European majors explicitly mention a commitment to shifting beyond fossil fuels to achieve a net-zero-emissions business model. As of June 2024, ExxonMobil's $490 billion market cap dwarfed Shell's $220 billion, with Chevron ($279 billion) and TotalEnergies ($156 billion) trailing further behind. Meanwhile, European majors grapple with the financial and strategic burdens of over-investing in energy transition initiatives that have yet to deliver meaningful returns. In the near term, ExxonMobil's approach appears vindicated by financial performance. The longer-term question is whether the valuation premium reflects genuine strategic superiority or a systematic underpricing of transition risk.

ExxonMobil's own 2025 Global Outlook acknowledges the uncertainty: global CO₂ emissions fall 25% in its base case; affordability will drive the pace of any transition, and sustained oil and natural gas investment is more important than ever. But even ExxonMobil concedes that under more aggressive climate scenarios, oil demand could fall to 65 million barrels per day by 2050 — a 40% decline from today's levels. The strategy must be robust across this range of outcomes.

## 9. Strategic Recommendations

The following recommendations are sequenced to address the most urgent vulnerabilities first, while building toward long-run strategic resilience.

### Recommendation 1: Accelerate and Ring-Fence the Low Carbon Solutions Business

**Rationale:** ExxonMobil's LCS business is currently a strategic option rather than a fully committed business unit. The $20–30 billion investment envelope for 2025–2030 is explicitly contingent on policy and market conditions, which creates strategic ambiguity. Execution of these opportunities is contingent on the right policy and regulation as well as continued technology and market development. This conditionality, while financially prudent, risks signalling to industrial customers that ExxonMobil is not a reliable long-term CCS partner.

**Action:** Separate the LCS business into a distinct reporting segment with its own P&L, capital allocation, and management incentives. Ring-fence a minimum committed CCS investment of $3–4 billion per annum, regardless of hydrocarbon price conditions. Accelerate the Baytown low-carbon hydrogen project to a final investment decision, as this single project will serve as a definitive signal of strategic commitment. Pursue long-term CCS offtake contracts with industrial emitters (steel, cement, chemicals) to create revenue certainty that reduces policy dependency.

**Theoretical grounding:** From the RBV perspective, the Denbury pipeline network is a VRIN asset only if it is actively developed into a customer-facing business. First-mover advantage in CCS infrastructure is time-limited — competitors will begin building rival networks if ExxonMobil hesitates.

### Recommendation 2: Develop a Credible Scope 3 Engagement Strategy

**Rationale:** Scope 3 emissions account for 90% of ExxonMobil's carbon footprint, yet the company has taken legal action to prevent shareholders from even voting on Scope 3 targets. This posture is strategically unsustainable. Institutional investors managing trillions of dollars are increasingly incorporating Scope 3 into their portfolio risk frameworks, and regulatory requirements (particularly in the EU and increasingly in the US) are moving in the direction of mandatory Scope 3 disclosure.

**Action:** Rather than opposing all Scope 3 engagement, ExxonMobil should develop a product-level carbon intensity roadmap that demonstrates how its portfolio — including low-carbon fuels, CCS-enabled industrial products, and lithium — reduces end-use emissions over time. This reframes Scope 3 from a liability into a market opportunity: customers who use ExxonMobil's CCS services are reducing their own Scope 3 emissions. This approach transforms a governance confrontation into a commercial proposition.

**Theoretical grounding:** From a stakeholder theory perspective (Freeman, 1984), ExxonMobil's adversarial posture toward activist investors is creating unnecessary reputational costs. A proactive Scope 3 narrative would reduce litigation risk, lower the cost of capital, and open new commercial relationships with ESG-conscious industrial buyers.

### Recommendation 3: Deepen the Permian–Guyana Core While Maintaining Capital Discipline

**Rationale:** ExxonMobil's advantaged asset base in the Permian and Guyana is its most durable source of competitive advantage and cash generation. The Pioneer acquisition more than doubled ExxonMobil's Permian footprint, and the company is on track to achieve net-zero Scope 1 and 2 GHG emissions across all its Permian operations by 2035 — 15 years faster than Pioneer had planned for its assets before the merger. The Stabroek Block's estimated breakeven of US$35/bbl ensures robust margins even under significant price pressure.

**Action:** Continue to prioritise capital allocation to the Permian and Guyana, but with an explicit "green premium" investment layer: fund the accelerated Permian net-zero Scope 1 and 2 programme, methane monitoring technology, and flaring elimination. This positions these assets as the lowest-carbon-intensity barrels available from any major — a genuine competitive differentiator as carbon border adjustment mechanisms (CBAMs) and carbon pricing spread globally. Maintain the structural cost savings discipline, targeting the raised $20 billion cumulative savings goal by 2030.

**Theoretical grounding:** From the Ansoff Matrix perspective, this is the market penetration quadrant — the lowest risk, highest return strategy. The key is to execute it in a way that also builds long-run legitimacy by demonstrating that hydrocarbon production and emissions reduction are not mutually exclusive.

### Recommendation 4: Scale the Lithium Business Through Strategic Partnerships

**Rationale:** ExxonMobil's direct lithium extraction programme uses a family of separation technologies that has not yet been deployed at commercial scale. This technology risk, combined with the need to develop downstream relationships with battery manufacturers and EV companies, represents a genuine capability gap. ExxonMobil has the subsurface expertise; it lacks the battery value chain relationships and DLE technology at scale.

**Action:** Pursue a joint venture or strategic alliance with a leading battery technology company (e.g., a major EV manufacturer or battery cell producer) to co-develop the Arkansas lithium project. This would provide ExxonMobil with market access, technology validation, and offtake certainty, while providing the partner with a domestic US lithium supply chain — strategically valuable given geopolitical tensions with China over critical mineral supply. Structure the partnership to retain operational control while sharing technology development costs.

**Theoretical grounding:** From the Ansoff Matrix, lithium is a diversification play — the highest risk quadrant. Partnerships reduce risk by sharing it. From the RBV, the goal is to complement ExxonMobil's geological and drilling capabilities (VRIN) with battery market capabilities (which it lacks) to create a more complete competitive position.

### Recommendation 5: Pursue Selective Divestment of Non-Advantaged Assets to Fund the Transition

**Rationale:** The industry trend shows oil majors divesting non-core assets amid the energy transition, with 2024 energy transition M&A hitting $497 billion as companies prioritise electrification and carbon capture. ExxonMobil has already begun this process — selling European chemical plants and Singapore fuel stations — but the pace should be accelerated to free capital for higher-return investments.

**Action:** Conduct a systematic portfolio review using a "carbon cost of capital" framework: assets with high Scope 1 and 2 emissions intensity and limited reduction potential should be divested or restructured. Proceeds should be allocated to CCS infrastructure expansion and the Baytown hydrogen project. This also reduces ExxonMobil's absolute emissions footprint, improving its standing with ESG investors without requiring Scope 3 commitments.

**Theoretical grounding:** Porter's Five Forces analysis shows that competitive rivalry in commodity refining and retail is intense, margins are thin, and buyer power is high. These are structurally unattractive segments. Capital reallocation from low-return, high-rivalry segments to high-barrier, first-mover segments (CCS, lithium) is consistent with both Porter's competitive positioning logic and the RBV emphasis on deploying capital toward VRIN resources.

## 11. Conclusion

ExxonMobil's strategic situation is defined by a genuine and unresolved tension between near-term hydrocarbon dominance and long-run energy transition risk. The analysis across PESTEL, Porter's Five Forces, the Resource-Based View, SWOT, and the Ansoff Matrix reveals a company with formidable competitive advantages — scale, integration, cost discipline, and a portfolio of genuinely rare assets — but one that faces mounting structural threats from substitution, regulatory pressure, and investor activism that its current strategy addresses only partially.

The "dual-track" approach is coherent but insufficiently committed on the low-carbon side. ExxonMobil's updated 2030 plan — with increases of $5 billion in earnings and cash flow growth versus the prior plan with no capital spending increase — demonstrates the power of its transformation. But financial transformation must be accompanied by strategic transformation: a credible Scope 3 narrative, a ring-fenced LCS business, and a lithium strategy built on partnerships rather than solo execution.

The chain of logic is clear: ExxonMobil's VRIN resources (scale, technology, CCS infrastructure, advantaged assets) provide a platform for competitive advantage that is currently underutilised in the energy transition. The recommendations above are designed to convert that platform into durable strategic positions across multiple future energy scenarios — ensuring that ExxonMobil wins not just in the world it expects, but in the range of worlds it might face.
