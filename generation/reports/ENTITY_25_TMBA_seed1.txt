# 📡 PAN-AFRICAN TELECOMS CASE STUDY
## **MTN Group: Navigating the Platform Pivot — From Connectivity Provider to Digital Ecosystem Leader**

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

MTN Group, Africa's largest telecommunications operator by subscribers, stands at a critical strategic inflection point. Having built an unrivalled pan-African connectivity footprint across 16 markets, MTN now faces a paradox: its core voice and traditional data business is under structural pressure from declining average revenue per user (ARPU), currency volatility, and rising operating costs — yet it sits atop one of the world's most valuable and underexploited digital infrastructure assets. This case study examines MTN's strategic situation using **Porter's Five Forces**, the **Resource-Based View (VRIO)**, **PESTEL analysis**, **Ansoff's Growth Matrix**, and **McKinsey's Three Horizons framework**. The analysis leads to a structured strategic action plan for MTN to execute its "Ambition 2030" pivot from a traditional telco to a pan-African digital platform business.

# PART ONE: CASE BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

## 1.1 Company Overview

MTN Group was founded in South Africa in 1994 and has since grown into Africa's dominant telecommunications operator, with operations across 16 African markets including Nigeria, South Africa, Ghana, Uganda, and Ivory Coast. Its primary competitors are Airtel Africa (14 African markets), Orange Africa (Francophone Africa), Vodacom, and Safaricom.

MTN Group reported its strongest annual performance to date in 2025, with service revenue rising nearly 25% to R218 billion and adjusted headline earnings per share up 67%. The pan-African telecoms operator also announced a new Ambition 2030 strategy built around three platforms: connectivity, fintech, and digital infrastructure. The results cap a year in which MTN surpassed 307 million voice subscribers, 172 million data users, and 70 million Mobile Money customers across 16 African markets. Data traffic grew 27%, with average monthly consumption per user reaching 12.5 GB, up from 10.8 GB the previous year. MTN's fintech division processed more than 23 billion transactions in 2025, with total transaction value exceeding $500 billion.

These headline results, however, mask a deeply complex strategic landscape. In 2024 — the year immediately preceding this recovery — adjusted headline earnings per share (HEPS) decreased by 32% to 816 cents, impacted mainly by the sharp devaluation in the naira. Nigeria contributes approximately 40% of MTN Group's total revenue, making the company acutely exposed to single-market macroeconomic shocks. MTN Nigeria posted a loss after tax of N400.44 billion in 2024. Nigerian authorities devalued the naira twice in less than a year, as part of government measures to try to stabilise the currency, which had for years been pegged at an artificially high level against the US dollar.

This volatility crystallises MTN's central strategic dilemma: how does a pan-African telecommunications company with extraordinary scale build a sustainable, resilient, and diversified business model that is less exposed to the structural weaknesses of African markets — currency instability, energy insecurity, regulatory fragmentation, and declining voice revenue — while simultaneously capitalising on the continent's enormous digital growth opportunity?

## 1.2 The Pan-African Telecoms Landscape

The Africa Entertainment and Telecommunication Market is expected to reach USD 88.16 billion in 2025 and grow at a CAGR of 5.06% to reach USD 120.03 billion by 2030. The mobile industry's $140 billion contribution to GDP in 2023 is projected to reach $170 billion by 2030 if key connectivity barriers are addressed.

With approximately 835 million registered mobile money accounts in Sub-Saharan Africa in 2023, telco operators are leveraging their infrastructure to offer financial services, increasing financial inclusion and creating new revenue streams. Yet the digital divide is stark: mobile internet penetration in Sub-Saharan Africa reached 27% by the end of 2023, yet a substantial usage gap of 60% remains, representing millions who live within network coverage but face barriers such as device affordability, digital literacy, and the cost of data. In 2024, internet usage in cities stood at 57%, compared to just 23% in rural communities — the most pronounced gap found in any ITU region.

The top eight African telcos by revenue have seen strong growth in the number of mobile subscribers, but their average revenue per user has declined since 2020 as a result of inflation, low income levels, and increasing competition. Despite this, total revenue continues to grow because of rising mobile penetration and data adoption.

# PART TWO: STRATEGIC ANALYSIS

## 2.1 PESTEL Analysis

### 🔵 Political
MTN operates across 16 African markets, each with distinct and often unstable political environments. The conflict in Sudan materially impacted MTN's 2024 results. Operators building pan-African service layers — from mobile money to cloud infrastructure — must navigate dozens of incompatible compliance regimes, raising capital costs precisely when convergence demands scale. Mobile termination rates — the fees operators pay to carry voice traffic across networks — still vary wildly across borders, creating additional layers of complexity and overhead that are ultimately passed on to the consumer.

### 🟡 Economic
In 2024, MTN and Airtel collectively repaid a combined $1.2 billion in foreign currency debt to alleviate the pressures of foreign exchange fluctuations and steer their operations back towards profitability. Despite these headwinds, Nigeria's telecommunications industry delivered a record performance in 2024, with total revenue surging to ₦7.67 trillion. MTN led with 84.6 million subscribers, representing a 51.4% market share, and approximately ₦3.94 trillion ($2.63 billion) in revenue. Airtel followed with 56.6 million subscribers (34.4%) and ₦2.64 trillion ($1.76 billion).

### 🟢 Social
Africa has the world's youngest population, creating a digitally native consumer base with strong appetite for mobile services and digital payments. Financial exclusion remains widespread, with large segments of the population unbanked but mobile-connected — making mobile money a transformative social tool. The disparity between urban and rural areas is acute, with internet usage in cities at 57% versus 23% in rural communities — representing both a social equity challenge and a commercial opportunity.

### 🟠 Technological
As of mid-2025, fewer than 30% of African countries — just 16 — had assigned 5G spectrum, a stark contrast to the over 90% in Europe. 5G FWA adoption is on the rise across the continent, with operators already offering services in key markets, including Angola, Kenya, Nigeria, South Africa, Zambia, and Zimbabwe. On the data centre frontier, demand for data centre capacity in Africa is expected to rise from about 0.4 gigawatts today to 1.5 to 2.2 GW by 2030, requiring $10 billion to $20 billion in new investment and unlocking an estimated revenue pool of $20 billion to $30 billion across the value chain by 2030.

### 🔴 Environmental
Energy insecurity is one of the most acute operational challenges for African telcos. More than 500,000 telecommunication towers across Africa remain dependent on diesel for power. In some remote areas of the continent, diesel accounts for 30% to 60% of a tower's operational costs. Logistical expenses further exacerbate the problem, with fuel transportation alone increasing costs by 15% to 30%. In Sub-Saharan Africa, 60–80% of telecom towers face grid outages lasting 8–12 hours daily.

### ⚫ Legal
South Africa's ICASA, Nigeria's NCC, and Ghana's NCA each operate distinct licensing, pricing, and spectrum policy regimes. By June 2025, fewer than 30% of African countries — just 16 nations — had allocated spectrum for 5G services, compared to over 90% in Europe, illustrating how regulatory divergence is materially slowing the transition to converged next-generation networks.

## 2.2 Porter's Five Forces Analysis

### ⚔️ Force 1: Competitive Rivalry — **HIGH**

The pan-African telecoms market is characterised by intense oligopolistic competition. Airtel Africa's mobile money processes nearly $200 billion annually, and Airtel Money is now ranked among Africa's top three mobile-money providers, alongside M-Pesa and MTN Mobile Money. In Francophone Africa, Orange Money processed $190+ billion in transaction volume across 9 billion transactions in 2024, making it a major financial infrastructure in Francophone Africa. Price competition and regulatory costs, like biometric SIM registration in Kenya and Ghana, are putting downward pressure on ARPU.

### 🚪 Force 2: Threat of New Entrants — **LOW-MEDIUM**

The capital intensity of building a pan-African telecommunications network creates formidable barriers to entry. However, the threat is evolving. Fintech-native challengers are entering the mobile money space without the burden of network infrastructure. Emerging challengers leverage super-app strategies, telecom infrastructure, and low-fee models, poised to disrupt or complement incumbents. Satellite providers are also beginning to serve enterprise and rural markets, potentially bypassing traditional MNO infrastructure.

### 🔄 Force 3: Threat of Substitutes — **HIGH**

OTT services represent the most significant substitution threat to traditional telco revenue. WhatsApp, Viber, and other messaging and VoIP applications have substantially eroded voice and SMS revenues. OTTs present a threat to mobile network operators' traditional sources of revenue — voice and SMS. As OTT services evolve from instant messaging to all forms of communication, some mobile operators feel increasingly threatened while others use it as an opportunity to gain market share. McKinsey estimates that telcos failing to adapt could see revenue declines of 15–30% from OTT substitution alone.

### 💰 Force 4: Bargaining Power of Buyers — **MEDIUM AND RISING**

Individual consumers have limited direct bargaining power, but the collective effect of price sensitivity in low-income markets is significant. In South Africa, the average revenue per user (ARPU) is projected to decline to R90, down from R120, as companies strive to retain customers in a price-sensitive environment, impacting overall profitability and investment in infrastructure. Enterprise customers have increasing bargaining power as they demand bundled services, and many consumers hold multiple SIMs, weakening operator lock-in.

### 🏭 Force 5: Bargaining Power of Suppliers — **MEDIUM**

Tower companies (IHS Towers, American Tower Corporation) represent significant suppliers. In Nigeria, smaller tower operators such as Pan African Towers (764 towers) and Africa Mobile Networks (4,000 towers) manage far fewer sites, mostly in niche or rural areas. This concentration means most mobile network operators rely heavily on IHS and American Tower. If MTN prioritises its own lease amendments and 5G upgrades across thousands of existing tenancies, competitors could worry about the sequencing of similar upgrades on shared sites, reinforcing MTN's market position if tower access becomes a competitive bottleneck.

Its weakest area relative to strategic ambition is enterprise/B2B digital services, where it lacks the depth of global cloud providers and local challengers.

# PART THREE: RECOMMENDATIONS AND ACTION PLAN

---

## 🎯 Recommendation 1: Execute a Structured FX Risk Mitigation Programme

**Strategic Rationale:** The naira devaluation crisis exposed MTN's dangerous revenue concentration. Sustainable pan-African strategy requires structural, not tactical, FX risk management.

**Action Plan:**
- **Accelerate local-currency reinvestment:** Prioritise capex deployment in naira, cedi, and other local currencies to reduce the proportion of USD-denominated obligations. Continue the programme of FX debt repayment — MTN and Airtel collectively repaid $1.2 billion in 2024 to alleviate the pressures of foreign exchange fluctuations.
- **Revenue diversification by market:** Invest to grow MTN South Africa, Ghana, and Ivory Coast as counterweights to Nigeria concentration. Target a maximum of 30% revenue contribution from any single market within five years.
- **Natural hedging through local procurement:** Source tower equipment, energy solutions, and services locally where possible to match revenue and cost currency profiles.
- **Timeline:** 12–36 months for structural debt reduction; 36–60 months for material revenue diversification.

---

## 🌿 Recommendation 2: Launch a Pan-African "Green Tower" Programme

**Strategic Rationale:** Energy costs represent the single largest controllable operating expense. In some remote areas, diesel accounts for 30% to 60% of a tower's operational costs, with fuel transportation alone increasing costs by 15% to 30%.

**Action Plan:**
- **Phase 1 (Year 1–2):** Convert the highest-cost diesel towers (top 20% by energy spend) to solar-hybrid systems in Nigeria, South Africa, Ghana, and Uganda. Partner with specialist energy providers under long-term power purchase agreements (PPAs) to minimise upfront capex.
- **Phase 2 (Year 2–4):** Extend solar-hybrid conversion across all off-grid and weak-grid tower sites. Integrate battery storage for 24-hour energy independence on high-priority rural sites.
- **Phase 3 (Year 4–5):** Develop an **energy-as-a-service** offering, selling excess solar capacity to local communities and enterprises — creating a new revenue stream and strengthening community relationships.
- **Financial Target:** Reduce average tower energy cost by 35–45% within five years.

---

## 🤝 Recommendation 3: Deepen and Accelerate the MTN-Airtel Network Sharing Alliance

**Strategic Rationale:** MTN Group and Airtel Africa have agreed to share mobile infrastructure in Nigeria and Uganda, with negotiations for further collaboration on fibre and wireless infra-sharing in other markets now underway. This alliance should be treated as a strategic platform, not merely a cost-cutting exercise.

**Action Plan:**
- **Expand RAN sharing to five additional markets** (Congo-Brazzaville, Rwanda, Zambia, Cameroon, Tanzania) within 24 months, within regulatory frameworks.
- **Develop joint fibre backbone infrastructure** in markets where neither operator has sufficient scale to justify independent deployment, particularly in rural corridors.
- **Establish a neutral joint infrastructure management entity** to manage shared assets, reducing transaction costs and ensuring equitable access.
- Engage other operators in the market as the MTN-Airtel agreement explicitly does not preclude the parties from collaborating with other operators in any respective market.
- **Financial Target:** Reduce network infrastructure capex by 20–30% in shared markets, enabling redeployment of capital into digital platform development.

---

## 💳 Recommendation 4: Accelerate MoMo Platform Deepening and Pursue Independent Listing

**Strategic Rationale:** Airtel Money processed nearly $200 billion annually and is now ranked among Africa's top three mobile-money providers. MTN MoMo's transformation into a standalone fintech entity marks a defining moment in Africa's digital finance landscape, with a $14 billion valuation in sight. MTN must move decisively to protect and extend its fintech leadership.

**Action Plan:**
- **Product deepening:** Expand MoMo beyond payments into savings, microcredit (AI-driven credit scoring using transaction history), insurance, and merchant acquiring. MTN's partnership with Mastercard, announced in 2024, is a prime example of its forward-thinking approach and will empower MoMo merchants to seamlessly accept card payments, connecting millions of people and small businesses across Africa.
- **Cross-border payments:** Leverage MTN's 16-market footprint to build a proprietary cross-border remittance network, competing on price and convenience. Interoperability is high on the agenda, with MTN advocating for seamless transactions across borders.
- **Independent listing:** Pursue a partial IPO or strategic listing of MTN MoMo (valued at approximately $14 billion) within 24–36 months to unlock capital, attract fintech talent, and create an acquisition currency.
- **Developer ecosystem:** Launch an open API platform enabling third-party developers, fintechs, and enterprises to build on MoMo infrastructure, creating network effects and a platform moat.
- **Timeline:** Product deepening: 12 months; Cross-border rollout: 18–24 months; Independent listing: 24–36 months.

---

## 🏢 Recommendation 5: Build a Differentiated Enterprise Digital Services Business

**Strategic Rationale:** VRIO analysis identifies enterprise/B2B digital services as MTN's most significant capability gap. Telcos are advancing in markets where bundled connectivity and managed services give them an edge, leveraging existing enterprise relationships and government trust.

**Action Plan:**
- **Establish a dedicated MTN Enterprise division** with independent P&L accountability, separate from the consumer business.
- **Partner with hyperscalers** (AWS, Microsoft Azure, Google Cloud) to offer co-branded cloud and AI services, leveraging MTN's distribution reach and local market knowledge.
- **Target government digital transformation contracts** in each of MTN's 16 markets, including e-government infrastructure, smart city connectivity, and national broadband projects under PPP structures.
- **Develop IoT capabilities** for key African verticals: precision agriculture, logistics (fleet tracking), utilities (smart metering), and healthcare (remote patient monitoring).
- **Financial Target:** Grow enterprise revenue to 20% of total service revenue within five years.

---

## 📜 Recommendation 6: Champion Pan-African Regulatory Harmonisation

**Strategic Rationale:** Operators building pan-African service layers must navigate dozens of incompatible compliance regimes, raising capital costs precisely when convergence demands scale. MTN, as the continent's largest operator, has both the incentive and the credibility to lead advocacy for harmonisation.

**Action Plan:**
- **Engage the African Union and GSMA** to advocate for harmonised spectrum allocation frameworks, enabling faster and cheaper 5G rollout across multiple markets simultaneously.
- **Advocate for mobile money interoperability standards** across African markets, enabling seamless cross-border transactions.
- **Support Smart Africa's Digital Single Market initiative**, which received $20 million from institutional investors in early 2024, as a vehicle for advancing MTN's regulatory agenda through a credible multilateral channel.
- **Engage national regulators proactively** on spectrum pricing reform, arguing that lower spectrum costs translate to faster network rollout and higher government tax revenues from a larger digital economy.
- **Timeline:** Ongoing; measurable outcomes (5G spectrum allocation in five additional markets) within 24 months.

# CONCLUSION

MTN Group's strategic situation encapsulates the defining challenge of pan-African telecoms in the mid-2020s: extraordinary opportunity constrained by structural complexity. The continent's growing population, expanding digital landscape, rising demand for mobile services, and emerging appetite for new technologies allow the telco sector to leapfrog outdated infrastructure and create new value for businesses and consumers alike. Yet currency volatility, energy insecurity, regulatory fragmentation, and intensifying competition mean that the "Ambition 2025" playbook — build the network, grow subscribers, add mobile money — is no longer sufficient.

In a classic case of "What got you here won't get you there," telco strategies of today may not be sufficient to win the future. MTN's VRIO analysis reveals that its most durable competitive advantages — network infrastructure, brand, regulatory relationships, and subscriber scale — are the foundations upon which a platform business must be built, not the platform itself. The six recommendations in this case study are designed to protect and leverage those foundations while systematically building the new capabilities — fintech depth, enterprise digital services, energy resilience, and regulatory influence — that will determine MTN's competitive position in 2030 and beyond.

The strategic transformation from connectivity provider to digital platform leader is not a choice MTN can defer. Airtel Africa's fintech unit has emerged as a serious challenger, with transaction volumes approaching $200 billion annually. OTT services continue to erode voice revenue. Energy costs are compressing margins. The window for decisive platform investment is open — but it will not remain so indefinitely.
