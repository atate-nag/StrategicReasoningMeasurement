# Mercedes-Benz at the Crossroads: Strategic Analysis and Recommendations

## Case Study in Business Strategy

## 1. INTRODUCTION AND CASE OVERVIEW

Mercedes-Benz Group AG — the world's oldest automobile manufacturer, tracing its lineage to Carl Benz's 1886 patent — finds itself at a pivotal strategic inflection point. Between 2023 and 2025, the company has experienced a cascade of compounding pressures: a sharp deterioration in its most important single market (China), a collapse in battery electric vehicle (BEV) demand that confounded its own forecasts, a reversal of its "electric-only" strategy, intensifying competition from Chinese luxury EV brands, and the added shock of US trade tariffs. The result is a company whose financial performance has declined dramatically — the company's 2024 operating results showed a 31% decline to €13.6 billion, with passenger car segment margins falling from 12.6% to 8.1% — and whose strategic direction is under active reconstruction.

This case study analyses the strategic situation using four interlocking frameworks: PESTEL analysis (to map the external environment), Porter's Five Forces (to assess industry structure), the Resource-Based View (to identify internal competitive advantages), and Porter's Generic Strategies combined with the Ansoff Matrix (to evaluate strategic options). The analysis culminates in a set of prioritised recommendations. Throughout, each claim is sourced, each claim connects to the analytical chain, and each inferential step explicitly states why the evidence warrants the conclusion drawn.

## 2. BACKGROUND: MERCEDES-BENZ'S STRATEGIC TRAJECTORY

Mercedes-Benz is one of the world's most recognised luxury brands. Competitors include BMW, Audi, Porsche, and Tesla across performance, EVs, and software; Mercedes emphasises brand cachet, dealer network, captive finance, and a 140-year heritage as differentiators. The company's strategy from 2021 onwards was premised on a decisive pivot to electric mobility. In July 2021, Mercedes-Benz announced that it was getting ready to go all electric by the end of the decade, shifting from "electric-first" to "electric-only", accelerating toward an emissions-free and software-driven future.

That strategy has since been materially revised. In 2021, the firm promised to go all-electric by 2030, where market conditions allow, and set an interim target of 50 percent electrified vehicle sales by the middle of the decade; by 2024, the company was pushing back sales targets for electrified vehicles, targeting 50 percent electrified sales by 2030, rather than 2025. This retreat is not merely a scheduling adjustment — it reflects a fundamental mismatch between the company's strategic ambition and the market conditions it confronted. Understanding *why* this mismatch occurred, and *what* Mercedes-Benz should do next, is the central problem this case addresses.

## 3. PESTEL ANALYSIS: MAPPING THE EXTERNAL ENVIRONMENT

PESTEL analysis identifies the macro-environmental forces acting upon the firm. The warrant for using PESTEL here is that Mercedes-Benz is a globally exposed, capital-intensive manufacturer whose strategy is highly sensitive to regulatory, economic, and technological shifts. Each factor below is sourced and connected to the strategic problem.

### 3.1 Political

The most acute political risk is US trade policy. Mercedes-Benz said its net profit had plummeted from €6.1 billion to €2.7 billion in the first half of 2025, a 56% year-on-year drop, with the company predicting that US tariffs would knock €360 million off its bottom line. More specifically, a 25% Section 232 tariff raises the cost of German-built Mercedes models significantly if they are imported to one of the brand's largest markets. This matters strategically because it directly erodes the margin advantage that underpins Mercedes-Benz's premium pricing model: if tariff costs cannot be fully passed on to consumers without demand destruction, the company's profitability per unit — already under pressure — deteriorates further.

Additionally, the 2025 Trump administration imposed tariffs on imports from China, Canada, and Mexico, targeting automotive parts, steel, aluminium, and finished vehicles, with some products facing tariffs as high as 25%. This is strategically significant because Mercedes-Benz's supply chain is deeply globalised; input cost inflation compounds the revenue pressure from weaker sales volumes.

### 3.2 Economic

The year 2024 has been a difficult year for Mercedes-Benz: between the slowdown in demand for electric vehicles, the stagnation of the Chinese market, the crisis in the luxury sector, and the difficulties of the German industry as a whole, the car manufacturer showed a sharp decline in annual results. This economic context is crucial: the simultaneous weakening of two of Mercedes-Benz's most important pillars — the Chinese market and the EV segment — means the company cannot rely on either to compensate for weakness in the other, creating a compounding rather than offsetting dynamic.

China's economic slowdown is particularly consequential. The 3% decline to 1.98 million vehicles sold was led by a slump in deliveries in China, its largest market, where consumers are shunning models like the S-Class and Maybach in favour of local brands. Sales in the country dropped 7% to 683,600 cars from a year earlier. The inference is direct: China is not a marginal market for Mercedes-Benz — it is the single largest one, and its deterioration cannot be absorbed elsewhere without deliberate strategic action.

### 3.3 Social

Consumer preferences in China have shifted structurally, not merely cyclically. High-end models such as the S-Class and Maybach saw a sharp decline in demand, as local consumers increasingly favoured domestic brands like BYD over foreign luxury cars. This social shift — the growing prestige attached to Chinese technology brands — challenges a core assumption of Mercedes-Benz's luxury positioning: that European heritage commands an automatic premium. If Chinese consumers increasingly associate cutting-edge technology with domestic brands rather than German ones, the brand equity that Mercedes-Benz has spent 140 years building faces structural erosion in its largest market.

### 3.4 Technological

Mercedes-Benz has made significant technological investments. In the area of car software, Mercedes-Benz has announced its own proprietary MB.OS operating system, developed in-house. It will allow Mercedes-Benz to centralise the control of all the vehicles' domains and consumer interfaces, with proprietary software development allowing greater speed and more frequent updates, designed around scalable architectures that will control future development costs.

Furthermore, Mercedes-Benz has updated its conditionally automated driving system DRIVE PILOT, receiving approval by the German Federal Motor Transport Authority, making it the fastest certified system for conditionally automated driving (SAE Level 3) in a standard production vehicle, optionally available on the S-Class and EQS. This is strategically significant because Level 3 certification is a regulatory and technological differentiator that competitors have not yet matched in production vehicles — it represents a potential source of sustainable competitive advantage.

However, the technological challenge from Chinese rivals is intensifying. Local players like BYD, Li Auto, and NIO have changed the game; industry insiders revealed that the Li Auto L9 caught the attention of Mercedes executives, with one senior official reportedly stating, "We need to benchmark against this vehicle." Meanwhile, Huawei-backed brands such as the AITO M9 and the Zhijie S7 have demonstrated smart driving capabilities that Mercedes now sees as an urgent learning objective. This confirms that the technological threat is not abstract — Mercedes-Benz's own executives have acknowledged it as a benchmark-setting challenge.

### 3.5 Environmental

The company achieved a 75% reduction in carbon emissions in 2024, surpassing its 2030 target of 80% by a year, and is now prioritising circular economy practices, such as using 100% green electricity at its Rastatt plant. While this demonstrates credible environmental progress, it also represents a sunk cost: the investments made to achieve these targets cannot be easily redirected. The environmental imperative thus creates a path dependency that constrains strategic flexibility, particularly if the pace of EV adoption continues to lag behind the infrastructure required to support it.

### 3.6 Legal

Regulatory pressure on internal combustion engines remains a structural force. The EU's effective ban on new ICE vehicle sales from 2035 sets a legal boundary that constrains Mercedes-Benz's long-run product portfolio regardless of near-term consumer preferences. This creates a strategic tension: the company must invest in electrification for long-run compliance while managing near-term demand that still favours ICE and hybrid powertrains.

**PESTEL Summary and Strategic Implication:** The PESTEL analysis reveals that Mercedes-Benz faces an environment characterised by *simultaneous* pressures across multiple dimensions — political (tariffs), economic (China slowdown), social (shifting brand preferences), technological (Chinese EV competition), and legal (EV mandates). No single factor is decisive; their combination is. This means that a single-lever response (e.g., cutting costs alone, or doubling down on EVs alone) will be insufficient. The analysis therefore warrants a multi-front strategic response, which the subsequent frameworks help to specify.

## 4. PORTER'S FIVE FORCES: INDUSTRY STRUCTURE ANALYSIS

Porter's Five Forces framework assesses the structural attractiveness of the industry and identifies where competitive pressure is most intense. The warrant for using this framework is that it reveals *why* profitability is declining — not merely *that* it is declining — by locating the source of value erosion in the industry structure.

### 4.1 Competitive Rivalry — **HIGH and Intensifying**

The competitive rivalry within the luxury automotive sector is exceptionally intense. Established rivals like BMW and Audi continuously challenge market share through innovation in performance and design, while new EV-focused entrants such as Tesla and Polestar also exert significant pressure with advanced technology, and Chinese OEMs are gaining ground rapidly. The key implication is that rivalry is no longer confined to the traditional European luxury triad (Mercedes, BMW, Audi). The competitive set has expanded structurally, meaning that Mercedes-Benz must now defend its position on multiple fronts simultaneously — traditional luxury, EV technology, and software-defined vehicle experience — against different classes of competitor.

Sales of top-end cars like the S-Class and Maybach sedans fell 14% in 2024, which is directly attributable to this intensified rivalry: Chinese luxury EV brands have made inroads precisely in the flagship segment that Mercedes-Benz has historically relied upon for its highest margins.

### 4.2 Threat of New Entrants — **LOW for Traditional Luxury, HIGH for Software/EV**

The bulk of existing premium and luxury automobile firms are well-established and old; the danger of new entrants into the industry is low. The automobile business has high entry barriers, and any new company intending to join the market would have to invest a large amount of money and overcome legal and operational challenges before becoming a well-known and popular company.

However, this analysis requires qualification. The traditional barriers (capital, manufacturing scale, dealer networks) remain high for conventional automakers. But the EV transition has lowered certain barriers — software-first companies like Tesla, and Chinese OEMs backed by state investment, have entered at scale. This bifurcated threat landscape means that while the *manufacturing* barriers remain high, the *software and technology* barriers have been materially reduced, creating a new class of entrant that Mercedes-Benz must treat seriously.

### 4.3 Bargaining Power of Buyers — **MODERATE to HIGH**

In the luxury segment, buyers historically exercised limited price sensitivity — the willingness to pay a premium was a defining feature of the segment. However, this is changing. Mercedes-Benz has been forced to offer consumer incentives to combat slowing EV demand, particularly in China where luxury spending has been hit by economic slowdown. The need to offer incentives is a direct indicator that buyer power has increased: consumers now have credible alternatives (Chinese luxury EVs) and are exercising their bargaining power by demanding lower effective prices or switching brands entirely.

This shift in buyer power is particularly damaging because it erodes the pricing discipline that is central to Mercedes-Benz's margin strategy. The Mercedes-Benz Cars division reported an adjusted EBIT of €8.7bn, a decline from €14.3bn in 2023, primarily due to lower sales volumes, particularly in China, negative net pricing, and an unfavourable model mix. The phrase "negative net pricing" directly confirms that buyer power is translating into margin compression.

### 4.4 Bargaining Power of Suppliers — **MODERATE**

Mercedes-Benz's scale and long-term supplier relationships moderate supplier power. However, the transition to EVs introduces new supply chain dependencies — particularly in battery raw materials (lithium, cobalt, nickel) — where suppliers have greater leverage. The "Next Level Performance" programme explicitly addresses this: a key component of this strategy is a 10% reduction in production costs by 2027, with the company working closely with suppliers to optimise material costs while also streamlining internal operations to cut fixed expenses. This confirms that supplier cost management is identified as a material lever, implying that supplier power is a genuine constraint on profitability.

### 4.5 Threat of Substitutes — **LOW to MODERATE**

In the near term, substitutes for luxury automobiles (public transport, ride-sharing) do not threaten the core luxury buyer segment. However, the rise of autonomous ride-hailing services represents a longer-term substitution risk, particularly in urban markets. More immediately, within the automotive category itself, Chinese luxury EVs function as substitutes for Mercedes-Benz's flagship models — offering comparable or superior technology at lower prices, effectively substituting for the brand's value proposition rather than the product category.

**Five Forces Summary and Strategic Implication:** The most urgent forces are *competitive rivalry* (intensified by Chinese OEMs) and *buyer power* (manifesting as negative net pricing). Together, these two forces explain the bulk of Mercedes-Benz's margin deterioration. The strategic implication is that Mercedes-Benz must either reinforce its differentiation to justify its price premium (reducing buyer power) or find structural cost efficiencies to protect margins even at lower price points. The following section assesses whether the company's internal resources support a differentiation strategy.

## 5. RESOURCE-BASED VIEW: INTERNAL CAPABILITIES ANALYSIS

The Resource-Based View (RBV) holds that sustainable competitive advantage derives from resources that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (the VRIN framework). Applying RBV to Mercedes-Benz identifies which of its assets can anchor a durable strategy and which are vulnerable.

### 5.1 Brand Heritage and Equity — VRIN Satisfied

Mercedes-Benz's brand is its most durable strategic asset. Mercedes-Benz Group AG built market-leading brand equity over 140 years, enabling durable price premiums and customer loyalty. Brand heritage of this depth is genuinely rare and inimitable — no competitor can replicate 140 years of engineering history, motorsport heritage, and cultural association with luxury. However, the evidence also shows that this asset is not infinitely durable: Mercedes-Benz's global brand value decreased by approximately five percent in 2024, declining to 22.8 billion U.S. dollars, down from 23.98 billion in 2023. This decline, while modest in percentage terms, is strategically significant because it signals that brand equity is not self-sustaining — it requires active investment and delivery on the brand promise. The inference is that Mercedes-Benz must protect and reinvest in its brand, particularly in markets (like China) where it is under competitive attack.

### 5.2 Engineering and Technology Capabilities — VRIN Partially Satisfied

Mercedes-Benz's engineering heritage is a genuine source of competitive advantage. Since Carl Benz registered his patent for the motor car, high-tech becomes everyday reality: future technologies are systematically developed, tested, and brought into series production; quality, design, and engineering expertise shape its unmistakable identity. The DRIVE PILOT Level 3 certification exemplifies this: it is the fastest certified system for conditionally automated driving (SAE Level 3) in a standard production vehicle, representing a capability that competitors have not yet matched in certified production form.

However, in the software domain, this advantage is less secure. The MB.OS operating system is still being deployed, and Chinese competitors have demonstrated superior software integration in their vehicles. This suggests that engineering capability is VRIN-satisfying in hardware and safety systems, but not yet in software — a gap that requires urgent investment.

### 5.3 Manufacturing Scale and Global Footprint — Valuable but Not Rare

Mercedes-Benz's global production network is valuable but not rare — BMW, Volkswagen Group, and others possess comparable manufacturing scale. The "Next Level Performance" programme recognises this: rather than treating manufacturing scale as an advantage, it is rationalising capacity. Global production capacity for passenger cars is planned to be reduced from 2.5 million in 2024 to between 2–2.2 million units by 2027, while retaining the flexibility to readjust as the product launch campaign gains traction. This deliberate capacity reduction is strategically rational: it prioritises margin over volume, consistent with a differentiation strategy. The warrant is that if manufacturing scale is not a source of differentiation, then *over-capacity* is a cost burden rather than an asset, and rationalisation improves return on assets.

### 5.4 Sub-Brand Portfolio — VRIN Satisfied

Mercedes-Benz's portfolio of sub-brands (AMG, Maybach, G-Class, and the new Mythos series) represents a rare and inimitable asset. The company has projected the share of top-end vehicle sales to rise by around 60% by 2026, through a product offensive from Maybach, AMG, and G-Class with dedicated EV characteristics and greater individualisation, plus the "MYTHOS Series" of highly exclusive collectible cars. This portfolio allows Mercedes-Benz to pursue a tiered luxury strategy — capturing ultra-high-net-worth consumers through Maybach and Mythos, performance enthusiasts through AMG, and adventure luxury through G-Class — in a way that competitors cannot easily replicate because each sub-brand carries its own distinct heritage and customer community.

### 5.5 Digital and Software Capabilities — Emerging, Not Yet VRIN

As of its announcement, the company was on track to exceed its own goal of €1 billion EBIT from digital services in 2025, with software becoming a substantial part of the company's product development. This is a promising development, but digital revenue at this scale remains modest relative to the group's overall EBIT. The inference is that digital services represent an *emerging* rather than an *established* source of competitive advantage — one that must be developed urgently, particularly as Chinese competitors demonstrate more advanced software integration.

**RBV Summary and Strategic Implication:** Mercedes-Benz's most durable and VRIN-satisfying assets are its brand heritage, its sub-brand portfolio, and its certified autonomous driving technology. Its weakest internal position is in software and digital services. The strategic implication is that the company should *anchor* its strategy in its strongest VRIN assets (brand, sub-brands, Level 3 autonomy) while *investing urgently* to close the software gap. This directly informs the choice of generic strategy.

## 6. PORTER'S GENERIC STRATEGIES AND ANSOFF MATRIX: STRATEGIC POSITIONING

### 6.1 Porter's Generic Strategies

Porter's framework posits that sustainable competitive advantage requires a clear choice between cost leadership, differentiation, or focus. The big automotive brands such as Mercedes-Benz, BMW, Ford, and General Motors all seek a differentiated position. For Mercedes-Benz specifically, a cost leadership strategy is structurally inconsistent with its brand positioning — competing on price against Chinese manufacturers with lower labour costs would destroy the brand premium that is its primary asset. The RBV analysis above confirms this: Mercedes-Benz's VRIN assets are all on the differentiation side (brand, sub-brands, technology).

The relevant question is therefore whether Mercedes-Benz should pursue *broad differentiation* (serving the full luxury market) or *focused differentiation* (concentrating on the ultra-luxury segment). The evidence suggests the company is moving toward focused differentiation. The company has projected the share of top-end vehicle sales to rise by around 60% by 2026, through a product offensive from Maybach, AMG, and G-Class. This is a deliberate narrowing of focus toward the highest-margin, most brand-loyal segment — where Chinese competitors have the least traction and where Mercedes-Benz's 140-year heritage commands the greatest premium.

However, the company has not fully committed to focused differentiation. In 2024, demand for the A- and B-Class cars made up more than a quarter of total passenger car sales, indicating that entry-level models still represent a substantial volume base. The tension between volume (entry-level) and value (top-end luxury) is a central strategic dilemma. Porter's framework warns explicitly against being "stuck in the middle" — pursuing neither cost leadership nor differentiation consistently. The evidence suggests Mercedes-Benz risks this trap if it does not make a clear strategic choice.

### 6.2 Ansoff Matrix: Growth Strategy Options

The Ansoff Matrix maps growth options across existing/new products and existing/new markets, providing a structured way to evaluate Mercedes-Benz's strategic options.

**Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets):** The "Next Level Performance" programme represents a form of market penetration — selling more effectively in existing markets by improving cost efficiency and product quality. The group announced plans to slash production costs by a tenth by 2027 and also gave a bleak outlook for this year, saying it expected lower sales and leaner profit margins. While cost reduction is necessary for survival, market penetration alone is insufficient given the structural shift in Chinese consumer preferences and EV demand — it addresses symptoms rather than causes.

**Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets):** This is the most active quadrant of Mercedes-Benz's current strategy. Beginning with the all-new CLA, Mercedes-Benz will start the most intense product launch programme in its history, including the major upgrade of the S-Class in 2026 as well as a string of launches at Mercedes-AMG; overall, Mercedes-Benz plans for dozens of new or refreshed models to reach markets until 2027. The product offensive is the company's primary growth lever, and it is strategically coherent because it leverages existing brand equity and distribution networks while refreshing the product portfolio.

The CLA is particularly important because it introduces the new MMA platform with powertrain flexibility: the upcoming CLA replacement exemplifies this approach, offering EV and gasoline-powered versions, using a flexible platform designed to support electric and ICE powertrains. This platform flexibility is a direct response to the uncertainty about EV adoption rates — it hedges against demand risk by allowing the same vehicle to be sold with different powertrains depending on market conditions. The inference is that this is strategically prudent: it avoids the over-commitment to a single powertrain technology that proved costly in 2023–2024.

**Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets):** Mercedes-Benz is investing in deepening its presence in China rather than retreating. Mercedes-Benz plans to invest more than RMB 14 billion ($2 billion) in China with local partners to further diversify its localised product lineup of passenger cars and light commercial vehicles. The strategic warrant for this investment is that China, despite its current difficulties, remains the world's largest automotive market; a retreat would permanently cede ground to local competitors in a market that is growing in the ultra-luxury segment even as it becomes more competitive in the mainstream luxury segment.

**Diversification (New Products, New Markets):** The digital services and software revenue stream represents a form of related diversification. Software will become a substantial part of the company's product development, with the company on track to exceed €1 billion EBIT from digital services in 2025. This is the highest-risk Ansoff quadrant, but the related diversification into software services is relatively lower risk than unrelated diversification because it leverages the existing vehicle platform and customer base.

## 7. SYNTHESIS: THE CORE STRATEGIC PROBLEM

The frameworks converge on a coherent diagnosis. Mercedes-Benz faces a *strategic coherence problem*: its historical strategy (broad luxury differentiation, volume-driven growth, aggressive EV-only transition) has been disrupted by a combination of external shocks (China slowdown, EV demand collapse, tariffs) and competitive threats (Chinese OEMs) that have simultaneously eroded its revenue, margins, and brand equity. The company is in the process of recalibrating — but the recalibration is not yet fully coherent.

The key tension is between:
1. **Volume vs. Value**: Maintaining entry-level models to sustain volume vs. concentrating on top-end luxury for higher margins.
2. **EV commitment vs. powertrain flexibility**: The original "electric-only" pledge vs. the market reality of slower-than-expected adoption.
3. **Global standardisation vs. local adaptation**: Applying a uniform global strategy vs. tailoring specifically for China's distinct competitive dynamics.

These tensions are not merely operational — they are strategic, and resolving them requires explicit choices rather than hedging across all options simultaneously.
