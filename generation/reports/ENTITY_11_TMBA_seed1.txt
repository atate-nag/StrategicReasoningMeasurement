# **STRATEGIC CASE STUDY: DE BEERS GROUP — DIAMONDS IN DISTRESS**
### *A Strategic Analysis of Anglo American's Subsidiary amid Market Disruption, Portfolio Restructuring, and Stakeholder Complexity*

---

## **EXECUTIVE SUMMARY**

This case study examines the strategic crisis facing **De Beers Group**, the world's most iconic diamond company and a subsidiary of Anglo American plc (LSE: AAL). Between 2023 and 2025, De Beers has confronted a perfect storm: collapsing rough diamond prices, the explosive rise of lab-grown diamonds (LGDs), a $2.9 billion write-down by its parent company, a failed Lightbox strategy, geopolitical pressure from African sovereign governments, and Anglo American's own existential restructuring triggered by a $49 billion hostile takeover bid from BHP. The case analyses the situation using Porter's Five Forces, SWOT Analysis, the BCG Growth-Share Matrix, Stakeholder Mapping, the Ansoff Matrix, and Johnson, Scholes & Whittington's Strategic Lenses. The analysis culminates in a structured set of strategic recommendations designed to restore De Beers' competitive position and long-term viability.

---

## **PART 1: BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT**

### **1.1 Anglo American and the De Beers Relationship**

Anglo American plc is one of the world's largest diversified mining companies, with operations spanning copper, iron ore, platinum group metals (PGMs), diamonds, and fertilisers. Anglo American announced its restructuring in May 2024, amid a successful rebuttal of a $49 billion takeover approach from BHP, the world's biggest miner. After restructuring, copper will represent 60% of its business, making Anglo a major force in the green energy space.

De Beers Group, founded in 1888, is Anglo American's diamond subsidiary — Anglo American's sale of De Beers represents one of the mining industry's most significant divestitures, marking the end of an era that began when Sir Ernest Oppenheimer founded both companies over a century ago. Anglo American currently holds an 85% stake in De Beers, with the Government of Botswana holding the remaining 15%.

### **1.2 The Strategic Situation: A Crisis in Diamonds**

Anglo American's De Beers unit faces a US$2.3B write-down amid a diamond market slump, leading to a US$3.7B annual loss. Restructuring focuses on copper and iron ore. More specifically, De Beers' revenue fell 23 percent year-over-year to $3.29 billion ($4.28 billion in 2023), primarily due to a 25 percent decline in rough diamond sales. Rough sales totalled $2.7 billion in 2024, compared with $3.6 billion in 2023.

The London-based miner suffered a pretax loss of USD924 million in 2024, flipped from a profit of USD3.59 billion in 2023. The diversified mining group posted attributable loss of USD3.07 billion, compared to profit of USD283 million. It booked "special items and remeasurements" of USD5.36 billion, including a USD2.9 billion impairment at De Beers due to tough market conditions.

### **1.3 The Divestiture Plan**

Anglo American's restructuring plan includes a sale or divestment of its 85% stake in De Beers, the world's largest diamond producer by value, to focus on copper, iron ore, and other commodities. Anglo American is in advanced discussions to sell its De Beers diamond unit to a consortium that includes several African governments. CEO Duncan Wanblad indicated the sale could be finalised this year, despite challenges in the diamond market. The transaction could see Botswana increase its current 15% stake, with Angola and Namibia also expressing interest.

### **2.2 Porter's Five Forces Analysis**

Using a combination of Porter's Five Forces analysis, we can assess De Beers' competitive landscape.

#### **Force 1: Threat of New Entrants — HIGH (Increasing)**

Historically, the capital-intensive nature of diamond mining created high barriers to entry. However, the lab-grown diamond sector has fundamentally changed this calculus. The Global Lab-Grown Diamonds Market was valued at USD 27.24 billion in 2024 and is expected to grow at a CAGR of around 11.77% during the forecast period (2025–2033). The relatively low cost of Chemical Vapour Deposition (CVD) and High Pressure High Temperature (HPHT) manufacturing means new entrants can produce gem-quality stones without owning a single mine.

#### **Force 2: Bargaining Power of Buyers — HIGH (Increasing)**

Buyers — both trade (Sightholders) and consumers — now have unprecedented choice. De Beers CEO Al Cook noted that lab-grown diamonds had shifted to "low-margin fashion jewellery," undermining their appeal for high-value purchases like engagement rings. Retail jewellers increasingly stock both natural and synthetic diamonds, empowering consumers to compare and substitute.

#### **Force 3: Bargaining Power of Suppliers — LOW (Stable)**

De Beers controls significant upstream supply across Botswana (Debswana), Namibia (Namdeb), South Africa, and Canada. Its integrated model from mine to retail gives it leverage over rough supply. However, this is eroding as African governments demand larger ownership shares.

#### **Force 4: Threat of Substitutes — VERY HIGH (Critical)**

This is De Beers' most existential threat. Wholesale prices for a two-carat lab-grown diamond fell 34% from 2020 to 2024, while retail prices dropped 25%, squeezing margins. Consumer psychology shifts toward value-based purchasing particularly affect younger demographics, who increasingly prioritise ethical sourcing and cost efficiency over traditional luxury positioning. This demographic represents the core engagement ring market, making the 50% synthetic penetration especially damaging to natural diamond profitability.

#### **Force 5: Competitive Rivalry — HIGH (Intensifying)**

Competitive rivalry is high, with numerous competitors vying for market share, including other mining companies, diamond cutters, and jewellers. Rio Tinto (Argyle mine closure), Alrosa (Russian sanctions), and Petra Diamonds all compete in the rough market, while LGD producers such as Diamond Foundry and Pandora compete at the retail level.

**Porter's Five Forces Summary:** The overall attractiveness of the natural diamond industry has deteriorated sharply. Four of five forces are unfavourable for De Beers, signalling a structural — not merely cyclical — shift.

### **2.3 SWOT Analysis**

#### **STRENGTHS**
- **Brand heritage and recognition:** De Beers' "A Diamond is Forever" campaign (1947) remains one of the most powerful brand associations in consumer goods history.
- **Vertical integration:** Control of the entire value chain from mining, sorting, and trading (through the Diamond Trading Company / Sightholder system) to retail (De Beers Jewellers, Forevermark).
- **Traceability technology:** De Beers developed the Tracr platform — an advanced technology using blockchain, artificial intelligence, the Internet of Things, and advanced security technologies — to enable confidence in a diamond's source throughout the diamond value chain.
- **Geological assets:** Premium mines in Botswana, Namibia, South Africa, and Canada producing high-quality rough.
- **DiamondProof technology:** De Beers developed DiamondProof, designed to distinguish between natural and lab-grown diamonds, creating a critical authentication tool.

#### **WEAKNESSES**
- **Overleveraged parent:** Anglo American's financial distress limits capital available to De Beers for marketing and investment.
- **Failed LGD strategy:** The Lightbox experiment was costly and sent mixed signals to the market about De Beers' commitment to natural diamonds.
- **Revenue collapse:** De Beers' revenue fell 23 percent year-over-year to $3.29 billion in 2024.
- **Ownership uncertainty:** The ongoing divestiture process creates organisational paralysis, talent attrition, and partner uncertainty.
- **Declining production:** De Beers' 2026 production guidance reduction from 26–29 million carats to 21–26 million carats represents strategic recognition of sustained demand weakness rather than temporary market cyclicality.

#### **OPPORTUNITIES**
- **Luxury repositioning:** Growing ultra-high-net-worth consumer segment in Asia, the Middle East, and India with strong appetite for authenticated natural luxury goods.
- **Tracr/provenance premium:** Blockchain-verified provenance can command a price premium among ethically conscious consumers.
- **Industrial synthetics:** Element Six, De Beers' synthetic diamond production subsidiary, will continue operations with a sharpened focus exclusively on industrial applications in semiconductors, quantum computing, and optics.
- **African sovereign partnership:** Converting geopolitical pressure into a genuine co-ownership model that aligns African government interests with De Beers' long-term stability.
- **Self-divestiture as reset:** Independence from Anglo American could allow De Beers to pursue a focused strategy without being subordinated to a copper-centric parent.

#### **THREATS**
- **Structural LGD disruption:** The Global Lab-Grown Diamonds Market is expected to grow at a CAGR of around 11.77% during the forecast period (2025–2033), driven by growing ethical and environmental concerns, cost competitiveness, and changing consumer preferences.
- **Resource nationalism:** Botswana's President Duma Boko has declared his government's determination to finalise a deal for majority control of De Beers by October 2025.
- **Macroeconomic headwinds:** US tariffs on India, where most rough diamonds are polished, have disrupted trade flows. The diamond sector has been squeezed by several forces at once. US tariffs on India, where most rough diamonds are polished, have disrupted trade flows.
- **Reputational risk:** Any association with conflict diamonds or environmental destruction in a post-ESG investment landscape could be catastrophic.

## **PART 3: CORE STRATEGIC PROBLEM IDENTIFICATION**

The analysis reveals **three interlocking strategic problems**:

**Problem 1 — Identity Crisis:** De Beers has lost strategic clarity. Its entry into lab-grown diamonds via Lightbox contradicted its core identity as the guardian of natural diamond value. De Beers has confirmed it will shut down Lightbox, its lab-grown diamond (LGD) jewellery brand launched in 2018. The company attributes the decision to a sustained collapse in LGD prices and a strategic return to its original positioning: mined diamonds as the only product category with lasting consumer and financial value. The closure is strategically correct but leaves a credibility gap to repair.

**Problem 2 — Ownership Transition Risk:** The Anglo American divestiture creates a dangerous ownership vacuum. Anglo American's "dual-track" approach — simultaneously exploring direct sale and potential IPO — reflects sophisticated process management given market uncertainty. However, prolonged uncertainty risks talent loss, Sightholder defection, and African government impatience.

**Problem 3 — Demand Structural Decline:** The rise of lab-grown diamonds is not a temporary disruption. By 2025, the company aims to solidify natural diamonds as luxury assets through storytelling, traceability, and high-end branding. The pivot aligns with a sector-wide trend: natural diamonds are being positioned as irreplaceable symbols of rarity. De Beers must execute this repositioning flawlessly or risk permanent commoditisation of its core product.

## **PART 4: STRATEGIC RECOMMENDATIONS AND ACTION PLAN**

### **Recommendation 1: Execute a Clean, Rapid Ownership Transition — Prioritise the African Consortium Model**

**Rationale:** The uncertainty of Anglo American's dual-track sale process is itself a strategic liability. A prolonged IPO process would expose De Beers to quarterly earnings scrutiny incompatible with the long-term brand investment it needs. A direct sale preference makes strategic sense given current market conditions. An IPO would expose the company to public market volatility and ongoing scrutiny of quarterly performance, potentially constraining management's ability to execute necessary long-term investments. Private ownership could provide the patient capital and operational flexibility that De Beers urgently needs.

**Action Plan:**
- **Immediate (0–6 months):** Anglo American should prioritise the African government consortium model, facilitating Botswana's ambition to hold a majority stake. Botswana's President Duma Boko has declared his government's determination to finalise a deal for majority control of De Beers by October 2025.
- **Short-term (6–12 months):** Structure a co-ownership model where Botswana holds 51%, with Angola and Namibia taking minority positions, creating a "Pan-African Diamond Champion" narrative that reframes resource nationalism as a strength.
- **Medium-term (12–24 months):** Establish an independent De Beers Board with sovereign wealth fund representatives, ensuring geopolitical alignment and long-term supply security.

**Strategic Logic:** This converts the biggest threat (resource nationalism) into a competitive advantage — a diamond company majority-owned by the nations that produce the diamonds is a uniquely powerful ESG and provenance story.

---

### **Recommendation 2: Reposition Natural Diamonds as Authenticated Luxury — The "Rarity Premium" Strategy**

**Rationale:** The pivot aligns with a sector-wide trend: natural diamonds are being positioned as irreplaceable symbols of rarity. De Beers must make the distinction between natural and lab-grown diamonds viscerally meaningful to consumers.

**Action Plan:**
- **Immediate:** Scale the **DiamondProof** authentication programme globally across all retail partners. Every De Beers natural diamond should carry a verifiable digital certificate accessible via smartphone.
- **Short-term:** Expand **Tracr** blockchain provenance to cover 100% of De Beers production. Tracr brings together blockchain, artificial intelligence, the Internet of Things, and advanced security and privacy technologies to track diamonds from source to store. This turns ethical sourcing from a cost into a premium.
- **Medium-term:** Launch a premium "Origin Collection" — diamonds marketed with the full story of their geological formation (billions of years), their mine of origin, and the community impact of their extraction. Price these at a 20–30% premium to untraced diamonds.
- **Long-term:** Partner with luxury goods conglomerates (LVMH, Richemont) to embed Tracr-verified natural diamonds into their supply chains, leveraging their marketing reach and luxury positioning.

---

### **Recommendation 3: Pursue Aggressive Demand Stimulation in Emerging Markets**

**Rationale:** The US market is mature and under LGD pressure. Growth must come from India, the Middle East, and Southeast Asia. Moving into H2 2024, De Beers Group is anticipating the recovery of diamond demand and increasing customer confidence. This recovery must be actively manufactured, not passively awaited.

**Action Plan:**
- **India:** Partner with Bollywood and Indian luxury media to reinvigorate the cultural association between natural diamonds and milestone life events (weddings, anniversaries). India's growing middle class and wedding industry represent a multi-billion dollar opportunity.
- **Middle East:** Leverage the gifting culture and high per-capita luxury spend in UAE, Saudi Arabia, and Qatar. Create bespoke "Sovereign Diamonds" collections positioned as generational wealth assets.
- **China:** As Chinese luxury consumption recovers, relaunch the "natural diamond as love" narrative through WeChat, Douyin (TikTok China), and partnerships with Chinese luxury KOLs (Key Opinion Leaders).
- **Digital-first:** Create a direct-to-consumer digital platform where millennials and Gen Z can design, trace, and purchase natural diamonds with full provenance data — competing with LGD's digital-native advantage.

---

### **Recommendation 4: Transform Element Six into a Strategic Growth Engine**

**Rationale:** Element Six — De Beers' synthetic diamond production subsidiary — will continue operations with a sharpened focus exclusively on industrial applications, leveraging more than seven decades of technical expertise in synthetic diamond development.

**Action Plan:**
- **Immediate:** Ring-fence Element Six as a separate profit centre with its own P&L, leadership, and investment thesis — preventing its performance from being obscured by the natural diamond business.
- **Short-term:** Accelerate R&D partnerships with semiconductor companies, quantum computing firms (IBM, Google), and defence contractors. Synthetic diamonds excel in industrial uses such as quantum computing, semiconductors, and optics.
- **Medium-term:** Explore a partial IPO or strategic investment in Element Six to unlock its valuation independently of the natural diamond market, potentially raising capital for De Beers' broader transformation.

---

### **Recommendation 5: Implement the "Origins" Cost Restructuring with Strategic Discipline**

**Rationale:** De Beers' Origins strategy aims to streamline operations and reduce costs by $100 million annually. Cost reduction is necessary but must not cannibalise brand-building investment.

**Action Plan:**
- **Immediate:** Implement the full $100m cost reduction programme, focusing on back-office, logistics, and non-strategic marketing spend.
- **Short-term:** Reduce production volumes in line with the revised guidance. De Beers' 2026 production guidance reduction from 26–29 million carats to 21–26 million carats represents strategic recognition of sustained demand weakness. Reducing supply supports price recovery — a classic supply management strategy that De Beers pioneered historically.
- **Ring-fence:** Protect spending on Tracr, DiamondProof, and consumer marketing. These are not costs — they are the foundations of the premium repositioning strategy.

## **PART 6: CONCLUSION**

De Beers stands at the most consequential strategic crossroads in its 137-year history. The confluence of lab-grown diamond disruption, a collapsing parent company, African resource nationalism, and a generational shift in consumer values has created a crisis that demands not incremental adjustment but fundamental strategic reinvention.

The analysis reveals that De Beers' competitive advantage — its brand, its geological assets, its provenance technology — remains intact but is being systematically undermined by strategic incoherence, ownership uncertainty, and passive market management. The Lightbox experiment, while understandable as a defensive move, diluted the brand's most precious asset: the belief that natural diamonds are irreplaceable.

The recommended strategy is built on a clear strategic logic: **own the rarity narrative, authenticate it with technology, distribute it through sovereign partnerships, and grow it in markets where luxury aspiration is rising**. This is not a strategy of retreat — it is a strategy of focused renewal.

As negotiations intensify, the century-old diamond empire's future hangs in the balance between financial pragmatism and political complexity, with implications extending far beyond Anglo American's balance sheet to the economic destinies of diamond-dependent African nations. The stakes could not be higher — and the window for decisive strategic action is narrow.
