## 1. Executive Summary

MTN Group, Africa's largest mobile network operator, stands at a strategic inflection point. The group connected 290.9 million subscribers (up from 284.7 million in 2023), enabled internet access for 157.8 million active data users, and extended broadband coverage to 93% of the population across its markets by end-2024. Yet simultaneously, its most important subsidiary — MTN Nigeria — posted a net loss of approximately N400 billion ($1.1 billion USD), driven not by competitive failure but by catastrophic foreign exchange losses following the naira's devaluation. This case examines the strategic tension between MTN's expansionary "platform company" ambitions and the structural macroeconomic risks that undermine the financial returns from those very investments. The analysis applies PESTEL, Porter's Five Forces, the Resource-Based View (RBV) with dynamic capabilities, and a SWOT synthesis to diagnose the situation and produce a coherent, evidence-based set of recommendations.

## 2. Case Background and Context

### 2.1 MTN Group: Origins and Scale

MTN Group Limited, headquartered in Johannesburg, South Africa, was founded in 1994 as M-Cell with assistance from the South African government. Over three decades it evolved from a domestic operator into a pan-African telecommunications conglomerate. In October 2025 MTN announced it had surpassed 300 million customers, the first African-headquartered telco to reach that milestone, making it the sixth-largest in the world by subscriber count. It has a footprint in sixteen countries in Africa and the Middle East.

MTN's strategic evolution has been shaped by successive five-year plans. Its most recently completed cycle, **Ambition 2025** (announced in 2020), was organised around the intent to "lead digital solutions for Africa's progress," transforming the group from a traditional connectivity provider into a digital platform company. Three years into the execution of Ambition 2025, MTN was still driven by its intent to lead digital solutions for Africa's progress by harnessing the power of its leading brand, broad presence and skills, connectivity infrastructure and technology platforms. In 2026, MTN unveiled **Ambition 2030**, which streamlines execution into three principal platforms of choice for consumers, homes and businesses: Connectivity; Fintech; and Digital Infrastructure.

### 2.2 The Nigerian Dilemma

Nigeria is MTN's single most important market, accounting for approximately 40% of group revenue. Yet the subsidiary became the source of the group's most acute financial crisis in 2023–2024. The Nigerian Exchange Group (NGX) reports that MTN Nigeria suffered staggering financial losses, recording a forex loss amounting to N740.4 billion in 2023. The situation worsened in 2024: due to the large FX exposure, the forex losses went from N740 billion in 2023 to N925 billion in 2024. The report showed that despite having over 80 million customers in Nigeria, the naira devaluation from N907/$1 as of December 31, 2023, to N1,535/$ by the end of 2024 took a deep cut into the company's earnings. Despite achieving a significant revenue milestone of N3.36 trillion in 2024, a 36.03% increase from the N2.47 trillion reported in 2023, MTN Nigeria's financial health deteriorated, with losses skyrocketing by 192% compared to the previous year. The paradox — strong local-currency revenue growth accompanied by catastrophic dollar-denominated losses — encapsulates the central strategic challenge this case addresses.

### 2.3 The Continental Opportunity

Against this backdrop of Nigerian turbulence, the broader African digital economy presents a compelling structural growth opportunity. In 2024, mobile technologies and services generated 7.7% of Africa's GDP, amounting to $220 billion in economic value. The mobile industry's economic contribution will reach $270 billion by 2030, driven by the expansion of digital technologies on the continent, including 4G, 5G and AI. While 416 million people are now using mobile internet in Africa, almost 75% of the population remain unconnected. 960 million people in Africa, 64% of the population, are not using mobile internet despite living in an area where coverage is available.

## 3. The Strategic Situation: Framing the Problem

The strategic situation facing MTN Group can be summarised as a **dual imperative**:

- **Growth imperative**: Africa's digital economy is in its early-growth phase. MTN is targeting 40–50 million new mobile users and 60–70 million new active data users by 2030, with data consumption doubling from 14GB per user per month and data alone climbing past 50% of group revenue. Failure to invest aggressively now risks ceding ground to Airtel Africa, Orange, Safaricom/M-Pesa, and new entrants.

- **Stability imperative**: MTN's multi-market exposure to currencies that are structurally weak, inflation-prone, and subject to government intervention creates recurring financial losses that can erase years of operational progress. The Nigerian case is the most extreme example, but similar dynamics apply in Ghana, Uganda, and other markets.

The strategic question, therefore, is: **How should MTN Group restructure its strategy, capital allocation, and organisational model to sustain continental growth while insulating shareholder value from recurring macroeconomic shocks?**

## 4. Environmental Analysis: PESTEL

### 4.1 Political

African telecommunications markets are heavily shaped by political dynamics. The NCC on October 20, 2015, imposed a fine of N1.04 trillion on MTN for infraction of the provision of the NCC Telephone Subscribers Registration Regulations 2011; for failure to disconnect 5.2 million improperly-registered SIM lines. Although ultimately negotiated down and settled, the episode revealed the political vulnerability of operating as a foreign-headquartered company in markets where telecoms are considered strategic national infrastructure. More recently, MTN Nigeria and MultiChoice Group's DStv operations faced renewed regulatory uncertainty following a Senate debate proposing licence revocation as an expression of Nigeria–South Africa diplomatic tensions. In the DRC, while MTN operates in numerous African markets, it does not hold a licence in the DRC, where Orange, Vodacom, Africell and Airtel are the authorised mobile operators — highlighting the sensitivities surrounding cross-border spectrum.

### 4.2 Economic

Compounding the effects of inflation, local currencies were volatile, with limited availability of hard currency, especially in Nigeria. These conditions affect MTN in two distinct ways. First, they generate direct forex translation losses on dollar-denominated debt and equipment costs. Second, they compress real consumer purchasing power, limiting ARPU growth in local-currency terms. However, there is a counterforce: the NCC's January 2025 approval of a 50% telecom tariff adjustment produced a significant ARPU recovery, with MTN Nigeria's monthly ARPU rising to $3.60 in 2025 from $2.17 in 2024. This demonstrates that regulatory-approved pricing adjustments can partially offset devaluation pressures, though they are not automatic and require sustained advocacy.

### 4.3 Social

Africa's demographic profile is uniquely favourable for digital growth. As of early January 2024, around 74% of web traffic in Africa was via mobile phones, over 14 percentage points higher than the world average. Financial exclusion remains widespread: Nigeria, with a population of 127.6 million adults and an unbanked rate of approximately 50%, has potential for the roll-out and adoption of mobile money services, to transform the currently cash-based economy. These social dynamics create enormous demand for both connectivity and digital financial services, directly validating MTN's platform strategy.

### 4.4 Technological

Technological change is simultaneously an enabler and a disruptive threat. On the enabling side, 4G expansion is set to drive connectivity, projected to account for half of all connections in Sub-Saharan Africa by 2030. Fixed Wireless Access (FWA) has emerged as the leading 5G application across the continent. By late 2025, 25 operators in countries such as Angola, South Africa, Nigeria, Kenya, Zambia, and Zimbabwe had launched 5G FWA services. Strategic partnerships using OpenRAN technology have enabled MTN to deploy cost-effective rural solutions, while the company's Network as a Service strategy has facilitated collaboration with competitor operators to allow national roaming.

### 4.5 Environmental

Energy costs are a major operational variable. Rising energy prices and the Central Bank of Nigeria's increased interest rate further pressured consumer spending and business operations. MTN has committed to achieving net-zero carbon emissions by 2040, and is investing in solar-powered base stations and energy efficiency — both as an ESG commitment and as a cost-reduction strategy.

### 4.6 Legal

The legal environment across MTN's markets is fragmented. South Africa's highest appeal court has ruled that a Turkish telecoms group claiming MTN bribed Iranian officials for a lucrative mobile phone licence may proceed with its $4.2 billion damages claim. The structural separation of MoMo requires navigating multiple, often inconsistent, African regulatory systems. MTN Uganda was the pioneer, with shareholders approving the movement of the mobile money unit to MTN Group Fintech Holdings B.V. on July 22, 2025. This transition is accompanied by plans for a local listing on the Uganda Securities Exchange over the next few years.

## 5. Industry Analysis: Porter's Five Forces

### 5.1 Threat of New Entrants — **Low to Moderate**

The African telecom industry has high structural barriers to entry. MTN's own capital expenditure guidance for 2025 was R33–38 billion, and MTN Group announced plans to expand its footprint on the continent with a $10 billion investment by 2030, injecting $2 billion annually. These sums are prohibitive for most potential entrants. However, the entry threat is not zero: by June 2025, fewer than 30% of African countries — just 16 nations — had allocated spectrum for 5G services, compared to over 90% in Europe. This lack of spectrum creates regulatory barriers, but satellite internet providers like Starlink represent a non-traditional form of entry that bypasses the need for terrestrial infrastructure.

### 5.2 Bargaining Power of Suppliers — **Moderate**

MTN's key suppliers are network equipment vendors and energy providers. The concentration of global network equipment suppliers gives vendors moderate bargaining power. MTN has sought to reduce this through OpenRAN partnerships. By sharing passive (towers, masts) and active (Radio Access Network or RAN) infrastructure, operators typically cut capital expenditure by up to 30–40%, while slashing operating costs through shared site maintenance and energy.

### 5.3 Bargaining Power of Buyers — **Low to Moderate**

Individual consumers have limited switching power in most African markets due to number portability frictions and network effect lock-in, particularly in mobile money. In Nigeria, MTN saw its market share rise to 51% in January [2025], adding over 3 million new subscribers, bringing its total to 87.5 million. However, in more competitive markets such as Kenya, Safaricom's M-Pesa held 89% of the mobile money market as of December 2025, down from 91% a year earlier, illustrating how buyer power and competitive dynamics vary significantly by market.

### 5.4 Threat of Substitutes — **Moderate and Rising**

The primary substitutes for MTN's connectivity services are WiFi, satellite internet, and over-the-top (OTT) messaging applications that substitute for SMS and voice revenue. For mobile money, the substitutes include bank apps and fintech startups. In 2024, MoMo's fortunes changed in Nigeria. The number of active wallets fell to 2.8 million, and cash deposits from wallet users dropped by half, from ₦7.6 billion to ₦3.8 billion. The agent and merchant networks also saw significant declines. This decline is partly attributable to competition from OPay, Flutterwave, and bank-backed digital wallets.

### 5.5 Competitive Rivalry — **High, with Emerging Co-opetition**

The pan-African telecom market is characterised by intense oligopolistic rivalry. Airtel Africa's ability to compete across multiple markets with a unified brand and strategy gives it a significant advantage in the pan-African telecom landscape. The company has focused heavily on mobile data and mobile money, with its Airtel Money service competing directly with MTN MoMo and M-Pesa in key markets. Notably, the competitive dynamic is evolving: driven to extend digital and financial inclusion across Africa, MTN Group and Airtel Africa have entered into agreements to share network infrastructure in Uganda and Nigeria, while ensuring compliance with local regulatory and statutory requirements. These sharing agreements target improved network cost efficiencies, expanded coverage and the provision of enhanced mobile services to millions of customers. This co-opetition reflects the maturation of the industry and the shared recognition that margin erosion from pure competition is mutually destructive in an environment of currency devaluation and rising energy costs.

## 6. Internal Analysis: Resource-Based View and Dynamic Capabilities

### 6.1 Resource-Based View (VRIN Framework)

The Resource-Based View holds that sustained competitive advantage derives from resources and capabilities that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (Barney, 1991).

**Valuable and Rare Resources:**

- **Brand**: African telecom giants like MTN ($2.7B brand value) and Safaricom (BSI 88.1) dominate the continent's brand landscape. MTN's recognition across 19 markets is a genuine pan-African asset that no competitor matches in breadth.

- **Network Infrastructure**: MTN has also simplified its balance sheet, reducing net-debt-to-EBITDA leverage to 0.5x from 0.7x in 2024, while maintaining a robust liquidity headroom of R39.1 billion. The company's capital expenditure guidance for 2025 was raised to R33–38 billion, reflecting its commitment to expanding 5G and fiber infrastructure.

- **MoMo Platform**: MTN MoMo's 63.2 million active users have driven transaction volumes to 11.1 billion and transaction values to $212.2 billion, cementing its role as a financial inclusion engine. Mastercard's substantial investment of $200 million into MTN Group's fintech division in February 2024 validated the platform's strategic value and brought global payment network capabilities.

- **Bayobab Fibre Infrastructure**: In the subsea fibre business, Bayobab celebrated the landing of the 2Africa subsea cable in Ghana in November 2023, followed by the Nigeria landing in February 2024. In May 2023, Bayobab entered into a partnership with Africa50 to develop Project East2West, "a terrestrial fibreoptic cable network connecting the eastern shores of Africa to those on the continent's west."

**Limitations of the RBV in Emerging Markets:**

As Banalieva and Sarathy (2010) observe, in emerging markets, institutional factors can weaken the link between resources and performance, challenging the RBV's assumption of universal applicability. MTN's Nigerian experience is a case in point: its valuable, rare, and inimitable resources — its subscriber base, its network, its brand — did not prevent a $1.1 billion net loss in 2024, because the loss was driven by macroeconomic institutional factors (currency policy) rather than competitive dynamics. This necessitates supplementing the RBV with a dynamic capabilities perspective.

### 6.2 Dynamic Capabilities (Teece, Pisano & Shuen, 1997)

Dynamic capabilities refer to the firm's ability to integrate, build, and reconfigure internal and external competences in response to rapidly changing environments. Three dynamic capabilities are relevant:

**Sensing**: MTN has demonstrated an ability to sense macro-level shifts. MTN Group leads Africa's digital transformation via B-BBEE compliance, fintech expansion, and infrastructure optimization, targeting 8.5% telecom and 12.3% fintech CAGR growth. Its early recognition of the mobile money opportunity — predating most Western telecom operators — led to the MoMo platform.

**Seizing**: Mastercard and MTN Group Fintech partnered to drive acceleration of the mobile money ecosystem in Africa across 13 markets, empowering MoMo merchants to seamlessly accept card payments and enhancing the platform's instant cross-border money remittance services. The MTN–Airtel network-sharing agreement similarly represents a seizing of cost-reduction opportunities.

**Reconfiguring**: MTN has exited non-core markets like the Middle East, reallocating resources to Africa. The Ambition 2030 strategy accelerates this reconfiguration into three distinct platform businesses. However, the reconfiguring capability has been slower in addressing forex risk management — the group has been reactive (in 2024, these telecom giants took decisive steps to slash their foreign debt, repaying a combined $1.2 billion to alleviate the pressures of foreign exchange fluctuations) rather than proactive in building systematic hedging and local-currency financing structures.

## 8. The Core Strategic Tension — A Coherent Chain of Analysis

The PESTEL, Five Forces, and RBV analyses converge on a coherent diagnosis. The logical chain is as follows:

**Step 1 — The Opportunity is Real and Structurally Grounded.** Africa's digital growth opportunity is well-established. Approximately 108 billion transactions, totalling over $1.68 trillion, were processed through mobile money accounts in 2024. Year-on-year, transaction volumes increased by 20%, while transaction values grew by 16%. Africa processed 65% of global mobile money transaction value last year, or $1.1 trillion. The continent also led in volume, accounting for nearly 82 billion of the 108 billion global transactions. MTN's brand, network, and MoMo platform are the strongest pan-African assets to capture this opportunity.

**Step 2 — The Institutional Environment Transforms Operational Success into Financial Loss.** However, the institutional environment in MTN's key markets systematically converts operational success — measured in subscriber growth, revenue growth, and data traffic — into financial loss at the group level, because operating costs and debt are denominated in hard currencies while revenues are earned in depreciating local currencies. The root causes of MTN Nigeria's financial woes can be traced back to the broader economic volatility affecting the country, notably the devaluation of the Nigerian naira. This devaluation has had a cascading effect on MTN Nigeria's earnings, given the company's significant exposure to foreign currency transactions. The telecom sector, by its very nature, is heavily reliant on international trade and investment, making companies like MTN Nigeria particularly susceptible to losses stemming from forex volatility.

**Step 3 — The Five Forces Analysis Reveals Co-opetition as the Rational Competitive Response.** The competitive analysis shows that rivalry is intense but increasingly co-operative at the infrastructure level. This strategic collaboration is aimed at reducing escalating operational costs while enhancing mobile coverage in underserved regions. The unusual partnership highlights a larger trend among African telecom giants towards cost optimization as devaluation of currency and economic challenges put pressure on profitability. By sharing towers, base stations, and fibre, both operators can reduce capex and opex by an estimated 20–40%, freeing capital to service debt and invest in growth.

**Step 4 — The RBV Identifies the Fintech Platform as the Key Value Unlock.** The RBV analysis identifies MoMo as MTN's most strategically valuable, rare, and inimitable asset — but one whose value is currently suppressed within the conglomerate structure. As MTN's fintech operations become valued independently, a precise market valuation will emerge, especially with the anticipated listing on the Uganda Securities Exchange. This will set a distinct price for MoMo's business operations. The structural separation is not merely an organisational change; it is a capital structure optimisation that reduces the group's reliance on dollar-denominated telecom debt by unlocking fintech-level valuations and attracting specialist investors.

**Step 5 — The Usage Gap is the Strategic Growth Engine with the Lowest Incremental Capex.** The usage gap is nine times the size of the coverage gap. The least connected region globally is Sub-Saharan Africa, where only 27% of the population are using mobile internet services, leaving a 13% coverage gap and a 60% usage gap. Converting even 10% of this usage gap into active data subscribers within existing coverage areas would add approximately 96 million users with minimal incremental capex, generating local-currency revenue that reduces the forex risk ratio. MTN's CEO has emphasised the importance of affordability in driving mass adoption. With a $20 smartphone enabling 10GB of data per month, MTN is targeting the inflection point where digital access becomes a utility for millions.

## 9. Recommendations and Action Plan

The following five recommendations flow directly from the analysis above. They are sequenced by urgency and are mutually reinforcing: each addresses a different layer of the strategic problem, and together they form a coherent programme.

---

### Recommendation 1: Implement a Systematic Forex Risk Management Framework *(Urgency: Immediate)*

**Rationale**: The single most damaging element of MTN's recent performance has been unhedged forex exposure. MTN Nigeria topped the list with over N1.03 trillion foreign exchange loss in 2024, posting N550.33 billion loss in 2024 from N177.89 billion loss declared in 2023. The group and Airtel Africa took a reactive step in 2024 by repaying a combined $1.2 billion in foreign debt, but this is insufficient as a long-term framework.

**Actions**:
- **Shift to local-currency financing**: Work with development finance institutions (IFC, African Development Bank, Development Bank of Southern Africa) to issue naira-denominated bonds and cedi-denominated instruments for Nigerian and Ghanaian capex respectively. DFIs have a mandate to support local capital market development and can offer concessional terms.
- **Natural hedging through local procurement**: Increase the proportion of network equipment and services sourced from local or regional suppliers to reduce the dollar-denominated cost base. OpenRAN's multi-vendor architecture facilitates this.
- **Tariff escalation advocacy**: Maintain proactive engagement with regulators in all markets to ensure tariff structures include inflation and devaluation adjustment mechanisms. The surge in ARPU followed the implementation of a 50% telecom tariff adjustment approved by the Nigerian Communications Commission early last year, demonstrating that regulatory advocacy can yield meaningful ARPU recovery.

**KPI**: Reduce the ratio of hard-currency debt to local-currency EBITDA in Nigeria and Ghana by 50% within three years.

---

### Recommendation 2: Accelerate the Structural Separation and Independent Listing of MoMo *(Urgency: High)*

**Rationale**: The restructuring plan encompasses the mobile money platform MoMo, which boasts 60 million active wallets spread across various African nations. This venture into fintech requires navigating the complex corporate landscape across multiple African regulatory systems. The platform is a fintech asset of global significance currently valued at telecom multiples within the MTN conglomerate.

**Actions**:
- **Complete separation across all 13 target markets by 2027**: Prioritise markets where the regulatory framework is most supportive. The separation across MTN operations of the fintech and MoMo businesses from the GSM businesses aims to drive accelerated scale, efficiency and better service delivery to customers.
- **Pursue a dual listing on JSE and Lagos Stock Exchange**: The Uganda Securities Exchange listing planned for the MoMo Uganda entity should serve as the template for a broader pan-African fintech listing strategy.
- **Expand the Mastercard partnership**: The partnership will empower MoMo merchants to seamlessly accept card payments, enhancing the platform's instant cross-border money remittance services and capabilities. MTN should negotiate extension to all MoMo markets, leveraging Mastercard's merchant acceptance network to accelerate MoMo's transition from peer-to-peer transfers to merchant payments — the highest-growth segment globally.
- **Address MoMo PSB Nigeria's wallet decline**: When MoMo PSB lost 2.5 million active wallets in 2024, the CEO acknowledged the challenge during an investors' call. MTN should invest in agent network quality, USSD reliability, and targeted incentives for small merchants, competing directly with OPay and Flutterwave on user experience.

**KPI**: MoMo to achieve independent valuation of at least $5 billion within 36 months of full separation; Nigeria active wallet count to recover to 5 million by end-2026.

---

### Recommendation 3: Convert the Usage Gap into Revenue Through an Affordable Ecosystem Strategy *(Urgency: High)*

**Rationale**: Connecting those with access to existing mobile internet services would deliver an estimated additional $3.5 trillion in total GDP during 2023–2030. Converting even a fraction of the 960 million covered-but-unconnected Africans into active data subscribers would generate local-currency revenue (reducing forex risk) with minimal incremental capex.

**Actions**:
- **Launch an "MTN Digital Access" programme**: Partner with smartphone manufacturers (particularly Transsion/Tecno, which dominates the African budget smartphone market) to bundle subsidised 4G handsets with 12-month data packages, financed through micro-credit repaid via MoMo. This aligns connectivity and fintech growth.
- **Develop digital literacy programmes**: Barriers to getting more people using mobile internet services include the ongoing need for collaboration between governments, mobile network operators and international organisations. MTN's Ayoba instant messaging app (20 million monthly active users) can serve as the digital onboarding gateway.
- **Leverage OpenRAN for rural densification**: Expand OpenRAN rollout in partnership with Airtel (under the existing network-sharing agreement) to densify coverage in peri-urban and rural areas where the usage gap is largest.
- **Introduce tiered data pricing**: Offer zero-rated or heavily subsidised access to essential services (government portals, agricultural price information, health services) as a gateway to paid data consumption.

**KPI**: Add 30 million new active data subscribers within existing coverage areas by end-2027, with average ARPU of at least $1.50/month.

### Recommendation 4: Institutionalise Regulatory Relationship Management as a Core Competence *(Urgency: Medium-High)*

**Rationale**: The PESTEL analysis and the history of the NCC fine, the NIN-SIM directive, and the licence revocation threat in Nigeria all point to regulatory risk as a recurring, material threat to MTN's business continuity. Nigeria is currently going through serious security challenges. Unfortunately, MTN has a history of flouting NCC regulations and despite being fined, merely viewed such fines as a 'cost of doing business' in Nigeria. This perception — whether or not fully accurate — creates ongoing political vulnerability that must be systematically addressed.

**Actions**:
- **Establish a Group Chief Regulatory Officer with C-suite representation**: Currently, regulatory affairs is managed at the operating company level. Group-level oversight would ensure consistent standards across all 19 markets and provide strategic regulatory intelligence.
- **Create a "Social Contract" framework for each market**: For each operating company, develop a publicly committed 5-year plan showing MTN's contribution to national digital development goals (coverage targets, rural connectivity, digital skills). This reframes MTN from a foreign extractive entity to a national development partner, reducing the political incentive for punitive regulatory action.
- **Proactive compliance investment**: Allocate a dedicated compliance budget (1–2% of operating company revenue) to ensure full adherence to SIM registration, consumer protection, and data privacy regulations — far cheaper than the reputational and financial cost of regulatory fines.
- **Engage with AfCFTA digital trade provisions**: The African Continental Free Trade Area is developing a digital trade protocol that would reduce cross-border roaming costs and facilitate multi-market service delivery. MTN should position itself as a champion of AfCFTA digital provisions, building goodwill with the African Union and member-state governments simultaneously.

**KPI**: Zero material regulatory fines (>$10 million) across all markets for three consecutive years; positive regulatory engagement scores in annual stakeholder surveys.

---

### Recommendation 5: Reposition Bayobab as a Pan-African Open-Access Digital Infrastructure Platform *(Urgency: Medium)*

**Rationale**: MTN has invested heavily to grow the Bayobab business, and is undertaking a R6bn project between East and West Africa. The group has long argued that the value of such assets is not truly reflected in its share price. The RBV analysis identifies this infrastructure as a rare and inimitable asset, but its value is maximised only if it is monetised across the widest possible customer base — including competitors, hyperscalers, and African governments.

**Actions**:
- **Open Bayobab's infrastructure to wholesale customers**: Offer capacity on the East2West fibre and 2Africa cable to Airtel, Orange, Vodacom, and hyperscalers (AWS, Microsoft Azure, Google Cloud) on commercial wholesale terms. This generates additional revenue from largely fixed-cost assets, improving return on invested capital.
- **Pursue data centre co-location partnerships**: MTN Group targets South Africa and Nigeria for AI-enabled data centres under Ambition 2030. The data centre push forms one of three strategic pillars under MTN's new Ambition 2030 strategy. Bayobab should develop co-location facilities at cable landing stations and major fibre nodes, targeting hyperscalers that need African points of presence for latency-sensitive applications.
- **Seek external investment in Bayobab**: As with MoMo, a partial external investment in Bayobab (following the model of the Africa50 partnership for East2West) would validate the asset's value and provide expansion capital without increasing group-level debt. Infrastructure funds (Brookfield, IFM) are actively seeking African digital infrastructure exposure.
- **Expand into frontier markets**: Bayobab has announced plans to deploy fibre in South Sudan, where internet users increased by 22,000 (1.7%) between January and the following period. Extending this to Central African Republic, Chad, and other landlocked markets creates long-term optionality and positions Bayobab as the backbone of Africa's digital infrastructure.

**KPI**: Bayobab to generate 20% of revenues from non-MTN customers within three years; at least one external investment in Bayobab infrastructure at a valuation of $1 billion or above.

## 10. Conclusion

MTN Group's strategic situation is one of genuine complexity: a company with the continent's strongest telecom assets, the most ambitious digital platform strategy, and the deepest market penetration is simultaneously experiencing its worst financial results in a generation, driven by macroeconomic forces largely outside its direct control. This case has argued that the resolution of this tension requires not a retreat from growth ambitions, but a fundamental restructuring of the capital architecture and organisational model through which those ambitions are pursued.

The analytical chain is coherent and explicit. PESTEL reveals that institutional risk — currency, regulatory, political — is the dominant environmental force shaping MTN's performance. Porter's Five Forces shows that co-opetition on infrastructure is the rational competitive response to shared cost pressures in an oligopolistic market. The RBV identifies MoMo and Bayobab as the highest-value assets whose worth is currently suppressed by the conglomerate structure. And the SWOT synthesis confirms that the usage gap represents the largest addressable growth opportunity with the lowest incremental capex requirement.

The five recommendations form a mutually reinforcing programme. Forex risk management stabilises the financial base. MoMo separation unlocks capital and reduces conglomerate risk. Usage gap conversion drives local-currency revenue growth, which further reduces forex risk. Regulatory institutionalisation protects the licence to operate. And Bayobab monetisation generates infrastructure returns that fund the entire programme without additional group-level debt. MTN Group posted excellent operational and financial results for 2025, delivering significantly on its Ambition 2025 strategy and transitioning to Ambition 2030 priorities to capture value from the attractive structural growth opportunities brought about by Africa's digital transformation.

Executed together, these recommendations would position MTN not merely as Africa's largest telecom operator, but as the continent's foundational digital infrastructure company — a platform on which Africa's digital economy is built, and from which MTN captures durable, currency-resilient returns for decades to come.
