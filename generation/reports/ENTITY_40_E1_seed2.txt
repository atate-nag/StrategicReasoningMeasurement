# VATTENFALL AB: NAVIGATING THE DUAL MANDATE
## A Strategic Case Study of Sweden's State-Owned Energy Champion

---

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Vattenfall AB is wholly owned by the Swedish state, with its headquarters in Stockholm. It is one of Europe's largest energy companies, and its declared purpose — "to enable the fossil freedom that drives society forward, making it possible to move, make and live fossil free, as a profitable energy business" — captures a fundamental strategic tension: the company must simultaneously act as a commercially profitable enterprise and as an instrument of Swedish public energy policy.

This case study examines Vattenfall's strategic situation as it enters the second half of the 2020s. Three interlocking tensions define the situation: (1) a **dual mandate conflict** between commercial returns and public mission; (2) a **portfolio reorientation challenge** as the company retreats from certain offshore wind markets while doubling down on new nuclear; and (3) a **structural market problem** — falling Nordic electricity prices that erode revenues even as investment requirements escalate. Drawing on PESTEL, Porter's Five Forces, the Resource-Based View (RBV/VRIO), SWOT, the Ansoff Matrix, and Stakeholder Analysis, the analysis leads to a coherent set of strategic recommendations centred on disciplined capital allocation, a Nordic-first competitive strategy, and a phased approach to new nuclear development.

## 2. CASE BACKGROUND & CONTEXT

### 2.1 Corporate Profile

Vattenfall traces its origins to 1909, when the Swedish state established it to harness the country's hydroelectric resources for national industrialisation. Following the deregulation of the Swedish electricity market in 1996 — which transformed Vattenfall from a statutory monopoly into a state-owned commercial company — the firm pursued aggressive pan-European expansion, acquiring lignite assets in Germany and Poland and heat networks in Berlin and Hamburg.

The Swedish state-owned power utility Vattenfall posted a revenue of SEK 246 billion (€22 billion, -15% compared to 2023) and a profit of SEK 33 billion (€3 billion, ×3.3) in 2024. The company's power generation declined by 1% to 100 TWh. Vattenfall has a strong portfolio of offshore and onshore wind in combination with large-scale solar and batteries, operating more than 1,400 wind turbines with a total operated capacity of 6.6 GW in Sweden, Germany, the Netherlands, Denmark and the UK.

### 2.2 The Owner: The Swedish State

The Swedish state is Vattenfall's sole shareholder. This creates a governance dynamic fundamentally different from a publicly listed energy company. The national daily newspaper *Dagens Nyheter* calls it "the owner without principles", and the tenor of political and media debate has been that the state had no control, was weak and incompetent to steer management, and did not ask questions as long as big profits washed into the public coffers.

This dual mandate — commercial profitability *and* public mission — is not merely rhetorical. Vattenfall's owner structure with just one shareholder, the state, adds to the turmoil the company has to struggle with. After Germany's unification the then social democratic government of Sweden gave Vattenfall the green light to expand into Europe in order to gather economic resources to be able to meet the foreign competitors on their way to conquer Sweden's just deregulated electricity market. That expansion ultimately generated massive losses rather than the intended competitive buffer.

### 2.3 The Strategic Pivot: 2016–2024

Vattenfall's past expansion strategy involved the acquisition of multiple brown coal (lignite)-fired power plants, which was highly controversial in Sweden and Germany. In each of the second quarters of 2015 and 2016, Vattenfall filed impairments of SEK 28 billion, mostly due to lignite power stations in Germany. The 2016 sale of these assets to Czech group EPH marked a decisive inflection point.

The company then pivoted towards offshore wind — but even this pivot encountered turbulence. Through strategic divestments of the heating operations in Berlin and wind power projects off the coast of Norfolk in the UK, Vattenfall has been strengthening its preparedness for the future. The agreed purchase price for the Norfolk Offshore Wind Zone corresponded to an enterprise value of £963 million. The deal ensures the continued development of the projects and fossil-free electricity for the future.

Simultaneously, Vattenfall has decided to pause the development of the offshore wind power project Swedish Kriegers Flak until further notice, due to unviable investment prerequisites in Sweden. Swedish Kriegers Flak, located about 30 km south of Trelleborg, is Sweden's most mature offshore wind power project.

Most significantly, Vattenfall has pivoted towards new nuclear. Swedish utility Vattenfall has applied for state financing to build new reactors at its Ringhals plant — the first company to do so under a scheme the government hopes will lead to a renaissance in nuclear power. Vattenfall subsidiary Videberg Kraft plans to build small modular reactors at Ringhals, on the Värö Peninsula, southwest Sweden.

## 3. THE STRATEGIC SITUATION: THREE INTERLOCKING TENSIONS

### Tension 1: Dual Mandate — Commercial vs. Public Mission

The Swedish government simultaneously expects Vattenfall to generate returns for the state treasury and to act as an instrument of energy policy — investing in assets that may not be commercially optimal on a standalone basis. Sweden faces considerable problems with volatile electricity prices for households and businesses and imbalances in the electricity system. Sweden needs to build new nuclear reactors to safeguard future electricity needs, improve supply reliability in the electricity system, and help ensure competitive electricity prices for customers nationwide. The question of who bears the cost and risk of achieving these goals — the state, Vattenfall, or the market — is precisely the dual mandate tension.

### Tension 2: Portfolio Reorientation Under Falling Prices

Electricity prices dropped significantly during 2023, which had a major impact on Vattenfall's earnings. Electricity prices continued to fall in 2024. Yet Vattenfall is committed to enabling a fossil-free future and therefore plans to invest SEK 170 billion over the period 2025 to 2029, focusing on fossil-free electricity generation and the electricity grid. The company must allocate this capital across competing priorities — nuclear, wind, and grid — in a low-price environment that reduces the internal cash generation available to fund these investments.

### Tension 3: The Grid-Generation Mismatch in Sweden

According to the Swedish government, one of Sweden's core challenges is that while the south has a strong grid but limited production, the north has abundant generation capacity but weaker grid capacity. The Swedish government believes that these structural issues are undermining the country's competitiveness. Sweden has added 1 GW each of solar and wind in 2024, bringing the respective totals to 5 GW and 17 GW, mainly in the north. The result is dramatic price divergence: on the afternoon of 11 December 2024, the spot price in southern Sweden reached 5 kronor per kilowatt hour, while northern Sweden remained cheap. This structural bottleneck simultaneously reduces the commercial attractiveness of new renewable projects in the south, undermines industrial competitiveness, and creates political pressure on Vattenfall — as the dominant state-owned generator — to solve a problem that is fundamentally a grid infrastructure issue.

## 4. ANALYTICAL FRAMEWORK

### 4.1 PESTEL Analysis

**Political.** Sweden has set a target to become carbon neutral by 2045. Electricity demand in the country is expected to reach 300 terawatt-hours by 2040. The policy shift from "100% renewable" to "100% fossil-free" electricity by 2040 is decisive: the time agreement announced that the energy policy target would be changed from 100% "renewable" to 100% "fossil-free" electricity production in 2040. This change deliberately opens the door to nuclear power as a permanent fixture of the energy mix. In May 2025, Sweden's parliament — the Riksdag — approved the government's proposals for providing state aid to companies that want to invest in new nuclear reactors in the country. The loans — aimed at lowering the cost of financing new nuclear — will be limited to the equivalent of four large-scale reactors (about 5,000 MWe of capacity).

**Economic.** Sweden leads Europe for negative price hours. The structural driver is clear: as more intermittent renewable capacity enters the market, average prices trend lower, compressing margins for all generators including Vattenfall's existing hydro and nuclear fleet. However, the long-run outlook is more favourable. Electrification through fossil-free energy is key, not only in the energy transition, but also in securing Sweden and Europe's future competitiveness. Extensive investments in fossil-free electricity generation, distribution and storage solutions will be needed to meet future energy demand.

**Social.** Swedish public opinion on nuclear power has shifted materially. The HYBRIT partnership — launched in 2016 as a partnership between SSAB, LKAB, and Vattenfall, HYBRIT plans to replace coal with fossil-free hydrogen derived from renewable electricity — has generated significant positive reputational capital, positioning Vattenfall as an enabler of industrial decarbonisation.

**Technological.** The Swedish government has received an application for state aid to support proposals for either five GE Vernova Hitachi BWRX-300 reactors or three Rolls-Royce SMRs to provide about 1,500 MW capacity at Ringhals on the Värö Peninsula. SMR technology promises lower upfront capital costs and modular construction timelines compared to large-scale nuclear, but no unit has yet been built at commercial scale — a significant execution risk.

**Environmental.** Most of Sweden's electricity supply comes from hydro and nuclear, along with a growing contribution from wind. Heating is supplied mainly through bioenergy-based district heating and heat pumps. Vattenfall's existing portfolio is already highly decarbonised, but the ecological sensitivity of hydro expansion and nuclear waste management remain live environmental tensions.

**Legal/Regulatory.** The Kriegers Flak pause was directly attributable to regulatory failure: Vattenfall said it had paused development of Sweden's Kriegers Flak offshore wind power project due to a lack of clarity on how it will connect to the national grid. This represents a systemic risk for Vattenfall's Swedish renewable pipeline. Furthermore, the European Commission will have to approve the financing for new nuclear under EU state-aid rules — adding a layer of regulatory uncertainty to the SMR investment case.

### 4.2 Porter's Five Forces

**Competitive Rivalry (Moderate–High).** The Nordic electricity market features several large, well-capitalised state-owned incumbents — Fortum (Finland), Statkraft (Norway) — alongside private players. The electric producers in Sweden include Vattenfall, Fortum, E.ON and Sydkraft. Vattenfall is a 100% state-owned company and is the 5th top electricity producer in Europe. Competition in wholesale generation is primarily on price at Nord Pool; competition in B2C retail is intense, with easy switching.

**Threat of New Entrants (Low–Moderate).** High capital requirements, long permitting timelines, and specialist operational expertise create substantial barriers in generation. However, digital energy platforms and distributed energy resources (rooftop solar, demand response) lower barriers in the retail and demand-management segments, representing an emerging competitive threat in Vattenfall's Customers & Solutions segment.

**Bargaining Power of Suppliers (Moderate–High).** For new nuclear construction, Vattenfall is dependent on a small number of SMR vendors and a constrained global supply of nuclear-grade materials. For offshore wind, the investment prerequisites for offshore wind in Sweden are currently not viable — partly reflecting the pricing power of specialised installation vessel operators and supply chain inflation that made the Norfolk project financially untenable.

**Bargaining Power of Buyers (Moderate).** Large industrial customers can negotiate long-term PPAs that reduce Vattenfall's upside exposure. The Swedish government, as both owner and policy-setter, has unique leverage — it can set the rules of the game itself.

**Threat of Substitutes (Low–Moderate).** For baseload electricity there are few near-term substitutes. However, demand-side management and energy efficiency can moderate demand growth. Green hydrogen — which Vattenfall itself is developing through HYBRIT — could substitute for direct electricity use in some industrial applications, though this would likely increase rather than decrease demand for Vattenfall's electricity output.

**Overall Industry Attractiveness: Moderate.** The energy transition creates structural demand growth, but also price volatility, capital intensity, and regulatory complexity that moderate overall attractiveness. Vattenfall's scale and state backing provide a competitive buffer, but do not eliminate these pressures.

## 5. THE CORE STRATEGIC PROBLEM: THE DUAL MANDATE DILEMMA

The analytical frameworks converge on a single root cause underlying all three tensions: **the absence of a clearly negotiated, stable, and enforceable strategic mandate between the Swedish state and Vattenfall**.

The PESTEL analysis reveals that political tailwinds (nuclear support, fossil-free targets) and headwinds (price regulation, grid policy) are both generated by the same principal — the government. The RBV analysis shows that Vattenfall's strongest competitive advantages are concentrated in Sweden, yet historical strategy has repeatedly diverted capital to continental markets where the company has no structural edge. The Stakeholder analysis reveals that the government's dual role as owner and policy-setter creates governance ambiguity that has historically led to strategic drift.

The consequence is a pattern of costly strategic reversals: the lignite expansion and retreat (SEK 56 billion in write-downs across 2015–2016); the UK offshore wind development and sale; the Kriegers Flak pause. Each reversal has destroyed value and eroded internal strategic clarity. Vattenfall's CEO Anna Borg has stated: "We will continue on the path towards fossil freedom for our customers, in our operations and for our suppliers and partners. We will do this by focusing on financial stability and prioritising profitable business opportunities." This is a coherent vision — but its execution is constrained by the ambiguity of the ownership mandate.

A secondary but related problem is **capital allocation discipline**. Vattenfall's CEO states that Europe needs more transmission capacity in order to allow power to flow more easily — but Vattenfall's own investment plan must compete internally between nuclear new build, offshore wind in Germany and the Netherlands, grid modernisation, and industrial decarbonisation partnerships. The Ansoff analysis suggests the current portfolio is over-diversified; the RBV analysis suggests the highest-return investments are those that leverage Vattenfall's unique Swedish assets.

## 6. STRATEGIC OPTIONS

Three broad strategic options present themselves:

**Option A — Nordic Concentration Strategy:** Radically concentrate capital and management on the Swedish home market — nuclear (new build and life extension), hydro optimisation, grid modernisation, and industrial electrification partnerships. Divest or partner out most continental European assets.

*Risk:* Reduced diversification increases exposure to Nordic price cycles. Divestment may crystallise losses.

**Option B — Dual-Track Strategy (Recommended):** Maintain a focused but selective continental presence (primarily the Nordlicht offshore wind project in Germany, where a final investment decision has already been taken) while concentrating the majority of new investment in the Swedish home market. This preserves optionality and diversification without the strategic overstretch of pursuing all markets simultaneously.

*Risk:* Requires disciplined capital allocation governance to prevent mission creep back towards continental overexpansion.

**Option C — Full European Energy Transition Leader:** Aggressively expand across all segments and all European markets, positioning Vattenfall as the continent's leading fossil-free energy company.

*Risk:* Capital requirements far exceed internal generation capacity; execution risk is very high; continental markets lack the structural advantages Vattenfall enjoys in Sweden.

**Assessment:** Option B is recommended. It is consistent with the RBV finding that Vattenfall's durable advantages are Sweden-concentrated; with the Ansoff analysis favouring lower-risk growth strategies in existing markets; and with the Stakeholder analysis that the Swedish government's primary interest is Swedish energy security, not European energy leadership per se.

## 7. RECOMMENDATIONS & ACTION PLAN

### Recommendation 1: Formalise the Strategic Mandate — Governance Reform *(Immediate: 0–12 months)*

**The Problem:** The "owner without principles" governance failure must be addressed at its root. Currently, the Ministry of Enterprise holds the ownership mandate for Vattenfall, but energy policy is set by the Ministry of Energy — creating coordination failures and strategic ambiguity that have driven costly reversals.

**The Action:** The Swedish government should produce a formal, multi-year **ownership directive** for Vattenfall that explicitly:
- Defines commercial return targets (e.g., return on capital employed above cost of capital)
- Defines public interest obligations (e.g., energy security, contribution to 100% fossil-free target)
- Specifies how conflicts between these objectives are to be resolved
- Establishes a competent ownership board with energy sector expertise

**Theoretical Basis:** This addresses the **principal-agent problem** (Jensen & Meckling, 1976) by reducing information asymmetry between owner and management, and by aligning incentives through clear, measurable objectives. Mendelow's stakeholder matrix identifies the government as the highest-power, highest-interest stakeholder — the quality of this relationship is the single most important determinant of strategic coherence. Without this foundation, every other strategic initiative risks being undermined by the same political ambiguity that has caused costly reversals in the past.

### Recommendation 2: Anchor Investment in Competitive Advantage — Nuclear and Hydro First *(Short–Medium Term: 1–3 years)*

**The Problem:** Vattenfall's VRIO analysis reveals that its hydro portfolio and nuclear operational expertise are genuinely rare and inimitable assets. Yet these assets have been under-invested relative to the offshore wind portfolio, where Vattenfall competes with better-capitalised specialists.

**The Actions:**

- **Hydro optimisation:** Invest in digitalisation and predictive maintenance of the hydro fleet to improve availability and flexibility value. Hydro's ability to ramp rapidly is increasingly valuable as intermittent renewables grow.

- **Nuclear life extension:** Together with the other co-owners of the nuclear power plants Forsmark and Ringhals, Vattenfall has made a strategic decision to prolong operation of its five reactors from 60 to 80 years. This is strategically sound and should be resourced as a priority — each additional year of operation from a largely depreciated nuclear plant generates high-margin, near-zero-carbon baseload electricity.

- **SMR development (stage-gated):** Vattenfall has decided to proceed with American company GE Vernova and British company Rolls-Royce SMR. Both suppliers produce modular reactors and are considered to have the potential for a relatively lower cost of electricity. The final investment decision (targeted for 2029) should be contingent on: (a) state financing terms confirmed; (b) supplier cost estimates validated by independent technical review; and (c) electricity demand forecasts updated to reflect actual industrial electrification progress.

**Theoretical Basis:** The **VRIO framework** (Barney, 1991) indicates that investment in resources that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Organisationally embedded generates sustained competitive advantage. Offshore wind development, by contrast, generates only competitive parity. Capital should follow competitive advantage.

### Recommendation 3: Resolve the Grid-Generation Mismatch — Advocate for Regulatory Reform *(Short Term: 0–18 months)*

**The Problem:** The Kriegers Flak pause and the persistent SE1–SE4 price divergence reveal a systemic failure in Swedish grid policy. Vattenfall has previously communicated that one of the main prerequisites for investing in the project is a reasonable connection point to the national grid offshore. This is a regulatory design failure, not a project viability failure per se.

**The Actions:**
- **Advocacy:** Vattenfall should use its position as the state-owned energy champion to advocate forcefully with the government and Svenska kraftnät for: (a) mandatory offshore grid connection standards for new projects in Sweden; (b) accelerated investment in the SE3–SE4 transmission corridor; and (c) a clearer framework for cost allocation between generators and the grid operator.
- **Conditional re-engagement with Kriegers Flak:** Hold the project in reserve with a clear set of regulatory conditions that, if met, would trigger re-engagement. This avoids permanent abandonment of a strategically valuable asset while maintaining capital discipline.
- **Grid investment priority:** Over the next five years, Vattenfall plans to invest nearly SEK 47 billion to continue ensuring a secure and stable electricity grid. This investment generates regulated returns and directly addresses the structural north-south imbalance.

**Theoretical Basis:** The **PESTEL analysis** identifies regulatory uncertainty as the primary environmental threat to Vattenfall's Swedish renewable pipeline. Vattenfall, as the dominant state-owned player, has unique leverage to shape the regulatory environment — this is a form of **non-market strategy** that can create competitive advantage unavailable to private competitors.

### Recommendation 4: Adopt a Selective Continental Strategy *(Medium Term: 2–4 years)*

**The Problem:** Vattenfall's continental European operations have historically been a source of value destruction. The company lacks the structural advantages in continental markets that it enjoys in Sweden.

**The Actions:**
- **Proceed with Nordlicht (Germany):** During 2025, Vattenfall took final investment decisions for the Nordlicht I and II offshore wind farms in the North Sea, Germany, and progressed plans for new nuclear power by selecting two small modular reactor suppliers. This is a committed project with a 49% partner (BASF), reducing Vattenfall's capital exposure and sharing execution risk. Deliver it.
- **Resist speculative expansion:** Any new continental market entry should be subject to a strict competitive advantage test: does Vattenfall have a structural edge unavailable to competitors? If not, do not invest.
- **Review underperforming retail positions:** The German retail business has faced increasing regulatory costs. A strategic review should assess whether retail in Germany and France generates sufficient returns to justify the capital and management attention.

**Theoretical Basis:** The **Ansoff Matrix** indicates that Market Development (existing products in new markets) carries higher risk than Market Penetration or Product Development. The **RBV analysis** confirms that Vattenfall's advantages are not portable to continental markets. Ansoff himself argued that diversification should only be pursued if it leverages existing capabilities — a condition only partially met in continental offshore wind.

### Recommendation 5: Scale Industrial Electrification Partnerships — Monetise the HYBRIT Advantage *(Medium–Long Term: 3–7 years)*

**The Problem / Opportunity:** Sweden's industrial electrification — steel, mining, data centres — is expected to drive a near-doubling of electricity demand by 2040. Vattenfall is uniquely positioned to serve this demand. SSAB has manufactured the first fossil-free steel in the world and delivered it to a customer. The test delivery is an important step toward a fully fossil-free iron and steel production value chain.

**The Actions:**
- **Structured PPA programme:** Develop a standardised, scalable PPA product for large industrial customers, backed by Vattenfall's nuclear and hydro baseload. This provides revenue certainty for Vattenfall and price certainty for industrial customers — reducing exposure to spot price volatility for both parties.
- **Hydrogen scale-up:** Building on HYBRIT, develop a commercial green hydrogen supply business targeting industrial customers in northern Sweden. In the HYBRIT pilot, the fossil-free (green) hydrogen is produced by water electrolysis using fossil-free electricity. The storage facility is a pilot plant measuring 100 cubic metres and contains hydrogen gas pressurised up to 250 bar. Scaling this to commercial levels requires the cheap, abundant renewable electricity that northern Sweden uniquely provides.
- **Data centre partnerships:** Sweden's cold climate and fossil-free electricity make it an attractive location for hyperscale data centres. AI has become a prerequisite for staying competitive. The technology is advancing rapidly and creates enormous new demand for electricity. Vattenfall should develop a dedicated energy supply offering for this segment, including waste heat recovery for district heating networks.

**Theoretical Basis:** The **dynamic capabilities framework** (Teece, 2007) emphasises the importance of sensing and seizing new market opportunities. The HYBRIT partnership demonstrates that Vattenfall has the dynamic capability to co-create new markets with industrial partners. Scaling this capability into a systematic industrial electrification business transforms the public mission from a constraint into a commercial growth engine — directly resolving the dual mandate tension.

### Recommendation 6: Strengthen Financial Discipline — Capital Allocation Framework *(Immediate: 0–6 months)*

**The Problem:** With SEK 170 billion of planned investment over 2025–2029 and falling electricity prices reducing internal cash generation, Vattenfall faces a capital allocation challenge. The risk is that political pressure to "do everything" leads to capital being spread too thinly, with no single initiative receiving sufficient resources to succeed.

**The Actions:**
- **Establish a clear capital allocation hierarchy:**
  1. *Priority 1* — Maintenance and life extension of existing nuclear and hydro (protect existing competitive advantage)
  2. *Priority 2* — Swedish grid investment (regulated returns, strategic necessity)
  3. *Priority 3* — Nordlicht and committed offshore wind (honour existing commitments)
  4. *Priority 4* — SMR development (stage-gated, conditional on milestones)
  5. *Priority 5* — Industrial electrification partnerships (high-return, mission-aligned)
  6. *Priority 6* — All other growth initiatives (subject to competitive advantage test)

- **Define financial hurdle rates by risk category:** Nuclear life extension should require a lower hurdle rate (low risk, high certainty) than SMR new build (high risk, high uncertainty). Continental offshore wind should require a higher hurdle rate than Swedish investments, reflecting the absence of structural competitive advantage.

- **Link CEO remuneration to both commercial and mission KPIs:** Adding explicit mission KPIs (e.g., CO₂ avoided per SEK invested, contribution to Swedish energy security) alongside financial metrics aligns management incentives with the dual mandate.

**Theoretical Basis:** This recommendation draws on **agency theory** (Jensen & Meckling, 1976) — aligning principal and agent interests through explicit, measurable incentive structures — and on the **resource allocation literature** (Bower, 1970), which emphasises that strategy is ultimately expressed through capital allocation decisions, not mission statements.

## 8. CONCLUSION

The chain of analysis in this case study leads to a coherent strategic logic: **anchor strategy in competitive advantage, resolve the governance ambiguity that generates strategic incoherence, and transform the public mission from a constraint into a commercial growth engine.**

Vattenfall occupies a strategically privileged but strategically precarious position. Its hydro and nuclear assets, its distribution grid, and its industrial decarbonisation partnerships represent genuine, durable competitive advantages that no private competitor can easily replicate. Yet these advantages have been repeatedly diluted by strategic overreach into continental markets where Vattenfall competes on equal terms with better-focused rivals, and by the governance ambiguity that flows from the dual mandate of a state-owned commercial enterprise.

Sweden is a global leader in building a low-carbon economy, with the lowest share of fossil fuels in its primary energy supply among all IEA member countries. Vattenfall is central to sustaining that leadership. The electrification of Swedish industry — driven by the same climate imperatives that define Vattenfall's mission — represents the largest demand growth opportunity in the company's history. Capturing it requires not dispersed continental ambition, but disciplined Nordic concentration, backed by a governance structure that gives the company the clarity of mandate it has historically lacked.

The most important single action remains governance reform: a clearly negotiated, publicly accountable ownership directive that makes both the commercial and the public interest objectives explicit, measurable, and enforceable. Without this foundation, every other strategic initiative risks being undermined by the same political ambiguity that has caused costly reversals in the past. Vattenfall's history is, in many ways, the history of Sweden's energy policy — a reflection of the country's ambitions, contradictions, and capacity for reinvention. The strategic situation it faces today is demanding, but its asset base, its balance sheet, and the structural demand growth from industrial electrification give it the resources to navigate it successfully — if it can achieve the strategic clarity that has so often eluded it.
