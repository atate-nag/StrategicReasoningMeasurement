# Strategic Case Study: Trustpilot and the Platform Integrity Dilemma

## The Tension at the Heart of an Open Review Marketplace

---

## 1. Introduction and Case Framing

This case study examines the central strategic challenge facing Trustpilot Group plc: a structural tension between its commercial model and its value proposition. Trustpilot's value to consumers rests on the perceived authenticity of its reviews; yet its revenue depends on selling services to the very businesses those reviews evaluate. This tension is not merely reputational — it is architecturally embedded in the platform's design. The analysis proceeds in five stages: (i) situational description; (ii) two-sided platform analysis; (iii) Porter's Five Forces; (iv) SWOT; (v) a strategic options assessment leading to a prioritised action plan. Each analytical step draws explicitly on the evidence gathered, and each inferential move is warranted.

---

## 2. Situational Description

Trustpilot is an open platform, meaning anyone can write a review about any business, and every business can read and respond, for free. It monetises through a subscription model with a dual-sided platform that leverages network effects and provides businesses with tools to invite reviews, respond to feedback, showcase content, and benchmark performance.

Revenue grew 18% in constant currency to $210.7 million in 2024 (2023: $176.4 million), and Trustpilot delivered a record adjusted EBITDA of $24.1 million (2023: $15.5 million). Profit before tax was $5.2 million (2023: a $1.9 million loss). These headline numbers suggest a business gaining operational momentum. However, beneath this trajectory lies a strategic fault line.

This creates an interesting tension: Trustpilot has every incentive to satisfy paying businesses, which represent its primary revenue source, while maintaining consumer trust from the users who generate free content. This tension is the organising problem of the case: if consumers lose confidence in review authenticity, businesses lose the conversion benefit that motivates their subscription; if Trustpilot enforces integrity too aggressively, it risks alienating paying clients. The strategic situation is therefore not simply an operational problem of fraud detection — it is a governance and positioning problem that goes to the heart of what the platform is for.

## 3. Framework 1 — Two-Sided Platform Analysis (Rochet & Tirole)

### 3.1 The Platform Architecture

Two-sided markets are defined as markets where one or several platforms enable interactions between two sides and get the two sides "on board" by appropriately charging each side (Rochet & Tirole, 2006). Trustpilot is a textbook case: consumers (Side A) provide the review content that generates credibility and traffic; businesses (Side B) pay subscriptions to access the audience, tools, and reputational signals that content creates.

Trustpilot operates a dual-sided platform that exhibits network effects. As a SaaS business, it consistently achieves gross margins over 80%. The high gross margin reflects the near-zero marginal cost of hosting an additional review — the platform scales without proportionate cost increases, which is the economic hallmark of a two-sided digital marketplace.

### 3.2 The Cross-Side Network Effect and the Growth Flywheel

Trustpilot's strategy comprises five pillars. Trust is at the heart of everything it does. Active use of the platform by consumers and businesses generates a strong growth flywheel. This drives efficient growth. The flywheel logic is: more reviews → more consumer traffic → more business value from subscription tools → more business sign-ups → more review invitations → more reviews. This is a classic cross-side network effect: each additional consumer reviewer makes the platform more valuable to businesses, and each additional business that invites reviews increases the volume of content available to consumers.

**The warrant for the flywheel claim:** Trustpilot had 301 million total active reviews at end of December 2024 — a 23% year-on-year increase — with 61 million reviews written in 2024, a 15% year-on-year increase. The sustained double-digit growth in review volume is consistent with a functioning flywheel. However, the flywheel's integrity depends on consumers believing the reviews are authentic. If fake reviews erode that belief, the consumer side of the platform atrophies, the cross-side network effect reverses, and the entire commercial model unwinds.

### 3.3 The Monetisation Asymmetry and Its Strategic Risk

In Rochet-Tirole two-sided markets, platforms must decide which side to subsidise and which to charge. Trustpilot charges Side B (businesses) while keeping Side A (consumers) free. This is rational: businesses capture measurable commercial value from positive reviews (higher conversion rates), while consumers provide content as a public good. However, the platform's business model relies on a particularly revealing freemium system. Companies can create a free profile and receive reviews, but to unlock advanced features — such as responding to reviews, customising their page, or accessing detailed analytics — they must subscribe to a paid plan.

The strategic risk of this asymmetry is that businesses — as paying clients — may expect preferential treatment in how reviews are managed. Grizzly Research concluded that Trustpilot has failed to balance this conflict, and, due to pressure to present investors with growing revenues and profits, has gone all in on becoming what they describe as an extortion racket. While such tactics might boost revenues in the short term, they lead to a rapid degradation in the value proposition for website visitors who want objective reviews when researching a service. Even if the Grizzly characterisation is contested — Trustpilot rejected the claims as misleading — the perception of bias is itself strategically damaging because consumer trust is a credence good: it cannot be directly observed and must be inferred from signals.

**The inferential step:** If consumers perceive that paying businesses receive preferential treatment, they will discount the reviews, reducing the informational value of the platform. This reduces the conversion benefit businesses obtain from subscriptions, which in turn weakens the business case for paying. The tension is therefore self-undermining unless resolved.

## 4. Framework 2 — Porter's Five Forces

### 4.1 Threat of New Entrants — **Moderate**

The platform benefits from significant scale advantages: 301 million active reviews represent a data asset that a new entrant cannot replicate quickly. However, the barriers to entry in review hosting are not absolute. The technology stack is replicable, and niche vertical platforms (e.g., sector-specific review sites) can enter without needing to match Trustpilot's breadth. The regulatory environment may paradoxically reduce entry barriers: compliance with the EU Digital Services Act creates fixed costs that larger incumbents absorb more easily, but it also standardises the operating environment, reducing proprietary advantage. **Assessment:** The volume of existing reviews creates switching costs for businesses already embedded in the ecosystem, but the openness of the internet means entry threat is real at the margin.

### 4.2 Bargaining Power of Buyers (Businesses) — **Moderate to High**

Trustpilot reported approximately 60% of revenue from subscription products in 2024, highlighting dependence on SaaS monetisation. This concentration means that business churn directly threatens revenue. Businesses have alternatives: Google reviews are better for local search visibility and are completely free. A business dissatisfied with Trustpilot's pricing or enforcement decisions can migrate to Google Reviews at zero incremental cost. This gives paying businesses meaningful leverage, which structurally pressures Trustpilot to accommodate their preferences — including, potentially, in review moderation. **The warrant:** High buyer power, combined with a freemium model, creates a rational incentive for Trustpilot to be lenient with paying clients, which is precisely what critics allege.

### 4.3 Bargaining Power of Suppliers (Consumers/Reviewers) — **Low individually, High collectively**

Individual consumer reviewers have negligible bargaining power — they provide content for free and are individually replaceable. However, the collective body of reviewers is the platform's core asset. If systemic distrust causes consumers to stop writing reviews, or to migrate to alternative platforms, the supply of authentic content dries up. This is a diffuse but existential risk.

### 4.4 Threat of Substitutes — **High**

Google Reviews and Amazon control distribution channels that generate more than 50% of first-touch review exposures for many categories. This is the most acute competitive threat. Google Reviews is embedded in search results — the point at which consumers are already making decisions — whereas Trustpilot requires a deliberate navigation step. For local and e-commerce businesses, Google's integration with Maps and Shopping creates a substitute that is simultaneously free, more visible, and frictionless. Google Reviews is Trustpilot's top competitor, leveraging search and Maps dominance to capture local feedback. Trustpilot counters with stronger verification, business tools, and SEO value. The key insight is that Trustpilot's defence against substitution rests almost entirely on its superior verification and trust signals. If those are compromised by fake review problems, the competitive moat disappears.

**The inferential step:** The threat of substitution and the platform integrity problem are therefore not independent issues — they are structurally linked. Fake reviews undermine the one differentiator that justifies choosing Trustpilot over a free Google alternative.

### 4.5 Competitive Rivalry — **High**

Beyond Google, Yelp remains a significant competitor in specific verticals. The review platform market is not winner-takes-all: different platforms dominate different verticals and geographies, meaning Trustpilot faces ongoing rivalry across multiple fronts simultaneously.

### 4.6 Five Forces Summary

The most strategically significant forces are the **threat of substitutes** (very high, anchored by Google's structural advantage) and **buyer power** (high, creating integrity risks). Together, they create a competitive environment in which Trustpilot's only sustainable differentiation is the credibility of its reviews — making the integrity problem not a peripheral compliance matter but a core strategic one.

## 5. Framework 3 — SWOT Analysis

### 5.1 Strengths

**Scale and data network effects:** Trustpilot hosts over 330 million customer reviews on its platform by 2025, growing from about 80 million in 2019. This four-fold expansion in six years demonstrates compounding network effects and creates a proprietary data asset for AI-based fraud detection.

**Financial momentum:** In 2024, Trustpilot's net dollar retention improved from 99% to 103%, marking a record for the business. A net dollar retention above 100% means existing customers are expanding their spend, indicating that the subscription product is delivering measurable value.

**AI-driven integrity infrastructure:** In 2024, of the 4.5 million detected fake reviews removed from the platform, 90% were automatically detected by fake review detection models. This is an improvement from 82% in 2023, demonstrating a strengthening technical capability that is both operationally valuable and a reputational signal to regulators and consumers.

### 5.2 Weaknesses

**The business-model integrity tension:** As established in the two-sided platform analysis, Trustpilot has every incentive to satisfy paying businesses, which represent its primary revenue source, while maintaining consumer trust from the users who generate free content. This structural conflict is not resolvable by operational improvements alone — it requires governance architecture.

**Persistent fake review volume:** Trustpilot removed 4.5 million fake reviews — amounting to 7.4% of the total number of reviews submitted in 2024. In 2023, it was 6.1% — demonstrating how its technology is scaling and improving as the number of reviews on the platform increases. While the detection rate is improving, the absolute volume of fake reviews is rising, and the proportion detected (7.4%) almost certainly understates the true prevalence of manipulation, since only detected fakes are counted.

**Reputational vulnerability:** The extortion narrative surrounding Trustpilot highlights a troubling aspect of the platform's business model, where companies are often pressured into paying for premium services to maintain their reputations. Even where such allegations are contested, the perception damages the consumer credence that underpins Trustpilot's value proposition.

### 5.3 Opportunities

**Regulatory tailwind:** The EU Digital Services Act creates compliance obligations that favour platforms with robust verification infrastructure. Since February 2024, the DSA has applied to all online services in the EU, with penalties of up to 6% of global turnover for non-compliance. Trustpilot's investment in AI-based detection and transparency reporting positions it ahead of less-prepared competitors, and its engagement with regulators creates an opportunity to shape industry standards in ways that entrench its verification advantage.

**Enterprise upselling and product expansion:** A 2024 innovation uses predictive analytics to identify SMBs with high organic review volume but no paid subscription, allowing for a high-conversion, product-led outreach strategy that targets warm leads within the Trustpilot platform. This product-led growth approach reduces customer acquisition cost while expanding the paying base.

**Embedding in commerce infrastructure:** Trustpilot's 2023 strategy to integrate directly with platforms like Shopify Plus and Stripe fuelled a 22% year-on-year surge in enterprise revenue by Q4 2024. Deeper embedding in e-commerce workflows increases switching costs and reduces the substitution threat from Google Reviews for e-commerce-specific use cases.

### 5.4 Threats

**DSA exploitation by businesses:** German businesses systematically exploit Digital Services Act notification mechanisms to remove critical reviews, while platforms like Google, TripAdvisor, and Trustpilot err on caution to avoid legal liability. This creates a new vector through which businesses can suppress negative reviews using legal mechanisms, undermining platform integrity from a direction that AI fraud detection cannot address.

**Google's structural dominance:** Google Reviews and Amazon control distribution channels that generate more than 50% of first-touch review exposures for many categories. Google's integration with Search and Maps means it captures consumer intent at the highest-value moment of the purchase journey, a structural position Trustpilot cannot replicate.

**Escalating fraud sophistication:** The volume of fake reviews submitted is increasing alongside total review volume, demonstrating how fraud scales with platform growth. As Trustpilot's detection models improve, adversarial actors will adapt, creating an ongoing arms race in which Trustpilot must continuously invest.

## 6. Synthesis: The Core Strategic Problem

The three frameworks converge on a single diagnosis. From the two-sided platform lens: Trustpilot's commercial model creates a structural incentive to accommodate paying businesses in ways that may compromise review integrity, and this compromises the cross-side network effect on which the entire flywheel depends. From Porter's Five Forces: the only sustainable defence against substitution by Google Reviews is superior verification credibility, meaning integrity is not a cost centre but the primary competitive asset. From the SWOT: the platform has the technical capability and regulatory positioning to strengthen integrity, but must resolve the governance tension between revenue extraction from businesses and the independence of review content.

**The central strategic recommendation is therefore:** Trustpilot must institutionally separate content integrity governance from commercial relationships, and make that separation visible and verifiable to consumers and regulators. This is not simply an ethical imperative — it is the condition under which the growth flywheel remains commercially sustainable.

## 7. Strategic Options and Evaluation

### Option A: Intensify AI-Based Fraud Detection (Incremental)

Continue investing in automated detection, targeting >95% automated removal of fake reviews. This is consistent with the current trajectory — from 82% (2023) to 90% (2024) automated detection — and is low-disruption to the commercial model.

**Limitation:** AI detection addresses supply-side fraud (fake positives injected by businesses or third parties) but does not address demand-side manipulation (businesses using DSA notifications to remove legitimate negative reviews, or the perception of preferential treatment for paying clients). The structural conflict remains unresolved.

### Option B: Governance Separation — An Independent Content Integrity Board

Establish a formally independent Content Integrity Board with published decision-making criteria, external membership (consumer advocates, academics, regulators), and audit rights over moderation decisions. Publish quarterly transparency data disaggregated by paying vs. non-paying businesses to demonstrate that subscription status does not affect review treatment.

**Rationale:** Not all businesses who pay for Trustpilot's services have good TrustScores. Some do, some don't. Businesses with better TrustScores tend to be those that actively engage with consumers through the platform, listening and responding to feedback. If this is genuinely true — that payment does not influence scores — then making the governance architecture that ensures this separation visible costs Trustpilot nothing operationally but converts a contested claim into a verifiable one. The reputational benefit of credible independence far exceeds the cost of the governance structure.

### Option C: Vertical Deepening and Differentiation from Google

Rather than competing with Google Reviews on breadth, concentrate subscription-product investment on verticals where depth of verification matters most: financial services, healthcare, and high-value e-commerce. In these categories, consumer stakes are highest, regulatory scrutiny is greatest, and Google's generalised, unverified review model is weakest.

**Rationale:** Trustpilot's integration with Shopify Plus and Stripe demonstrates that embedding in sector-specific commerce infrastructure creates durable switching costs. Extending this logic to regulated industries — where DSA and sector-specific regulations mandate verified review processes — creates a defensible niche that Google's open model cannot easily replicate.

## 8. Recommended Action Plan

The following recommendations are ranked by strategic priority, with each recommendation grounded in the preceding analysis.

### Recommendation 1 (Priority: Critical) — Establish Structural Governance Separation

**What:** Create an independent Content Integrity function, reporting to a board-level committee with external membership, with a published charter that prohibits commercial teams from influencing moderation decisions.

**Why this is warranted:** The Grizzly Research allegations, the academic literature on two-sided platform conflicts, and the consumer perception data all point to the same structural problem: Trustpilot has every incentive to satisfy paying businesses, which represent its primary revenue source, while maintaining consumer trust from the users who generate free content. Operational improvements (better AI detection) cannot resolve a structural conflict; only structural separation can. Moreover, the DSA's transparency requirements create a regulatory context in which such governance architecture will be required — acting proactively allows Trustpilot to define the standard rather than comply reactively.

**Metric:** Quarterly publication of moderation outcome data disaggregated by paying/non-paying status; independent audit of moderation decisions annually.

### Recommendation 2 (Priority: High) — Weaponise the Transparency Report as a Competitive Asset

**What:** Expand the annual Trust/Transparency Report into a real-time public dashboard, showing rolling fake review removal rates, flagging volumes by category, and DSA notification outcomes.

**Why this is warranted:** Trustpilot removed 4.5 million fake reviews in 2024, with 90% taken down automatically by AI tools, as the customer feedback platform stepped up efforts to protect its integrity amid rising regulatory pressure and record volumes of user content. This is genuinely impressive performance, but it is currently communicated through an annual report that consumers rarely read. Real-time transparency converts a compliance cost into a marketing differentiator — it is the signal that distinguishes Trustpilot from Google Reviews (which publishes no equivalent data) and from the perception of opacity that feeds reputational risk.

**Metric:** Monthly active engagement with the transparency dashboard; media citations of transparency data as a proxy for credibility signalling.

### Recommendation 3 (Priority: High) — Counter DSA Exploitation with Proactive Notification Review

**What:** Implement a proactive review of all DSA-based removal notifications, with a published appeals process for reviewers whose content is challenged, and public reporting on the proportion of notifications rejected.

**Why this is warranted:** German businesses systematically exploit Digital Services Act notification mechanisms to remove critical reviews, while platforms like Google, TripAdvisor, and Trustpilot err on caution to avoid legal liability. This is a new and growing vector for review manipulation that operates through legal channels rather than technical fraud. If Trustpilot errs on the side of removal to avoid liability, it effectively allows paying businesses to suppress legitimate criticism — precisely the conflict the governance separation (Recommendation 1) is designed to prevent. A robust, transparent appeals process signals that Trustpilot defends reviewer rights, strengthening consumer-side trust.

**Metric:** Proportion of DSA notifications resulting in removal vs. rejection; time-to-resolution for reviewer appeals.

### Recommendation 4 (Priority: Medium) — Accelerate Vertical Specialisation in Regulated Industries

**What:** Develop sector-specific subscription tiers for financial services and healthcare, incorporating verification standards that meet or exceed regulatory requirements (e.g., FCA consumer duty in the UK, EU consumer protection directives).

**Why this is warranted:** Google Reviews are better for local search visibility and are completely free. Competing with Google on cost or search visibility is strategically futile. The defensible competitive space is where verification depth matters more than search integration. In regulated industries, a verified, auditable review record is a compliance asset, not merely a marketing tool. Trustpilot is expanding beyond review collection into conversion optimisation, customer engagement, and B2B buyer intent signals to improve merchant ROI and ad targeting. The logical extension of this product trajectory is into regulated verticals where the value of verified trust signals is highest and switching costs are greatest.

**Metric:** Revenue from regulated-industry subscriptions as a share of total ARR; net dollar retention in regulated-industry cohort vs. platform average.

### Recommendation 5 (Priority: Medium) — Product-Led Growth to Expand the Paying Base

**What:** Systematically deploy the predictive analytics capability to convert high-organic-volume non-paying businesses into subscribers, using demonstrated ROI evidence from similar businesses as the conversion argument.

**Why this is warranted:** Trustpilot's net dollar retention improved from 99% to 103% in 2024, marking a record for the business. Net dollar retention above 100% means the existing base is expanding spend, validating the product's value. Trustpilot's 2024 innovation uses predictive analytics to identify SMBs with high organic review volume but no paid subscription, allowing for a high-conversion, product-led outreach strategy that targets warm leads within the platform. Scaling this capability converts latent platform value into revenue without requiring new customer acquisition spend, improving unit economics while growing the paying base. Crucially, this recommendation is only commercially viable if Recommendation 1 (governance separation) is in place — because businesses will only pay for a subscription if the review platform retains consumer credibility.

**Metric:** Conversion rate of targeted non-paying businesses; CAC for product-led vs. sales-led acquisition channels.

## 9. Conclusion

Trustpilot's strategic situation is defined by a structural tension that is simultaneously its greatest vulnerability and, if resolved, its greatest competitive advantage. The platform's commercial model — charging businesses for tools to manage reviews written by consumers — creates an inherent conflict of interest that undermines the consumer trust on which the entire value proposition rests. Porter's Five Forces analysis shows that Trustpilot's only sustainable defence against substitution by Google Reviews is superior verification credibility; the two-sided platform framework shows that fake reviews and perceived bias degrade the cross-side network effect that drives the growth flywheel; and the SWOT analysis shows that the technical capability and regulatory positioning exist to resolve the problem — but only if governance architecture matches operational capability.

The recommended action plan therefore prioritises structural governance separation above all other measures, because without it, every operational improvement (better AI detection, more transparency data, deeper vertical integration) is built on a foundation that reputational risk can collapse. With it, Trustpilot can convert its compliance investment into a durable competitive moat — one that Google, whose review model remains open and unverified, cannot easily replicate.
