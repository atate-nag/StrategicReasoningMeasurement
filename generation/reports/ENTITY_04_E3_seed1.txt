# STRATEGIC CASE STUDY: LOCKHEED MARTIN CORPORATION
## Navigating the Execution–Dependency Trap in the Defence Industrial Base

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Lockheed Martin Corporation, the world's largest defence contractor, faces a paradox at the heart of modern industrial strategy: its competitive position is simultaneously its greatest vulnerability. The firm commands a backlog of nearly $160 billion — greater than two times annual revenue, yet in 2024 it recorded $2.0 billion in losses on fixed-price development programmes and delivered every single one of its F-35 aircraft late. This case study examines why a structurally advantaged firm is failing on execution, and what strategic actions can resolve the contradiction. Using Porter's Five Forces, the Resource-Based View (RBV) and Dynamic Capabilities framework, a Value Chain analysis, and a TOWS matrix, the analysis develops a coherent chain of reasoning from evidence to recommendation.

---

## SECTION 1: CASE BACKGROUND AND STRATEGIC SITUATION

### 1.1 Company Overview

Net sales in 2024 were $71.0 billion, compared to $67.6 billion in 2023. Net earnings in 2024 were $5.3 billion, or $22.31 per share, including $2.0 billion ($1.5 billion, or $6.16 per share, after-tax) of losses for classified programs. The firm's backlog spans aeronautics (F-35, F-22), missiles and fire control (PAC-3, HIMARS), rotary and mission systems, and space.

Yet beneath these headline numbers, a structural crisis is developing on three fronts. First, the firm derived 73% of its total consolidated net sales from the U.S. Government in 2024, including 65% from the Department of Defense (DoD). Second, in 2024, Lockheed Martin delivered 110 aircraft, and all were late by an average of 238 days, up from 61 days in 2023. Third, fixed-priced contracts place the risk and responsibility for any cost overruns on the contractor, and Lockheed's classified programme losses show this exposure is material.

The strategic situation, therefore, is this: Lockheed Martin has a near-monopolistic position in several critical defence segments and a record order book, yet it is simultaneously haemorrhaging cash on fixed-price contracts, failing to deliver its flagship product on time, and facing escalating structural risks from workforce shortages, supply chain fragility, and customer concentration. The question is whether the firm's competitive advantages are durable, or whether an execution failure will erode the institutional trust upon which those advantages rest.

## SECTION 2: ENVIRONMENTAL ANALYSIS — PORTER'S FIVE FORCES

Porter's Five Forces (Porter, 1979) provides a framework for assessing the structural profitability of an industry. In the defence sector, the configuration of forces is unusual: very high barriers to entry and low threat of substitutes coexist with extreme buyer power, creating a structurally mixed competitive environment.

### 2.1 Threat of New Entrants — LOW

Complicated government-contracting regulations are a barrier to entry. Understanding the 2,002-page Federal Acquisition Regulation is no easy task for a business. As a result of these conditions, there are fewer players in the defence industry than there used to be, from the prime contractors down to their suppliers' suppliers. Beyond regulatory compliance, entering this market requires cybersecurity, cost accounting systems and domestic content compliance, and the defence industrial base has been consolidating over time.

**Analytical warrant:** Because barriers to entry are high and rising as defence technology becomes more complex and specialised, barriers to entry only appear to be increasing as defence technology continues to get more complex and specialised, and today's incumbents are likely to become further entrenched. This means the threat of new entrants does not constitute a near-term strategic risk for Lockheed. However, it also means that the discipline of market competition — which would normally force operational improvement — is largely absent, a structural condition that connects directly to Lockheed's execution failures.

### 2.2 Bargaining Power of Suppliers — MODERATE TO HIGH (and rising)

Supplier power is elevated on two distinct dimensions: material inputs and human capital.

On materials: China mines over 60% and processes over 80% of the world's rare earths, and produces around 90% of the world's high-performance rare earth magnets, creating significant global dependence on a single country. China has already restricted the exports of seven heavy rare earth elements, which are vital for manufacturing advanced semiconductors and other aerospace and defence technologies. Until diversified supply chains mature, aerospace manufacturers, MRO providers and defence planners must confront a sobering reality: access to rare earth elements is now a determinant of supply chain resiliency and industrial power.

On labour: Retirements will create a projected gap of 3.5 million workers by 2026, increasing the pressure to hire in a competitive marketplace. The Aerospace and Defence industry is facing a global shortage of semiconductor engineers, projected to exceed one million by 2030. Boeing experienced further production challenges when machinists began a strike in September 2024 over wage and benefit concerns, incurring an estimated $5 billion in losses — illustrating how labour supply constraints translate directly into financial damage.

**Analytical warrant:** Supplier power is elevated and rising on both material and human capital dimensions. This directly constrains Lockheed's ability to meet its production commitments — connecting to the F-35 delivery failures — and increases the cost pressure on fixed-price contracts, connecting to the $2 billion in programme losses.

### 2.3 Bargaining Power of Buyers — HIGH

The bargaining power of buyers in the aerospace and defence industry is high. The primary buyer is the U.S. government, which has significant influence over pricing and contract terms. The buyer has increasingly shifted risk to contractors: "Our prior work has shown that fixed-priced contracts are beneficial because the government generally pays a set price and the contractor generally assumes the risk and responsibility for any cost overruns," according to a 2023 Government Accountability Office report. Boeing's CFO made the consequences explicit: "Rest assured, we haven't signed any fixed-price development contracts nor (do we) intend to."

**Analytical warrant:** High buyer power combined with fixed-price risk transfer is the proximate cause of Lockheed's $2.0 billion in programme losses. This is not an isolated financial event but a structural feature of the buyer–seller relationship. The strategic implication is that Lockheed must either improve its cost estimation and execution capability — to absorb the risk profitably — or negotiate a different contractual relationship with its buyer.

### 2.4 Threat of Substitutes — LOW

There are no commercially available substitutes for fifth-generation fighter aircraft, hypersonic missile systems, or satellite constellations. The best businesses are unregulated monopolies, and barriers to entry only appear to be increasing as defence technology continues to get more complex and specialised. Non-traditional entrants have penetrated adjacent segments (e.g., launch) but have not displaced prime contractors in weapons systems.

**Analytical warrant:** The low threat of substitutes confirms the structural durability of Lockheed's market position, but it also removes a key incentive for the firm to innovate rapidly in its production processes. The absence of competitive threat from substitutes, combined with high barriers to entry, creates an environment in which execution mediocrity can persist longer than it would in a competitive market — making proactive self-correction all the more important.

### 2.5 Competitive Rivalry — LOW TO MODERATE

"The trend toward fewer and larger prime contractors has the potential to affect innovation, limit the supply base, pose entry barriers to small, medium and large businesses, and ultimately reduce competition — resulting in higher prices." Fewer programmes start, and they start further apart, meaning incumbents are not competing head-to-head continuously.

**Five Forces Summary:** The industry structure is highly favourable for incumbents in terms of profitability potential. However, extreme buyer power — and the contractual mechanisms through which it is exercised — represents the critical structural threat. Lockheed's strategic challenge is not competitive displacement but operational failure under buyer-imposed risk.

## SECTION 3: INTERNAL ANALYSIS — RESOURCE-BASED VIEW AND DYNAMIC CAPABILITIES

The Resource-Based View (Barney, 1991) holds that sustained competitive advantage derives from resources and capabilities that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN). The Dynamic Capabilities Approach (Teece, Pisano, and Shuen, 1997) extends this by suggesting that the method in which resources are applied within a firm can create a competitive advantage, and emphasises the firm's ability to sense, seize, and reconfigure its asset base.

### 3.1 VRIN Resource Audit

**Strengths:**

1. **Programme incumbency and switching costs.** Demand for Lockheed Martin's defence technology solutions remains robust, with a backlog of nearly $160 billion, greater than two times annual revenue. Once a prime contractor is selected for a major programme, switching costs are prohibitive: intellectual property, tooling, supply chain relationships, and security clearances are all programme-specific and non-transferable. This resource satisfies all four VRIN criteria.

2. **Customer relationships and classified programme access.** Just three main customers — the U.S. Department of Defence, NASA, and foreign military buyers — represent 80% of total revenue. In the defence world, forging a deep relationship with the Pentagon can provide decades of dependable contracts.

3. **Systems integration capability.** Lockheed's ability to integrate complex multi-domain systems (air, space, cyber, missile) is a rare organisational capability built over decades. Companies like Lockheed Martin, RTX, and Northrop Grumman are at the forefront of advancing military technology, from missile defence systems to cyber solutions and space exploration.

**Weaknesses (resource deficiencies):**

1. **Software development and systems engineering maturity.** The modernisation drive, known as Block 4, depends on a package called Technology Refresh 3 (TR-3). Valued at $1.9 billion, TR-3 includes upgraded processors, memory, and sensor systems meant to power new weapons, radar, and electronic warfare capabilities. Instead, TR-3 has become a choke point. Hardware design was immature, deliveries were late, and software testing uncovered persistent stability issues — from radar malfunctions to cockpit display failures.

2. **Fixed-price contract cost estimation.** Some existing development programmes are contracted on a fixed-price basis, and customers are increasingly implementing procurement policies that shift risk to contractors. Competitively bid programmes with fixed-price development work increase the risk of a reach-forward loss upon contract award and during the period of contract performance.

3. **Customer concentration.** This heavy reliance on a single customer can be risky, especially if there are changes in government spending or policy shifts that affect defence budgets.

### 3.2 Dynamic Capabilities Assessment

Lockheed Martin's 1LMX initiative aims to create a model-based enterprise with a fully integrated digital thread throughout the design, build and sustain product lifecycle, transforming end-to-end business processes and systems. The goal of 1LMX is to use digital tools and technologies and new processes to accelerate delivery to customers, incorporating digital technologies such as digital twins and advanced modelling into existing programmes.

However, the evidence from the F-35 programme suggests this dynamic capability is not yet operational at scale. In 2024, Lockheed Martin delivered 110 aircraft, and all were late by an average of 238 days, up from 61 days in 2023 — a deterioration, not an improvement, occurring during the 1LMX rollout period.

**Analytical warrant:** The RBV analysis reveals a firm with durable structural advantages (backlog, incumbency, clearances) but a widening gap between strategic intent (digital transformation, programme excellence) and operational reality (escalating delays, cost overruns). The dynamic capabilities framework predicts that unless the firm can reconfigure its software development and supply chain management processes, its structural advantages will erode as the DoD loses confidence in its ability to deliver.

## SECTION 4: VALUE CHAIN ANALYSIS

Porter's Value Chain (1985) disaggregates the firm's activities into primary activities (inbound logistics, operations, outbound logistics, marketing and sales, service) and support activities (firm infrastructure, human resource management, technology development, procurement). In the Aerospace and Defence industry, support functions like IT, HR, Finance, Legal and Compliance, Data Analytics, and Cybersecurity act as the backbone, enabling operational excellence.

**Primary Activities:**

- **Inbound Logistics — Critical vulnerability.** The contractors delivered all engines and aircraft late in 2024, in part due to TR-3 delays and parts shortages. These parts shortages connect directly to the rare earth supply chain dependency identified in the Five Forces analysis.
- **Operations — Significant weakness.** Contractors for the programme, Lockheed Martin and Pratt & Whitney, continued delivering aircraft and engines late. In 2024, Lockheed delivered 110 aircraft. All were late by an average of 238 days, up from 61 days in 2023.
- **Outbound Logistics — Constrained.** TR-3 is running three years late, forcing the Pentagon to accept 174 provisionally delivered aircraft in a non-combat-capable configuration.
- **Marketing & Sales — Strength.** In 2024 alone, U.S. defence exports hit $117.9 billion, a record driven by demand for systems like the PAC-3 MSE, which Lockheed Martin is scaling to 650 units annually by 2027.

**Support Activities:**

- **Technology Development — Mixed.** 1LMX is strategically sound but unproven at scale; TR-3 failures indicate software engineering weaknesses.
- **Human Resource Management — Weakness.** During 2024, Lockheed hired nearly 9,200 employees and 3,900 college hires and interns, but this pipeline is insufficient to offset the sector-wide retirement wave.
- **Procurement — Vulnerability.** The DoD has invested roughly $400 million in MP Materials, owner of the only active rare earth mine in the United States, but domestic alternatives remain years from maturity.

**Value Chain Analytical Warrant:** Value destruction is concentrated in two nodes: (1) inbound logistics/procurement (supply chain fragility, parts shortages) and (2) operations/technology development (software integration failure). These are not independent problems — the TR-3 delays are caused by both parts shortages and immature software. The 1LMX initiative, if successfully deployed in exactly these two nodes, would address both simultaneously. This connection from diagnosis to prescription drives Recommendation 1.

**Strengths (S):** $160bn backlog; programme incumbency; systems integration capability; record FMS demand; 1LMX digital transformation; strong R&D investment (~$4bn in 2023)

**Weaknesses (W):** $2bn fixed-price programme losses; 238-day average F-35 delivery delay; 73% government revenue concentration; software development immaturity; workforce gap

## SECTION 6: COHERENT STRATEGIC DIAGNOSIS

The analytical chain now permits a coherent diagnosis. The Five Forces analysis establishes that buyer power — exercised through fixed-price contracts — is the critical external threat. The RBV analysis confirms that the firm's structural advantages (backlog, incumbency) are durable but that its dynamic capabilities (software development, supply chain agility) are deficient. The Value Chain analysis pinpoints the two nodes where value is being destroyed: procurement and operations/technology development. The TOWS matrix identifies that the intersection of these weaknesses with the threats of rare earth restrictions and workforce shortages creates the most acute risk, while the intersection of strengths with the NATO/FMS opportunity creates the most accessible strategic upside.

**The core strategic problem is therefore:** Lockheed Martin's structural advantages are generating demand (backlog) faster than its operational capabilities can fulfil it, and its contractual exposure (fixed-price development) means that execution failure translates directly into financial loss. Block 4 costs are now more than $6 billion over original estimates and the completion date is five years later than originally anticipated. If this gap between demand and delivery is not closed, the DoD will have both the incentive and the institutional justification to reduce its dependency on Lockheed — splitting programmes, introducing second-source suppliers, or restructuring contracts in ways that further penalise delays.

## SECTION 7: RECOMMENDATIONS AND ACTION PLAN

### Recommendation 1: Accelerate 1LMX Digital Thread Deployment Specifically for F-35 Production

**Analytical basis (Value Chain → Dynamic Capabilities):** The Value Chain analysis identifies operations and technology development as the primary nodes of value destruction. TR-3 is running three years late, forcing the Pentagon to accept 174 provisionally delivered aircraft in a non-combat-capable configuration. The 1LMX initiative aims to create a digital thread that would directly address these failures by enabling real-time visibility into engineering change impacts and parts availability.

**Action:** Ring-fence a dedicated 1LMX deployment team for the F-35 programme with a mandate to implement digital twin-based configuration management and predictive parts-shortage detection within 18 months. Measure progress by reduction in average delivery delay (target: below 90 days by end of 2026).

**Warrant:** Among the GAO's recommendations are that the Defence Department evaluate Lockheed Martin's capacity to meet planned deliveries on time and expand and formalise leading practices. If Lockheed does not self-correct, the DoD has both the mandate and the institutional mechanism to impose corrective action. Proactive deployment of 1LMX on F-35 is therefore not merely an operational improvement but a defence of the firm's programme incumbency — its most valuable VRIN resource.

---

### Recommendation 2: Negotiate a Hybrid Contractual Risk-Sharing Framework with the DoD

**Analytical basis (Five Forces → RBV):** The Five Forces analysis establishes that buyer power — exercised through fixed-price development contracts — is the structural mechanism by which execution risk is transferred to Lockheed. Competitively bid programmes with fixed-price development work or fixed-price production options increase the risk of a reach-forward loss upon contract award and during the period of contract performance. Due to the complex and often experimental nature of development programmes, the firm may experience cost overruns.

**Action:** Engage the DoD — through the Aerospace Industries Association — to advocate for a hybrid contract structure: cost-plus for the first 24 months of development (where technical uncertainty is highest), transitioning to fixed-price for production once a stable baseline is established. This mirrors real-options logic: the government retains the option to lock in a price once technical risk is resolved, while the contractor is not penalised for inherent development uncertainty.

**Warrant:** Boeing's CFO stated in October 2023: "Rest assured, we haven't signed any fixed-price development contracts nor (do we) intend to." Boeing's public refusal has created an industry precedent. Lockheed, as the largest prime contractor, is best positioned to lead this negotiation. The DoD has a countervailing interest in contractor financial health: a financially distressed Lockheed Martin would threaten the entire F-35 programme and multiple national security programmes simultaneously.

---

### Recommendation 3: Diversify the Rare Earth Supply Chain Through Strategic Partnerships and Vertical Integration

**Analytical basis (Five Forces → Value Chain):** The Five Forces analysis identifies supplier power as elevated and rising. Under China's new rules, starting December 1, 2025, companies with any affiliation to foreign militaries — including those of the United States — will be largely denied export licences for rare earth materials. The Value Chain analysis identifies inbound logistics/procurement as the second critical node of value destruction, directly linked to parts shortages that contributed to TR-3 delays.

**Action:** (a) Expand investment in or offtake agreements with domestic rare earth processors, building on the DoD's existing $400 million commitment to MP Materials; (b) engage in co-investment with allied-nation mining firms in Australia, Canada, and Greenland; and (c) accelerate R&D into magnet designs that reduce dependency on the most restricted heavy rare earth elements.

**Warrant:** The DoD has invested roughly $400 million in MP Materials, owner of the only active rare earth mine in the United States, signalling that the U.S. government views rare earth diversification as a national security imperative. Lockheed's participation aligns its commercial interests with government policy, creating the possibility of co-funded supply chain investment — reducing the firm's capital outlay while strengthening operational resilience.

---

### Recommendation 4: Reduce Customer Concentration Through Systematic International Market Development

**Analytical basis (RBV → TOWS SO):** The RBV analysis identifies customer concentration as a structural weakness limiting strategic flexibility. This heavy reliance on a single customer can be risky, especially if there are changes in government spending or policy shifts that affect defence budgets. The TOWS SO strategy identifies the NATO/European defence spending supercycle as the primary accessible opportunity.

**Action:** Establish a dedicated International Growth Office with authority to pursue co-production agreements, technology transfer arrangements, and offset deals with NATO allies. Target: increase international revenue from approximately 27% to 35% of total sales by 2028, prioritising European markets where Germany's defence spending increased by over $23 billion between 2022 and 2024.

**Warrant:** Diversification into international markets reduces customer concentration risk and provides a hedge against U.S. budget volatility. NATO members have endorsed a new national spending benchmark of 5% of GDP to be invested annually in core defence requirements — a structural demand driver that is largely independent of U.S. legislative budget cycles. The FMS mechanism, which routes international sales through the U.S. government, also means international expansion does not require Lockheed to navigate foreign regulatory environments independently.

---

### Recommendation 5: Address the Workforce Gap Through a Structured Defence Talent Pipeline Initiative

**Analytical basis (Five Forces → Value Chain HRM):** The supplier power analysis and Value Chain HRM assessment both identify the workforce shortage as a structural constraint on production capacity. Retirements will create a projected gap of 3.5 million workers by 2026, increasing the pressure to hire in a competitive marketplace. As of 2023, roughly 29% of A&D employees were over 55, and more than 70,000 security-cleared positions were unfilled across the defence sector.

**Action:** (a) Establish multi-year apprenticeship and upskilling partnerships with community colleges and HBCUs in manufacturing hub states; (b) invest in digital manufacturing tools (robotic assembly, AI-assisted quality inspection) that reduce the labour content per unit; and (c) create a "cleared talent pipeline" programme that funds security clearance processing for STEM graduates.

**Warrant:** The workforce gap is a sector-wide constraint, but a firm that solves it faster than its competitors gains a relative production capacity advantage. Employee onboarding, development experiences, and skill gaps continue to drive engagement challenges and contribute to higher-than-average quit rates, especially within the touch-labour population. Since the F-35 delivery failures are partly attributable to assembly labour constraints and parts shortages, improving workforce availability and deploying labour-reducing digital tools is a direct lever on the core operational problem identified throughout the case.
