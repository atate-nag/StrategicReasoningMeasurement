## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Manchester United simultaneously holds the record for the highest revenue ever generated by a Premier League club and the ignominy of its worst-ever league finish in the same season. This paradox is not a coincidence. It is the logical outcome of two decades of strategic misalignment, compounded by a dual-principal governance structure following INEOS's minority acquisition in 2024. This case study applies PESTLE, Porter's Five Forces, SWOT, the Resource-Based View (VRIN), and the McKinsey 7-S framework to diagnose the situation. The analysis reveals a club whose commercial engine remains powerful but whose on-pitch performance has begun to erode the very foundation that sustains it. Recommendations centre on three interlocking imperatives: (1) resolving governance ambiguity between the Glazers and INEOS; (2) investing in a coherent, long-horizon football model; and (3) using the proposed new stadium as a platform for transformational rather than merely incremental commercial growth.

## 2. INTRODUCTION & CASE BACKGROUND

### 2.1 The Club and Its Legacy

Manchester United Football Club is, by almost any measure, the most commercially powerful football club in English history. Founded in 1878, the club won 13 Premier League titles under Sir Alex Ferguson between 1993 and 2013, building a global fanbase of over one billion people. Forbes 2024 estimates value the club at around $6.2 billion, reflecting sustained commercial strength. Yet since Ferguson's retirement in 2013, United have not won the Premier League, and have employed seven permanent managers in twelve years.

### 2.2 The Ownership Situation

The Glazer family completed a leveraged buyout of the club in 2005, and the brothers Avram and Joel Glazer act as executive co-chairmen, with the other Glazer siblings collectively holding around 97% of the voting power. The Glazers' takeover brought huge financial obligations, with £60 million repayments due every year, and on top of that was £200 million in other loans which were sold into hedge funds. The Glazers chose to use revenue generated by the club to service these debts. The debt at United has grown to over £730 million under the Glazers, and the club has paid over £750 million of interest on that debt over the past 20 years.

In February 2024, British billionaire Sir Jim Ratcliffe and his industrial conglomerate INEOS acquired a minority stake. Ratcliffe purchased an initial 27.7% stake at Old Trafford in February 2024 after agreeing a £1.25bn deal with the Glazers. Ratcliffe had invested a further £79.2 million by 31 December 2024, and his ownership is now at 28.9%. Critically, the club's football operation is now overseen by INEOS, but the Glazer family, who have owned United since 2005, remain in overall charge, which includes just about everything else. This dual-principal structure — Glazers owning, INEOS operating — defines the strategic context of the entire case.

### 2.3 The Immediate Situation: 2024/25

Manchester United finished 15th in the Premier League, their worst league placement in Premier League history, breaking their previous all-time low of eighth place set the season before. With nine wins, 11 draws, and a record 18 defeats, the Red Devils ended the campaign in 15th, ahead of 16th-placed Wolves on goal difference alone. United scored almost as many goals in 15 Europa League games (35) as they did in 38 Premier League outings (44). Yet paradoxically, the club achieved record revenues of £666.5m for 2024/25, with net losses of £33m down from £113m in 2024.

## 3. SITUATIONAL ANALYSIS

### 3.1 PESTLE Analysis

**Political**

The Chancellor of the Exchequer, Rachel Reeves, said in January 2025 that she supported the regeneration of the area around Old Trafford. The stadium will be part of a wider regeneration of the Old Trafford area, and United say the project could create as many as 92,000 new jobs. This political goodwill is a genuine asset, though it is contingent on the project delivering measurable public benefit. At the same time, the Football Governance Bill in Parliament proposes an Independent Football Regulator with powers over ownership structures and financial sustainability — a development that will impose new compliance obligations on United's dual-principal ownership model.

**Economic**

United's debt has reduced somewhat, by around £39m to £471.9m, but that reduction is because of a change in the exchange rate between the British pound and the US dollar, rather than any of the debt being specifically paid down. The leadership's approach is a paradox: cutting costs while failing to deliver results. The Glazer family's legacy of leveraging the club to the hilt — £1 billion in debt and £370m in five years of losses — has created a financial straitjacket. Compounding this, Manchester United said the club was at risk of breaking PSR/FFP requirements in future years, significantly impacting their ability to compete on the pitch. The Premier League's PSR rules permit a maximum of £105m in losses over a rolling three-year period — a hard constraint on the pace of squad rebuilding.

**Social**

On 9 March 2025, what was described as the first major protest since Ratcliffe's partial takeover saw thousands of fans protest against the direction of the club. The Manchester United Supporters Trust (MUST) has raised questions about financing the new stadium while expressing cautious excitement about the potential. The redundancy of over 250 staff, including long-serving academy coaches, has further damaged institutional morale and fan trust.

**Technological**

The club combines a sophisticated sponsorship engine that monetises reach, heritage, and cultural relevance. Manchester United reportedly signed a world-record shirt sponsorship deal, striking a £60 million-a-year agreement with Snapdragon. The club's digital revenue reached £61.5 million in the 2022/2023 financial year. The new stadium, designed by Foster + Partners, represents a once-in-a-generation opportunity to embed technology infrastructure — from fan experience to data monetisation — into the club's physical core.

**Legal**

Under the existing PSR regime, clubs' profit and loss position is assessed over a rolling three-year period, with maximum permitted losses of £105 million over each three-year period. UEFA's new Financial Sustainability Regulations apply a 70% squad cost ratio ceiling from 2025/26, meaning clubs cannot spend more than 70% of their revenue on player wages, transfers, and agent fees. By providing equity allowances, INEOS enabled United to stay above the PSR line, avoiding a points deduction in the 2024/25 campaign.

**Environmental**

The new stadium development will be subject to environmental impact assessments and sustainability requirements in a sensitive planning environment. Increasingly, major sponsors and commercial partners embed ESG criteria into partnership agreements, meaning environmental credentials are becoming commercially relevant — an area where United's ageing, underinvested Old Trafford currently underperforms relative to modern facilities.

### 3.2 Porter's Five Forces

Porter's Five Forces framework (1979) analyses industry-level competitive dynamics. Applied to elite European football, it reveals the structural pressures United faces.

**Threat of New Entrants — LOW**

The barriers to entry at the elite level of European football are extremely high: financial requirements, UEFA licensing, stadium standards, and academy infrastructure all represent formidable obstacles. The exception is state-backed "project clubs." Manchester United faces significant competitive pressure from clubs like Manchester City, Liverpool, and other top European teams. Manchester City (Abu Dhabi-backed), Newcastle United (Saudi Arabia-backed), and PSG (Qatar-backed) represent a form of structural disruption to the competitive order that the traditional barriers to entry do not prevent.

**Bargaining Power of Buyers (Fans & Broadcasters) — MODERATE**

Broadcast rights are negotiated collectively by the Premier League, limiting individual club leverage but providing a revenue floor. Fan bargaining power is individually low but collectively significant — as the March 2025 protests demonstrate. The rise of streaming and direct-to-consumer platforms means broadcast buyers are gaining relative power over time, making the current collective deal structure increasingly contested.

**Bargaining Power of Suppliers (Players & Agents) — HIGH**

There was a substantial reduction in wages, which dropped £52m (14%) from £365m to £313m, mainly due to lower performance bonuses and the impact of restructuring, though player amortisation continued to rise. Even after this reduction, the wage bill at £313.2m reflects the structural power of elite player labour. The club's failure to qualify for the Champions League for 2025/26 further weakens its negotiating position with top-tier transfer targets, as rival clubs can offer both superior wages and European football.

**Threat of Substitutes — LOW TO MODERATE**

Fan loyalty to football clubs is deeply embedded and largely inelastic. However, at the margin, entertainment competition from streaming services, gaming, and other sports represents a long-term threat to audience share, particularly among younger demographics. The key risk is not fans switching clubs, but fans reducing their overall engagement with football as a leisure category.

**Competitive Rivalry — HIGH**

This is the dominant force. Online sales represent 42% of total merchandise revenue in 2024, and the competitive landscape is characterised by significant financial investments, with total player transfer spending for Manchester United in 2023 at £216.7m. Yet this investment has not translated into competitive performance. United finished 15th below clubs such as Brentford, Fulham, and Bournemouth — clubs with a fraction of United's resources. The competitive gap between resource base and on-pitch output is the central strategic problem.

### 3.3 SWOT Analysis

**Strengths**

- *Record commercial revenue*: Net revenue growth was driven by new club highs in both matchday (up £23m, 17%, to £160m) and commercial (up £30m, 10%, to £333m), offset by a large decrease in broadcasting which fell £49m (22%). United have signed a world-record shirt sponsorship deal with Snapdragon worth approximately £60 million per year.

- *Global brand*: Manchester United has converted 146 years of history into a global marketing powerhouse since its 1878 founding, turning cultural relevance into commercial scale.

- *Academy heritage*: Beckham, Best, Charlton, Edwards, Giggs, Pogba, Rashford, and Mainoo are just some of the famous names to emerge from Manchester United's legendary Academy, spanning 4,000+ games and over 50 trophies.

- *Stadium opportunity*: The Old Trafford Regeneration Task Force determined that a new-build stadium would allow capacity to reach 100,000, and initial findings suggest the development could deliver an extra £7.3bn gross value added to the UK economy and more than 90,000 employment opportunities.

**Weaknesses**

- *Legacy debt*: Over half of Premier League debt is at just three clubs, with Manchester United carrying £547m — the lingering effects of the Glazers' leveraged buyout.

- *Governance ambiguity*: Ratcliffe warned United would have "gone bust at Christmas" if they had not taken "really tough decisions." This extraordinary admission from a minority shareholder signals the structural dysfunction of the dual-principal model.

- *On-pitch collapse*: United endured their worst season since the Premier League began, finishing 15th on 42 points — that points total being just half that of the champions.

- *Cultural damage*: Manchester United's decision to lay off several long-serving academy staff as part of Sir Jim Ratcliffe's cost-cutting measures marks a significant shift in the club's operational strategy. Notable figures such as Stuart Leicester and Neil Harris, who were instrumental in developing talents like Marcus Rashford and Axel Tuanzebe, were at risk.

**Opportunities**

- *New stadium*: The new stadium project, costing around £2 billion and designed by Foster + Partners, would have a proposed 100,000-seat capacity.

- *Amorim's system*: Ruben Amorim's approach in the summer transfer window was heavily focused on attacking talent, reacting to the club's problems last season where scoring was a decisive issue, with over £200 million spent primarily on offensive players.

- *Digital monetisation*: The Snapdragon partnership and growing digital revenue streams point to significant upside in content, data, and direct-to-fan monetisation aligned with technology partners.

- *New investor*: Manchester United could soon be looking for a new investor to join the club to meet their cash requirements.

**Threats**

- *Champions League exclusion*: Absence from European competition in 2025/26 reduces revenue, damages recruitment, and risks a negative spiral of worse results leading to worse recruitment.

- *PSR tightening*: The Premier League, from the 2026/27 season, will replace its existing PSR framework with a new system, representing one of the most significant changes to financial regulations in over a decade.

- *Talent drain*: Without Champions League football and a credible sporting project, United risk losing key assets to clubs offering European competition.

### 3.4 Resource-Based View (VRIN Framework)

The Resource-Based View (RBV), associated with Barney (1991) and Wernerfelt (1984), holds that sustainable competitive advantage derives from resources that are Valuable, Rare, Imperfectly Imitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN). A study specifically examining Manchester United found that playing resources, fans' goodwill, and coaching resources were the key drivers in creating sustainable competitive advantage, with fans' goodwill and coaching resources being particularly critical as they are more difficult to duplicate and are usually invisible assets.

This framework reveals a critical strategic problem: United's most durable VRIN resources are being eroded by its own strategic choices.

| Resource / Capability | V | R | I | N | Strategic Status |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Global brand & fanbase | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | **Eroding** — poor performance undermines brand premium |
| Commercial infrastructure (£333m revenue) | ✓ | Partial | Partial | Partial | **Stable** — but decoupling from results is a risk |
| Academy system & coaching tradition | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | **Under threat** — key coaches made redundant |
| Old Trafford heritage | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | **Degrading** — ageing, underinvested infrastructure |
| Ferguson-era culture & winning identity | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | **Lost** — seven managers in 12 years |
| Amorim's coaching capability | ✓ | ✓ | Partial | Partial | **Potential** — too early to assess |

The RBV analysis reveals a fundamental misalignment: the club's most commercially valuable asset — its global brand — is built on a foundation of on-pitch success and cultural identity. The key resources are difficult to duplicate and are usually invisible assets, thus critical to firms wishing to sustain their competitive advantage. Yet decisions taken since 2013 have systematically hollowed out the football-side resources (coaching stability, academy investment, squad coherence) that sustain that brand. The commercial engine is currently running on the fumes of the Ferguson era.

### 3.5 McKinsey 7-S Framework

The McKinsey 7-S Framework (Waterman, Peters & Phillips, 1980) examines organisational effectiveness through seven interdependent elements. It is particularly useful for diagnosing why an organisation with significant resources is underperforming — the answer typically lies in misalignment between the elements.

**Strategy**: United's stated strategy is to have the world's best football team playing in the world's best stadium. This is aspirational but operationally vague. The arrival of Rúben Amorim at Manchester United in the middle of the 2024/2025 campaign was met with cautious optimism, yet the reality of his early tenure underscored the magnitude of the task he had inherited. There is no publicly articulated playing philosophy, recruitment profile, or pathway to Champions League return. The strategy is currently reactive (cost-cutting, manager changes) rather than proactive.

**Structure**: The dual-principal ownership model creates structural ambiguity. According to media reports, INEOS completed its $300m equity commitment during 2024, increasing its stake to 28.94%. While this strengthens liquidity and signals ownership backing, financial flexibility remains influenced by operating performance. INEOS controls football operations but does not control the capital allocation decisions that fund those operations. This is a structurally dysfunctional arrangement.

**Systems**: Manchester United's Financial Results for 2024/25 show that the club executed a net drawdown of £130 million from its syndicated Revolving Credit Facility, as the injection of £80 million in equity proceeds from INEOS was mathematically insufficient to cover the capital shortfall. Financial management systems have been reactive and crisis-driven. Amorim's transfer approach this summer was heavily focused on attacking talent, reacting to the club's problems last season where scoring was a decisive issue, with over £200 million spent primarily on offensive players to provide options — though midfield reinforcements remain pending.

**Shared Values**: The "Manchester United way" — attacking football, youth development, winning — is invoked rhetorically but has been operationally absent. Manchester United's academy shake-up saw long-serving staff face redundancies under INEOS's regime, marking a significant shift in the club's operational strategy. The redundancy of coaches who built the academy pipeline signals that the club's commitment to its own developmental tradition is conditional on short-term financial targets.

**Style**: The billionaire has pushed forward a series of decisions to reduce the club's cost base, including making around 250 employees redundant last year with plans to further reduce staffing levels, reportedly by another 100–200. This corporate efficiency logic is at odds with the emotional, community-oriented culture of a football club.

**Staff**: More than 250 members of staff at Old Trafford were made redundant in the first round of cuts, with employee expenses falling 14.1%, or £51.5m, to £313.2m, achieved by high-earning players leaving and the impact of restructuring. This represents a significant loss of institutional knowledge alongside the playing squad transition.

**Skills**: The club's core footballing skills — player development, tactical coherence, squad building — have atrophied. The commercial skills (sponsorship, brand management, digital monetisation) remain strong. United's issues go beyond just a system — they have systemic issues, but not formation ones. The mismatch between commercial and footballing skill sets is the most visible symptom of the strategic disconnect.

**7-S Diagnosis**: The analysis reveals that United's "hard" elements (Strategy, Structure, Systems) are misaligned with each other and with the "soft" elements (Shared Values, Style, Staff, Skills). The financial restructuring strategy is in direct tension with the cultural and footballing values that underpin the club's commercial power. Until this misalignment is resolved, incremental improvements will not produce sustainable competitive recovery.

## 4. THE CORE STRATEGIC PROBLEM: A COHERENT CHAIN OF CAUSATION

The five frameworks above converge on a single, coherent diagnosis. The chain of causation runs as follows:

```
[1] Glazer Leveraged Buyout (2005)
         ↓
[2] Chronic Underinvestment in Football Infrastructure
         ↓
[3] On-Pitch Decline (15th in 2024/25, no Champions League)
         ↓
[4] Revenue-Performance Decoupling (record £666.5m revenue, record-low league finish)
         ↓
[5] PSR Constraint & Financial Fragility (close to breach, emergency equity injections)
         ↓
[6] Governance Ambiguity (Glazers own, INEOS operates — conflicting incentives)
         ↓
[7] Cultural Erosion (academy redundancies, managerial instability, fan alienation)
         ↓
[8] Risk: Brand Erosion → Commercial Revenue Decline → Vicious Cycle
```

Each step in this chain is evidenced:

**Step 1→2**: The debt at United has grown to over £730 million under the Glazers, and the club has paid over £750 million of interest on that debt over the past 20 years. This diverted capital that could otherwise have funded football infrastructure.

**Step 2→3**: Manchester United finished 15th in the Premier League, their worst league placement in Premier League history, breaking their previous all-time low of eighth place set the season before. Seven permanent managers in twelve years is the structural indicator of football-side underinvestment.

**Step 3→4**: The net revenue growth was driven by new club highs in both matchday and commercial revenue, but this was offset by a large decrease in broadcasting, which fell £49m (22%). The broadcasting decline directly reflects on-pitch failure; the commercial resilience reflects brand equity accumulated during the Ferguson era.

**Step 4→5**: Manchester United stated the club was at risk of breaking PSR/FFP requirements in future years, significantly impacting their ability to compete on the pitch.

**Step 5→6**: While INEOS's equity commitment strengthens liquidity and signals ownership backing, financial flexibility remains influenced by operating performance — creating a structural dependency where INEOS must keep injecting capital to fund operations it controls but does not own.

**Step 6→7**: Manchester United's decision to lay off several long-serving academy staff marks a significant shift in the club's operational strategy, with notable figures instrumental in developing Marcus Rashford and others at risk.

**Step 7→8**: The brand that generates £666.5m in revenue is built on a legacy of on-pitch success and cultural authenticity. Playing resources, fans' goodwill, and coaching resources are the key drivers in creating sustainable competitive advantage — and these are more difficult to duplicate and are usually invisible assets. Eroding these invisible assets today will not appear in the revenue line immediately — but it will eventually.

The strategic problem is therefore not primarily financial, and not primarily tactical. It is a **governance and alignment problem**: the club's ownership structure, financial model, footballing model, and cultural identity are pulling in different directions. Revenue without results is the symptom; misalignment is the disease.

## 5. STRATEGIC OPTIONS & EVALUATION

### Option A: Accelerated Cost Optimisation (Current Trajectory)
Continue the INEOS-led restructuring: further staff reductions, wage bill compression, asset sales through player trading, and commercial revenue maximisation.

**Evaluation**: This option stabilises the balance sheet in the short term. Total operating expenses were down 4.5 per cent, and employee expenses by 14.1 per cent, or £51.5m, to £313.2m. However, this approach does not address the root cause of on-pitch underperformance. Without Champions League football, commercial revenue will come under sustained pressure. The 7-S analysis shows this approach deepens the misalignment between financial strategy and footballing values. **Verdict: Necessary but insufficient. Risk of vicious cycle of decline.**

### Option B: Full Ownership Change
Attract a major new investor or facilitate a full Glazer exit, bringing in ownership with both capital and sporting ambition.

**Evaluation**: This is the highest-upside option but faces significant obstacles. Manchester United could soon be looking for a new investor to join the club to meet their cash requirements, according to sources. However, a full sale at current valuations ($6.2bn) requires a buyer of extraordinary resources, and the Glazers have shown no inclination to sell at a discount. The incoming IFR may impose conditions on new owners. **Verdict: Desirable but uncertain. Should be pursued in parallel with other options.**

### Option C: Long-Horizon Football Model Investment
Commit to a 5-year plan centred on Amorim's system and philosophy, a clear recruitment profile, reinvestment in the academy, and the new stadium as a commercial transformation catalyst.

**Evaluation**: This is the most strategically coherent option. It directly addresses the causal chain identified above by rebuilding the VRIN resources (academy, coaching identity, cultural coherence) that underpin long-term competitive advantage. United's issues go beyond just a system — they have systemic issues that require systemic solutions. **Verdict: Most coherent long-term path, but requires governance stability the current structure does not guarantee.**

### Option D (Recommended): Governance Resolution + Focused Football Investment
Resolve governance ambiguity — through a new investor, a revised shareholder agreement, or a phased Glazer exit — while simultaneously committing to Option C's football model. This addresses both the structural root cause and the operational manifestation.

## 6. RECOMMENDATIONS & ACTION PLAN

The following five recommendations are derived directly from the analytical frameworks and are sequenced to address root causes in the causal chain.

---

### Recommendation 1: Resolve the Governance Structure *(Priority: Immediate; Timeline: 0–18 months)*

**Rationale**: The McKinsey 7-S analysis identifies structural misalignment as the primary organisational dysfunction. The club's football operation is overseen by INEOS, but the Glazer family remain in overall charge, which includes just about everything else. This creates conflicting incentives and prevents coherent long-term planning.

**Actions**:
- Negotiate a revised shareholder agreement giving INEOS clear authority over capital allocation for football operations, not merely operational control.
- Actively pursue a new investor to join the club to meet cash requirements — this would inject equity, reduce Glazer leverage, and provide a pathway to a cleaner ownership structure.
- Engage proactively with the incoming Independent Football Regulator to demonstrate governance reform.

**KPI**: Reduction in governance-related decision delays; clarity in public communications about capital allocation authority; debt-to-revenue ratio reduced below 50% within 3 years.

---

### Recommendation 2: Establish and Commit to a Defined Football Model *(Priority: High; Timeline: 6–24 months)*

**Rationale**: The RBV analysis shows that United's most durable competitive advantages — academy heritage, coaching identity, cultural coherence — have been eroded by the absence of a stable football philosophy. Seven managers in twelve years is not a strategy; it is the absence of one.

**Actions**:
- Publicly commit to Amorim's 3-4-2-1 system and associated player profile for a minimum of three seasons, insulating the manager from short-term results pressure. The transfer strategy must focus on buying the right players — talented but also able to deal with the pressure, who can take the squad forward — not just signing elite talent.
- Reinstate and reinvest in the academy coaching staff. Long-serving staff who were instrumental in developing talents like Marcus Rashford and Axel Tuanzebe were made redundant — their institutional knowledge is irreplaceable and the cost of replacing academy-developed talent in the transfer market far exceeds the savings from redundancies.
- Define a clear playing identity in all public communications — specific principles (pressing intensity, positional structure, youth integration) that create accountability.

**KPI**: Top-6 Premier League finish within 3 seasons; academy-to-first-team pipeline producing at least 2 senior squad members per season; Champions League return within 3 years.

---

### Recommendation 3: Manage Financial Constraints Strategically, Not Reactively *(Priority: High; Timeline: Immediate–36 months)*

**Rationale**: The PESTLE and Porter analyses show that PSR constraints and legacy debt are structural, not temporary. Reactive cost-cutting addresses symptoms without addressing causes.

**Actions**:
- Adopt a player trading strategy as a deliberate revenue source. Clubs such as Brighton and Brentford have demonstrated that smart recruitment and development can generate significant transfer profits that offset PSR losses. United's academy pipeline, if properly resourced, is a natural vehicle for this.
- Prioritise contract extensions for key VRIN assets — Kobbie Mainoo, Bruno Fernandes — before Champions League absence makes retention harder. The cost of losing these players far exceeds the cost of extended contracts.
- Negotiate the stadium financing structure carefully, with the Manchester United Supporters Trust having raised questions about financing the new stadium. A diversified financing model — including naming rights, commercial partnerships, and government infrastructure support for surrounding regeneration — is preferable to a Personal Seat Licence model that risks significant fan backlash.

**KPI**: PSR compliance maintained; net transfer profit or breakeven within 3 years; debt reduced through operational improvement rather than currency movements.

---

### Recommendation 4: Leverage the New Stadium as a Strategic Transformation Platform *(Priority: Medium; Timeline: 18 months–10 years)*

**Rationale**: The new-build stadium would allow capacity to reach 100,000, and initial findings suggest the development could deliver an extra £7.3bn gross value added to the UK economy and more than 90,000 employment opportunities. Tottenham's new stadium demonstrates that purpose-built modern infrastructure can add £30–50m per year in matchday and commercial revenue.

**Actions**:
- Position the new stadium as a multi-use destination (concerts, NFL games, conferences, retail) generating year-round revenue independent of football results — mirroring the Tottenham model.
- Integrate technology infrastructure from the ground up: data monetisation, premium digital experiences, and direct-to-fan content platforms aligned with the Snapdragon partnership.
- Engage fans genuinely in the design and naming process. The emotional connection to Old Trafford is a VRIN asset; alienating fans through a top-down, commercially-driven transition would be strategically self-defeating.
- Ensure the stadium plan is contingent on a credible football recovery plan. Manchester United's chief executive Omar Berrada stated: "Our long-term objective as a club is to have the world's best football team playing in the world's best stadium." These two objectives must be developed in parallel, not sequentially.

**KPI**: Planning permission secured within 24 months; construction commenced within 36 months; stadium revenue exceeding £200m per year at full operation.

---

### Recommendation 5: Rebuild Cultural and Institutional Identity *(Priority: Ongoing)*

**Rationale**: The 7-S analysis shows that "Shared Values" is the most misaligned element in United's current organisational configuration. The Ferguson-era values — attacking football, youth development, community connection, winning mentality — are the foundation of the brand that generates £666.5m in revenue. Fans' goodwill is a key driver of competitive advantage and is more difficult to duplicate — it is a critical invisible asset.

**Actions**:
- Reinstate the principle of youth integration as a non-negotiable club value, not a marketing message — giving academy players meaningful first-team opportunities even at short-term performance cost.
- Repair the relationship with fans through transparent communication. A major protest in March 2025 — described as the first significant demonstration since Ratcliffe's partial takeover — saw thousands of fans march against the direction of the club. A structured fan engagement programme — including meaningful consultation on the stadium project and ticket pricing — would begin to rebuild trust.
- Invest in club heritage and storytelling as a commercial and cultural asset. The stories of Best, Law, Charlton, Cantona, Giggs, Scholes, and Ronaldo are differentiating brand assets in a globalised commercial landscape that no competitor can replicate.

**KPI**: Fan satisfaction index improvement (measured via independent survey); increased season ticket renewal rates; reduction in protest activity.

## 7. CONCLUSION

Manchester United's strategic situation in 2024/25 is best characterised as a **crisis of alignment, not a crisis of resources**. The club achieved record revenues of £666.5m, yet made an overall loss of £33m and finished 15th in the Premier League. This paradox is not a coincidence; it is the logical outcome of a causal chain that begins with the Glazer leveraged buyout of 2005 and runs through governance ambiguity, financial fragility, on-pitch decline, and cultural erosion.

The five analytical frameworks applied in this case study — PESTLE, Porter's Five Forces, SWOT, VRIN, and McKinsey 7-S — converge on a single diagnosis: the club's "hard" elements (Strategy, Structure, Systems) are misaligned with its "soft" elements (Shared Values, Style, Staff, Skills), and its ownership structure creates incentives that are fundamentally at odds with long-term sporting success. The leadership's approach is a paradox: cutting costs while failing to deliver results.

The recommended strategic path — governance resolution, a committed long-horizon football model, strategic financial management, stadium transformation, and cultural renewal — is demanding. It requires patience from owners who have historically shown little of it, investment from shareholders whose primary interest has been extraction, and cultural humility from a leadership team that has approached a football club with a corporate restructuring mindset. But the alternative — continuing the current trajectory — risks the irreversible erosion of the brand assets that make Manchester United commercially exceptional.

Fans' goodwill and coaching resources are the key drivers of sustainable competitive advantage, and they are more difficult to duplicate — they are invisible assets critical to firms wishing to sustain their competitive advantage. The commercial engine is currently running on the fumes of the Ferguson era. Those fumes will not last indefinitely. The window for strategic recovery is open — but it is not unlimited.
