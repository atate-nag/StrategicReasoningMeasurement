# STRATEGIC CASE STUDY: POLESTAR AUTOMOTIVE
## *"Caught Between Stars: Resolving the Identity, Scale, and Profitability Trilemma"*

---

## 1. INTRODUCTION & BACKGROUND

Polestar Automotive Holding UK PLC (Nasdaq: PSNY) is a Swedish electric vehicle (EV) performance brand headquartered in Gothenburg, Sweden. Originally founded as Volvo's high-performance tuning division in 1996, it was acquired as a joint venture by Volvo Cars and its Chinese parent, Geely Holding Group, in 2015, and relaunched in 2017 as a standalone EV brand. Polestar is a design-focused electric vehicle (EV) performance brand that aims to accelerate a climate-neutral and sustainable future for transport, acquired by both Volvo and its parent company Geely in 2015.

The company leverages its association with Volvo, a brand known for safety and quality, while simultaneously forging its own identity through design, sustainability, and a direct-to-consumer sales model. Its primary product lines include the Polestar 2, a compact executive EV, and the Polestar 3, an electric SUV. The Polestar 4 SUV coupé entered production in 2024.

By 2025, Polestar delivered over 60,000 vehicles to customers, a 34% increase from 2024, and revenue surpassing USD 3 billion. Yet the company simultaneously posted a net loss of $2.36 billion in 2025, widened 15% from $2.03 billion in 2024. Based on the 60,119 vehicles the company sold last year, this results in an average loss of $39,200 per vehicle. This paradox — growing sales alongside widening absolute losses — is the central strategic challenge this case study addresses.

---

## 2. THE STRATEGIC SITUATION

Polestar faces what can be described as a **trilemma**: it must simultaneously resolve questions of *identity* (who is it for, and how is it different?), *scale* (can it grow fast enough to achieve unit economics viability?), and *profitability* (can it survive long enough to get there?).

Key facts establishing the situation:

- Polestar's adjusted gross margin turned positive at 1.9% in Q4 2025 — an improvement of 40.9 percentage points from negative 39.0% in the comparable period — marking the first quarter of adjusted gross profit since the company's public debut.
- Polestar Automotive Holding UK Plc took a $739 million writedown on the Polestar 3 due to higher US tariffs and disappointing demand for electric cars. The non-cash impairment charge contributed to a wider second-quarter net loss of $1.03 billion.
- The latest Nasdaq deficiency notice, issued October 31, 2025, confirms a renewed period of non-compliance, with the stock reportedly trading around $0.26. This persistent struggle highlights deeper underlying issues beyond mere stock price fluctuations.
- Polestar closed its final China store in Shanghai as sales collapsed. Earlier in 2025, Polestar unveiled its Polestar 5 grand tourer in Munich, with plans to skip initial sales in China and the US, reinforcing a focus on Europe amid tariff and market headwinds.

## 3. EXTERNAL ENVIRONMENT ANALYSIS

### 3.1 PESTEL Analysis

**Political**

The geopolitical environment has become one of Polestar's most acute external threats. The US imposed a 100% tariff on Chinese-made EVs in 2024, followed by a 25% blanket tariff on all imported vehicles in 2025. These policies directly targeted Polestar's manufacturing footprint. The US tariffs on Chinese goods surged to 145% in April 2025, the culmination of a years-long trade war. The levies apply to nearly all Chinese-manufactured products, including Polestar's EVs, which are currently produced in China and South Korea. The tariffs have created a "financial and operational minefield" for companies reliant on Asian supply chains.

**Economic**

The company experienced a 15% decline in global EV sales in 2024, delivering just 44,851 units worldwide. The premium EV segment, where Polestar competes, is particularly exposed to interest rate sensitivity, as high financing costs suppress lease and purchase decisions for vehicles priced between $50,000 and $90,000. Garrett Nelson, senior equity analyst at CFRA Research, commented: "We think their big problem is going to be EV demand in the absence of incentives combined with liquidity."

**Social**

Consumer environmental consciousness is driving market growth, with 73% of global consumers willing to pay a premium for sustainable products. This is a structural tailwind for Polestar's sustainability-led positioning. However, the adjusted gross margin was partially offset by pressure on pricing and higher tariffs, as well as lower adjustment of residual value guarantees in North American markets — indicating that resale value concerns are materialising financially.

**Technological**

Polestar uses batteries from multiple suppliers — including CATL (China), LG Energy Solution (South Korea), and Northvolt (Sweden) — depending on vehicle model, region, and battery chemistry. Software-defined vehicle architecture is becoming a key differentiator; Polestar's Android Automotive OS integration with Google is a meaningful asset, but rivals are closing the gap.

**Environmental**

Environmental sustainability remains central to Polestar's mission, with the company setting aggressive targets to halve per-vehicle greenhouse gas emissions by 2030. Carbon credit sales of $123 million were achieved in the first nine months of 2025, facilitated by new EU pooling agreements and sales in the US, contributing positively to profitability.

**Legal**

Polestar's repeated delays in filing annual reports triggered Nasdaq deficiency notices and raised governance concerns. While the company's CFO confirmed regaining reporting compliance in mid-August 2024, the confluence of these issues paints a picture of operational and financial instability that has undoubtedly contributed to significant stock volatility.

### 3.2 Porter's Five Forces Analysis

**1. Competitive Rivalry — HIGH**

The premium EV segment is intensely contested. In 2024, Tesla still leads the US EV market with 50% share. BMW, Mercedes-Benz, Audi, and Porsche have all launched credible electric alternatives backed by decades of brand equity and deep R&D budgets. Critically, Geely Holding controls one of the broadest premium-to-luxury portfolios in the global auto industry: Zeekr, Lynk & Co, Polestar, Lotus, Volvo Cars, Smart, and the Geely Galaxy sub-brand — most of which share the SEA (Sustainable Experience Architecture) platform. This intra-group rivalry dilutes Polestar's differentiation and creates platform commoditisation risk.

**2. Threat of New Entrants — MODERATE**

Capital barriers to EV manufacturing are high. Polestar's challenges reflect broader difficulties faced by EV startups, which are encountering weakening demand amid the absence of consumer incentives. Several competitors, including Fisker, Lordstown, and Arrival, have filed for bankruptcy. The threat is moderated by Polestar's established brand, existing manufacturing infrastructure, and regulatory type-approval complexity.

**3. Supplier Power — HIGH**

Polestar's dependence on international production and supply chains adds "complexity to its operations" and raises "doubts about long-term survival of the business," according to Bank of America analyst Daniel Roeska. Polestar vehicles are sold and serviced through Volvo's extensive dealer network, giving the brand physical presence at scale without the capital expenditure of building its own retail footprint from scratch. However, while Volvo has been reducing its stake, Geely has been increasing its investment, despite significant paper losses.

**4. Buyer Power — MODERATE TO HIGH**

Premium EV buyers are well-informed and have growing alternatives. Polestar's direct-to-consumer model means the company absorbs all customer acquisition costs directly. Polestar has implemented targeted discount campaigns to attract new customers, including offering up to $20,000 off the lease of a new 2025 model for Tesla owners in the United States. This tactical discounting signals elevated buyer power.

**5. Threat of Substitutes — MODERATE**

ICE vehicles and hybrid alternatives remain widely available and, in many markets, cheaper. The substitute threat is declining structurally as EV infrastructure improves and regulatory ICE phase-out dates approach, but it remains material in the near term.

## 4. INTERNAL ANALYSIS

### 4.1 VRIO Framework

The VRIO framework (Barney, 1991) evaluates resources and capabilities across four dimensions: **V**aluable, **R**are, **I**nimitable, and **O**rganised to capture value. The VRIO framework provides a four-part test for identifying resources and capabilities that sustain competitive advantage. Resources meeting all four criteria generate sustained competitive advantage; those meeting fewer criteria yield only temporary advantage or competitive parity.

| Resource / Capability | V | R | I | O | Competitive Implication |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Scandinavian design identity | ✓ | ✓ | Partial | ✓ | Temporary advantage |
| Sustainability & blockchain traceability | ✓ | ✓ | Partial | ✓ | Temporary advantage |
| Google/Android Automotive OS integration | ✓ | ✗ | ✗ | ✓ | Competitive parity |
| Geely/SEA platform access | ✓ | ✗ | ✗ | Partial | Competitive parity |
| Volvo distribution & manufacturing | ✓ | ✗ | ✗ | Partial | Dependency, not advantage |
| Direct-to-consumer sales model | ✓ | ✗ | ✗ | ✓ | Competitive parity |
| Carbon credit monetisation | ✓ | Partial | ✗ | ✓ | Temporary advantage |
| Brand equity in premium EV niche | ✓ | Partial | Partial | Partial | Temporary advantage |

CEO Lohscheller stated that Scandinavian minimalist design and the brand's sustainability framing remain the two differentiators that allow Polestar to coexist with Volvo Cars and Zeekr under the Geely umbrella. However, the VRIO analysis shows these are not yet fully inimitable — Polestar has **no resource meeting all four VRIO criteria**, meaning it has no *sustained* competitive advantage in the Barney (1991) sense. Its advantage is contingent on execution speed.

### 4.2 Value Chain Analysis

**Primary Activities:**

- *Operations:* Manufacturing is outsourced — to Volvo's Chengdu plant (China), a shared Ridgeville, SC plant (with Volvo), and Renault's Busan plant (South Korea for Polestar 4). To avoid tariffs, Polestar is reconfiguring its manufacturing footprint: the Polestar 3 is now built in South Carolina, avoiding tariffs and qualifying for US EV tax credits. This asset-light model reduces capex but limits operational control and scale economies.
- *Outbound Logistics:* In 2025, Polestar opened 71 new retail sales points, with a total of 211 sales points at the end of 2025. During 2025, Polestar signed up 54 new retailers, representing an increase of 52%.
- *Service:* Reliance on Volvo service centres is efficient but creates brand blurring.

**Support Activities:**

- *Firm Infrastructure:* Governance weaknesses evidenced by repeated financial reporting delays; Polestar announced a leadership change, with Michael Lohscheller, a seasoned automotive leader with extensive experience in driving operational excellence, appointed CEO in August 2024. Previously, Michael served as the CEO of Nikola Motor Company, VinFast and Opel, and before that he was CFO of Opel, Volkswagen and Mitsubishi.
- *Technology Development:* Polestar uses blockchain technology and supplier audits to ensure ethical sourcing of materials and parts, tracing cobalt, mica, nickel and lithium back to their origin as the first step towards achieving more responsible sourcing.
- *Procurement:* Northvolt's financial difficulties expose supply chain vulnerability at the European battery supply node.

**Value Chain Conclusion:** Polestar's value chain is characterised by strategic dependence at nearly every stage. The company adds value primarily through design curation and brand management — a "hollow firm" risk where these activities alone are insufficient to justify its cost structure.

## 6. STRATEGIC PROBLEM DEFINITION

The SWOT and framework analyses converge on a single, coherent strategic problem:

> **Polestar has a compelling brand identity and genuine sustainability credentials, but its value chain is too dependent, its scale too small, and its product portfolio too narrow to generate the unit economics necessary for survival as an independent premium EV brand. The window to establish a self-reinforcing competitive position is closing, as rivals scale faster and geopolitical forces restructure its cost base.**

This problem has three interlocking dimensions:

1. **The Identity Problem:** Polestar is simultaneously Swedish, Chinese-owned, Volvo-adjacent, and performance-oriented — a combination that creates brand confusion rather than clarity. Its VRIO analysis reveals no sustained competitive advantage, meaning its differentiation is not yet durable.

2. **The Scale Problem:** At 60,000 units annually, Polestar operates far below minimum efficient scale for a premium OEM. BMW sells approximately 2.5 million vehicles per year; even niche premium brands like Porsche sell over 300,000. Polestar's losses of ~$39,200 per vehicle are a direct function of fixed cost dilution at low volumes. The company faces a disadvantage in terms of economies of scale because its production levels remain lower than those of major electric vehicle manufacturers.

3. **The Profitability Problem:** The path to profitability depends on volume growth, product mix improvement, and fixed cost reduction — but each requires capital that is difficult to access given the Nasdaq position and investor confidence issues.

These three problems form a **mutually reinforcing negative feedback loop**: low scale → high unit losses → poor investor confidence → limited capital → constrained product investment → weak brand differentiation → limited scale. Breaking this loop is the strategic imperative.

## 7. STRATEGIC OPTIONS: ANSOFF MATRIX

The Ansoff Matrix (1957) evaluates growth options across two dimensions: products (existing vs. new) and markets (existing vs. new).

**Option A — Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets)**
Deepen share in current markets (Europe, North America) with existing models through expanded retail presence and targeted conquest campaigns. Polestar sales in Europe were up 76% year-on-year, from around 7,000 in Q1 2024 to around 12,000 in Q1 2025. The UK alone accounted for 25% of Polestar's Q1 2025 sales. Risk: low-to-moderate, but margin compression is a real danger if growth is purchased through discounting.

**Option B — Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets)**
Enter new geographic markets. Polestar expanded to 28 global markets and increased sales points to 192, with sales points outside China surging 54% to 191, reflecting a strategic pivot toward direct retail expansion in Europe and North America. China re-entry would face intense domestic competition from BYD, NIO, and Zeekr. Risk: moderate.

**Option C — Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets)**
Expand the portfolio with new models targeting higher-margin and higher-volume segments. Polestar announced the largest model offensive in its history, with four new cars planned in the next three years. Polestar plans to bring to market: Polestar 5 — the four-door Grand Tourer presented in 2025, with deliveries expected from summer 2026; Polestar 7 — a premium compact SUV from Europe; a Polestar 4 new variant in Q4 2026; and a completely redesigned next-generation Polestar 2 planned for early 2027. Risk: high, but evidenced by Polestar 4 success in driving Q4 2025 margin improvement.

**Option D — Diversification (New Products, New Markets)**
Enter adjacent mobility businesses (energy storage, software licensing). Risk: very high (Ansoff's highest-risk quadrant). Polestar lacks the resources and management bandwidth for meaningful diversification at this stage. Not recommended as primary strategy.

## 8. EVALUATION OF OPTIONS: SAFe CRITERIA

Johnson, Scholes & Whittington's SAFe criteria evaluate strategic options against three tests: **Suitability** (does it address the strategic problem?), **Acceptability** (is it acceptable to stakeholders?), and **Feasibility** (can it be implemented with available resources?).

| Option | Suitability | Acceptability | Feasibility | Overall |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| A: Market Penetration | Partially — addresses scale but not identity or margin | High — low risk, incremental | High — within current capabilities | ★★★ |
| B: Market Development | Partially — addresses scale but not margin or identity | Moderate — China re-entry politically complex | Moderate — requires new infrastructure | ★★ |
| C: Product Development | High — addresses all three trilemma dimensions | Moderate — requires capital; investors cautious | Moderate — requires execution discipline | ★★★★ |
| D: Diversification | Low — does not address core problem | Low — investors want focus, not distraction | Low — lacks capabilities | ★ |

**Conclusion:** No single option resolves the trilemma. The recommended strategy combines **A and C**: aggressive market penetration in the near term (to generate volume, cash, and brand visibility) with disciplined product development in the medium term (to improve mix, margins, and differentiation). This is consistent with Polestar's stated strategy, but requires sharper prioritisation and execution discipline than has been demonstrated to date.

## 9. RECOMMENDATIONS & ACTION PLAN

The five recommendations below are structured across three time horizons and designed to address each dimension of the strategic trilemma in sequence, forming a coherent chain.

---

### Recommendation 1: Stabilise the Financial Position *(Immediate: 0–12 months)*

**Problem addressed:** Profitability dimension; Nasdaq compliance risk.

Prioritise adjusted gross margin over volume growth in the immediate term. The adjusted gross margin turned positive at 1.9% in Q4 2025, an improvement of 40.9 percentage points from negative 39.0% in the comparable period, mainly due to favourable product and margin mix, especially due to Polestar 4 sales, higher carbon credits sales, and lower adjustment of residual value guarantees. This milestone is genuine but fragile. Polestar must resist the temptation to sacrifice margin for volume via discounting.

Maintain carbon credit revenue through active EU pooling agreement management. Polestar achieved its target of three-digit million-dollar carbon credits sales ahead of plan, at $123 million for the first nine months of 2025. This is a legitimate near-term profitability lever.

Polestar plans to reduce operating expenditure by a planned 20%, to coincide with the largest model offensive in its history. This cost discipline must be maintained while protecting R&D investment.

Execute the ADS ratio change (1:30) as implemented in December 2025: following the change in the ratio of American Depositary Shares to ordinary shares from 1:1 to 1:30, effective 9 December 2025, Polestar received notice from Nasdaq that it had regained compliance with Nasdaq's Listing Rule 5450(a)(1).

**Rationale:** Without financial stabilisation, Polestar cannot fund its product offensive. The negative feedback loop must be interrupted at the profitability node first.

---

### Recommendation 2: Sharpen and Defend the Brand Identity *(Immediate to Medium-term: 0–18 months)*

**Problem addressed:** Identity dimension; VRIO gap (no sustained competitive advantage).

Formally separate the Polestar brand experience from Volvo in customer-facing touchpoints. CEO Lohscheller has stated that Scandinavian minimalist design and the brand's sustainability framing remain the two differentiators that allow Polestar to coexist with Volvo Cars and Zeekr. This clarity must be operationalised across all touchpoints — dedicated Polestar service zones, distinct branding, and separate customer communications.

Invest in brand-building activations that reinforce this identity: the Polestar 5 is a playable vehicle in Gran Turismo 7 for the PlayStation 4 and 5, as of December 2025. The Gran Turismo franchise has surpassed 100 million sales, and its popularity among gamers and car enthusiasts can only help Polestar as it plans its product offensive.

Publish annual Life Cycle Assessment reports with full transparency, reinforcing the "No Greenwashing" brand promise. Polestar uses blockchain technology and supplier audits to ensure ethical sourcing of materials and parts, tracing cobalt, mica, nickel and lithium to their origin. This transparency is a reputational moat that rivals cannot easily replicate.

**Rationale:** Porter's (1980) differentiation strategy requires consistent, credible, and hard-to-imitate brand signals. The VRIO analysis shows Polestar's design and sustainability credentials are valuable and partially rare, but not yet inimitable. Brand investment is the mechanism by which temporary advantage becomes sustained advantage.

---

### Recommendation 3: Execute the Product Offensive with Disciplined Prioritisation *(Medium-term: 12–36 months)*

**Problem addressed:** Scale and identity dimensions; Ansoff Product Development option.

**Prioritise the Polestar 7** (premium compact SUV, manufactured in Europe) as the volume driver. Polestar plans to mitigate tariff risks by producing future models like the Polestar 7 in Europe, as part of a localised production strategy aimed at reducing dependence on Chinese manufacturing and qualifying for EV incentives. The compact SUV segment is the single largest EV category globally; a European-manufactured model avoids tariff exposure.

**Protect the Polestar 5** as the margin and brand halo driver. The Polestar 5 is a high-performance electric grand tourer inspired by the Precept concept, aimed at taking on Porsche Taycan and Audi e-tron GT. Limit discounting on this model to protect its premium positioning.

**Apply rigorous demand forecasting discipline** to avoid a repeat of the Polestar 3 impairment. The $739 million impairment on the Polestar 3 was attributed to weaker-than-expected performance of the model, with management identifying a decline in projected demand driven by US tariffs and EV price pressure — factors that were knowable in advance. Scenario planning with explicit tariff and demand downside cases must be embedded in all product investment decisions.

**Rationale:** The Ansoff analysis shows product development is the highest-suitability option but also the highest risk. The risk is manageable with demand discipline. The Polestar 4's contribution to the Q4 2025 adjusted gross margin improvement demonstrates that the product development capability exists — it requires sequencing and prioritisation, not abandonment.

### Recommendation 4: Reduce Strategic Dependency on Geely and Volvo *(Medium to Long-term: 24–48 months)*

**Problem addressed:** VRIO gap; value chain hollow-firm risk; supplier power.

**Diversify manufacturing geography** away from China. Polestar is preparing to shift production of cars it plans to sell into Europe to its US plant from China in view of rising geopolitical tension. This geographic diversification must be formalised as a strategic imperative, not merely a tariff response.

**Renegotiate the Volvo relationship** with explicit governance boundaries. Volvo is reducing its Polestar stake; this creates an opportunity to renegotiate the partnership on more arm's-length terms, reducing brand blurring while retaining manufacturing and service network access.

**Develop proprietary software capabilities.** The Android Automotive OS integration is a strength today but creates platform dependency on Google. Polestar should invest in proprietary software layers — particularly the sustainability data platform (blockchain traceability) and the customer digital experience — that are genuinely owned and differentiated.

**Rationale:** The resource-based view (Barney, 1991) is explicit: the resource-based view focuses attention on an organisation's internal resources as a means of organising processes and obtaining a competitive advantage. Resources hold potential as sources of sustained competitive advantage when they are valuable, rare, inimitable, and organised. Shared platform access and outsourced manufacturing cannot provide these characteristics; proprietary capabilities must be built.

---

### Recommendation 5: Commit to Europe as the Primary Growth Market *(Medium-term: 12–36 months)*

**Problem addressed:** Market development (Ansoff); geopolitical risk mitigation.

Formalise Europe as Polestar's primary growth market for 2025–2028. Polestar sales in Europe were up 76% year-on-year, with the UK alone accounting for 25% of Q1 2025 sales. This momentum must be capitalised upon.

To support this ambitious growth trajectory, Polestar is implementing a comprehensive commercial transformation that includes a 75% expansion of its global retail presence by 2026 and the launch of sales operations in France by 2025. Germany and France — the two largest European premium car markets — should be the priority for retail network expansion.

Pursue fleet and corporate sales aggressively. Buyers view recycled and techno-fabric interiors as luxury cues; sustainability can justify a price premium of 5–10% in the premium EV segment. European corporate fleet managers under ESG pressure to decarbonise vehicle fleets represent a natural target segment for Polestar's sustainability credentials.

Deprioritise China re-entry in the near term. In 2025, Polestar closed its final China store in Shanghai as sales collapsed — and the competitive dynamics (BYD, NIO, Zeekr, and dozens of domestic brands) make China an unattractive market for a premium Western-positioned brand at Polestar's current scale.

**Rationale:** Market development is a moderate-risk Ansoff option when pursued in geographically proximate, culturally aligned markets. Europe is Polestar's most natural home market given its Swedish heritage, the EU Green Deal regulatory environment, and existing brand recognition. Concentrating resources here before attempting global scale is consistent with a focused differentiation strategy (Porter, 1980).

## 10. CONCLUSION

Polestar occupies a genuinely distinctive position in the EV landscape: a brand with credible sustainability credentials, compelling Scandinavian design, and a clear performance identity, but one that is financially fragile, strategically dependent, and operating far below the scale required for unit economics viability. The strategic situation is not one of a single, identifiable crisis but of a **structural trilemma** — identity, scale, and profitability — that must be addressed in sequence and with coherence.

The analytical chain of this case study runs as follows: the **PESTEL analysis** identifies geopolitical tariff risk and demand deceleration as dominant external threats; **Porter's Five Forces** reveals a structurally unattractive industry position for a sub-scale premium entrant; the **VRIO analysis** exposes the absence of any sustained competitive advantage; and the **value chain analysis** reveals dangerous strategic dependence at every stage. The **SWOT synthesis** crystallises these findings into a coherent problem statement, and the **Ansoff/SAFe evaluation** points to a combined market penetration and product development strategy as the most suitable, acceptable, and feasible path forward.

The five recommendations form a coherent strategic chain, not a list of independent initiatives: financial stabilisation funds the product offensive; brand sharpening increases the premium pricing power that improves margins; European focus concentrates resources where Polestar has the strongest structural advantage; and dependency reduction builds the proprietary capabilities that can eventually generate sustained competitive advantage in the Barney (1991) sense.

Polestar's CEO stated: "2025 was a record year for Polestar, with retail sales of over 60,000 cars and revenue surpassing USD 3 billion." The trajectory is real. But Polestar's strong sales growth in 2025 is a positive signal, but it must translate into improved gross margins and a clear, expedited path to sustainable profitability to truly satisfy the market and its shareholders. The question is not whether Polestar *can* resolve its trilemma — it is whether it can do so *fast enough*, and with sufficient strategic discipline, before capital markets or geopolitical forces close the window entirely.
