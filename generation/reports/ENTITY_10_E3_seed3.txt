# COMAC AND THE C919: A STRATEGIC CASE STUDY OF CHINA'S STATE-OWNED AIRCRAFT MANUFACTURER

## Strategic Situation, Multi-Framework Analysis, and Recommendations

---

## PART I — CASE BACKGROUND AND STRATEGIC SITUATION

### 1.1 The Organisation

The Commercial Aircraft Corporation of China (COMAC) is a centrally managed, state-owned enterprise (SOE) headquartered in Shanghai. COMAC was founded on 11 May 2008 with the primary mission of developing and producing large commercial passenger aircraft to foster domestic technological self-reliance and compete with established Western producers like Boeing and Airbus. The State-owned Assets Supervision and Administration Commission of the State Council (SASAC) is COMAC's principal shareholder and overseer.

COMAC's flagship product is the C919, a narrow-body, single-aisle airliner. The development programme was launched in 2008 as a competitor to the Airbus A320 and Boeing 737. Production began in December 2011, with the first prototype rolled out on 2 November 2015; the maiden flight took place on 5 May 2017. On 29 September 2022 the C919 received its CAAC type certificate, and the first production airframe was delivered to China Eastern Airlines on 9 December 2022.

### 1.2 The Strategic Situation

COMAC sits at the intersection of three interlocking tensions that constitute the core strategic problem:

1. **A geopolitical supply-chain trap**: The C919 is heavily dependent on Western-sourced components, making it vulnerable to US export controls even as it is designed to reduce China's dependence on Western aircraft.
2. **A market credibility gap**: Without Western regulatory certification (FAA/EASA), the C919 cannot access international markets, confining it to a captive domestic base.
3. **A state-ownership governance paradox**: Unlimited state funding insulates COMAC from commercial discipline, but it also embeds political objectives into production targets, generating a persistent gap between announced ambition and operational reality.

These tensions are not independent; they compound one another. The analysis below demonstrates how each framework illuminates a different dimension of the same underlying problem.

## PART II — INDUSTRY ANALYSIS: PORTER'S FIVE FORCES

Porter's Five Forces (Porter, 1979) maps the structural attractiveness of the large commercial aircraft (LCA) industry and explains why COMAC faces a structurally hostile competitive environment despite state backing.

### Force 1 — Threat of New Entrants: Very Low (but COMAC is the exception)

The barriers to entry in commercial airplanes are exceedingly high. The combination of high capital requirements, technological expertise, regulatory compliance, brand reputation, and long product development cycles act as barriers to new entrants in the aerospace industry. No company has succeeded in joining the "big jet club" in 80 years except for COMAC. This observation is analytically important: COMAC's entry was only possible because the Chinese state absorbed costs that no private firm could bear. This means COMAC's existence does not lower the barriers for others; it instead represents a state-financed anomaly within an otherwise impenetrable structure.

**Connection to analysis**: Because barriers remain high for all non-state actors, COMAC's competitive threat to Boeing and Airbus is real in the long run but structurally bounded — it can only be sustained as long as the Chinese state continues to fund it.

### Force 2 — Competitive Rivalry: Extreme Duopoly

The competitive rivalry in the commercial airplane segment is fierce, primarily between Boeing and Airbus. Market share is highly concentrated, with these two giants dominating the global market. Exit barriers are exceptionally high due to the massive capital investments and specialized nature of the industry, ensuring continued competition even during downturns. Cirium projects that with more than 6,000 new single-aisle deliveries expected in China over the forecast period, the C919 is expected to capture around 25% market share compared to Boeing's 30% and Airbus's 45%.

**Connection to analysis**: The duopoly has decades of accumulated learning curve advantages, established MRO networks, and global certification. COMAC is not simply competing on product; it is competing against an entrenched system. The 25% domestic market share projection is plausible only because Chinese state-owned airlines are effectively mandated customers — not because of unaided commercial merit.

### Force 3 — Bargaining Power of Buyers: Moderate (Domestically Suppressed, Internationally High)

Domestically, China's Big Three carriers — China Eastern Airlines, Air China, and China Southern Airlines — have placed large orders for the C919, but these airlines are themselves state-owned, meaning their procurement decisions are subject to government direction. This suppresses buyer power domestically. Internationally, however, buyer power is very high: without FAA and EASA certification, the C919 cannot operate in most Western markets or be leased by international carriers, severely limiting its export potential. Airlines outside China will not accept a product that cannot fly their routes or be financed by international lessors.

**Connection to analysis**: The asymmetry between captive domestic buyers and empowered international buyers explains why COMAC's order book exceeds 1,000 units yet nearly all orders are from Chinese entities. International certification is not merely a regulatory milestone; it is the precondition for any genuine market power.

### Force 4 — Bargaining Power of Suppliers: High (and Weaponised)

The C919 uses CFM International LEAP-1C engines, produced through a joint venture between American GE Aerospace and French company Safran. This partnership has been fundamental to the aircraft's operation. Since essential components for the LEAP engines come from the United States, a US export restriction could disrupt the entire supply chain for these critical propulsion systems. The scale of this dependency is stark: over 55% of the C919's components — including the critical LEAP-1C engine — are sourced from American firms. US sanctions, imposed in May 2025, suspended export licences for these parts.

**Connection to analysis**: Supplier power is not merely a commercial variable here; it has been converted into a geopolitical instrument. This transforms a standard Five Forces risk into an existential vulnerability. The implication is that COMAC cannot achieve genuine competitive independence until it resolves this dependency — a conclusion that directly motivates the recommendations in Part V.

### Force 5 — Threat of Substitutes: Low for Airlines, High for COMAC's Market Position

For airlines, there is no substitute for a modern narrow-body jet on short-to-medium-haul routes. However, for COMAC specifically, the substitute threat is high in the sense that Boeing and Airbus offer technically superior, globally certified, and operationally proven alternatives. COMAC, dead set on the previous generation of Western narrow-body airliners and forced to delay their "answer", was left with an outdated set of goals. This, coupled with ever-mounting technical challenges, resulted in a sub-par airplane by contemporary standards. According to some estimates, the C919 burns about 10% more fuel per seat than the A320neo, which translates into higher operating costs and environmental impact.

**Connection to analysis**: The competitive inferiority of the C919 on operating economics means that outside the captive domestic market, airlines will prefer the substitute products of Boeing and Airbus. This reinforces the conclusion that COMAC's international strategy must rest on non-price and non-performance factors — specifically, diplomatic leverage and certification progress.

## PART III — INTERNAL ANALYSIS: RESOURCE-BASED VIEW AND VALUE CHAIN

### 3.1 Resources and Capabilities

The Resource-Based View (Barney, 1991) asks whether COMAC possesses resources that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN). The honest assessment is mixed.

**Valuable but not yet VRIN — State Capital Access**: A USD 6.2 billion capital injection from eight state-owned shareholders in late 2024 nearly doubled COMAC's registered capital, signalling Beijing's continued willingness to fund the programme through industrial bottlenecks rather than market discipline. Additionally, COMAC intends to invest 10 billion yuan annually in research and development in Shanghai over the upcoming three years, along with another 10 billion yuan annually in fixed asset investments. This financial backing is valuable and rare — no private aircraft manufacturer could sustain such losses. However, it is not inimitable in the long run if other states choose to subsidise their own aerospace industries.

**Valuable — Domestic Market Captivity**: APEX reports that according to IATA, China will account for 1.6 billion passengers annually by 2037, and both Airbus and Boeing expect China to buy somewhere in the range of 7,500 new aircraft over the next 20 years. COMAC's access to this market through state-directed procurement is a genuine strategic resource.

**Weak — Technological Capability**: China fell short of its ambitious goals related to commercial aviation and has not yet succeeded in developing the state-of-the-art sub-systems required for commercially competitive jet engines. The dependence on CFM LEAP-1C engines confirms that COMAC lacks a core technological capability — propulsion — that is central to any credible claim of aerospace independence.

**Weak — Operational Track Record**: In the same month, all Boeing 737-8 MAX and A320neo in service in China averaged 8.1 and 8.4 hours flown per day respectively, so the C919 is clearly being scheduled and flown conservatively, averaging around 70% of the Boeing and Airbus peers. Lower utilisation rates signal that airlines lack confidence in the aircraft's reliability — a critical gap in the service dimension of the value chain.

### 3.2 Value Chain Bottlenecks

COMAC's value chain analysis reveals two critical bottlenecks:

**Inbound Logistics / Procurement**: The core issue lies in COMAC's reliance on foreign suppliers for 50–60% of the C919's components. The aircraft depends on Western technology, including CFM International's LEAP-1C turbofan engines. In June and July 2025, escalating US–China trade tensions led to a temporary US export halt on these engines, stalling production. Although exports resumed in August, the disruption created a lasting backlog.

**Operations / Production Ramp**: COMAC slashed its 2025 production target from 75 to 25 C919 aircraft. China Eastern, Air China, and China Southern, in their financial reporting, expected 32 deliveries in 2025, but as of September only five had been delivered to customers. COMAC ended the year well short — shipping only 13 C919 jets, according to a December 2025 Bloomberg report. The shortfall laid bare a central vulnerability in China's commercial aviation ambitions: its reliance on Western-made aircraft engines.

These bottlenecks are not independent failures; they are causally linked. The engine dependency (procurement) directly causes the production shortfall (operations), which in turn undermines the service reputation needed to win international orders (marketing/sales). The value chain is broken at its most critical joint.

## PART IV — GOVERNANCE ANALYSIS: THE PRINCIPAL–AGENT PROBLEM IN A STATE-OWNED ENTERPRISE

### 4.1 The Multi-Principal Structure

Agency theory (Jensen & Meckling, 1976) analyses conflicts between principals (owners) and agents (managers). In a private firm, this is a bilateral problem. In COMAC, it is structurally more complex. Having multiple owners — shareholders and the government — significantly exacerbates the principal-agent problem. The varying self-interests in the multi-principal model of SOEs makes it difficult for the agent to act in the interests of both principals when they have potentially competing goals and objectives.

COMAC's principal hierarchy runs: Chinese Communist Party → State Council → SASAC → COMAC Board → Management. Accountability for most SOEs' performance is hindered by the chain of proxy — the board, management, firm ownership, ministries, and the government hierarchical dominancy — which poses challenges for corporate governance implementation. The complexity of SOEs' structure is the common frontier for accountability and competent decision-making, resulting in ineffective corporate governance implementation.

### 4.2 Divergent Objectives and Their Consequences

The Chinese state uses COMAC to pursue at least three objectives that are partially in tension:

1. **National security / strategic autonomy** — reducing dependence on Western aerospace.
2. **Industrial policy** — demonstrating China's technological capability under "Made in China 2025." In 2015, President Xi Jinping's Made in China 2025 strategy identified aerospace equipment as one of 10 targeted industries that will propel China from a middle-income economy to an advanced manufacturing power.
3. **Commercial viability** — building a globally competitive aircraft manufacturer.

These objectives conflict. The national-security objective pushes for rapid domestic substitution of components, even if inferior. The industrial-policy objective drives inflated production targets that serve political optics. The commercial objective requires patient investment in quality, certification, and after-sales support. The agent (COMAC management) cannot optimise for all three simultaneously, and the political hierarchy prioritises objectives 1 and 2 over objective 3. This explains the pattern of repeatedly announced, repeatedly missed production targets: repeated schedule slippage pushed the maiden flight from 2014 to 2017 and commercial entry from 2016 to 2023, a total development period of roughly 15 years.

### 4.3 The Soft Budget Constraint

A distinctive feature of SOE governance is the "soft budget constraint" (Kornai, 1986): the expectation that the state will always rescue the enterprise removes the commercial discipline that forces private firms to confront failure. A World Bank study concluded there is "increasing recognition that poor corporate governance of SOEs is at the heart of the matter" in explaining SOE underperformance, driven by exogenous factors but fundamentally rooted in governance. In COMAC's case, the soft budget constraint manifests as the ability to absorb decades of losses and repeated schedule failures without restructuring — because the state views the programme as a matter of national prestige rather than commercial investment. The Financial Times commented that China considers the success of the C919 a source of national pride.

**Connection to analysis**: The soft budget constraint is both COMAC's greatest strength (it cannot go bankrupt) and its greatest strategic weakness (it removes the incentive to resolve the component dependency problem urgently). The recommendations in Part V must therefore work within this governance reality rather than against it.

## PART V — SCENARIO ANALYSIS AND STRATEGIC OPTIONS

### 5.1 The Central Strategic Dilemma

COMAC faces what can be termed a **"Sovereignty–Viability Paradox"**: the path to commercial viability (retaining Western components, seeking EASA/FAA certification, competing on operating economics) conflicts with the path to strategic sovereignty (replacing Western components with domestic equivalents, expanding through Belt and Road markets, avoiding Western regulatory dependency).

### 5.2 Three Strategic Scenarios

**Scenario A — "Commercial First"**: Prioritise EASA certification, retain LEAP-1C engines, compete on price in Belt and Road markets, defer domestic engine substitution until the CJ-1000A is mature. This maximises near-term commercial prospects but deepens the geopolitical vulnerability.

**Scenario B — "Sovereignty First"**: Accelerate the CJ-1000A engine programme, substitute Western components regardless of performance penalty, expand through diplomatically secured markets. This reduces geopolitical risk but accepts commercial inferiority for a decade or more.

**Scenario C — "Sequenced Dual-Track"** (recommended): Run both tracks in parallel but with explicit sequencing — commercial viability in the near term (2025–2030) through EASA certification and engine stockpiling, while investing in domestic capability for the medium term (2030+). This is the most coherent response to the compound nature of the problem.

## PART VI — RECOMMENDATIONS AND ACTION PLAN

The following recommendations are derived directly from the analytical findings above. Each is explicitly linked to the framework that motivates it.

### Recommendation 1: Resolve the Engine Dependency Through a Dual-Engine Strategy (Addresses: Five Forces — Supplier Power; Value Chain — Procurement)

**The problem**: The C919 depends on Western technology, including CFM International's LEAP-1C turbofan engines. In June and July 2025, escalating US–China trade tensions led to a temporary US export halt on these engines, stalling production.

**The warrant**: Supplier power is only dangerous when there is no substitute. The Five Forces analysis showed that this supplier power has been weaponised geopolitically. Therefore, the strategic imperative is not merely to find alternative suppliers but to eliminate the single-source dependency.

**The action**:
- **Short term (2025–2028)**: Maintain a strategic stockpile of LEAP-1C engines sufficient for 18–24 months of production, and work with Safran (the French partner in CFM) to reclassify engines sourced from European facilities, reducing US-content exposure. Safran (EPA: SAF), a CFM International partner, stands to gain as COMAC seeks to reclassify engines as "European-made" to avoid tariffs.
- **Medium term (2028–2032)**: Accelerate the CJ-1000A certification. China is developing its own CJ-1000A jet engine, which is close to completing certification, but analysts predict mass production will not occur until around 2030. COMAC should offer domestic airlines a price incentive to accept CJ-1000A-powered variants as they become available, creating the operational data necessary for eventual international certification of the domestic engine.

### Recommendation 2: Pursue EASA Certification as the Single Highest-Priority Commercial Goal (Addresses: Five Forces — Buyer Power, Substitutes; RBV — Market Access)

**The problem**: EASA Director Florian Guillermet stated that certification would require "three to six years." COMAC had initially hoped to achieve EASA approval by 2025 to support international sales, but Guillermet confirmed that certification within that timeframe is not possible.

**The warrant**: The RBV analysis showed that COMAC's domestic market captivity is a resource, but it is not VRIN — it can be eroded if Chinese airlines are eventually permitted to choose freely. International certification is the only pathway to genuine commercial independence. The Five Forces analysis showed that buyer power internationally is extremely high; EASA certification is the precondition for any reduction in that power.

**The action**:
- Dedicate a ring-fenced team of 500+ engineers exclusively to the EASA validation programme, insulated from domestic production pressures.
- EASA must still validate the aircraft's design, components, and carry out flight testing. COMAC should proactively harmonise its design documentation and certification standards with EASA requirements, rather than seeking post-hoc validation of existing designs.
- Accept EASA's timeline of 2028–2031 as realistic and plan production ramp-up accordingly, rather than announcing targets that cannot be met.

### Recommendation 3: Implement Performance-Based Governance to Break the Soft Budget Constraint (Addresses: Principal–Agent Problem; SOE Governance)

**The problem**: The multi-principal governance structure creates divergent objectives that prevent COMAC from making commercially rational decisions. The repeated pattern of inflated targets and missed deliveries is a direct symptom of this governance failure.

**The warrant**: The complexity of SOEs' structure is the common frontier for accountability and competent decision-making, resulting in ineffective corporate governance implementation. The World Bank concluded there is "increasing recognition that poor corporate governance of SOEs is at the heart of the matter" in explaining SOE underperformance. Without governance reform, additional capital injections will be absorbed without producing proportional improvements in output.

**The action**:
- SASAC should establish a Board-level Commercial Performance Committee with independent directors drawn from the airline industry and international aerospace, with authority to set and enforce production targets independently of political cycles.
- Replace politically driven annual targets (which create the principal-agent distortion of over-promising to satisfy the political principal) with rolling 3-year delivery plans tied to verifiable supply chain commitments.
- Link executive compensation explicitly to delivery rate, unit cost reduction, and EASA certification milestones — not to production announcements.

### Recommendation 4: Pursue a "BRI-First" International Market Strategy While Awaiting Western Certification (Addresses: Five Forces — Rivalry; RBV — Market Access)

**The problem**: COMAC cannot access Western markets without EASA/FAA certification, but it needs international operational experience to build the safety record that supports certification.

**The warrant**: Cirium's Fleet Forecast signals that only around 250 aircraft are expected to be sold to export customers, largely in Belt and Road countries where political influence can drive sales campaigns. This is a realistic near-term market, and it serves a dual purpose: generating revenue and accumulating flight hours that support the EASA certification data package.

**The action**:
- Reports from the Bangkok Post suggest that the C919 could operate in Southeast Asia without European or US certifications, provided it meets the regulatory standards of individual countries. COMAC should systematically pursue bilateral certification agreements with ASEAN, African, and Latin American regulators, establishing operational presence in these markets by 2026–2027.
- Establish regional MRO centres in Singapore, Dubai, and Nairobi to demonstrate the after-sales support infrastructure that international airlines require — addressing the value chain weakness in service identified in Part III.

### Recommendation 5: Align Production Targets with Supply Chain Reality (Addresses: Value Chain — Operations; Principal–Agent — Objective Divergence)

**The problem**: COMAC slashed its 2025 production target from 75 to 25 C919 aircraft. As of September only five had been delivered to customers. This pattern of target inflation followed by revision damages COMAC's credibility with airlines, lessors, and potential international customers.

**The warrant**: The value chain analysis showed that the production bottleneck is causally upstream — in engine supply. Announcing production targets that exceed engine availability is not a commercial strategy; it is a governance symptom. Credibility with buyers (reducing buyer power, per the Five Forces) requires demonstrated delivery consistency, not ambitious announcements.

**The action**:
- Adopt a "supply-chain-anchored" planning methodology: production targets should be set at 90% of confirmed engine and critical component availability, with a 10% buffer for disruption.
- Publish quarterly delivery data transparently — mirroring the practice of Boeing and Airbus — to build market confidence and create accountability pressure on management.
