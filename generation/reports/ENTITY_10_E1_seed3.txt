# COMAC AND THE C919: NAVIGATING GEOPOLITICAL TURBULENCE AND THE QUEST FOR GLOBAL COMPETITIVENESS
## A Strategic Case Study

---

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Commercial Aircraft Corporation of China (COMAC), a state-owned enterprise (SOE) headquartered in Shanghai, represents China's most ambitious attempt to challenge the Boeing–Airbus duopoly in commercial aviation. Its flagship narrow-body aircraft, the C919, entered commercial service in May 2023 and has accumulated an order backlog of between 713 and 1,000 aircraft. Yet by mid-2025, COMAC had delivered only five aircraft against an original target of 75, exposing a profound strategic vulnerability: deep dependency on Western—particularly American—technology at a time of escalating geopolitical tension.

This case study analyses COMAC's strategic situation using **PESTEL**, **Porter's Five Forces**, the **VRIO framework** (Resource-Based View), and a **SWOT synthesis**, before applying the **Ansoff Growth Matrix** to generate a coherent set of strategic recommendations. The central argument is that COMAC's long-term viability depends on a three-track strategy: (i) accelerating domestic supply-chain localisation to reduce geopolitical exposure; (ii) pursuing a phased internationalisation through Global South markets while EASA certification is pending; and (iii) leveraging state backing to build the organisational capabilities—quality systems, after-sales networks, and regulatory expertise—that will be required to compete globally.

## 2. CASE BACKGROUND

### 2.1 Company Overview

COMAC was established in 2008 by the Chinese central government as a state-owned enterprise, with a founding mandate that was explicitly strategic: to end China's dependence on Western aircraft manufacturers, develop indigenous aerospace industrial capabilities, and capture a share of one of the world's largest aviation markets. Its two primary commercial programmes are:

- **ARJ21**: A 78–97 seat regional jet that entered service in 2016.
- **C919**: A 158–192 seat narrow-body jet, directly comparable to the Airbus A320neo and Boeing 737 MAX.

The C919, China's first domestically developed jet built to international airworthiness standards, entered commercial service in May 2023 with China Eastern Airlines. COMAC is also developing the wide-body **C929** in partnership with Russia's United Aircraft Corporation.

### 2.2 The Strategic Situation in Numbers

Initially targeting 75 deliveries in 2025, COMAC has slashed its goal to 25 units, with only five aircraft delivered by mid-2025. This compares to a very different competitive landscape: the company delivered just five C919 aircraft in 2025, far below its original targets, and has since reduced its near-term production goals. By contrast, Airbus and Boeing each deliver more than 500 single-aisle jets annually.

Yet the longer-term ambitions remain formidable. By 2035, COMAC aims to capture one-fifth of the global narrow-body aircraft market and one-third of the Chinese market, with plans to sell 2,000 C919 aircraft over the next 20 years.

## 3. CONTEXT: THE TECHNOLOGY DEPENDENCY CRISIS

The most acute trigger of COMAC's current strategic crisis is the weaponisation of its supply chain. The C919 relies heavily on US technology, with 48 of its major suppliers based in the United States, including GE, Honeywell, and Collins. Restrictions on US exports have slowed the pace of deliveries, impacting COMAC's ability to meet its targets.

In late May 2025, the US Department of Commerce suspended export licences allowing American companies to supply aircraft engines and related technologies to COMAC. The restrictions cover Electronic Computer-Aided Design (ECAD) software, chemical precursors, and engine components.

The domestic alternative is years away. According to a January 2026 comment from analyst Mayur Patel from aviation data consultancy OAG Aviation, Chinese certification and initial deliveries of the CJ-1000A engine are likely to begin in 2027 or 2028 while mass production is expected from around 2030. Domestic alternatives like the CJ-1000A engine won't be ready until 2030, hindering COMAC's global expansion.

Meanwhile, COMAC's capital position is strong. As competition from the two Western giants for contracts heats up amid a supply chain crunch, the Shanghai-based firm received a huge capital injection of 44 billion yuan (US$6.2 billion) from its stakeholders, boosting its total registered capital by 88% to more than 94 billion yuan. Furthermore, COMAC has raised its procurement budget for the C919 by 70%, bringing this year's expenditure to approximately 34 billion yuan ($4.7 billion).

## 4. EXTERNAL ENVIRONMENT ANALYSIS: PESTEL

### 4.1 Political

The most powerful political factor is the deteriorating US–China relationship. Trade friction with Washington threatens to prevent COMAC from securing core parts for the programme that has been supported by huge Chinese government subsidies. "COMAC faces significant risk from the volatile policy environment, with its supply" chain exposed to unilateral regulatory action. Conversely, China's Belt and Road Initiative creates political goodwill in developing markets that could translate into aircraft orders, and the government's political will to achieve aerospace self-sufficiency is itself a driver of enormous magnitude.

### 4.2 Economic

One of the principal growth factors for the COMAC C919 market is the surging demand for narrow-body aircraft, especially in the Asia Pacific region. Airlines are increasingly favouring single-aisle jets like the C919 for short- and medium-haul routes. COMAC's order backlog is largely secured from captive state-owned carriers: COMAC's order backlog of C919s is estimated to be between 713–1,000 aircraft, largely from Chinese carriers: Air China, China Eastern, and China Southern. Air China, China Southern and China Eastern earlier each purchased 100 C919 aircraft, and the planes are expected to be delivered before 2031.

### 4.3 Social

Passenger acceptance data is encouraging. As of December 2024, the C919 had carried over 1 million passengers and has entered the stage of scaled development and multi-user operations. During the 2024 Lunar New Year holiday, C919 utilisation rates rose 40% year-over-year, signalling strong market acceptance. Internationally, however, brand perception remains a challenge. According to a representative from the Royal Aeronautical Society, "There's nothing in the ARJ21 or the C919 from China that keeps those planes" from being technically sound—but overcoming decades of Western brand dominance in aviation safety perception is a slow social process.

### 4.4 Technological

Technology is simultaneously COMAC's greatest vulnerability and its most critical strategic battleground. COMAC has yet to secure certification from the European Union Aviation Safety Agency (EASA), a critical hurdle for international sales. The utilisation rate improvement is a positive signal: COMAC also plans to develop shortened and stretched variants of the C919, mirroring the successful strategies of its competitors. On the engine front, the CJ-1000A is being developed as the domestic powerplant for the COMAC C919, a narrow-body aircraft, offering a thrust range of 10,000–20,000 kgf. As of May 2018, AECC projected certification by 2027 and entry into service by 2030, roughly eight years behind the original timeline.

### 4.5 Environmental

The global aviation industry faces intensifying pressure to decarbonise. The C919, powered by CFM LEAP-1C engines, offers fuel efficiency broadly comparable to the A320neo and 737 MAX. COMAC's transition to domestic CJ-1000A engines will need to demonstrate equivalent environmental performance to maintain competitiveness internationally, particularly as sustainable aviation fuel (SAF) compatibility becomes a standard procurement criterion.

### 4.6 Legal

COMAC first applied for European certification in 2019, but progress was stalled by the COVID-19 pandemic. In November 2023, the company re-submitted its application, aiming for certification by 2026. EASA's certification process is known for its rigour, making a rapid approval unlikely. Industry sources suggest that political factors may also influence the pace of approval, even as technical requirements are addressed. A further legal concern is intellectual property: in February 2020, Reuters reported that the US government was considering blocking GE from selling the LEAP-1C engine to COMAC, citing concerns that its technology could be stolen and put into the CJ-1000A engine.

## 5. INDUSTRY ANALYSIS: PORTER'S FIVE FORCES

### Force 1: Threat of New Entrants — LOW

Commercial aircraft manufacturing has among the highest barriers to entry of any industry. Capital requirements run into tens of billions of dollars: the C919's announced development budget was CN¥58 billion (US$9.5 billion) but its actual cost was estimated at well over CN¥125 billion (US$20 billion). COMAC's struggle to scale production reflects challenges that took Boeing and Airbus decades to overcome. Complex aerospace supply chains, quality control at volume, and workforce training all require sustained investment and operational maturity. COMAC itself, despite full state backing, took 15 years from founding to first commercial delivery.

### Force 2: Bargaining Power of Suppliers — VERY HIGH

This is COMAC's most acute competitive vulnerability. COMAC's C919 jet faces production delays due to US export restrictions on critical components like engines and avionics. This dependency on Western suppliers has exposed a strategic weakness in COMAC's supply chain strategy, forcing the company to scale back production targets and delay its global expansion plans. The May 2025 export control action demonstrates that the US government can exercise supplier leverage as a geopolitical weapon, transforming a commercial issue into a national security one.

### Force 3: Bargaining Power of Buyers — MODERATE (rising internationally)

COMAC's current buyers are primarily Chinese state-owned airlines, creating a quasi-captive buyer relationship. As COMAC seeks international buyers, buyer power will increase significantly. In September 2024, Brazilian cargo airline TOTAL Linhas Aéreas was reportedly eyeing an order for up to four C919 jets. Beyond Vietnam, COMAC has been expanding its global footprint through international expos and agreements with airlines worldwide. Recently, the company secured an order from GallopAir in Brunei for 15 ARJ21 and 15 C919 jets. These international buyers have strong alternatives and will demand competitive pricing, robust after-sales support, and proven reliability records.

### Force 4: Threat of Substitutes — LOW to MODERATE

In the narrow-body segment, there are no direct substitutes for jet aircraft on medium-haul routes. High-speed rail competes on shorter domestic routes in China but is not a substitute for medium-haul international travel. Within the aircraft category, however, the A320neo and 737 MAX are effective substitutes for the C919, blurring the line between substitutes and competitive rivalry.

### Force 5: Competitive Rivalry — VERY HIGH

In 2024, aviation consultancy IBA estimates that COMAC will deliver nine jets—that's less than a third of Boeing's monthly deliveries, pointing to the tall task the Chinese group faces. Looking forward, by 2030, IBA forecasts COMAC will have secured around 65% of new narrowbody deliveries to Chinese operators, although this will equate to just 7% of the total in-service fleet when legacy Airbus and Boeing types are included. COMAC is intensifying its efforts to disrupt the long-standing dominance of Boeing and Airbus in the global narrow-body market, but the incumbents have decades of manufacturing experience, established global service networks, and deep customer relationships that cannot be replicated quickly.

The five recommendations below follow logically from the analytical chain: PESTEL identifies geopolitical exposure and certification as critical external threats; Five Forces identifies supplier power as the dominant competitive force; VRIO identifies state resources as the primary advantage and operational capabilities as the critical gap; SWOT confirms that the technology dependency crisis is the central strategic problem; and the Ansoff Matrix provides the growth pathway.

### RECOMMENDATION 1: Immediate Supply-Chain Triage and Localisation Acceleration (2025–2030)
**Priority: CRITICAL | Time Horizon: Immediate**

**The Problem**: COMAC's dependency on Western suppliers has exposed a strategic weakness in COMAC's supply chain strategy, forcing the company to scale back production targets and delay its global expansion plans.

**Actions**:
- **Accelerate the CJ-1000A programme**: Redirect state funding to prioritise CJ-1000A certification by 2027–28 and serial production by 2030. AECC projected certification by 2027 and entry into service by 2030—this timeline must be treated as a national strategic priority. Assign dedicated COMAC engineering teams to work alongside AECC to accelerate integration testing.
- **Map and triage the 48 US suppliers**: Categorise each US-sourced component by (a) criticality to airworthiness and (b) availability of domestic or non-US alternative. For Tier 1 critical components (engines, avionics core), fund parallel domestic development. For lower-tier components, source from European suppliers (Safran, Thales, Liebherr) where certification rules permit.
- **Establish a strategic component stockpile**: Use a portion of the 44 billion yuan capital injection to create a 12–24 month buffer of the most critical US-sourced parts against future export control actions.
- **Deepen European supplier relationships**: Safran (France) co-manufactures the CFM LEAP-1C alongside GE. Deepening European aerospace partnerships would create a more geopolitically diversified supply chain less vulnerable to unilateral US action.

**Expected Outcome**: Stabilise C919 production at 30–50 units per year by 2027; achieve 60%+ domestic content by 2030 with the CJ-1000A engine.

**Rationale**: The VRIO analysis shows that Western-sourced technology is not a sustainable competitive advantage—it is a fragile dependency. The Five Forces analysis confirms that supplier power is the dominant competitive force. Converting this weakness into a strength through localisation is the prerequisite for all other strategic moves.

### RECOMMENDATION 2: Pursue a Phased "Global South First" Internationalisation Strategy (2025–2030)
**Priority: HIGH | Time Horizon: Near-term**

**The Problem**: COMAC's growth ambitions require international market access, but EASA certification is uncertain and FAA certification is politically impossible in the current climate. However, many of the world's fastest-growing aviation markets do not require EASA/FAA certification.

**Actions**:
- **Target Southeast Asia as the primary international market**: COMAC aims to open new routes to Southeast Asia for its C919 jet by 2026. Negotiate bilateral airworthiness agreements with ASEAN aviation authorities modelled on the CAAC-EASA bilateral framework.
- **Leverage BRI relationships in Africa and the Middle East**: Ethiopian Airlines, EgyptAir, and carriers in Pakistan and Bangladesh are potential early international customers. Chinese state banks (China Development Bank, Export-Import Bank of China) should offer concessional financing to make the C919 financially attractive relative to Airbus and Boeing alternatives.
- **Build regional MRO hubs**: International airlines will not order the C919 without confidence in maintenance, repair, and overhaul support. COMAC has already strengthened its presence in Southeast Asia with new offices in Hong Kong and Singapore. Priority MRO hubs should be established in Singapore, Dubai, and Nairobi, with a 24-hour AOG (Aircraft on Ground) response commitment.
- **Use the ARJ21 as a market entry vehicle**: The company secured an order from GallopAir in Brunei for 15 ARJ21 and 15 C919 jets—this bundling strategy should be replicated. The smaller ARJ21 establishes COMAC's operational presence and service reputation before the C919 arrives.

**Expected Outcome**: Secure 50–100 firm international orders from non-Chinese carriers by 2028, building the operational track record that supports the EASA certification case.

**Rationale**: The Ansoff Market Development quadrant identifies this as a moderate-risk growth strategy. The Five Forces analysis shows that buyer power is lower in markets where COMAC has political and financial leverage through BRI relationships. The PESTEL analysis confirms that Global South markets represent the most accessible near-term opportunity.

### RECOMMENDATION 3: Treat EASA Certification as a Strategic Priority, Not a Technical Exercise (2025–2027)
**Priority: HIGH | Time Horizon: Near-term**

**The Problem**: Without EASA type certification, the C919 is legally prohibited from operating in the EU and the ~60 countries that use EASA standards as their airworthiness benchmark. Industry experts emphasise that obtaining type certifications from the US Federal Aviation Administration and European Union Aviation Safety Agency (EASA) will be essential for COMAC's global credibility.

**Actions**:
- **Appoint a Chief Certification Officer** at C-suite level, reporting directly to the CEO, with a dedicated team exclusively managing the EASA process. Certification requires not just technical compliance but sustained regulatory relationship management.
- **Engage independent technical advisors**: Hire former EASA certification engineers as consultants to identify and close technical gaps proactively—a practice used successfully by Embraer in its E-Jet certification campaigns.
- **Address the IP concern proactively**: Commission an independent technical audit demonstrating the design independence of the CJ-1000A from the LEAP-1C, and share findings with EASA as part of the certification dossier. This addresses the concern that the US government was considering blocking GE from selling the LEAP-1C engine to COMAC, citing concerns that its technology could be stolen and put into the CJ-1000A.
- **Target EASA certification by 2027**: Chinese civil aviation authorities express optimism about receiving EU certification in the near term; a realistic, firm strategic deadline of 2027 should be set and publicly committed to.

**Expected Outcome**: EASA type certificate by 2027, unlocking European leasing companies (which supply aircraft globally) as customers and dramatically expanding the C919's international addressable market.

**Rationale**: The PESTEL legal analysis identifies certification as the single most consequential legal barrier to COMAC's international strategy. The VRIO analysis identifies EASA certification as a critical gap that, once filled, would become a valuable and rare resource. Without it, Recommendation 2 is permanently limited to non-EASA markets.

### RECOMMENDATION 4: Build Organisational Capabilities to Match State Resources (2025–2030)
**Priority: HIGH | Time Horizon: Medium-term**

**The Problem**: The VRIO analysis reveals that COMAC's competitive advantages are concentrated in state-provided resources rather than in organisational capabilities. This is the classic SOE capability trap: resources are abundant but capabilities lag. Complex aerospace supply chains, quality control at volume, and workforce training all require sustained investment and operational maturity that took Boeing and Airbus decades to overcome.

**Actions**:
- **Implement a world-class quality management system**: Partner with a leading aerospace quality consultancy to implement AS9100 (the aerospace quality management standard) across all C919 production lines and suppliers. Target zero production-line escapes by 2027.
- **Invest in workforce capability**: COMAC's Shanghai facility already employs a workforce of 10,000. Establish a COMAC Aerospace Academy in partnership with Beihang University and Nanjing University of Aeronautics to build a pipeline of aerospace manufacturing talent.
- **Develop a Customer Support and Services division**: Create a dedicated customer support organisation modelled on Airbus Customer Services, offering 24/7 technical support, spare parts logistics, training simulators, and performance monitoring analytics. This is critical for international market credibility.
- **Benchmark production efficiency**: The number of C919 aircraft to be delivered in 2024 is 17, which is 567% of the actual delivery quantity in 2023. According to the standard production ramp-up pattern, the C919 project may reach the required production rate around 2027 and achieve stable production rates around 2030. Implement lean manufacturing and digital twin technology to accelerate this trajectory.

**Expected Outcome**: Achieve production of 150 aircraft per year by 2028 (as targeted), with defect rates and delivery reliability comparable to industry benchmarks.

**Rationale**: The Resource-Based View (Barney, 1991) holds that sustained competitive advantage requires capabilities that are not only valuable and rare but also inimitable and organised. The Dynamic Capabilities framework (Teece et al., 1997) further emphasises that in rapidly changing environments—such as the current geopolitical landscape—the ability to sense, seize, and reconfigure resources is the ultimate source of competitive advantage. COMAC must convert its state-provided resources into genuinely inimitable capabilities.

### RECOMMENDATION 5: Develop C919 Variants to Extend the Product Lifecycle (2027–2032)
**Priority: MEDIUM | Time Horizon: Long-term**

**The Problem**: The C919 in its current configuration competes in only one segment of the narrow-body market. Airbus and Boeing have extended their narrow-body families to cover a range from ~100 seats to ~240 seats, giving them far greater market coverage. COMAC's single variant limits its addressable market and reduces its attractiveness to airlines seeking fleet standardisation.

**Actions**:
- **Develop a C919+ stretched variant** (~220 seats): This would compete with the A321neo and 737 MAX-10, the fastest-growing segment of the narrow-body market. COMAC plans to develop shortened and stretched variants of the C919, mirroring the successful strategies of its competitors.
- **Develop a C919- shortened variant** (~130 seats): This would address thinner domestic routes and compete with Embraer's E2 family in regional markets.
- **Pursue a C919F freighter variant**: A freighter variant would address the growing e-commerce-driven air cargo market in Asia and provide a lower-risk entry point for international operators, as cargo airlines face fewer passenger safety perception barriers.
- **Accelerate C929 wide-body development**: Beyond the C919, COMAC is developing wide-body aircraft, including the C929 and C939, designed to compete with Boeing's wide-body portfolio and address long-haul international routes.

**Expected Outcome**: A full C919 family covering 130–220 seats by 2032, doubling COMAC's addressable narrow-body market and providing Chinese airlines with a single-type solution for their entire domestic network.

**Rationale**: The Ansoff Product Development quadrant identifies this as a moderate-risk growth strategy for existing markets. The competitive rivalry analysis shows that Airbus and Boeing's family strategies are a key source of their competitive advantage—COMAC must replicate this to remain relevant across the full market.

## 11. CONCLUSION

COMAC occupies a uniquely paradoxical strategic position. It is simultaneously one of the world's best-resourced aircraft manufacturers—backed by the full financial and political weight of the Chinese state—and one of the most strategically vulnerable, dependent on foreign technology that can be cut off at the stroke of a regulatory pen. The ripple effects are immediate when that pen moves.

The strategic analysis presented in this case study demonstrates that COMAC's path to becoming a genuine global competitor is long but navigable. The PESTEL analysis identifies geopolitical decoupling and certification as the dominant external forces. Porter's Five Forces reveals that supplier power is the critical competitive vulnerability. The VRIO framework shows that state resources are genuine advantages, but only if converted into organisational capabilities. The SWOT synthesis confirms that the technology dependency crisis is the central strategic problem. And the Ansoff Matrix provides a growth pathway sequenced by risk and time horizon.

IBA forecasts that COMAC will more than triple its rate of aircraft deliveries to 145 per year by 2030, marking steady progress in China's ambition to establish an indigenous commercial aviation industry. Whether this trajectory is achieved depends not on the availability of state capital—that is assured—but on COMAC's ability to execute the capability-building agenda described in these recommendations.

The strategic lesson is universal: **state ownership can provide the resources to enter an industry, but only genuine organisational capability can sustain competitive advantage within it.**
