# 🚗 POLESTAR AUTOMOTIVE: NAVIGATING THE CROSSROADS
## A Strategic Management Case Study

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Polestar Automotive Holding UK PLC (Nasdaq: PSNY) is a Swedish premium electric vehicle (EV) manufacturer founded in 2017 as a joint venture between Volvo Cars and Geely Holding. Once heralded as a bold challenger in the luxury EV space, Polestar now finds itself at a critical strategic crossroads. Polestar reported a 53% revenue decline and 55% drop in deliveries for Q1 2025, raising urgent questions about the Swedish EV brand's viability as it battles tariffs, Chinese ownership complications, and intensifying competition from Tesla, BYD, and legacy automakers.

This case study examines Polestar's strategic situation using PESTEL, Porter's Five Forces, SWOT, the Resource-Based View (RBV)/VRIO Framework, Porter's Generic Strategies, and the Ansoff Matrix — before concluding with a set of evidence-based strategic recommendations.

---

## PART 1: COMPANY BACKGROUND

### 1.1 Origins and Identity

Polestar, a subsidiary of Volvo, has quickly emerged as a leading player in the electric vehicle (EV) market since its founding in 2017 as a collaboration between Volvo and Geely. The brand's identity was built on three pillars: **Scandinavian minimalist design**, **performance-oriented electric engineering**, and **progressive sustainability**. Polestar's name is an interpretation of the North Star — a guiding star — and its visual identity was developed to be iconic and timeless.

Polestar didn't just create a new brand of cars — they created an entirely new buying process and customer journey. Their digital-first strategy shatters the mould of the traditional automotive environment and path to purchase.

### 1.2 The Strategic Situation: A Company Under Pressure

By 2024–2025, the optimism of the early years had given way to acute strategic stress:

- Polestar reported a gross loss of $414.7 million in FY2023, representing a significant shift from a profit of $98.4 million in 2022, with a gross margin of negative 17.4% compared with a positive 4% in 2022. The net loss increased to **$1.17 billion** in 2023.
- In 2023, Polestar delivered roughly 54,600 electric cars to customers, 6% more than in 2022 — but the company initially planned to sell 80,000 electric cars in 2023, and later updated guidance to 60,000–70,000, which also was not achieved.
- In February 2025, Polestar secured up to US$450m in 12-month loans, following a previous funding infusion in December 2024 which secured more than US$800m in 12-month term facilities — highlighting the company's persistent cash dependency.
- Volvo Cars reduced its stake in Polestar from 48% to 18% in early 2024, making Geely Holding the dominant shareholder. "Polestar will thus become a completely independent part of the Geely Group," a spokeswoman for Polestar Automotive Germany explained.
- In August 2024, Michael Lohscheller, who has previously served as CEO of Opel, VinFast and Nikola, was appointed CEO — signalling a shift from brand-building to operational turnaround.

## PART 2: MACRO-ENVIRONMENTAL ANALYSIS — PESTEL

### 2.1 Political

The US imposed a 100% tariff on Chinese-made EVs in 2024, followed by a 25% blanket tariff on all imported vehicles in 2025. These policies directly targeted Polestar's vehicles produced in China and South Korea. The U.S. tariffs on Chinese goods surged to 145% in April 2025 — a historic high — and the decision underscores the growing challenges for global automakers caught in the crosshairs of the U.S.–China trade war.

As a direct response to high U.S. tariffs exceeding 100% on China-made cars, Polestar made the notable strategic decision that the Polestar 5 will not initially be sold in the United States, compelling Polestar to concentrate on the European market.

### 2.2 Economic

The Luxury EV Market is worth USD 254.08 billion in 2026 and is growing at a CAGR of 15.86% to reach USD 530.16 billion by 2031. However, near-term macroeconomic headwinds and price wars initiated by Tesla and BYD are compressing margins across the segment.

Many emerging EV players — such as VinFast from Viet Nam, Polestar from Europe, and Canoo, Fisker, Lucid and Nikola from the United States — are missing sales targets and trading low.

### 2.3 Social

Polestar's marketing strategy focuses on brand positioning and creating a futuristic image, targeting affluent, environmentally conscious consumers who prioritise design, performance, and advanced technology. However, growing consumer nationalism in the US regarding Chinese-owned brands, and concerns about Polestar's Geely parentage, may erode brand trust in key Western markets.

### 2.4 Technological

Geely Holding controls one of the broadest premium-to-luxury portfolios in the global auto industry — Zeekr, Lynk & Co, Polestar, Lotus, Volvo Cars, Smart — most of which share the SEA (Sustainable Experience Architecture) platform family. With core "three-electric" technology homogenising across brands, the premium differentiation falls heavily on styling, tuning, software, and dealer experience.

### 2.5 Environmental

Polestar has made sustainability a core brand pillar, publishing transparent Life Cycle Assessment reports for each vehicle. The EU's tightening emissions regulations and carbon border adjustment mechanisms (CBAM) create structural advantages for pure-play EV brands.

### 2.6 Legal

Polestar's cycle of non-compliance and regaining compliance with Nasdaq minimum bid price requirements encapsulates not only the company's individual financial vulnerabilities but also the broader turbulence within the electric vehicle industry. Potential scenarios range from successful compliance to, in the worst case, delisting from Nasdaq.

## PART 3: INDUSTRY ANALYSIS — PORTER'S FIVE FORCES

### 3.1 Rivalry Among Existing Competitors — **HIGH**

The company operates within the premium electric vehicle (EV) segment, competing with both established luxury brands transitioning to EVs and other dedicated EV manufacturers. This positioning targets affluent, environmentally conscious consumers who prioritise design, performance, and advanced technology. Competitors include Tesla, BMW, Mercedes-Benz, Porsche, Audi, Lucid Motors, and Zeekr (a sister brand under Geely). Tesla's shares were on average 15% lower in 2023 than in 2021–2022, and the combined market capitalisation of pure-play EV carmakers fell by nearly 20% on average relative to 2022 — illustrating the brutal competitive environment.

### 3.2 Threat of New Entrants — **MODERATE**

Capital requirements, manufacturing complexity, and brand-building costs create significant barriers to entry. However, Chinese OEMs are aggressively entering Western premium markets with competitive pricing and rapidly improving quality.

### 3.3 Bargaining Power of Suppliers — **HIGH**

As of 2024, Polestar relies on a restricted pool of specialised EV battery suppliers, with critical technology dependencies concentrated among a small number of global manufacturers. Polestar's relatively low production volumes mean it lacks the scale leverage to negotiate favourable supply terms.

### 3.4 Bargaining Power of Buyers — **MODERATE TO HIGH**

Unlike traditional automotive manufacturers that rely heavily on dealership networks, Polestar operates primarily through online sales channels. By bypassing dealerships, Polestar offers a seamless and hassle-free purchasing experience. However, as alternatives multiply, buyer power is increasing.

### 3.5 Threat of Substitutes — **MODERATE**

Premium ICE vehicles (declining due to regulation), premium hybrid vehicles, and premium used EVs all constitute substitutes. Regulatory direction strongly favours EVs, but compelling hybrid alternatives maintain a moderate substitute threat.

## PART 4: INTERNAL ANALYSIS — RESOURCE-BASED VIEW (RBV) AND VRIO FRAMEWORK

The Resource-Based View (Barney, 1991) holds that sustainable competitive advantage derives from resources and capabilities that are **Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Organised (VRIO)** to exploit.

| Resource / Capability | Valuable? | Rare? | Inimitable? | Organised? | Competitive Implication |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Scandinavian design identity & brand | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ | **Sustained Competitive Advantage** |
| Direct-to-consumer digital sales model | ✅ | Partially | Partially | ✅ | **Temporary Competitive Advantage** |
| Geely/Volvo technology platform (SEA) | ✅ | ❌ | ❌ | ✅ | **Competitive Parity** |
| Sustainability/ESG transparency | ✅ | Partially | Partially | ✅ | **Temporary Competitive Advantage** |
| Manufacturing asset-light model | ✅ | ❌ | ❌ | ✅ | **Competitive Parity** |
| Premium performance engineering | ✅ | Partially | Partially | Partially | **Temporary Competitive Advantage** |
| Financial resources / cash position | ❌ | — | — | — | **Competitive Disadvantage** |

**Key Insight**: With core EV technology homogenising across brands, Polestar's differentiation falls heavily on styling, software, and brand experience — making its Scandinavian design identity and ESG narrative its most genuinely rare and inimitable assets.

## PART 5: SWOT ANALYSIS

### Strengths
1. **Distinctive brand identity**: Scandinavian minimalism, sustainability-first ethos, and performance credentials create a coherent and differentiated brand proposition.
2. Polestar runs an asset-light model that leverages Volvo and Geely manufacturing strengths while keeping a direct-to-consumer sales approach and digital services.
3. **Expanding product portfolio**: Strong product momentum: Polestar 3 and Polestar 4 represent 56% of order intake in Q4 2024.
4. **New CEO with turnaround experience**: Michael Lohscheller brings extensive experience in driving operational excellence and strategic growth from his previous roles at Opel, VinFast, and Nikola.

### Weaknesses
1. A gross loss of $414.7 million in FY2023 and a net loss of $1.17 billion represent severe financial deterioration, with ongoing cash burn requiring repeated emergency funding.
2. The company still faces a $350 million funding gap and may need to issue new shares to strengthen its balance sheet.
3. **Chinese manufacturing dependency**: Acute tariff exposure in the US market due to China and South Korea production locations.
4. **Nasdaq compliance risk**: Stock price below minimum bid requirements creates delisting risk.

### Opportunities
1. The Luxury EV Market is projected to grow at a CAGR of 15.86% to reach USD 530.16 billion by 2031 — a powerful structural tailwind.
2. Polestar targets compound annual retail sales volume growth of 30–35% for 2025 to 2027 and positive adjusted EBITDA in 2025, with positive free cash flow after investments expected in 2027.
3. Polestar 7, a premium compact SUV, is planned to be produced in Europe, strengthening global manufacturing diversification.
4. **IRA tax credits**: A US manufacturing facility would qualify Polestar vehicles for up to $7,500 in US federal EV tax credits.

### Threats
1. The U.S. tariffs on Chinese goods surged to 145% in April 2025 — a historic high — severely impacting Polestar's vehicles produced in China and South Korea.
2. While Tesla has diversified its supply chain and secured North American production hubs, Polestar's reliance on Asian manufacturing leaves it more exposed.
3. **Geely brand cannibalisation**: Zeekr, Lynk & Co, Polestar, Lotus, and Volvo Cars all share the SEA platform under the broader Geely Holding umbrella — risking internal cannibalisation across overlapping segments.

## PART 6: COMPETITIVE STRATEGY — PORTER'S GENERIC STRATEGIES

Porter (1980) identifies three generic strategies: **Cost Leadership**, **Differentiation**, and **Focus**. A firm that fails to commit to one risks being "stuck in the middle."

Polestar has historically pursued a **Differentiation Focus** strategy — targeting affluent, design-conscious, sustainability-oriented EV buyers and differentiating through brand, design, and sustainability rather than price. The company's strategy focuses on high-end features and sustainable materials rather than competing on price with more budget-oriented EVs.

However, this strategy is under stress. The new CEO has acknowledged that "Polestar is a bit too modest at the moment. US and Chinese buyers don't want too much understatement." This signals a need to sharpen the brand's performance confidence while preserving its Scandinavian soul. The strategy must be **reinforced and sharpened**, not abandoned.

## PART 7: GROWTH STRATEGY — ANSOFF MATRIX

The Ansoff Matrix (1957) maps growth strategies across existing/new products and existing/new markets:

| | **Existing Markets** | **New Markets** |
|---|---|---|
| **Existing Products** | **Market Penetration**: Deepen Polestar 2/3/4 share in Europe | **Market Development**: Southeast Asia, Middle East, Latin America |
| **New Products** | **Product Development**: Polestar 5 GT, Polestar 7, software/services | **Diversification**: US manufacturing; energy/charging services |

The Polestar 5 GT launch and Polestar 7 compact SUV represent the centrepiece of Polestar's product development strategy, with SUV models already accounting for 56% of orders in Q4 2024. The Polestar 7 will be produced in Europe, strengthening global manufacturing diversification and reducing tariff exposure.

The highest-priority quadrant for Polestar is **Product Development** — launching the Polestar 5 and 7 in existing premium markets — combined with **Market Development** through a US manufacturing re-entry strategy.

## PART 8: STRATEGIC RECOMMENDATIONS

### ✅ Recommendation 1: Double Down on European Market Leadership *(Immediate: 0–12 months)*

**Rationale**: Europe is Polestar's strongest, most brand-aligned, and least tariff-exposed market. The EU's 2035 ICE ban creates structural demand.

**Actions**:
- Redirect marketing investment to top 5 European markets (Germany, UK, Sweden, Norway, Netherlands).
- Accelerate Polestar 3 and 4 deliveries to capitalise on SUV momentum.
- Strengthen the direct-to-consumer digital platform with localised content and financing partnerships.
- Leverage EU EV subsidies to reduce effective customer price without discounting the brand.

**KPI**: +20% year-on-year European retail sales volume growth by end of 2025.

---

### ✅ Recommendation 2: Achieve Financial Stabilisation Through Cost Discipline and Revenue Diversification *(Immediate: 0–12 months)*

**Rationale**: Polestar's survival is contingent on closing its funding gap and reaching positive adjusted EBITDA. No strategy is executable without financial stability.

**Actions**:
- Execute workforce restructuring with minimal disruption to product development and customer-facing teams.
- Develop software-as-a-service revenue: subscription features (enhanced driver assistance, performance modes, connectivity), OTA updates, and data monetisation.
- Target positive adjusted EBITDA in 2025 and positive free cash flow after investments in 2027 as non-negotiable milestones.
- Evaluate selective equity issuance to close the $350 million funding gap, timed to coincide with positive news catalysts.

**KPI**: Positive adjusted EBITDA by end of 2025; gross margin improvement to +5% by 2026.

---

### ✅ Recommendation 3: Sharpen Brand Differentiation Through Design and Sustainability Leadership *(Ongoing)*

**Rationale**: Polestar's brand is its most durable VRIO asset. In a market where EV technology is rapidly commoditising, brand identity is the primary source of sustainable competitive advantage.

**Actions**:
- Invest in the Polestar 5 GT as a halo product — an 884hp performance flagship that unambiguously stakes out the performance-luxury territory against the Porsche Taycan and Lucid Air.
- Develop a "Polestar Sustainability Index" — a publicly accessible carbon benchmark for the automotive industry — to own the ESG conversation and generate earned media.
- Launch signature "Polestar Design Spaces" in key global cities to make the brand tangible and aspirational.
- Pursue the new design direction under Philipp Römers to project greater performance confidence, particularly for US and Chinese buyers, while preserving the Scandinavian minimalist core.

**KPI**: Brand consideration scores; Net Promoter Score; ESG media share of voice.

---

### ✅ Recommendation 4: Accelerate US Manufacturing and Market Re-Entry *(Medium-Term: 24–48 months)*

**Rationale**: The US is the world's second-largest premium automotive market. Tariff exposure has effectively excluded Polestar. Domestic US manufacturing is the only viable path back.

**Actions**:
- Prioritise a US production facility (in partnership with Geely/Volvo infrastructure) for the Polestar 7.
- Engage US federal and state governments for IRA manufacturing incentives (up to $7,500 per vehicle tax credit).
- Develop a US-specific brand campaign emphasising Swedish design identity, distancing from Chinese manufacturing narrative.
- Partner with US charging networks (Electrify America, ChargePoint) to address range anxiety.

**KPI**: US manufacturing operational by 2027; US sales representing 15% of global deliveries by 2027.

---

### ✅ Recommendation 5: Manage the Geely Relationship as a Strategic Asset, Not a Liability *(Medium-Term: 6–18 months)*

**Rationale**: Geely provides critical technology, manufacturing, and financial backing — but also creates brand perception challenges in Western markets.

**Actions**:
- Formalise a **"Polestar Independence Charter"** — a publicly communicated governance framework establishing Polestar's autonomy in design, brand strategy, and marketing.
- Ensure the Board of Directors includes a majority of independent, Western directors with credibility in automotive, technology, and ESG domains.
- Communicate transparently about the Geely relationship — emphasising technology partnership rather than ownership control.
- Explore long-term options for reducing Geely's ownership stake through a secondary public offering as financial performance improves.

**KPI**: Brand perception surveys in US and European markets; institutional investor sentiment.

---

### ✅ Recommendation 6: Launch Polestar 7 as the Volume Enabler *(Medium-Term: 18–36 months)*

**Rationale**: Polestar 7, a premium compact SUV planned to be produced in Europe, will strengthen global manufacturing diversification. This is Polestar's most important strategic product — enabling entry into the highest-volume premium segment.

**Actions**:
- Price the Polestar 7 to compete directly with the BMW iX1, Volvo EX40, and Audi Q4 e-tron.
- Manufacture in Europe to eliminate tariff exposure and qualify for EU subsidies.
- Position as "accessible Scandinavian performance" — bringing the brand's DNA to a broader audience without diluting the premium positioning of the Polestar 5.
- Use the launch to expand the direct-to-consumer platform's capabilities, including AR configuration tools and subscription financing.

**KPI**: 10,000+ order intake within 6 months of launch; gross margin positive from launch.

## CONCLUSION

Polestar stands at a genuinely precarious strategic crossroads. The company possesses a distinctive and valuable brand identity, access to world-class technology platforms, and operates in one of the fastest-growing segments of the global automotive market. Polestar's focus on premium, high-margin models and its strategic partnerships with Geely and Volvo provide a buffer — but these advantages are being rapidly eroded by financial fragility and intensifying competition.

The frameworks applied in this analysis converge on a consistent diagnosis: **Polestar's Differentiation Focus strategy remains fundamentally sound, but it is being undermined by execution failures, financial weakness, and strategic drift**. The path forward requires ruthless prioritisation — defend and deepen the European base, stabilise the finances, launch the Polestar 5 as a brand-defining halo product, and build toward the Polestar 7 as the volume enabler. The US market must be addressed through domestic manufacturing rather than tariff exposure.

Polestar's North Star has always been to prove that sustainable performance is not a compromise. The strategic challenge now is to prove that sustainable *business performance* is equally achievable.
