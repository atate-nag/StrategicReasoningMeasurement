# Jarir Bookstore: Strategic Case Study
## *The Omnichannel Margin Squeeze — Navigating Competitive Disruption in Saudi Arabia's Digital Retail Era*

---

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Jarir Marketing Company, commonly known as Jarir Bookstore, is a leading Saudi Arabian retail chain specialising in books, office and school supplies, consumer electronics, toys, and educational products. Founded in 1974 in Riyadh as a modest bookstore, it has grown into a major retailer operating 74 showrooms across Saudi Arabia and other Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC) countries, including Qatar, the United Arab Emirates, Kuwait, and Bahrain. Despite its market-leading position, the company faces a compounding strategic dilemma: average revenue per store declined by 1.3% YoY to SAR 150.4 million in 2024, margins have been pressured by intensifying competition, and the company is opting for promotions, discounts, and price cuts to maintain market share — however, this is pressuring margins. Gross margins have declined by 288 basis points to 12.2% (2024 end) over the last five years, while operating margins have contracted by 299 basis points to 9.7% (2024 end).

This case study diagnoses the root causes of this "omnichannel margin squeeze," analyses the strategic situation through PESTEL, Porter's Five Forces, the Resource-Based View (RBV), and a Value Chain analysis, synthesises findings into a SWOT, evaluates strategic options via the Ansoff Matrix, and concludes with a prioritised action plan.

## 2. COMPANY BACKGROUND & CONTEXT

Jarir Marketing Co. was founded in Riyadh in 1974 as a small bookstore. It then became a Saudi joint-stock company and has been listed on the Saudi stock exchange since 2003, with a paid-up capital of 1,200 million Saudi riyals. With its functioning retail, wholesale, and e-commerce sectors, Jarir operates in Saudi Arabia as well as in the GCC countries.

Jarir operates through two divisions: retail (including online sales), under the trademark of Jarir Bookstore, and a wholesale division. Jarir is active in the Gulf Cooperation Council, trading in Office and School Supplies, Children's Toys and Educational Aids, Arabic and English Books and Publications, Arts and Crafts Materials, Computer Peripherals and Software, Mobile Phones and Accessories, Audio Visual Instruments, Photography Tools, Smart Television and Maintenance of Computers and Electronic items.

Jarir Bookstore has 73 showrooms inside and outside the Kingdom, of which 3 were opened during the year 2024: a showroom in Doha Mall, Doha, Qatar; a showroom in Al-Ared district on Abu Bakr Al-Siddiq Road, Riyadh; and during fiscal year 2025, Jarir aims to expand by opening 4 to 5 new showrooms.

Financially, full-year revenue climbed to SAR 10.83 billion in 2024, reflecting a compound annual growth rate of approximately 4–5% in recent years amid diversification into e-commerce and regional markets. Jarir reported a net profit of SAR 974 million in 2024, a slight increase from SAR 973 million a year earlier, with gross sales increasing 2.2% year-on-year, mainly due to higher sales in the smartphone segment.

## 3. THE STRATEGIC SITUATION

### 3.1 The Core Problem: A Structural Margin Squeeze

Jarir's strategic challenge is not a single crisis but a slow-burning structural deterioration driven by three intersecting forces.

**Force 1 — Sales-Mix Deterioration.** The H1 2024 profit decline of 3.3% was attributed to a 1.1% year-on-year decrease in gross profits, mainly due to a shift in the sales mix towards the relatively less profitable sections. Selling and marketing costs, as well as general and administrative expenses, also increased during the six-month period.

**Force 2 — Per-Store Revenue Erosion.** Retail outlet sales dropped by 2.4% YoY to SAR 7.6 billion in 2024, as customers continued their move towards online purchases, where Jarir saw a healthy 25.1% YoY increase in revenues. Performance in the online segment was boosted by the launch of a new version of the Jarir Bookstore app.

**Force 3 — Competitive Encirclement.** Apple is set to launch its online store in the Kingdom in 2025 and a physical store in 2026. This would be negative as Apple products constitute 25–30% of company's sales, however, margins on the aforementioned are quite thin.

### 3.2 The Counterweight: A Digital Bright Spot

Revenue growth is forecast at 4.3/5.3% YoY in 2025/26, driven by store additions; per-store revenue is expected to remain largely unchanged. Furthermore, Jarir signed a non-binding memorandum of understanding (MoU) with Aman Non-Bank Financial Services and Electronic Payments Co. (Egypt) to establish a consumer finance company, with an initial capital of SAR 100 million — which can act as a positive trigger for the stock.

### 3.3 Why This Matters Strategically

The double-digit increase in sales seen in 2023, driven by Buy Now Pay Later (BNPL), could not carry on in 2024 where revenue growth slowed down to 2.2% YoY. In the backdrop of increasing competition and changing consumer preferences, revenues are forecast to grow at a medium-term CAGR of 4.2% over 2024–28. The company is at a strategic inflection point where incremental adjustments are insufficient — a coherent strategic repositioning is required.

## 4. EXTERNAL ANALYSIS

### 4.1 PESTEL Analysis

**Political / Regulatory**

Saudi Arabia's Vision 2030 programme is a broadly positive structural tailwind. The Saudi Arabia Retail market's economic diversification under Vision 2030 is the driving factor for market growth. The government's Vision 2030 initiative aims to enhance digital infrastructure, further supporting online retail trends. Enhanced connectivity is expected to drive online bookstore sales, as more consumers engage with e-commerce platforms. However, the imminent arrival of Apple's own retail stores — facilitated by evolving FDI rules — represents a direct regulatory-enabled competitive threat.

**Economic**

The Saudi Arabia e-commerce market, which was worth US$24.67 billion in 2024, is expected to grow at a CAGR of 12.10% during the period from 2025 to 2033 and reach US$68.94 billion by 2033. The IMF reports that the nation has recorded a high per capita GDP, which is expected to continue growing, and a high individual net income. The nation's purchasing power may rise due to per capita GDP growth, predicted to fuel demand for the clothing and fashion sectors. Higher disposable incomes also drive consumers to look for ease and variety when making purchases, which makes e-commerce platforms a desirable choice.

**Social / Cultural**

Based on the CST Commission of Saudi Arabia, in 2023 up to 63.7% of internet users purchased goods or services online in the country, and 74.6% of these transactions were made by women. This demographic shift is directionally positive for Jarir's e-commerce ambitions but intensifies the need for a seamless, personalised digital experience.

**Technological**

There is a noticeable shift toward customised shopping experiences and marketing strategies that make use of data analytics as well as artificial intelligence to understand consumer preferences better. Anticipating the future, augmented reality (AR) and virtual reality (VR) technologies are expected to be more integrated into the shopping process, improving customer interaction and product visualisation.

**Environmental**

Jarir's supply chain is supported by a centralised, fully automated warehouse in Riyadh spanning 800,500 square feet, integrated with an enterprise resource planning (ERP) system for efficient inventory management and distribution to over 80 stores across Saudi Arabia and the GCC. Local procurement accounts for 78% of spending, totalling SAR 7.77 billion in 2024, with rigorous supplier audits ensuring compliance and resilience. In line with sustainability goals, Jarir has implemented eco-friendly packaging practices.

**Legal**

Jarir operates its stores under the Jarir Bookstore and Rocco banners, and the company has business presence across Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, Bahrain, Qatar, the UAE, and Egypt. Saudi Arabia's Electronic Commerce Law and the E-Commerce Council provide a structured regulatory environment that both protects existing operators and facilitates new entrants.

### 4.2 Porter's Five Forces

**Threat of New Entrants — MODERATE TO HIGH**

The leading e-commerce companies in Saudi Arabia include Amazon.sa, Noon, Jarir Bookstore, eXtra, Shein, AliExpress, Carrefour, Salla, and Nana Direct. These platforms span multiple categories from fashion and electronics to groceries and quick commerce. The capital required to replicate Jarir's showroom network and automated warehouse provides some physical-store protection, but the digital channel has no such moat.

**Bargaining Power of Suppliers — MODERATE TO HIGH (and rising)**

Apple is set to launch its online store in the Kingdom in 2025 and a physical store in 2026 — this would be negative as Apple products constitute 25–30% of the company's sales. This fundamentally alters the Apple relationship from partnership to competition, representing the single most material supplier-power shift in Jarir's recent history.

**Bargaining Power of Buyers — HIGH**

The rising number of online platforms and increase in physical stores has resulted in heightened competition for Jarir. The company is opting for promotions, discounts, and price cuts to maintain market share — however, this is pressuring margins. Saudi consumers are digitally empowered with near-zero switching costs.

**Threat of Substitutes — HIGH**

Change in consumer preferences towards HORECA and other entertainment avenues, the rising number of physical and online stores, and the launch of Apple stores dampen Jarir's investment case. Digital content (e-books, streaming) substitutes for physical books, while cloud software substitutes for boxed software.

**Competitive Rivalry — HIGH**

Jarir's top-line increased by +2% YoY to SAR 2.9 billion for Q4 2024, mostly driven by higher sales in electronics, computers, and smartphone products — a consistent driver throughout 2024. Sequentially, Jarir's total sales also improved, while gross profits decreased 15%. Gross margins followed suit, with these margin contractions happening despite greater margins from smartphones on the back of discounts from vendors.

**Five-Forces Summary:** The configuration is structurally unfavourable and deteriorating. Rising supplier power (Apple turning competitor), high buyer power (digitally empowered consumers), and intense rivalry (Amazon, Noon, eXtra) collectively compress margins regardless of operational efficiency. This is the structural engine of the margin squeeze.

## 5. INTERNAL ANALYSIS

### 5.1 Resource-Based View (RBV)

The RBV framework (Barney, 1991) asks whether a firm's resources and capabilities are **V**aluable, **R**are, **I**nimitable, and **N**on-substitutable (VRIN).

| Resource / Capability | V | R | I | N | Competitive Implication |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Brand equity & trust ("Jarir" name) | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | **Sustained competitive advantage** |
| 73-showroom physical network | ✓ | ✓ | Partially | Partially | **Temporary advantage** |
| Automated 800,500 sq ft warehouse + ERP | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | Partially | **Sustained logistics advantage** |
| Apple/Samsung authorised dealer status | ✓ | ✓ | ✗ | ✗ | **Eroding advantage** |
| Debt-free balance sheet + cash generation | ✓ | ✓ | Partially | Partially | **Strategic flexibility** |
| Customer loyalty / repeat purchase base | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | Partially | **Sustained advantage** |
| Rocco own-brand products | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | Partially | **Underexploited advantage** |
| E-commerce platform + app | ✓ | ✗ | ✗ | ✗ | **Parity, not advantage** |
| BNPL / financial services capability | ✓ | Partially | Partially | Partially | **Emerging advantage** |

Jarir was established in Riyadh in 1974 as a small bookshop on Jarir Street, from where it derives its name. In 2017, Jarir was recognised as the 7th "Most Valuable Retail Brand" in Saudi Arabia (BRANDZ Top 20 Most Valuable Saudi Arabian Brands). In 2018, Jarir figured in the "Top 100 Listed Companies" in the Arab World (Forbes, Middle East). These recognitions confirm that brand equity is a genuine VRIN asset.

The RBV analysis reveals that Jarir's most durable advantages are rooted in its physical presence and customer trust. Its weakest strategic position is in digital capabilities, where it has achieved parity but not superiority. Critically, the Apple dealer relationship — historically a source of competitive advantage — is being converted into a competitive threat, making it neither inimitable nor non-substitutable.

**Support Activities:**

*Firm Infrastructure:* Jarir maintains its commitment to shareholder returns with a payout ratio exceeding 90%, underscoring strong cash flow generation and earnings quality.

**Value Chain Summary:** Jarir's value chain is strongest in physical logistics, brand, after-sales service, and customer loyalty. It is weakest in digital technology development. The key strategic implication is that Jarir must invest in its digital weaknesses without destroying its physical strengths.

## 7. STRATEGIC OPTIONS: ANSOFF MATRIX

The Ansoff Matrix (Ansoff, 1957) provides a structured framework for evaluating Jarir's growth options across four quadrants.

**Quadrant 1 — Market Penetration (Existing Products / Existing Markets)**
Jarir can deepen its share in existing Saudi markets through enhanced loyalty programmes, personalised digital marketing, and after-sales service expansion. Channel-wise, online segment performance was boosted by the launch of a new version of the Jarir Bookstore app, with online revenues growing 25.1% YoY in 2024. *Risk: LOW. Return potential: MODERATE.* This is a defensive but necessary strategy to protect the core.

**Quadrant 2 — Market Development (Existing Products / New Markets)**
During fiscal year 2025, Jarir aims to expand by opening 4 to 5 new showrooms. Remote cities in Saudi Arabia — currently served only digitally — represent an underpenetrated domestic market. *Risk: MODERATE. Return potential: MODERATE-HIGH.*

**Quadrant 3 — Product Development (New Products / Existing Markets)**
Jarir signed a non-binding MoU with Aman Non-Bank Financial Services and Electronic Payments Co. (Egypt) to establish a consumer finance company, with an initial capital of SAR 100 million. This can act as a positive trigger for the stock. Own-brand expansion (Rocco) would improve margins and reduce supplier dependency. *Risk: MODERATE. Return potential: HIGH (margin-accretive).* This is the highest strategic priority.

**Quadrant 4 — Diversification (New Products / New Markets)**
Entry into educational technology, digital content, and smart home ecosystem services represents diversification aligned with Vision 2030's education agenda. *Risk: HIGH. Return potential: HIGH but uncertain.* This should be a medium-term option.

**Strategic Choice:** The coherent strategic direction is a combination of **Market Penetration** (defend the core through omnichannel excellence) + **Product Development** (expand own-brand and financial services to restore margins) + selective **Market Development** (GCC and remote-city expansion). Diversification into EdTech should be explored as a longer-term horizon.

## 8. RECOMMENDATIONS & ACTION PLAN

The following six recommendations are sequenced into three horizons — Immediate (0–12 months), Medium-Term (1–3 years), and Long-Term (3–5 years).

---

### Recommendation 1: Accelerate Own-Brand (Rocco) Expansion to Restore Margins

**Strategic Logic:** The root cause of margin compression is the sales-mix shift toward low-margin branded electronics. Jarir operates its stores under the Jarir Bookstore and Rocco banners. Rocco own-brand products carry structurally higher margins and are inimitable by digital competitors. Expanding Rocco across stationery, school supplies, accessories, and peripherals directly addresses the gross margin problem identified in the RBV analysis (Rocco is a VRIN asset currently underexploited).

**Actions:**
- Commission a product-category audit to identify the 10–15 SKU clusters where Rocco can achieve >20% gross margin within 18 months.
- Increase Rocco's share of total revenue to a target of 15–20% within 3 years.
- Invest in Rocco brand marketing to elevate it from a value-tier to a trusted quality brand.

**KPI:** Gross margin recovery to ≥13.5% by 2027.
**Horizon:** Immediate and Medium-Term.

---

### Recommendation 2: Launch and Scale the Consumer Finance Subsidiary

**Strategic Logic:** The double-digit increase in sales seen in 2023, which was driven by Buy Now Pay Later (BNPL), could not carry on in 2024 where revenue growth slowed down to 2.2% YoY. The BNPL-driven growth of 2023 demonstrates that financial services can be a significant demand driver. The Aman MoU is a strategic seed that must be formalised and scaled. Consumer finance — instalment plans, co-branded credit, loyalty-linked financing — would increase basket size, improve customer retention, and generate fee income structurally higher-margin than product sales.

**Actions:**
- Formalise and capitalise the Aman JV within 12 months.
- Integrate consumer finance options directly into the app and at point-of-sale in all showrooms.
- Use transaction data from the finance subsidiary to power personalised cross-sell and upsell campaigns.

**KPI:** Consumer finance revenue contributing SAR 150–200 million annually by Year 3.
**Horizon:** Immediate (formalise JV) and Medium-Term (scale).

---

### Recommendation 3: Transform Physical Showrooms into "Experience Hubs"

**Strategic Logic:** Jarir's 73-showroom network is a VRIN asset that digital competitors cannot replicate. However, if showrooms function merely as product warehouses, they will lose to online platforms on price and convenience. Analysts highlighted that the company was operating in an extremely competitive retail environment and was reinventing itself by adding new customer-oriented product categories and using state-of-the-art technology to provide a pleasant, stress-free shopping experience. The solution is to transform showrooms into experiential destinations — product demonstrations, tech workshops, gaming zones, and after-sales service hubs — that justify the physical visit and create dwell time no website can replicate.

**Actions:**
- Redesign the 10 highest-traffic showrooms as "Experience Hubs" with dedicated Apple/Samsung demo zones, a Jarir Academy (workshops on tech, art, coding), and a gaming lounge.
- Expand after-sales service capacity — already growing healthily — in all showrooms.
- Train store staff as product experts and brand ambassadors.

**KPI:** Per-store revenue growth returned to +3% YoY by 2026; Net Promoter Score improvement of 10+ points.
**Horizon:** Medium-Term.

---

### Recommendation 4: Invest in AI-Driven Digital Personalisation

**Strategic Logic:** There is a noticeable shift toward customised shopping experiences and marketing strategies that make use of data analytics as well as artificial intelligence to understand consumer preferences better. Jarir's online channel grew 25.1% in 2024, but sustaining this growth requires systematic investment in AI-driven personalisation, recommendation engines, and seamless omnichannel integration. Jarir leverages business intelligence tools to analyse customer buying patterns and preferences, enabling personalised marketing campaigns and product recommendations. This capability must be scaled from a tactical tool to a core competitive system.

**Actions:**
- Deploy a unified Customer Data Platform (CDP) integrating in-store, online, and app behaviour into a single customer profile.
- Implement AI-driven product recommendations and personalised marketing across all digital touchpoints.
- Expand same-day delivery to all major Saudi cities within 18 months, leveraging the automated Riyadh warehouse.
- Extend the remote-city digital advertising strategy — using remarketing lists populated with users who had previously visited the website in remote cities, viewed specific product pages, and watched relevant videos — to cover all cities without a physical Jarir presence.

**KPI:** Online channel to reach 25% of total revenue by 2027; customer retention rate improvement of 8%.
**Horizon:** Immediate and Medium-Term.

---

### Recommendation 5: Proactively Manage the Apple Transition — Diversify the Electronics Portfolio

**Strategic Logic:** Apple is set to launch its online store in the Kingdom in 2025 and physical store in 2026. This would be negative as Apple products constitute 25–30% of the company's sales, however, margins on the aforementioned are quite thin. Jarir must proactively diversify its electronics portfolio to reduce Apple dependency, while leveraging its existing Apple relationship for maximum value during the transition window.

**Actions:**
- Negotiate enhanced terms with Apple during the 2025 transition window, including exclusive after-sales service agreements, trade-in programme partnerships, and accessory exclusives.
- Expand partnerships with alternative premium brands: Samsung (already authorised), Huawei, Sony, Xiaomi, and OPPO.
- Increase the share of higher-margin categories — accessories, peripherals, smart home devices, and educational technology — to offset lower-margin smartphone sales.

**KPI:** Apple's share of total revenue reduced from 25–30% to below 15% by 2027, with no net revenue loss in electronics.
**Horizon:** Immediate (negotiation) and Medium-Term (portfolio shift).

---

### Recommendation 6: Selective GCC and Remote-City Physical Expansion

**Strategic Logic:** Revenue growth is expected to be driven by store additions; per-store revenue is expected to remain largely unchanged. This suggests that expansion is a necessary but not sufficient growth driver. Jarir should be selective — prioritising high-potential GCC markets and remote Saudi cities where digital demand is proven but physical presence is absent.

**Actions:**
- Open 4–5 showrooms per year as planned, prioritising UAE (Abu Dhabi, Dubai) and underserved Saudi cities (Hail, Tabuk, Jizan) where digital demand data justifies physical investment.
- Use the remote-city digital advertising data — including radius targeting and city-level conversion analytics — as a demand-signal tool to identify the next optimal showroom locations.
- Explore asset-light "Jarir Express" formats for secondary cities to reduce capital commitment.

**KPI:** 85+ showrooms by 2028; new showrooms achieving SAR 150 million+ revenue per store within 24 months of opening.
**Horizon:** Immediate and Medium-Term.

## 9. CONCLUSION

Jarir Bookstore's strategic situation is one of structural transition rather than existential crisis. Due to the COVID-19 pandemic, online sales rose six-fold in the second quarter of 2020 compared to the previous year — demonstrating that Jarir has successfully reinvented itself before. Its brand equity, logistics infrastructure, customer loyalty, and debt-free balance sheet are genuine and durable competitive assets. New store launches, a strong dividend yield of 5.8%, and plans to enter into consumer finance business are key positive triggers for the company.

The analytical chain is clear and internally consistent:

- The **PESTEL** analysis identifies a structurally favourable macro environment (Vision 2030, e-commerce CAGR of 12.1%) but a competitive landscape that is rapidly intensifying.
- **Porter's Five Forces** confirm that rising supplier power (Apple) and high buyer power (digitally empowered consumers) are the primary margin destroyers.
- The **RBV** reveals that Jarir's most durable assets are physical and relational — brand, network, logistics, loyalty — not digital, making digital investment a strategic imperative rather than an optional upgrade.
- The **Value Chain** analysis shows that the highest-margin activities (own-brand, after-sales service, financial services) are currently underscaled.
- The **SWOT** synthesis crystallises the core tension: the same electronics growth that drives top-line revenue is destroying margins, and the company's most important brand partner is about to become a direct competitor.
- The **Ansoff Matrix** directs strategy toward Product Development (own-brand, consumer finance) and selective Market Development (GCC, remote cities) as the highest-return, manageable-risk options.

Implemented in sequence, the six recommendations form a coherent programme: restoring margins through own-brand and financial services, defending the physical estate through experiential transformation, closing the digital capability gap through AI investment, managing the Apple transition proactively, and growing the physical footprint selectively. Jarir now is considered the market leader in the Middle East for consumer IT products, Electronics, Office supplies and Books. The next strategic chapter must convert that market leadership into a margin-resilient, digitally-amplified business model fit for Saudi Arabia's Vision 2030 economy.
