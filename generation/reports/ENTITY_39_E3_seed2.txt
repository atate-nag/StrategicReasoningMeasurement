# Strategic Case Study: Trustpilot and the Monetisation–Integrity Dilemma

## A Two-Sided Platform at a Crossroads

---

## 1. Introduction and Case Overview

Trustpilot Group plc was founded in Denmark in 2007 by Peter Holten Mühlmann. As of mid-2025, it hosts 330 million reviews and has 60 million monthly active users. From a student project in Aarhus, it has grown into a publicly listed company on the London Stock Exchange, positioning itself as the world's largest independent review platform. Trustpilot operates a dual-sided platform that exhibits network effects and, as a SaaS business, consistently achieves gross margins over 80%.

Yet beneath this financial success lies a structural tension that constitutes the central strategic problem of this case: **Trustpilot's revenue depends on charging businesses for subscription services, while its value to consumers depends on those businesses being unable to manipulate the reviews those subscriptions fund**. This is not merely a reputational risk — it is a contradiction embedded in the platform's architecture. The following analysis examines this dilemma through the lenses of two-sided market theory, Porter's Five Forces, a SWOT analysis, and a stakeholder trust framework, before proposing a coherent action plan.

## 2. Business Model Description

### 2.1 The Freemium–Subscription Architecture

Trustpilot operates on a freemium model with four paid tiers ranging from $299/month (annual billing) to custom enterprise pricing. The platform requires 12-month commitments across all plans. Businesses at the free tier can claim their profile and respond to reviews, but advanced tools — analytics dashboards, review-invitation automation, and the embeddable "TrustBox" widget — sit behind the paywall. The platform has approximately 26,740 paying business customers out of over 1.1 million businesses with profiles, meaning the vast majority of listed businesses contribute no revenue.

The business logic is straightforward: Trustpilot uses the open, free review ecosystem to build consumer traffic and review volume, then monetises that traffic by selling tools to businesses that want to leverage those reviews commercially. Businesses can engage meaningfully with feedback, but they can never pay to remove reviews, and the guidelines that govern the platform are applied fairly regardless of whether a business is a paying customer. This is Trustpilot's stated position. The strategic problem, examined below, is whether the architecture consistently delivers on it.

### 2.2 Scale and Network Effects

In 2024, more than 61 million reviews were written on Trustpilot, a 15% year-on-year increase, bringing total active reviews to 301 million by end of December 2024. This scale is strategically significant because, as two-sided platform theory holds, platforms generate trust among users and thereby remove or mitigate asymmetric information problems; network effects are the mechanism through which platform value compounds as participation increases. The more consumers write reviews, the more useful the platform is to businesses; the more businesses engage, the more consumers are invited to review — a classic cross-side positive feedback loop. This scale is also Trustpilot's primary defensive moat against competitors.

## 3. The Strategic Situation: The Monetisation–Integrity Dilemma

### 3.1 The Core Tension

The central strategic situation is this: Trustpilot sells tools to businesses to help them collect, display, and respond to reviews. The more reviews a business collects — especially positive ones — the more valuable those tools appear. This creates an incentive structure in which the platform's commercial interests and its integrity obligations point in opposite directions.

The most visible manifestation is the **TrustBox widget**. Paying Trustpilot subscribers using the TrustBox feature can filter review scores to only display high-scoring reviews. This feature is sold as a marketing tool, but from a consumer perspective it means that the reviews displayed on a business's own website have been curated by that business — the opposite of what an independent review platform is supposed to provide. This is not illegal, but it undermines the core value proposition: that Trustpilot reviews represent an unfiltered, authentic signal.

### 3.2 The Fake Review Problem

Trustpilot removed 4.5 million fake reviews in 2024, amounting to 7.4% of the total number of reviews submitted that year (up from 6.1% in 2023), with 90% removed by automated technology. The rising percentage is partly a sign of improving detection, but it also confirms that manipulation is growing in absolute terms. Critics argue that Trustpilot allows scam companies to inflate their reputations with fake positives while genuine critics are silenced. The allegation is that the platform's commercial model creates a structural bias: businesses that pay have faster escalation paths and more engagement with the platform's enforcement team, while non-paying businesses and individual consumers receive slower, less responsive service.

### 3.3 The Grizzly Research Allegation

In 2025, short-seller Grizzly Research published a report making the commercial tension explicit. Grizzly alleged that Trustpilot refused to remove obviously misleading reviews unless businesses paid for subscriptions, and that while revenue grew 18.4% in 2023 and 19.5% in 2024, "this growth relies on extorting businesses while eroding trust in the brand." Trustpilot rejected the claims as misleading, but the allegation crystallises the structural concern: if businesses perceive that subscribing is the only way to obtain fair treatment, then the subscription model is not selling analytics — it is selling access to justice. Whether or not the allegation is accurate, its plausibility is itself a strategic liability, because platform integrity depends not just on actual fairness but on perceived fairness.

### 3.4 Regulatory Pressure

The external environment compounds the internal tension. Throughout 2024, businesses systematically exploited Digital Services Act Article 16 reporting mechanisms to remove unfavourable reviews through false defamation claims, and platforms like Trustpilot erred on the side of caution to avoid legal liability. This creates a perverse dynamic: the very regulation designed to protect consumers is being weaponised by businesses to suppress legitimate criticism, and Trustpilot's risk-averse compliance response inadvertently facilitates this. Article 7 of the amended Unfair Commercial Practices Directive prohibits misleading practices like fake or manipulated reviews, and platforms face obligations to implement systems for review moderation and user education on authenticity. Non-compliance carries regulatory sanction; over-compliance (removing reviews too readily) destroys the consumer value proposition.

## 4. Strategic Analysis

### 4.1 Two-Sided Market Theory

Two-sided platform theory (following Rochet and Tirole's foundational work, and developed in the digital context) holds that a platform must balance the needs of two distinct user groups whose participation is interdependent. Platforms can be seen as reducing transaction costs, and network effects mean that the value each side derives from the platform increases with participation on the other side. For Trustpilot, this means:

- **Consumer side**: Value = access to authentic, unmanipulated reviews that reduce purchase risk.
- **Business side**: Value = access to consumer attention and tools to leverage positive reputation.

The dilemma arises because these two value propositions are in tension. Businesses want to maximise positive visibility; consumers want unfiltered truth. A platform that tilts too far toward business interests loses consumer trust and, with it, the consumer traffic that makes the platform valuable to businesses in the first place. This is the **platform integrity trap**: the more a platform monetises the business side, the more it risks degrading the consumer-side value that sustains the entire ecosystem.

A 2024 study found that 75% of consumers are concerned about encountering fake reviews online, and 89% of global consumers check online reviews as part of their buying journey, with 49% considering positive reviews one of their top three purchase influences. This data establishes why the integrity problem is commercially existential, not merely reputational: if consumers stop trusting Trustpilot reviews, they stop reading them, and the entire monetisation model collapses. The warrant is direct — consumer trust is not a soft value; it is the demand-side input that makes the supply-side (business subscriptions) viable.

### 4.2 Porter's Five Forces

**Threat of New Entrants (Medium–High)**: The review platform market has low marginal costs for additional reviews, but high switching costs for consumers who have built habits around specific platforms. However, Google remains the most-used website for reading online reviews at 81% usage in 2024 (down from 87% in 2023), while consumer use of Trustpilot has increased by 3% since 2022. Google's dominance as a free alternative represents a permanent structural threat: it offers reviews without a subscription fee to businesses and with greater search integration. The barrier to entry for a new standalone review platform is not capital — it is the chicken-and-egg problem of simultaneously acquiring consumers and businesses.

**Bargaining Power of Buyers (Businesses) (Medium)**: Businesses can choose not to subscribe, and many do not — only ~26,740 out of 1.1 million profiled businesses are paying customers. However, for businesses that depend on their Trustpilot score as a conversion signal, the cost of not subscribing (slower dispute resolution, no analytics, no widget tools) creates lock-in. The Grizzly allegation, if accurate, would represent extreme buyer power exploitation — charging for what should be a fair process.

**Bargaining Power of Suppliers (Reviewers/Consumers) (Low–Medium)**: Individual consumers have minimal bargaining power, but collectively they are the platform's entire value base. Around two-thirds of European consumers regard customer reviews (65%) and star ratings (63%) as either "very useful" or "useful" during buying decisions. If consumers migrate to Google Reviews or social proof mechanisms, Trustpilot's supply of authentic content evaporates. Consumer power is diffuse but existential.

**Threat of Substitutes (High)**: Google Reviews, Amazon ratings, social media recommendations, and sector-specific platforms (TripAdvisor, Glassdoor) all compete for the same consumer attention. Unlike Trustpilot, Google Reviews are free for businesses to manage and are embedded in search results. The substitute threat is the primary reason Trustpilot cannot afford to let integrity deteriorate.

**Competitive Rivalry (Medium)**: The review platform market is not a pure commodity — brand recognition and review volume create differentiation. Trustpilot's 301 million active reviews represent a data moat that is difficult to replicate quickly. However, the market is not winner-takes-all: different platforms dominate different verticals and geographies.

**Five Forces Summary**: The most dangerous forces for Trustpilot are the **threat of substitutes** (especially Google) and the **internal coherence risk** — that its own monetisation model erodes the consumer trust that differentiates it from free alternatives. This analysis connects to the core dilemma: Trustpilot's competitive position depends on being more trustworthy than free substitutes, yet its revenue model creates pressures that undermine that trustworthiness.

### 4.3 SWOT Analysis

| | **Internal** | **External** |
|---|---|---|
| **Positive** | **Strengths**: Scale (301M reviews, 61M/year); AI-driven fraud detection (90% automated removal); SaaS gross margins >80%; brand recognition; network effects | **Opportunities**: Growing consumer reliance on reviews; EU regulatory framework that could disadvantage less compliant competitors; AI-enhanced moderation; B2B data analytics expansion |
| **Negative** | **Weaknesses**: Structural conflict between monetisation and integrity; TrustBox filter feature undermines consumer trust; perceived pay-to-play dynamics; small paying customer base (~26,740) relative to total profiles | **Threats**: Google Reviews as free substitute; DSA abuse weaponising compliance against the platform; fake review sophistication growing; short-seller allegations damaging investor confidence |

**Key SWOT Insight**: Trustpilot's strengths (scale, AI, brand) are precisely the assets that can resolve its weaknesses — but only if the company treats integrity investment as a strategic priority rather than a cost centre. The opportunity presented by EU regulation is that stricter rules raise the floor for all competitors, potentially converting Trustpilot's existing compliance investment into a durable competitive advantage. The threat is that regulatory complexity creates operational drag that disproportionately burdens a mid-sized platform relative to Google's resources.

### 4.4 The Trust Paradox: A Stakeholder Analysis

Trustpilot faces a **three-party principal problem**:

1. **Consumers** need unfiltered, authentic reviews. Their trust is the platform's demand-side input.
2. **Businesses** need a fair, responsive process and commercial tools to leverage reviews. Their subscriptions are the platform's revenue.
3. **Regulators/Society** need the platform to function as a genuine public information good, free from manipulation.

The paradox is that Trustpilot's stated mission — "to be the universal symbol of trust" — is pursued through engagement with regulators and governments, including a founding role in the Coalition for Trusted Reviews. This is strategically rational: if Trustpilot can shape the regulatory standard for trusted reviews, it converts its compliance costs into a barrier to entry for competitors. But this strategy only works if the platform's actual practices match its stated principles. The Grizzly allegations and the TrustBox filter feature create a credibility gap between the mission statement and the product architecture.

## 5. Root Cause Diagnosis

The analysis above converges on a single root cause: **Trustpilot has not structurally separated its integrity function from its commercial function**. The same platform that sells subscription tools to businesses also adjudicates disputes between those businesses and their reviewers. This is analogous to a court that charges litigants for access to its dispute resolution services — structurally, it creates the appearance (and potentially the reality) of justice being available for purchase.

This root cause explains:
- Why the TrustBox filter feature exists (it is commercially valuable to business subscribers).
- Why businesses allege preferential treatment for paying customers (commercial teams and integrity teams share the same customer base).
- Why the DSA compliance problem emerged (risk-averse removal decisions are easier than nuanced adjudication when legal liability is asymmetric).
- Why fake review rates are rising in absolute terms despite improving detection (the incentive to manufacture reviews grows with the commercial value of a high TrustScore).

## 6. Recommendations and Action Plan

The recommendations below are derived directly from the analysis: each addresses a specific mechanism identified in the diagnosis, and each is warranted by the evidence.

### Recommendation 1: Structurally Separate Integrity Governance from Commercial Operations

**What**: Create an independent Trust Integrity Board (TIB) with a separate reporting line from the commercial organisation, staffed by external members (consumer advocates, legal experts, academics), with published quarterly transparency reports on review removal decisions, dispute outcomes, and enforcement actions.

**Why (Warrant)**: The root cause diagnosis establishes that the integrity–monetisation conflict is structural, not merely cultural. Cultural commitments ("businesses cannot pay to remove reviews") are insufficient when the same organisation sells to businesses and adjudicates their disputes. The two-sided market theory framework shows that consumer-side trust is the demand input that makes business-side monetisation possible; therefore, protecting consumer trust requires institutional separation, not just policy statements. The TIB model is analogous to how financial regulators require banks to separate proprietary trading from customer advisory functions — the purpose is to make the conflict of interest visible and governable.

**Evidence Base**: Grizzly Research's allegation that Trustpilot refused to remove misleading reviews unless businesses paid — whether accurate or not — is credible precisely because the current structure does not make such a conflict impossible. Structural separation would make the allegation implausible, not merely deniable.

### Recommendation 2: Reform the TrustBox Widget to Prohibit Score Filtering

**What**: Remove the feature that allows paying subscribers to display only high-scoring reviews in TrustBox widgets. Replace it with a "Verified Review Snapshot" that shows a randomly sampled, representative distribution of recent reviews.

**Why (Warrant)**: Paying Trustpilot subscribers using the TrustBox feature can filter review scores to only display high-scoring reviews. This feature is the most visible architectural contradiction between Trustpilot's mission and its product. If the mission is to be "the universal symbol of trust," a widget that curates away negative reviews is the antithesis of that mission. The five forces analysis shows that Trustpilot's competitive differentiation from Google Reviews depends on being *more* trustworthy, not merely more feature-rich. Removing score filtering removes a commercial feature but strengthens the asset (consumer trust) that justifies the subscription price. The short-term revenue loss from businesses that valued the filter is outweighed by the long-term credibility gain — this is a classic platform integrity investment.

### Recommendation 3: Invest in Verified-Purchase Review Pathways

**What**: Develop API integrations with major e-commerce platforms (Shopify, Stripe, WooCommerce) to enable cryptographically verified "confirmed purchase" review badges, where a reviewer's transaction is verified without exposing personal data.

**Why (Warrant)**: 4.5 million fake reviews were removed in 2024, representing 7.4% of all submissions. AI detection is improving, but it is reactive — it removes fakes after submission. Verified-purchase pathways are preventive: they raise the cost of fake review campaigns by requiring a real transaction. 75% of consumers are concerned about encountering fake reviews online — a "confirmed purchase" badge would directly address this concern and convert it into a premium signal that differentiates Trustpilot from unverified alternatives. This recommendation is consistent with the network effects logic: if verified reviews become a recognisable standard, businesses have a commercial incentive to adopt the verification pathway, which strengthens the consumer-side signal, which increases consumer traffic, which increases the value of business subscriptions.

### Recommendation 4: Publish a DSA Compliance Transparency Report with Audit Rights

**What**: Publish a detailed, independently audited report of all DSA Article 16 review-removal requests received, the proportion upheld and rejected, and the reasoning categories used. Engage a third-party auditor annually.

**Why (Warrant)**: Businesses have been systematically exploiting DSA Article 16 to remove critical reviews, and Trustpilot errs on the side of caution to avoid legal liability. This creates a legal-compliance-driven erosion of review integrity that is distinct from the commercial integrity problem but equally damaging. Transparency reporting serves two functions: it deters abusive DSA filings (businesses know their attempts will be publicly visible) and it demonstrates to regulators that Trustpilot is a good-faith actor, reducing the risk of punitive regulatory action. Trustpilot already engages with regulators and governments through its founding role in the Coalition for Trusted Reviews — a transparency report operationalises this engagement into verifiable evidence of compliance.

### Recommendation 5: Reframe the Commercial Value Proposition Around Data, Not Visibility

**What**: Shift the marketing and product emphasis of paid tiers from "get more positive reviews displayed" to "understand your customers better." Invest in analytics products — sentiment analysis, competitor benchmarking, customer journey insights — that are commercially valuable regardless of a business's review score.

**Why (Warrant)**: The current commercial model creates an implicit promise that subscription tools help businesses look better. This framing incentivises businesses to seek higher scores through manipulation and creates the perception of pay-to-play dynamics. Trustpilot's FY2024 revenue grew 18% to $210.7 million with a record adjusted EBITDA of $24.1 million — the business is financially strong enough to absorb the short-term cost of reframing its value proposition. An analytics-first model monetises the data asset (301 million reviews) without creating a commercial incentive for score manipulation. This aligns the revenue model with the integrity mission: businesses pay for insight, not for favourable presentation.

## 8. Conclusion

Trustpilot's strategic situation is not primarily a technology problem or a regulatory problem — it is a **platform governance problem**. The company has built a financially successful SaaS business on top of a consumer information good, but the architecture of that business creates structural incentives that undermine the information good's integrity. Trustpilot's own annual report acknowledges it operates a dual-sided platform with network effects, but network effects are double-edged: they amplify value creation when both sides trust the platform, and they amplify value destruction when one side loses confidence. The recommendations above are designed to restore the institutional credibility of the platform's integrity function — not as a cost of doing business, but as the primary strategic asset on which all commercial value depends.

With 89% of global consumers checking online reviews as part of their buying journey, the market for trusted review infrastructure is large and growing. The question is not whether Trustpilot can profit from that market — it demonstrably can. The question is whether it can do so in a way that preserves the trust that makes its product worth buying.
