# Strategic Case Study: Manchester United FC
## *"From Theatre of Dreams to Theatre of Crisis: Charting a Path Back to Greatness"*

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Manchester United Football Club stands at one of the most consequential strategic crossroads in its 147-year history. Once the undisputed commercial and sporting colossus of world football, the club has experienced a decade-long decline that accelerated dramatically in the 2024–25 season. Manchester United shares dropped sharply despite achieving record revenues for 2024/25 of £666.5m — after the club still made an overall loss of £33m. The club finished 15th in the Premier League, carries over £1 billion in debt, and is navigating a deeply complex governance restructure. This case study analyses Manchester United's strategic situation using **PESTEL, Porter's Five Forces, the Resource-Based View (RBV), the McKinsey 7S Framework, Ansoff's Growth Matrix, and SWOT Analysis**, before presenting an integrated set of strategic recommendations.

---

# PART ONE: BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

## 1.1 Club History and the Post-Ferguson Decline

Manchester United is one of the most globally recognised sports brands in the world. Under Sir Alex Ferguson's 27-year tenure, the club won 13 Premier League titles and 2 UEFA Champions League trophies. Since his retirement in 2013, however, the club has cycled through six permanent managers without winning a single league title.

The 2024–25 season marks the nadir of on-field performance during the Glazer era, with the club finishing 16th in the Premier League table, having secured 39 points from 36 games — their worst league performance since 1974 and placing them at theoretical risk of relegation. United finished 15th in the league table with 42 points, that points total being just half that of bitter rivals and eventual champions Liverpool.

## 1.2 Ownership, Governance, and the INEOS Intervention

The Glazers bought the club using the club's own assets and saddled it with debt. Their leveraged buyout in 2005 represented the first primary leveraged buyout of a Premier League club. The club was still over £1 billion in debt, having incurred £37 million in debt interest in the previous year.

Sir Jim Ratcliffe acquired a 25% stake in Manchester United for $1.3 billion and has since led major restructuring and redevelopment efforts at the club. The INEOS boss invested £200 million in the club in addition to the £1.3 billion he spent to acquire 27.7% of the team. Manchester United expects to make cost savings of between £40 million and £45 million a year due to its restructuring. The club reduced its headcount by 250 people over the summer as part of its cost-cutting programme.

## 1.3 The Central Strategic Paradox

The club faces a paradox: **commercial strength coexisting with on-pitch failure and financial fragility.** Commercial power and the strength of the Manchester United brand, even with an alarming decline on the pitch, was a significant factor in revenue performance. Commercial revenue grew 10% from 2023–24, underpinned by last season being the first year of a lucrative new partnership with Qualcomm via its Snapdragon brand, and a boost in retail, merchandising, and licensing following the launch of a new e-commerce model.

# PART TWO: STRATEGIC ANALYSIS

## 2.1 PESTEL Analysis — The Macro-Environment

### 🔴 Political
The UK government has expressed active support for the Old Trafford regeneration project. As of 2024, the government was also considering stricter regulations on sports investment, including an independent football regulator under the Football Governance Bill. Brexit has complicated player recruitment from EU markets, increasing the cost and complexity of signing European talent.

### 🟠 Economic
Manchester United's wage bill fell to £313 million in 2024/25, down from £365 million the previous year. The reduction was partly driven by the absence of Champions League participation, which lowered player bonuses, but also by a leaner workforce. Total headcount declined by more than 200 to 932, with cuts across Media, Commercial, and Administrative departments. Despite these savings, the club's staff costs amounted to £509 million, estimated to be the fifth highest in the Premier League.

### 🟡 Socio-Cultural
Manchester United's global fanbase is estimated to exceed 1.1 billion supporters worldwide. However, domestic sentiment has deteriorated sharply. On 9 March 2025, what was described as the first major protest since Ratcliffe's partial takeover saw thousands of fans protest against Glazer and Ratcliffe ownership following a ticket price increase to £66 and 450 staff redundancies in an aim to curb £131 million in annual losses.

### 🟢 Technological
The newly-effective shirt sponsorship agreement with Qualcomm is reportedly worth $75.0m per year, versus the prior agreement with TeamViewer that reportedly generated £47.0m per year. United bosses have hailed their five-year shirt sponsorship with Snapdragon as the most valuable of its kind in world sport. Digital transformation in scouting, fan engagement, and content monetisation represents both an opportunity and a competitive gap relative to Liverpool and other data-led clubs.

### 🔵 Environmental
Manchester United has thrown its support behind the Government's growth agenda by announcing its intention to pursue a new 100,000-seater stadium as the centrepiece of the regeneration of the Old Trafford area. The stadium and wider regeneration project have the potential to deliver an additional £7.3bn per year to the UK economy, including the possible creation of 92,000 new jobs, more than 17,000 new homes, and an additional 1.8 million visitors annually.

### ⚫ Legal
Financial Fair Play (FFP) was replaced by Profit and Sustainability Rules (PSR) in the 2015/16 season. The rules ensure that every Premier League club who has spent the last three seasons in the top-flight can't lose more than £105 million. Manchester United narrowly avoided a PSR breach for the three-year period up to 2023/24 after reporting significant losses over the past few seasons. Looking ahead, the Premier League has introduced a new set of financial rules — the Squad Cost Ratio (SCR) and Sustainability and Systemic Resilience (SSR) — which will replace PSR from the 2026/27 season.

## 2.2 Porter's Five Forces — The Competitive Environment

### ⚡ Threat of New Entrants: LOW–MODERATE
The barriers to entry in elite football are extremely high. However, state-backed ownership models represent a new form of "entrant" that circumvents traditional barriers. In 2005, Manchester United ranked second globally in revenue generation; by 2024, the club had fallen to fourth and is projected to drop further. This relative decline reflects the emergence of state-backed competitors.

### ⚡ Bargaining Power of Suppliers: HIGH
Player agents and elite footballers hold enormous bargaining power. The 2024–25 season saw record transfer spending from United, with £343 million invested in new signings. This illustrates the structural cost of competing for scarce elite talent.

### ⚡ Bargaining Power of Buyers: MODERATE
Broadcasters hold significant leverage given their role in distributing Premier League rights. However, United's global brand gives it disproportionate negotiating power with individual sponsors. Manchester United's commercial revenue increased year-on-year by £30.4m (10.0%) to £333.3m, with commercial revenue remaining the club's largest revenue segment, comprising 50.0% of total revenue.

### ⚡ Threat of Substitutes: MODERATE
Entertainment alternatives compete for consumer attention. The risk for United is less the substitution of football per se, and more the substitution of Manchester United specifically — fans switching allegiance to rival clubs with a stronger on-pitch product.

### ⚡ Competitive Rivalry: VERY HIGH
Manchester United's revenue was second among its domestic Big 6 peer group for both 2022/23 and 2023/24, but is expected to fall to fourth for 2024/25 as Manchester City and Liverpool will each exceed £700.0m. If things improve on the pitch, as is the hope, there is enormous potential as to how much the club's commercial department could capitalise and grow.

Marcus Rashford, Alejandro Garnacho, Kobbie Mainoo, and Scott McTominay have all graduated from Carrington in recent years. In fact, since 1937, over 250 players from the academy have made appearances in more than 4,000 matches. Forbes valued Manchester United at approximately $6.5 billion in 2024, reflecting unmatched recognition and sponsor appeal. This brand equity is the club's most durable competitive advantage — but it is not immune to sustained on-pitch deterioration.

## 2.4 McKinsey 7S Framework — Organisational Alignment

The McKinsey 7S Framework evaluates the alignment of seven interdependent organisational elements. Misalignment between any element creates strategic friction.

| Element | Current State | Alignment Status |
|---|---|---|
| **Strategy** | Commercial-first; cost reduction; football reinvestment | Partially defined; lacks sporting philosophy |
| **Structure** | Dual Glazer/INEOS authority; streamlined staff | Complex governance; potential misalignment |
| **Systems** | Tighter financial controls; weak scouting/analytics | Financial ✅; Football Operations ❌ |
| **Shared Values** | Historically: youth, community, attacking football | **Critical misalignment** with current cost-cutting |
| **Style (Leadership)** | Ratcliffe: industrial, data-driven, decisive | Cultural friction; reactive managerial changes |
| **Staff** | Headcount reduced by 200+; squad young but inconsistent | Institutional knowledge at risk |
| **Skills** | Commercial skills: world-class; Football ops: lagging | Significant gap in coaching and analytics |

More cost-cutting and difficult decisions are expected as INEOS completes its restructuring of the football club. Ratcliffe warned United would have gone "bust at Christmas" if they had not taken "really tough decisions."

**7S Diagnosis:** The most acute misalignment is between **Strategy, Shared Values, and Style** — the "soft" elements that drive cultural coherence. The club is operationally tightening (Structure, Systems, Staff) but has not yet articulated a coherent sporting philosophy that aligns with its heritage and values.

## 2.5 SWOT Analysis — Synthesis

### ✅ Strengths
- Global brand valued at approximately $6.5 billion in 2024, reflecting unmatched recognition and sponsor appeal.
- Commercial revenue grew 10% from 2023–24, with commercial revenue comprising 50% of total revenue — the highest proportion among the Big Six.
- The new Adidas deal is reportedly worth £90 million per season until the end of the 2034/35 season.

### ❌ Weaknesses
- Under current manager Ruben Amorim, the team achieved just six wins in 25 Premier League matches (24% win rate), the lowest figure for any Manchester United manager in the Premier League era.
- The club was still over £1 billion in debt, having incurred £37 million in debt interest in the previous year.
- Manchester United's commercial revenue growth from 2015/16 to 2023/24 exhibited a compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of just 1.5%, which compares very unfavourably to the CAGRs of 8.6% to 19.6% generated by the other Big 6 cohort clubs over the same period.

### 🚀 Opportunities
- The Task Force determined that a redeveloped Old Trafford could increase the capacity of the existing stadium to 87,000, whereas a new-build stadium would allow capacity to reach 100,000.
- In early 2025, Trafford Council approved Manchester United's strategic development framework, estimating that up to 48,000 jobs and 15,000 homes could be created, with an economic boost valued at more than £4 billion.
- Growing football markets in the USA (2026 World Cup), Southeast Asia, and Africa — where United's brand is historically strong.

### ⚠️ Threats
- The outlook for the 2025–26 season appears more challenging, with no European competition — meaning fewer matches, no UEFA distributions, and reduced commercial opportunities.
- The Premier League's new Squad Cost Ratio rules from 2026/27 will further reshape the club's financial planning.
- The relative decline of Manchester United reflects the emergence of state-backed competitors, with the club projected to drop further in global revenue rankings.

# PART THREE: STRATEGIC RECOMMENDATIONS

## 🔵 Recommendation 1: Establish a Long-Term Sporting Philosophy and Managerial Stability *(Immediate — Year 1)*

**The Problem:** Manchester United are searching for their seventh new manager since Sir Alex Ferguson's retirement in 2013. Each appointment has brought a different tactical philosophy, preventing squad coherence and long-term player development.

**Actions:**
- Appoint a **Director of Football** with genuine authority over the entire football operation — from scouting and recruitment to coaching appointment and player development strategy.
- Define and commit to a **clear footballing identity** and appoint a manager whose philosophy aligns with this vision for a minimum 3-year project, with clear KPIs (top-four finish within 2 seasons; Champions League return within 3 seasons).
- Leverage the **INEOS sports network** (OGC Nice, Team Ineos cycling) for knowledge transfer in sports science and talent identification.

**Theoretical Basis:** McKinsey 7S — resolving the misalignment between Strategy, Style, and Shared Values requires a coherent sporting philosophy embedded in organisational culture.

---

## 🟠 Recommendation 2: Implement a Disciplined, Data-Driven Recruitment Strategy *(Year 1–2)*

**The Problem:** The 2024–25 season saw record transfer spending of £343 million invested in new signings — yet produced a 15th-place finish. This represents a catastrophic failure of the recruitment process.

**Actions:**
- **Invest in a world-class data analytics and scouting infrastructure** at Carrington, modelled on Liverpool's analytics department or Brentford's Smarterscout approach.
- **Prioritise value-for-money acquisitions** — targeting players aged 20–26 with high development potential, rather than ageing stars on inflated wages.
- **Maximise the academy pipeline.** Since 1937, over 250 players from the academy have made appearances in more than 4,000 matches — this VRIN resource must be more systematically exploited, particularly under PSR constraints.
- **Enforce strict PSR/SCR discipline.** The rules ensure that every Premier League club who has spent the last three seasons in the top-flight can't lose more than £105 million over a three-year rolling window.

**Theoretical Basis:** RBV — the academy is a genuine VRIN resource; data analytics capabilities are increasingly rare and inimitable competitive advantages. Ansoff — market penetration through improving the core product.

---

## 🟢 Recommendation 3: Accelerate the Stadium Redevelopment as a Transformational Investment *(Year 1–5)*

**The Problem:** Old Trafford, built in 1910, was not selected as a host venue for UEFA Euro 2028. Its current capacity limits matchday revenues, and its ageing infrastructure is a reputational and commercial liability.

**Actions:**
- **Commit to the new 100,000-seat stadium.** A survey sent to Manchester United season ticket holders and members revealed that 52% of the club's core fanbase would prefer a new stadium over a renovated Old Trafford. Furthermore, more than 90% of supporters are positive about the ambition to deliver a world-class stadium.
- **Pursue the wider Old Trafford Stadium District masterplan** with Foster + Partners. The stadium and wider regeneration project have the potential to deliver an additional £7.3bn per year to the UK economy, create over 92,000 employment opportunities, and attract 1.8 million additional visitors annually.
- **Develop the stadium district as a year-round revenue generator** — incorporating hotels, retail, conference facilities, and entertainment venues — transforming United from a 20-matchday-per-season asset into a 365-day commercial destination.
- **Engage Trafford Council and the GMCA as strategic partners.** In early 2025, Trafford Council approved Manchester United's strategic development framework, estimating that up to 48,000 jobs and 15,000 homes could be created, with an economic boost of over £4 billion.

**Theoretical Basis:** Ansoff — Diversification into mixed-use property and entertainment; RBV — transforming the stadium into a VRIN strategic resource; Porter's Five Forces — creating an inimitable matchday experience that reduces buyer power.

## 🔴 Recommendation 4: Restructure the Financial Model to Achieve Sustainable Profitability *(Year 1–3)*

**The Problem:** A £33 million net loss is just a fraction of the £113.2 million loss from 12 months earlier — the situation is improving, but the club remains structurally loss-making. The club was still over £1 billion in debt, having incurred £37 million in debt interest in the previous year.

**Actions:**
- **Continue the wage bill restructuring.** Operating costs year-on-year fell by 4.5% from 2023–24 to 2024–25, and within that, the wage bill fell by 14%. The target should be a sustainable wage-to-revenue ratio of approximately 55–60%.
- **Explore a Glazer buyout or full ownership restructuring.** The Glazer family's leveraged buyout model has extracted value from the club for 20 years. A full acquisition by INEOS or a long-term institutional investor would remove structural debt and governance ambiguity.
- **Target £400 million in commercial revenue within three years**, driven by the new kit deal, expanded digital partnerships, and stadium district revenues. It speaks volumes that United still have that level of commercial pull, even when the football isn't doing the same talking it once was.
- **Achieve Champions League return as a financial imperative.** United will not have any income from European football in 2025–26 for the first time in 11 years, which significantly limits what could be earned in different circumstances.

**Theoretical Basis:** Porter's Five Forces — reducing supplier power (player wages); PESTEL — navigating PSR/SCR regulatory constraints; McKinsey 7S — aligning Systems and Structure with Strategy.

---

## 🟣 Recommendation 5: Rebuild Fan Trust and Community Connection *(Year 1–3)*

**The Problem:** The first major protest since Ratcliffe's partial takeover saw thousands of fans protest against Glazer and Ratcliffe ownership following a ticket price increase to £66 and 450 staff redundancies. Fan alienation reduces matchday revenues, merchandise sales, and the social legitimacy that underpins the club's commercial appeal.

**Actions:**
- **Freeze or reduce ticket prices** for at least two seasons, demonstrating commitment to accessibility and reversing the reputational damage of the £66 price point.
- **Establish a formal Fan Advisory Board** with genuine influence over pricing, matchday experience, and community engagement. This proactively aligns with the requirements of the incoming Football Governance Act.
- **Reinvest in the local community** through the Manchester United Foundation, embedding the club as a genuine anchor institution for the Old Trafford area — particularly given the regeneration project's potential to create 17,000 new homes and 92,000 jobs.
- **Recommit to the academy philosophy** as a cultural statement. The academy is not merely a cost-saving mechanism; it is central to Manchester United's identity. The third-place finisher in the 2024 Kopa Trophy, Kobbie Mainoo, burst onto the scene and showed why he has the potential to be one of the best midfielders in the world — stories like this must be celebrated and amplified.

**Theoretical Basis:** Stakeholder Theory (Freeman, 1984) — sustainable competitive advantage requires managing all key stakeholders, not merely shareholders; McKinsey 7S — restoring alignment between Shared Values and Strategy.

# CONCLUSION

Manchester United's strategic situation is simultaneously dire and full of latent potential. Manchester United has converted 146 years of history into a global marketing powerhouse, turning cultural relevance into commercial scale. Record revenues, a world-class commercial operation, and a celebrated academy system are genuine, durable competitive advantages. Yet these advantages are under threat.

The McKinsey 7S analysis reveals that the club's core misalignment is cultural — between its historic shared values (youth, community, attacking football) and its current operational reality (cost-cutting, managerial instability, fan alienation). Resolving this misalignment is not merely a communications exercise; it requires genuine strategic commitment to a long-term footballing philosophy, disciplined financial management, and a transformational infrastructure investment.

The new 100,000-seat stadium has the potential to deliver an additional £7.3bn per year to the UK economy — but it is more than a building project. It is the physical manifestation of a strategic reset. Combined with a coherent sporting philosophy, data-driven recruitment, financial discipline, and authentic fan engagement, it represents Manchester United's most credible pathway back to sustained relevance at the summit of European football.

The Theatre of Dreams can be restored — but only through a strategy that is as bold, patient, and values-driven as the club's greatest era demanded.
