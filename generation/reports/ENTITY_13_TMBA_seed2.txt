# PART ONE: CASE BACKGROUND

## 1.1 Company Overview

Diageo plc is the world's largest premium spirits company, headquartered in London, UK. Born from the 1997 merger of Grand Metropolitan and Guinness, Diageo operates across more than 180 countries and commands a portfolio of over 200 brands spanning Scotch whisky, tequila, vodka, rum, gin, beer, and, increasingly, non-alcoholic beverages. Its flagship brands include Johnnie Walker, Guinness, Smirnoff, Captain Morgan, Tanqueray, Don Julio, and Casamigos.

For most of the 2010s, Diageo was considered one of the FTSE 100's most dependable blue-chip stocks — a company with pricing power, global scale, and the tailwind of premiumisation driving consistent earnings growth. Diageo's share price peaked at over £40 at the end of 2021. By mid-2025, since that high, the company has been on a downward tear through 2023 and 2024, with shares falling 54.45%.

This case study examines the strategic situation facing Diageo between 2023 and 2025 — a period marked by revenue decline, leadership instability, structural market disruption, and rising geopolitical headwinds — and develops a rigorous strategic analysis and action plan to guide the company's recovery.

## 1.2 The Strategic Situation: "The Perfect Storm"

Diageo's crisis is not the result of a single failure. Instead, it represents the convergence of multiple internal and external pressures arriving simultaneously.

**Revenue Collapse:** Diageo's FY2024 (year ended June 30, 2024) saw reported net sales come in at $20bn, down 1.4% on the previous year. Organic net sales in Latin America and the Caribbean plunged 23%, driven by a reduction of elevated inventories to more appropriate levels for the current consumer environment, which remains challenging. US Spirits net sales declined 3%, driven by a volume decline of 5%.

**Brand-Level Divergence:** Performance across Diageo's portfolio has become deeply polarised. Casamigos saw its sales tumble by 21% but its stablemate Don Julio skyrocketed by 50% after the brand's US sales soared by 61%. The group's Scotch malts portfolio plummeted by 20%, while blended Scotch brand Buchanan's decreased by 13%.

**Leadership Instability:** Diageo, the world's biggest spirits maker, announced that CEO Debra Crew has stepped down after two challenging years in the top role. Crew's departure comes by mutual agreement and follows a 44% drop in Diageo's share price during her tenure. Nik Jhangiani, Chief Financial Officer, has assumed the role of Chief Executive Officer on an interim basis while a formal CEO search is underway.

**Structural Demand Disruption:** The rise of GLP-1 might also see the emergence of non-alcoholic drinks that offer not just taste and ritual, but also nutritional value. Research from Morgan Stanley suggests that GLP-1s can reduce alcohol consumption by as much as 75%, and 50% per occasion.

**Geopolitical and Trade Headwinds:** Diageo has warned it could take a $150m annual hit from new US tariffs on UK and EU spirits. Solid organic net sales growth in Europe, LAC and Africa was offset by weakness in CWS impacting Asia Pacific results and softer performance in North America.

## 2.1 PESTEL Analysis

### Political

US tariffs on UK and EU spirits, geopolitical instability in the Middle East affecting travel retail, and the Nigerian market restructuring all compound the political risk profile.

### Economic
The post-pandemic period has produced a "cautious consumer environment" across Diageo's key markets. While consumers continue to be cautious in this environment, Diageo is focused on strengthening the resilience of its business through operational excellence, productivity and strategic investments to win quality market share. With nearly 40% of sales from emerging markets, Diageo is exposed to currency volatility and trade-route disruptions that can erode reported earnings.

### Social
The most significant social trend facing Diageo is the structural moderation of alcohol consumption in Western markets. Younger consumers, especially Gen Z, were initially leading the 'sober curiosity' trend because of financial constraints. The GLP-1 phenomenon adds a pharmacological dimension: "GLP-1s are the biological accelerant to an existing 'sober curious' moderation trend, that's been driven by social shifts and cost of living challenges. GLP-1s now sound the death knell of the 'volume growth' model for mainstream alcohol." Simultaneously, however, with circa 550 million new legal purchase age consumers forecast to enter the TBA market by 2033, Diageo will respond with agility to the emerging trends that resonate with these new cohorts.

### Technological
Digital transformation is reshaping how alcohol brands reach consumers. Strategy includes AI-driven marketing optimisation, supply chain shifts to USMCA regions, and reinvestment in US premium spirits like Don Julio and Johnnie Walker. The rise of RTD cocktails — Diageo's launch of premium RTD offerings like Johnnie Walker Old Fashioned, catering to convenience-driven consumers — illustrates how technology and format innovation are reshaping the category.

### Environmental
Climate-driven yield variability affected agave and barley prices in 2024–2025; tequila production costs rose materially amid significant agave price swings. Diageo has set a target to achieve net zero carbon emissions across its direct operations by 2030, and its grain-to-glass sustainability strategy is increasingly a source of brand differentiation and regulatory compliance.

### Legal
Diageo holds 4,237 registered trademarks globally as of 2023. Diageo currently manages 37 active legal cases related to alcohol consumption impacts, with potential financial exposure estimated at £82.4 million. In the UK, the Alcohol Act 2010 imposes strict guidelines on the sale and advertising of alcohol. Compliance costs in 2021 were estimated at £1 billion for the UK alcohol industry.

## 2.2 Porter's Five Forces Analysis

### Force 1: Competitive Rivalry — HIGH

Diageo holds an estimated 5% share by volume of the global spirits market in fiscal 2025 and a materially larger share by value, reflecting its premium portfolio strength. Despite this scale, rivalry is intense. Walmart, Costco, and Tesco control 59.2% of global alcohol retail distribution channels. These retailers negotiate prices aggressively, demanding volume discounts. In the tequila category, Casamigos' sustained decline is partly attributable to increased category competition driving lower demand.

### Force 2: Threat of New Entrants — MODERATE

The capital-intensive nature of spirits production, the complexity of global distribution, and the time required to build brand equity create substantial barriers to entry at scale. However, in non-aged categories (vodka, gin, tequila), barriers are lower. The celebrity spirits phenomenon has demonstrated that new entrants can rapidly capture significant market share by leveraging personal brand equity and social media.

### Force 3: Bargaining Power of Suppliers — MODERATE

Climate-driven yield variability affected agave and barley prices in 2024–2025; tequila production costs rose materially amid significant agave price swings. Diageo's scale provides considerable leverage in most commodity markets, and long-term supply contracts and vertical integration in some categories mitigate supplier power. However, single-ingredient dependency (particularly for agave) remains a vulnerability.

### Force 4: Bargaining Power of Buyers — HIGH

Walmart, Costco, and Tesco control 59.2% of global alcohol retail distribution channels. These retailers negotiate prices aggressively, demanding volume discounts. At the consumer level, the proliferation of choice across both alcohol and non-alcohol alternatives has increased price sensitivity in mainstream tiers. However, at the ultra-premium end (brands priced above $100), consumer loyalty and brand prestige significantly reduce price sensitivity.

### Force 5: Threat of Substitutes — HIGH AND RISING

This is the most structurally significant force facing Diageo. Diageo is now the world's largest non-alcoholic spirits player, more than four times bigger than any of its competitors in this space. In fiscal 2025, its non-alc portfolio organic net sales grew approximately 40%. The substitutes operating against Diageo include non-alcoholic alternatives, cannabis/THC beverages, and premium wellness drinks. Among drinkers who cut back due to GLP-1 medications, wine shows the steepest decline — 52% reduced consumption, compared with 43% for beer and 40% for spirits.

## Recommendation 5: Fix the US Route-to-Market *(Immediate–Medium Term: 0–18 months)*

**The Problem:** The US market's decline has been exacerbated by structural weaknesses in Diageo's route-to-market. Diageo has made good progress on its strategic initiatives, including its US route-to-market enhancements. However, the inventory destocking crisis — which cost the company billions in revenue — reveals that more fundamental change is needed.

**The Recommendation:**

- **Rebuild distributor relationships through joint business planning.** Diageo should share demand forecasting data with key distributor partners and align incentives around sell-through (consumer depletion) rather than sell-in (shipments to distributors). This addresses the root cause of the inventory crisis.

- **Deploy AI-driven demand forecasting.** AI-driven marketing optimisation should be extended to supply chain and inventory management, providing real-time visibility of inventory levels and consumer depletion rates across the US distributor network.

- **Strengthen on-trade activation.** Bars and restaurants are critical brand-building environments for premium spirits. Diageo should invest in bartender education programmes and cocktail menu placements for its star brands.

- **Tariff mitigation.** Diageo says it already has plans in place to absorb around half of the $150m tariff impact. The remaining impact should be managed through selective price increases on brands with sufficient pricing power and accelerated US domestic production investment for applicable categories.

## Recommendation 6: Build a Future-Proof Operating Model *(Long Term: 3–5 years)*

**The Problem:** Diageo's current operating model — built for a world of consistent volume growth in premium spirits — is misaligned with a future characterised by moderation, fragmentation, and volatility.

**The Recommendation:**

- **Embrace a "portfolio of occasions" model.** Rather than organising around product categories (Scotch, vodka, tequila), Diageo should increasingly organise around consumer occasions — celebration, socialising, relaxation, health/wellness — and ensure it has a product (alcoholic or non-alcoholic) for every occasion. The landscape of socialising is evolving and Diageo will enter into new occasions to drive the growth of its categories.

- **Accelerate digital and e-commerce capabilities.** Direct-to-consumer channels reduce dependence on the traditional three-tier system and provide richer consumer data for more targeted marketing.

- **Sustainability as competitive advantage.** Diageo's grain-to-glass sustainability strategy and net zero by 2030 commitment are increasingly important to consumers and investors, particularly in premium and luxury segments where consumers are most values-driven.

- **Scale the innovation pipeline.** Diageo extended its non-alc leadership with the acquisition of Ritual Beverage Company LLC in the US, and this acquisitive innovation approach should be applied across all high-growth adjacent categories.

# PART FOUR: CONCLUSION

Diageo's current strategic situation is serious but not terminal. The company retains extraordinary assets: a world-class brand portfolio, unrivalled global distribution, financial scale, and a genuine leadership position in the fastest-growing segment of its industry.

The strategic imperative is clear: **Diageo must evolve from a volume-driven spirits company into a premium beverage experiences company** — one that meets consumers wherever they are on their drinking journey, whether that is a Don Julio reposado at a high-end restaurant, a Guinness 0.0 at a sports event, or a Seedlip cocktail at a wellness retreat.

Spirits are expected to outgrow total beverage alcohol through 2030 with premium-plus capturing disproportionate value; Diageo's reserve portfolio and geographic breadth position it to target high-single-digit EPS growth over the cycle once the current restructuring is complete. The Ansoff and BCG analyses point to a coherent near-term strategy: penetrate existing markets more effectively with star brands, develop new products in the no/low and luxury segments, and develop new markets aggressively in India, Africa, and Southeast Asia. The PESTEL and Porter's Five Forces analyses reinforce the urgency of responding to structural demand shifts while managing geopolitical and regulatory risks.

The ideal successor CEO will need to rebuild momentum in core markets, drive premiumisation strategies, and navigate regulatory complexities in the United States, Europe, and emerging regions. With the right leadership, the right portfolio focus, and the right geographic priorities, Diageo has everything it needs to navigate this perfect storm and emerge as a stronger, more resilient business for the next decade.
