# MTN Group: Navigating the Platform Pivot — A Strategic Case Study in Pan-African Telecoms

---

## Executive Summary

MTN Group, Africa's largest telecommunications operator, stands at a strategic inflection point. Having built its dominance on voice and basic connectivity across 19 markets, MTN is executing a radical pivot from a traditional mobile network operator (MNO) into a multi-sided digital platform business encompassing connectivity, fintech, and digital infrastructure. This transformation is being pursued against a backdrop of severe macroeconomic headwinds — most acutely the near-total devaluation of the Nigerian naira — intensifying competition from Airtel Africa and Safaricom, persistent structural barriers to digital adoption, and escalating regulatory complexity. This case study frames MTN's strategic situation, analyses it through Porter's Five Forces, a PESTLE audit, and a VRIO resource assessment, and concludes with a prioritised action plan.

---

## 1. The Strategic Situation

### 1.1 MTN Group: Scale, Scope, and the Platform Ambition

MTN Group posted exceptional operational and financial results in 2025, delivering significantly on its Ambition 2025 strategy and transitioning to Ambition 2030 priorities to capture value from the attractive structural growth opportunities brought about by accelerated data adoption and financial inclusion across Africa. The headline numbers confirm the scale of the organisation: MTN Group delivered strong revenue growth in the first half of 2025, with group service revenue rising 23.2 percent to R105.1 billion, driven by robust performances in Nigeria (up 54.1%), Ghana (up 39.9%), and Uganda (up 13.3%).

Yet scale alone does not resolve the strategic dilemma. MTN Group's Ambition 2030 strategy streamlines its execution approach into three principal platforms of choice for consumers, homes, and businesses: Connectivity; Fintech; and Digital Infrastructure. This is not merely a rebranding exercise; it represents a fundamental repositioning of the business model — from selling minutes and megabytes to owning the digital infrastructure layer on which African economic life increasingly runs.

### 1.2 The Core Strategic Problem: Concentration Risk and Currency Exposure

MTN's most acute strategic vulnerability is geographic and currency concentration. MTN Nigeria posted a net income of $736.6 million in 2025, accounting for 48 per cent of the group's total profit of $1.53 billion. Combined with Ghana's contribution, the two markets together generated roughly 90 per cent of MTN Group's total profit for the year. This concentration is a double-edged sword: it amplifies upside but catastrophically transmits downside shocks.

The downside materialised with devastating force in 2023. Africa's biggest telecoms operator MTN Group flagged an up to 80% fall in full-year profit as a sharp devaluation in the Nigerian naira pushed its biggest unit, MTN Nigeria, to a loss. Nigeria's central bank in June 2023 adopted new forex rules that led to an approximately 96.7% devaluation in the exchange rate. Specifically, MTN Nigeria recorded a forex loss amounting to N740.4 billion in 2023. The losses persisted: MTN Nigeria announced a N400.44 billion loss after tax for the financial year ending December 31, 2024, representing a 123% increase from the N137.02 billion loss recorded the prior year.

This is the central strategic tension: MTN's most important market is also its most financially precarious, creating a structural vulnerability that no amount of operational excellence can fully offset so long as revenues are earned in naira but costs and debt are denominated in hard currencies.

### 1.3 The Competitive Landscape: A Shifting Oligopoly

The African telecoms market is structured as a series of national oligopolies. In Nigeria, MTN led with 84.6 million subscribers, representing a 51.4% market share and approximately ₦3.94 trillion ($2.63 billion) in revenue; Airtel followed with 56.6 million subscribers (34.4%) and ₦2.64 trillion ($1.76 billion). Together, the two operators controlled over 85% of the market. In Kenya, Safaricom's dominance is similarly entrenched, though showing early signs of erosion: Safaricom's M-Pesa held 89% of the mobile money market as of December 2025, down from 91% a year earlier, while Airtel Kenya's share rose to 11%, more than doubling its position since early 2024.

The competitive dynamics are evolving in a critical direction: the battleground is shifting from voice and data connectivity to financial services. Airtel Africa has introduced the Airtel Money GlobalPay Card, a virtual payment solution backed by Mastercard, enabling its 150 million users across 14 African countries to make international payments. MTN has responded in kind, but the fintech race is far from settled.

### 1.4 The Structural Opportunity: A Continent Underserved

Despite these pressures, the demand-side opportunity is structurally compelling. Mobile internet penetration in Sub-Saharan Africa reached 27% by the end of 2023, yet a substantial usage gap of 60% remains — representing millions who live within network coverage but face barriers such as device affordability and digital literacy. This gap is simultaneously a challenge and an opportunity: closing it would unlock enormous value. The mobile industry's $140 billion contribution to GDP in 2023 is projected to reach $170 billion by 2030 if key connectivity barriers are addressed.

The fintech dimension of this opportunity is equally large. Africa's mobile money revolution is accelerating, stemming from an ecosystem of over 350 million unbanked adults, an underdeveloped banking infrastructure, and a dynamic informal economy that thrives on cash-based transactions.

## 2. Environmental Analysis: PESTLE

### 2.1 Political & Regulatory

The regulatory environment across Africa is characterised by what McKinsey describes as "regulatory complexities, including high taxation and spectrum licensing fees." African telcos face further financial risks resulting from regulatory complexities, including high taxation and spectrum licensing fees, as well as fluctuating foreign exchange rates. Nigeria exemplifies this dynamic acutely. Operators lobbied for a 100% tariff increase to restore economic viability; the Nigerian Communications Commission (NCC) approved a 50% tariff hike, framing the adjustment as aimed at addressing the significant gap between operational costs and current tariffs while ensuring service delivery is not compromised. The approved increase, while less than requested, represents a partial regulatory concession — but it also signals that MTN's revenue trajectory in Nigeria is partly contingent on political will rather than market forces alone.

Regulators are simultaneously tightening fintech oversight: regulators are responding to mobile money growth by tightening know-your-customer (KYC) requirements, strengthening anti-money laundering rules, and pushing for greater interoperability between platforms. This creates compliance cost pressure on MoMo precisely as MTN is scaling it.

### 2.2 Economic

The macroeconomic environment is defined by currency volatility and inflationary pressure. The naira devaluation discussed above is the most acute manifestation, but it is part of a broader pattern across African markets. Political risks in Africa include policy and regulatory uncertainty — frequent policy changes such as tax reforms, foreign exchange controls, or import bans — that can undermine investment planning. This systemic risk means that MTN's financial results are structurally exposed to macro forces beyond its control.

On the positive side, in 2024, mobile services contributed $220 billion to Africa's GDP, accounting for roughly 7.7% of total output, with unique mobile subscribers numbering around 710 million, or nearly 47% of the population. The sector's economic centrality gives operators leverage in regulatory negotiations.

### 2.3 Social

Africa's demographic profile is a long-run tailwind. Young, urbanising populations with rising smartphone adoption are the natural customer base for data services and mobile financial products. The mobile money revolution is driven by over 350 million unbanked adults and a dynamic informal economy — a social structure that makes mobile money not merely convenient but economically essential for a large portion of the population.

### 2.4 Technological

The 5G transition creates both opportunity and investment pressure. The mobile sector's overall contribution to Africa's economy is projected to grow from $220 billion in 2024 to $270 billion by 2030, with 5G as a primary driver of this digital transformation. However, Africa's 5G rollout is uneven: over half of those without mobile broadband access live in areas with no existing infrastructure, making expansion complex and costly, and Africa's network investments largely focus on 4G rather than 5G. This means MTN must simultaneously maintain 4G investment for mass-market inclusion while selectively deploying 5G for premium and enterprise segments — a capital allocation challenge.

### 2.5 Legal

Data protection regulation is tightening continent-wide, increasing compliance obligations for operators running large-scale platforms. The structural separation of fintech businesses — as MTN is executing in Nigeria and Ghana — also introduces new legal and governance complexity, requiring MTN to manage the regulatory relationships of both a telecoms licensee and a financial services provider simultaneously.

### 2.6 Environmental

Energy costs represent a significant operational pressure, particularly in markets with unreliable grid power where operators must rely on diesel generators for tower uptime. Infrastructure sharing — discussed below — is partly an environmental and cost-efficiency response to this challenge.

## 3. Industry Analysis: Porter's Five Forces

### 3.1 Threat of New Entrants — LOW to MODERATE

The capital intensity of building a mobile network creates formidable barriers to entry. Spectrum licensing, tower infrastructure, and regulatory approval requirements collectively require billions of dollars of upfront investment. However, the emergence of non-traditional entrants — technology companies, satellite operators (e.g., Starlink), and over-the-top (OTT) fintech players — represents a new form of entry that bypasses traditional infrastructure barriers. Starlink's entry into Kenya has already begun to erode Safaricom's broadband positioning. The barrier to entry for the connectivity layer remains high; for the digital services layer that MTN is pivoting toward, it is considerably lower.

### 3.2 Bargaining Power of Suppliers — MODERATE to HIGH

MTN's key suppliers are network equipment vendors (Ericsson, Huawei, Nokia), tower companies (IHS Towers), and energy providers. The concentration of network equipment supply in a small number of global vendors gives suppliers meaningful leverage, particularly as 5G equipment is technically complex and switching costs are high mid-deployment. MTN's proposed acquisition of IHS Towers — its major tower infrastructure partner — is partly motivated by the desire to internalise this supplier relationship and reduce dependency. MTN seeks growth through infrastructure, fintech acquisitions and artificial intelligence initiatives, including a bid for control of IHS Towers.

### 3.3 Bargaining Power of Buyers — LOW to MODERATE (rising)

Individual consumers have limited bargaining power in oligopolistic markets. However, the regulatory-enforced tariff cap in Nigeria — where operators requested 100% increases but received 50% — illustrates that consumer welfare considerations constrain pricing power indirectly through regulatory intervention. Enterprise customers have greater leverage, as they can negotiate volume contracts and threaten to route traffic through alternative providers. The emergence of Starlink and fibre alternatives for enterprise segments is gradually shifting power toward buyers in premium segments.

### 3.4 Threat of Substitutes — MODERATE and RISING

The most significant substitution threat is OTT communication services (WhatsApp, Telegram) displacing voice and SMS revenues — a trend that has been eroding traditional ARPU for years. MTN's strategic response has been to shift toward data revenues (which enable OTT usage) and fintech services (which OTT platforms cannot easily replicate without a distribution network). The fintech substitution threat comes from bank-led mobile apps and standalone fintech players, though MTN's distribution advantage — its agent network and existing subscriber relationships — provides meaningful protection. Satellite broadband (Starlink) is an emerging substitute for fixed and mobile broadband in urban and peri-urban markets.

### 3.5 Competitive Rivalry — HIGH

Ownership in Africa's telecom market is defined by duopolies and concentrated control, which theoretically reduces rivalry intensity. However, the competition between MTN and Airtel Africa is intensifying precisely in the highest-value segment: fintech. Both operators are racing to capture the unbanked population and build ecosystem lock-in through mobile money platforms. The competitive dynamic is not purely on price — it is a race to build platform depth, merchant acceptance networks, and cross-border payment capability. The winner of this race will benefit from powerful network effects that make displacement extremely difficult.

**Five Forces Summary:** The industry structure is broadly favourable for incumbents in connectivity but increasingly contested in digital services. The strategic imperative is to convert connectivity scale into platform lock-in before OTT substitutes and agile fintech competitors erode the value of the subscriber base.

## 4. Resource Analysis: VRIO Framework

The VRIO framework (Barney, 1991) asks whether a firm's resources are **V**aluable, **R**are, **I**nimitable, and **O**rganisationally exploited. Applied to MTN's key resources:

### 4.1 Pan-African Network Infrastructure (Bayobab)

- **Valuable?** Yes. Bayobab claims 127,000 km of open-access fibre, a presence in 54 African countries, access to 24 subsea cables, and 235 points of presence. This infrastructure is the physical backbone of digital Africa.
- **Rare?** Yes. No competitor has comparable pan-continental fibre reach.
- **Inimitable?** Yes, in the medium term. Replicating 127,000 km of terrestrial fibre across 54 countries would require a decade and tens of billions of dollars of investment.
- **Organised?** Partially. MTN's plan is to transfer all of its fibre assets to Bayobab by 2025, potentially setting up the company to take on fresh external partners willing to invest in digital infrastructure. The structural separation is designed to unlock this asset's value — but execution risk remains.

**Verdict:** Sustained competitive advantage, contingent on successful monetisation through the Bayobab vehicle.

### 4.2 MoMo Fintech Platform

- **Valuable?** Yes. In March 2024, MTN Group and Mastercard launched a prepaid virtual bank card for MTN MoMo users in 13 African countries, complemented by a physical Mastercard companion card, following Mastercard's substantial investment of $200 million.
- **Rare?** Moderately. Safaricom's M-PESA is a direct comparable in East Africa; Airtel Money competes across 14 markets.
- **Inimitable?** Partially. The network effects of an established mobile money platform create switching costs, but Airtel is demonstrating that a well-resourced competitor can gain share. As a result of the Mastercard deal, MoMo's valuation was propelled to an impressive $5.2 billion on a debt-free and cash basis — a signal of the platform's strategic value but also of the capital required to build it.
- **Organised?** Improving. The structural separation of MoMo into a standalone fintech entity is designed to attract external capital and enable independent scaling.

**Verdict:** Temporary to sustained competitive advantage, depending on execution speed and the ability to deepen ecosystem lock-in (merchant acceptance, credit, insurance) before Airtel Money achieves comparable scale.

### 4.3 Subscriber Scale and Distribution Network

- **Valuable?** Yes. MTN's 301.3 million subscribers provide the raw distribution base for any digital service.
- **Rare?** Moderately. Airtel Africa's 150 million subscribers and Safaricom's 47 million in Kenya represent comparable (if smaller) assets.
- **Inimitable?** Yes, in the short-to-medium term. Building a subscriber base of this scale requires years of network investment and brand building.
- **Organised?** Yes. Data services remained a key driver, expanding 34.3 percent in constant currency to contribute about 45 percent of total revenue, supported by higher subscriber numbers, smartphone penetration, and network quality improvements. MTN is successfully converting subscriber scale into data revenue.

**Verdict:** Competitive parity on subscriber scale; advantage lies in the ability to monetise subscribers more deeply than competitors.

### 4.4 Brand and Regulatory Relationships

MTN's 30-year operating history across Africa has generated regulatory relationships and brand recognition that are difficult to replicate. However, these relationships are also a source of vulnerability: MTN's size makes it a target for regulatory intervention, as the Nigerian tariff dispute illustrates.

## 5. Strategic Analysis: The Core Tension

The PESTLE and Five Forces analyses converge on a single structural tension: **MTN possesses the infrastructure and subscriber scale to win the African digital platform race, but its financial resilience is critically undermined by currency concentration risk in Nigeria, which simultaneously constrains its capacity to invest in the very capabilities needed to win that race.**

This tension can be decomposed into three interconnected strategic challenges:

**Challenge 1: Financial fragility from Nigeria concentration.** Nigeria and Ghana together generate roughly 90 per cent of MTN Group's total profit, meaning a single macro shock — as occurred in 2023-24 — can erase group-level earnings. This is not merely a financial risk; it is a strategic risk because it constrains capital allocation for the platform pivot.

**Challenge 2: The fintech race against Airtel.** Both MTN (MoMo) and Airtel (Airtel Money) are racing to capture Africa's 350 million unbanked adults. The winner will benefit from network effects that create durable competitive moats. Airtel Africa has introduced the Airtel Money GlobalPay Card, a virtual payment solution backed by Mastercard, enabling its 150 million users across 14 African countries to make international payments — directly mirroring MTN's own Mastercard partnership. The strategies are converging, which means execution speed and ecosystem depth will determine the winner.

**Challenge 3: The usage gap as both constraint and opportunity.** A substantial usage gap of 60% remains in Sub-Saharan Africa, representing millions who live within network coverage but face barriers such as device affordability and digital literacy. These unconnected consumers represent MTN's next growth frontier, but reaching them requires investment in affordability solutions (low-cost devices, subsidised data) that compress near-term margins.

## 6. Recommendations and Action Plan

The following recommendations are derived directly from the analytical framework above, with each recommendation linked to the specific strategic challenge it addresses.

---

### Recommendation 1: Accelerate Structural Separation of MoMo to Reduce Nigeria Dependency and Unlock Platform Capital

**Rationale:** The VRIO analysis identifies MoMo as MTN's most strategically significant resource — but one whose value is currently trapped within the consolidated group balance sheet. The structural separation of MoMo into a standalone fintech entity, already underway in Ghana and Nigeria, is the correct strategic move. MTN Ghana completed the structural separation of its fintech business, while MTN Nigeria secured shareholder approval for similar restructuring. Completing this separation across all major markets would achieve two objectives simultaneously: (a) it unlocks MoMo's valuation — currently assessed at $5.2 billion — for external capital raising, reducing MTN's dependence on group-level earnings from Nigeria to fund platform investment; and (b) it creates a standalone fintech entity that can attract fintech-specific investors, partnerships, and regulatory treatment.

**Why the evidence warrants this conclusion:** The Mastercard investment at a $5.2 billion valuation demonstrates that external investors assign significant standalone value to MoMo. If MTN can replicate this across multiple markets, it creates a capital source that is insulated from naira devaluation risk — because MoMo revenues in each country are earned and valued locally, rather than consolidated into a group P&L dominated by Nigerian naira exposure.

**Action:** Complete MoMo structural separation in all 19 markets within 24 months. Pursue a minority IPO of MoMo on a major exchange (Johannesburg, London, or Lagos) within 36 months to crystallise value and establish an independent capital structure.

---

### Recommendation 2: Deepen Infrastructure Sharing to Protect Margins During the Platform Transition

**Rationale:** The platform pivot requires sustained capital expenditure in data infrastructure, AI, and fintech at precisely the moment when currency losses are compressing cash flows. Infrastructure sharing with Airtel Africa is the most effective lever to protect EBITDA margins without sacrificing network quality. By sharing passive (towers, masts) and active (Radio Access Network or RAN) infrastructure, operators typically cut capital expenditure by up to 30–40%, while slashing operating costs through shared site maintenance and energy costs. MTN and Airtel have already implemented this in Nigeria and Uganda; the two companies are in advanced talks to share network infrastructure in Rwanda, Congo-Brazzaville, and Zambia.

**Why the evidence warrants this conclusion:** The Five Forces analysis shows that rivalry between MTN and Airtel is intensifying in fintech — but not in connectivity infrastructure, where both operators face the same cost pressures. Co-opetition (cooperation on infrastructure, competition on services) is therefore rational: it preserves capital for the higher-value contest in digital services while reducing the structural cost base. The 30-40% capex saving from active sharing would free approximately R10-15 billion annually (based on MTN's R33-38 billion capex guidance) for redeployment into MoMo scaling and Bayobab fibre expansion.

**Action:** Extend the MTN-Airtel infrastructure sharing agreement to at least five additional markets by 2027. Explore active RAN sharing (not merely passive tower sharing) in lower-density markets where the economics of dual-network deployment are weakest.

---

### Recommendation 3: Implement a Currency Hedging and Local-Currency Financing Strategy to Mitigate Nigeria Concentration Risk

**Rationale:** The PESTLE analysis identifies forex volatility as the single greatest threat to MTN's financial stability. As part of a broader strategy to stabilise its financials, MTN is lobbying for regulated tariff increases, reducing capital expenditures, and negotiating terms with lenders to manage the impacts of currency devaluation. These are necessary but insufficient responses. A more structural solution is required.

**Why the evidence warrants this conclusion:** MTN Nigeria's N740.4 billion forex loss in 2023 was not an operational failure — it was a balance sheet failure caused by dollar-denominated debt in a naira-revenue business. The solution is to match the currency of liabilities to the currency of revenues: issuing naira-denominated bonds in the Nigerian capital market, negotiating local-currency supplier contracts where possible, and using natural hedges (reinvesting naira revenues in naira-denominated assets). Additionally, MTN should accelerate the geographic diversification of earnings by investing disproportionately in markets with more stable currencies (e.g., South Africa, Ghana, Uganda) to reduce the group's structural dependence on Nigeria's 48% profit contribution.

**Action:** Refinance at least 50% of MTN Nigeria's dollar-denominated debt into naira-denominated instruments within 18 months. Establish a dedicated forex risk management function at group level with authority to implement natural hedging strategies across all 19 markets.

---

### Recommendation 4: Close the Usage Gap Through Affordable Device and Data Bundling Programmes

**Rationale:** The 60% usage gap in Sub-Saharan Africa represents millions who live within network coverage but face barriers such as device affordability and digital literacy. This gap is not merely a social problem — it is MTN's largest untapped market. The VRIO analysis shows that MTN's subscriber scale and distribution network are competitive advantages, but they are only valuable if the subscriber base is growing and deepening engagement. Stagnant penetration rates in covered areas indicate that the marginal subscriber requires a different go-to-market approach.

**Why the evidence warrants this conclusion:** MTN South Africa is pivoting its MoMo customer acquisition strategy from a digital-first methodology to an agent-led, "boots on the ground" strategy, which is better suited to MoMo's target market. This agent-led approach is directly applicable to the usage gap problem: reaching consumers who are not yet digitally literate requires physical touchpoints, not app-store downloads. Furthermore, closing the usage gap generates compounding returns: each new connected user becomes a potential MoMo customer, a data revenue contributor, and a node in the network that makes the platform more valuable for all other users — the classic network effect dynamic.

**Action:** Launch a pan-African "Connected Africa" programme offering subsidised entry-level smartphones bundled with 3-month data packages at below-cost pricing, cross-subsidised by MoMo transaction fees from the activated customer base. Target 50 million new connected users in the 60% usage gap segment by 2027.

---

### Recommendation 5: Accelerate Bayobab's Open-Access Fibre Strategy to Build a Wholesale Infrastructure Moat

**Rationale:** The VRIO analysis identifies Bayobab's 127,000 km fibre network as MTN's most inimitable asset. However, its value is currently underexploited: much of the network serves MTN's own traffic rather than generating wholesale revenue from third parties. Converting Bayobab into a genuine open-access wholesale infrastructure provider would (a) generate revenues from competitors, making MTN a supplier to its own rivals; (b) attract external capital investment; and (c) entrench MTN's position as the indispensable infrastructure layer of African digital commerce — a position that is far harder to dislodge than a retail subscriber relationship.

**Why the evidence warrants this conclusion:** The East2West project is designed to connect the eastern shores of the continent to the west, backed by Bayobab and Africa50, who are investing up to $320 million, connecting ten African countries by 2025. This project demonstrates that Bayobab can attract institutional co-investors and serve as a genuinely pan-continental infrastructure vehicle. The open-access model also reduces regulatory risk: regulators are less likely to impose punitive interventions on an operator that is actively enabling competition than on one that is perceived as using infrastructure to entrench monopoly.

**Action:** Commit to open-access pricing for all Bayobab fibre assets by 2026, with a target of generating 30% of Bayobab revenues from third-party wholesale customers (including Airtel, Safaricom, and national ISPs) by 2028.

## 7. Synthesis: The Coherent Strategic Logic

The five recommendations above form a coherent chain rather than a list of independent initiatives:

1. **MoMo structural separation** unlocks capital that reduces dependence on Nigerian naira earnings → addressing the concentration risk identified in the financial analysis.
2. **Infrastructure sharing** reduces capex by 30-40% → freeing capital for MoMo scaling and Bayobab investment without requiring additional debt.
3. **Currency hedging** protects the capital base from macro shocks → ensuring that strategic investments are not wiped out by the next devaluation cycle.
4. **Usage gap closure** grows the subscriber base and deepens MoMo engagement → strengthening the network effects that make MoMo increasingly inimitable.
5. **Bayobab open-access** monetises MTN's most inimitable asset and builds a wholesale revenue stream → creating a structural moat that competitors cannot replicate.

Together, these recommendations address the core strategic tension identified in Section 5: they simultaneously reduce MTN's financial fragility (Recommendations 1, 2, 3) and accelerate the platform capabilities that will determine competitive outcomes in the 2025-2030 period (Recommendations 4, 5). The logic is that financial resilience and strategic investment are not trade-offs — they are complements. A financially fragile MTN cannot sustain the investment required to win the platform race; a financially resilient MTN that fails to invest in platform depth will be outmanoeuvred by Airtel and OTT entrants.

## 8. Conclusion

MTN Group's strategic situation is one of the most complex in global telecoms: a dominant incumbent in a high-growth market, executing a platform transformation under conditions of severe macroeconomic stress. The PESTLE analysis reveals that the external environment is simultaneously enabling (a 60% usage gap, 350 million unbanked adults, a $170 billion GDP contribution opportunity) and threatening (naira devaluation, regulatory uncertainty, rising OTT substitution). The Five Forces analysis shows that the industry structure is broadly favourable for incumbents but increasingly contested in the fintech layer where the long-term value resides. The VRIO analysis confirms that MTN possesses genuinely inimitable resources — Bayobab's fibre network, MoMo's platform scale, and its 301-million subscriber distribution base — but that these advantages are only sustainable if they are actively exploited through the structural and strategic moves outlined above.

The overarching recommendation is this: MTN must use its infrastructure dominance to buy time and capital for its platform transformation, while using its platform transformation to reduce the financial fragility that makes its infrastructure dominance precarious. These are not sequential steps — they must be executed in parallel, with the discipline to resist the temptation to treat either financial stabilisation or platform investment as the sole priority.

MTN's Ambition 2030 strategy, built around connectivity, fintech, and digital infrastructure, represents the right directional bet — but the speed and financial discipline of its execution will determine whether MTN emerges as Africa's defining digital platform or as a cautionary tale of strategic overreach in a volatile macroeconomic environment.
