# Strategic Case Study: Charoen Pokphand Foods (CP Foods)
## *Navigating the Legitimacy–Competitiveness Tension in Global Agri-Food*

---

## 1. Introduction and Case Framing

Charoen Pokphand Foods (CP Foods) occupies a paradoxical position in the global food industry. It is simultaneously one of the world's most formidable agri-food conglomerates—a vertically integrated giant with operations spanning feed mills to retail shelves across five continents—and a company whose growth trajectory has been punctuated by severe reputational crises rooted in supply chain governance failures.

The central strategic problem this case examines is: **How can CP Foods sustain and legitimise its competitive advantage in global protein markets while resolving the deep-seated tension between its scale-driven business model and the rising ESG expectations of regulators, investors, and institutional buyers?**

This question is not merely normative. As the analysis below demonstrates, CP Foods' reputational liabilities—most visibly the 2014 forced-labour scandal—have produced measurable commercial consequences and now threaten to compound as EU supply chain due diligence legislation, science-based emissions targets, and alternative protein competition converge. The case therefore has both diagnostic and prescriptive value: it illustrates how a firm's operational model can simultaneously generate and undermine competitive advantage, and it demands a coherent strategic response.

## 2. Company Background and Context

CP Foods was established in 1978 with operations in animal feed production, livestock breeding, further processing, and trade, and currently invests overseas in nine countries, has subsidiaries in 17 countries, and exports to over 40 countries. It is a subsidiary of the Charoen Pokphand Group (CP Group), one of Asia's largest conglomerates, and is listed on the Stock Exchange of Thailand under the code CPF.

CPF is today the leading producer of feed and one of the largest producers of poultry in the world, and reported sales revenue of approximately US$17.8 billion in 2024, with a market capitalisation of over $5.9 billion that same year.

The company's geographic revenue structure is strategically significant. Approximately 64 percent of its revenue came from overseas operations, with 30 percent from its home market of Thailand, and six percent from export operations. The 2024 financial results represent a dramatic turnaround: CP Foods reported a net profit of THB 19.5 billion, a 476% turnaround from the previous year's loss, driven by strong overseas operations, strategic cost management, and improved market conditions in the livestock sector.

The company recently acquired Bellisio Foods, one of the largest frozen food suppliers in the United States, for US$1 billion, as well as Westbridge Foods, a major British poultry producer with turnover of over £340 million. These acquisitions mark a strategic shift from commodity exporter to branded food company in Western markets—a transition with profound implications for both margin quality and reputational exposure.

## 3. Macro-Environmental Analysis (PESTLE)

### 3.1 Political

Thailand's political environment has historically been supportive of CP Group. The Chearavanont family's investment philosophy emphasises long-term relationship building, deep market knowledge, and strategic positioning in essential industries, with strategic regional partnerships and political relationships that facilitate cross-border expansion and JV formation. However, this proximity to political power also creates risk: the Thai government's initial reluctance to acknowledge labour abuses in the fishing sector meant that CP Foods operated within a regulatory environment that, through laxity, permitted supply chain violations to persist.

The 2014 crisis triggered direct political pressure. CP Foods vowed to protect human rights of workers as the US mounted pressure on Thailand. More structurally, the EU's Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD) now imposes extraterritorial obligations on companies supplying European markets. For CP Foods—which derives substantial revenue from European markets through Westbridge Foods in the UK—these regulations represent a structural political-regulatory shift with direct operational implications.

### 3.2 Economic

CP Foods operates in commodity-linked markets characterised by cyclical volatility. In 2024, the company rebounded to a profit of 19.6 billion baht after incurring a net loss of 5.21 billion baht the previous year, a resurgence largely driven by successful pork operations in Vietnam and China, marking the highest annual profit since 2020.

On the cost side, CP Foods executives stressed that high feed raw material costs are nothing new, and that geopolitical issues and supply chain disruption are having an impact on imports of key ingredients, notably soybean, which CP Foods sources mainly from Brazil, prompting exploration of local alternatives. Consumer purchasing power has also been a constraint: "Consumer purchasing power is lower than it used to be; it is lower than what may have been expected after the COVID crisis. Today the focus is aiming to be more efficient and cost competitive."

The macroeconomic opportunity, however, is substantial. Vietnam alone accounted for the largest share of CPF's offshore sales—about 21% or THB 122 billion—and CPF expects total revenue to grow by 5% to 8% in 2025, with higher profit on further earnings improvement of overseas operations, especially in Vietnam and China.

### 3.3 Social

Consumer trends in CP Foods' key markets are bifurcating. In Western markets, growing demand for ethical sourcing and plant-based alternatives is intensifying. Over 69% of consumers stated they would pay a premium for products labelled with recognisable quality, safety, and sustainability certifications. In Asian markets, rising middle-class incomes are driving demand for higher-quality, safer, and more traceable animal protein. Both trends create pressure on CP Foods' traditional model, but also opportunity.

After the 2014 supply chain scandal, surveys indicated that 65% of consumers distrusted food labels, with 7% quitting meat entirely—a pattern that illustrates the broader commercial risk of supply chain legitimacy failures beyond the immediate buyer-suspension effects.

### 3.4 Technological

CP Foods has integrated digital technology, data management, and artificial intelligence (AI) into its processes under its "Sustainovation" concept, leveraging technology and innovation to improve efficiency and minimise environmental impact. The company has implemented SAP sustainability solutions to support real-time carbon accounting at corporate and product level—a prerequisite for meeting its SBTi-validated emissions targets.

Proprietary shrimp genetics and closed-cycle farming reduce unit costs versus peers, representing a knowledge-based competitive asset accumulated over decades of R&D investment. On the disruptive side, CPF launched "MEAT ZERO"—meat made from plants and manufactured to feel, taste, and appear like real meat through "PLANT-TEC" innovation—targeting the top alternative meat brand in Asia and the world's top three within three to five years.

### 3.5 Legal

CP Foods faces a growing body of legal exposure across jurisdictions. A Thai court accepted a class action lawsuit against CP Foods in 2025, alleging environmental damage from the rapid spread of an invasive fish species (blackchin tilapia) that devastated shrimp farms across Samut Songkhram and nearby provinces, with annual losses estimated at THB 150–350 million. This follows the 2014 forced-labour litigation in the US District Court (Northern California), which was resolved in CP Foods' favour in January 2017. The pattern of litigation—across jurisdictions and on both labour and environmental grounds—signals a structurally elevated legal risk profile that is unlikely to diminish as due diligence legislation tightens globally.

### 3.6 Environmental

CP Foods' environmental exposure is acute. In its climate action strategy, Scope 3 emissions constituted 92.4% of total emissions for the reporting period, and the company focuses on stakeholder engagement along the value chain to reduce these emissions. CP Foods is the first food processing company in the world with both near- and long-term forest, land, and agriculture (FLAG) sustainability targets validated by the Science-Based Targets initiative (SBTi), committing to a 42% reduction in all Scope 1 and 2 greenhouse gas emissions by 2030 and 90% by 2050, and to zero deforestation across primary deforestation-linked commodities by 2025.

These commitments are strategically significant but also expose the company to accountability risk: the gap between pledged targets and verifiable progress is a focal point for investor and NGO scrutiny.

## 4. Industry Analysis: Porter's Five Forces

### 4.1 Threat of New Entrants — LOW to MODERATE

The global protein production industry is characterised by high capital requirements, complex regulatory compliance, and significant economies of scale. Near-total vertical integration enables margin retention and quality control across food value chains, while proprietary shrimp genetics and closed-cycle farming reduce unit costs versus peers—creating structural barriers that a new entrant would need to replicate across an entire value chain. The threat of new entrants at the integrated conglomerate level is therefore low, though moderate at specific product or segment level (e.g., plant-based protein).

### 4.2 Bargaining Power of Suppliers — MODERATE

CP Foods manufactures its own aquaculture feed, controlling the nutritional inputs that determine shrimp health, growth rate, and quality from day one. This structural self-sufficiency limits supplier power in the core business. However, for key agricultural commodities—particularly soybean and corn—CP Foods remains exposed to global commodity markets. Geopolitical issues and supply chain disruption are having an impact on imports of key ingredients, notably soybean, which CP Foods sources mainly from Brazil, prompting exploration of local alternatives.

### 4.3 Bargaining Power of Buyers — HIGH (Dominant Force)

This is the most strategically significant competitive force. CP Foods' major institutional buyers—global supermarket chains and QSR groups—are large, concentrated, and increasingly wielding their purchasing power to enforce ESG standards. Carrefour decided to suspend its direct or indirect purchases from CP Foods due to reports of likely connections with slave labour, explaining this was a precautionary measure taken "until the issue is clarified." This 2014 suspension is the clearest illustration of buyer power in action: when reputational risk to the buyer from association with CP Foods' supply chain violations exceeds the cost of switching, buyer power becomes existential.

Companies that build transparency into daily operations gain more than regulatory alignment—they reduce the leverage that buyers can exercise by threatening to switch. This dynamic is intensifying as EU due diligence legislation creates legal liability for buyers who fail to audit their supply chains.

### 4.4 Threat of Substitutes — MODERATE and RISING

Social conversations about global food trends have risen 9.37% year-over-year, reflecting a growing interest in sustainability, plant-based diets, and health-focused meals. CP Foods' MEAT ZERO brand represents a strategic hedge against this substitution threat, but the company's core revenue base remains overwhelmingly dependent on conventional animal protein. The substitution threat is moderate today but rising as technology costs decline and consumer adoption accelerates.

### 4.5 Competitive Rivalry — HIGH

Major rivals of Charoen Pokphand in agribusiness include Betagro (Thailand), BRF and JBS (global poultry/meat), and New Hope Group (China) for feed and livestock. Betagro is the fastest-growing domestic rival in premium meat and integrated cold-chain solutions, with market share gains in Thai fresh meat segments, while Cargill and Tyson exert combined scale pressure in Asia/Europe processed proteins. The convergence of scale competition with ESG competition creates a two-dimensional rivalry that is structurally more complex than traditional cost-only competition.

**Five Forces Summary:** The industry structure is characterised by **high buyer power** and **high rivalry** as the dominant forces, with a **rising substitution threat**. This configuration places premium value on supply chain integrity (to manage buyer power), cost efficiency (to manage rivalry), and product innovation (to manage substitution).

## 5. Internal Analysis: Resource-Based View and Value Chain

### 5.1 Resource-Based View (Barney, 1991)

The Resource-Based View holds that sustainable competitive advantage derives from resources that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN).

**Vertical Integration Architecture (Valuable, Rare, Inimitable):** Agribusinesses benefit from better coordination between production and distribution phases, unified control over the value chain, and increased responsiveness to customer demands through vertical integration. CP Foods' integration—from feed ingredient procurement through animal genetics, farming, processing, and branded retail—is its most strategically distinctive resource. Vertical integration can improve market competitiveness, efficiency, quality, and transparency when done right. This architecture is difficult to imitate because it requires replication of an entire ecosystem, not a single capability.

**Proprietary Technology (Valuable, Rare, Inimitable):** Proprietary shrimp genetics and closed-cycle farming reduce unit costs versus peers, representing a knowledge-based asset accumulated over decades of R&D that is embedded in tacit organisational knowledge and long-term breeding programmes.

**Scale and Geographic Reach (Valuable, Rare):** The 2024 profit recovery was driven by successful pork operations in Vietnam and China, demonstrating how geographic diversification functions as a strategic buffer against country-level cyclicality—a risk management capability that smaller competitors cannot replicate.

**Retail Infrastructure (Valuable):** CP ALL Public Company Limited, the flagship of CP Group's marketing and distribution business, has been the Thai licensee of 7-Eleven since 1989 and operates 15,245 convenience stores in Thailand. This retail integration creates a closed-loop system from production to point-of-sale that provides unmatched distribution reach.

**Legitimacy Deficit (Strategic Liability):** However, CP Foods also carries a significant strategic liability. The investigation found that the world's largest prawn farmer, CP Foods, buys fishmeal from some suppliers that own, operate or buy from fishing boats manned with slaves—a 2014 finding whose commercial consequences (buyer suspensions, market exclusions) and regulatory implications continue to reverberate. Under the RBV, competitive position depends not only on the strength of resources but on the absence of disabling liabilities. CP Foods' legitimacy deficit functions as a constraint on the deployment of its otherwise formidable resource base.

### 5.2 Value Chain Analysis (Porter, 1985)

CP Foods' value chain is unusually integrated:

- **Upstream:** Feed ingredient procurement → proprietary feed manufacturing → animal genetics and breeding → owned farming. The ownership of the feed-to-farm pipeline is the source of cost leadership.
- **Processing:** CP Foods raises its animals in climate-controlled, closed facilities certified by the Department of Livestock Development's GAP standards.
- **Downstream:** Cold-chain distribution, branded retail (CP brand, Authentic Asia), food service supply, and frozen food brands (Bellisio in the US, Westbridge in the UK).
- **Support Activities:** R&D (MEAT ZERO, PLANT-TEC, shrimp genetics); Technology (SAP sustainability platform, AI in supply chain); Procurement (global commodity sourcing).

**Value Chain Vulnerability:** The critical vulnerability is the sub-Tier-1 supply chain—specifically, the fishmeal and feed ingredient suppliers who sit beyond CP Foods' direct control but whose practices create reputational and legal exposure. Men who fled enslavement on boats supplying the Thai shrimp industry told the Guardian of horrific conditions, including 20-hour shifts, regular beatings, torture and execution-style killings. The UK newspaper reported that slave ships catch huge quantities of "trash fish" which, on being landed, go to factories where the fish is ground down into fishmeal for onward sale to CP Foods and other shrimp producers. This illustrates the fundamental tension in the vertically integrated model: integration creates control in the tiers the company owns, but the boundaries of ownership do not coincide with the boundaries of reputational responsibility.

## 7. The Central Strategic Problem: The Legitimacy–Competitiveness Tension

The SWOT synthesis reveals a central strategic problem that cannot be resolved through any single framework: **CP Foods' competitive advantage—built on scale, vertical integration, and cost efficiency—is structurally in tension with the legitimacy requirements of the institutional buyers and regulatory environments on which its global market access depends.**

This tension can be articulated through Suchman's (1995) legitimacy theory, which distinguishes between *pragmatic legitimacy* (stakeholder approval based on self-interest), *moral legitimacy* (normative approval based on ethical standards), and *cognitive legitimacy* (taken-for-granted acceptance). CP Foods has strong pragmatic legitimacy with buyers who value its cost efficiency and supply reliability. However, its moral legitimacy was severely damaged by the 2014 forced-labour crisis and remains fragile, as evidenced by the Coller FAIRR Protein Producer Index's "medium risk" rating for CP Foods and ongoing AMR concerns.

The strategic significance of this legitimacy deficit is amplified by the Five Forces analysis: buyer power is high and rising. Carrefour's suspension of CP Foods prawn orders demonstrated that a single credible allegation can trigger immediate commercial disruption. As more buyers adopt mandatory supply chain due diligence under EU CSDDD, the threshold for such disruptions lowers further.

The problem is compounded by the nature of CP Foods' value chain. CPF admits that slave labour is part of its supply chain: "The issue of endemic slavery in Thailand's seafood supply chain affects all producers in Thailand as they all use by-catch to produce their fishmeal in the same way." This admission illustrates that the governance gap is not a failure of individual bad actors but a structural feature of the sub-Tier-1 supply chain that CP Foods' owned operations cannot unilaterally resolve without extending its governance reach beyond its formal ownership boundaries.

A further dimension is the emerging competitive threat from alternative proteins. The leading driver of global food trends is fitness, with natural food dominating as the most popular dietary adaptation, while plant-based alternatives are gaining traction across Asian markets. CP Foods' entire business model is premised on the continued dominance of conventional animal protein. If the protein transition accelerates, CP Foods' vertically integrated infrastructure risks becoming a stranded asset rather than a competitive advantage.

**The strategic problem therefore has three interconnected dimensions:**
1. **Governance gap:** Sub-Tier-1 supply chain opacity creates persistent legitimacy risk threatening market access with high-value institutional buyers.
2. **Transition risk:** The core business model is exposed to long-run disruption from alternative proteins and climate regulation.
3. **Credibility gap:** CP Foods has made ambitious ESG commitments but faces a credibility gap between pledges and verified performance—particularly for Scope 3 emissions, which constitute 92.4% of its total footprint and are the hardest to reduce.

## 8. Strategic Options Analysis

CP Foods currently operates a **hybrid strategy** combining *cost leadership* (in commodity protein production, where vertical integration drives efficiency) and *differentiation* (in branded processed foods, where CP and Authentic Asia command premium positioning). ## 9. Stakeholder and Legitimacy Analysis

Freeman's (1984) stakeholder theory holds that firms must manage relationships with all parties who affect or are affected by their activities. For CP Foods, the stakeholder map is unusually complex:

- **Institutional buyers** (Walmart, Carrefour, Tesco, Costco, KFC, McDonald's): High power, high interest. Their purchasing decisions directly determine CP Foods' market access. The company's total revenue of THB 580.7 billion, with 63% derived from overseas operations across 13 countries, means that any significant buyer withdrawal has material financial consequences.
- **Regulators** (Thai government, EU institutions): High power. EU CSDDD creates extraterritorial obligations; Thai regulatory laxity historically enabled supply chain violations.
- **Investors and ESG rating agencies** (FAIRR Index): The Coller FAIRR Protein Producer Index was established in 2018 to address the knowledge gap in the ESG risks associated with the food sector and assesses 60 of the largest listed global meat, dairy, and aquaculture companies on ten ESG factors. CP Foods' "medium risk" rating reflects investor pressure on ESG performance.
- **NGOs and investigative media:** The six-month Guardian investigation established that large numbers of men bought and sold like animals and held against their will on fishing boats off Thailand are integral to the production of prawns sold in leading supermarkets—demonstrating the capacity of civil society actors to trigger commercial crises through reputational exposure.
- **Workers in the supply chain:** Low power, high moral salience. The salient human rights issues identified in CP Foods' Human Rights Due Diligence Process include working conditions, health and safety of employees, community health and safety, data privacy, and use of illegal labour (child labour, forced labour, and illegal migrant workers) in supply chains.
- **Local communities:** Farmers across Samut Songkhram and nearby provinces say the invasive species has devastated shrimp farms, with annual losses estimated at THB 150 million to THB 350 million.

Suchman's (1995) legitimacy framework suggests that firms facing legitimacy deficits have three repair strategies: *excuses* (denying responsibility), *justifications* (reframing the issue), and *apologies* (acknowledging wrongdoing and committing to reform). CP Foods has primarily pursued justification and apology: the president and CEO posted a "Statement to Shareholders" vowing to purchase only from certified processing plants, only acquire product from certified Thai fisheries, and that supply chain fishing vessels and fishmeal processing plants "must be certified by Thailand's Labour Standard or have been audited by an external agency." However, the credibility of this response depends on the verifiability of reform commitments—and verifiability requires structural changes to supply chain governance, not merely procedural pledges.

### Recommendation 1: Close the Governance Gap Through Structural Supply Chain Reform
**Priority: Immediate**

**Why the evidence warrants this conclusion:** The governance gap—the opacity of sub-Tier-1 suppliers—is the *root cause* of CP Foods' legitimacy deficit and the primary driver of buyer power risk. The Five Forces analysis shows that buyer power is the dominant competitive force; the stakeholder analysis shows that institutional buyers are the most powerful stakeholder group; and the value chain analysis shows that the sub-Tier-1 supply chain is the node at which governance fails. Carrefour explained its suspension was a precautionary measure taken "until the issue is clarified"—meaning the commercial consequence was triggered not by proven guilt but by unresolved opacity. Resolving that opacity is therefore the single highest-leverage strategic action available.

The current response is insufficient. The Human Rights Due Diligence Process has been conducted regularly every three years—a periodic rather than continuous mechanism that cannot provide the real-time assurance that institutional buyers operating under EU CSDDD obligations require. Moreover, Carrefour's social audit of CP Foods' processing plant carried out in July 2013 "had revealed nothing abnormal at that time", demonstrating that periodic third-party audits can fail to detect systemic violations.

**Action Plan:**
1. **Deploy blockchain-based traceability technology** across the full supply chain, including sub-Tier-1 fishmeal and feed ingredient suppliers, enabling real-time visibility. This is consistent with CP Foods' existing digital transformation agenda (SAP sustainability platform) and its "Sustainovation" strategy. Supply chain mapping technologies can support this effort by helping companies visualise supplier relationships and identify gaps across multiple tiers.
2. **Extend vertical integration to the fishmeal supply chain** by acquiring or taking equity stakes in certified fishmeal producers—applying the same governance standards to this node that CP Foods applies to its owned operations. CP Foods pledged to terminate the use of fishmeal as a feed ingredient by 2021; this commitment should be accelerated and verified.
3. **Increase the HRDD cycle from triennial to annual**, with continuous monitoring between formal assessments and independent public verification of results.
4. **Establish a grievance mechanism** for supply chain workers that is accessible, confidential, and independently managed—a requirement under the UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights.

**Expected outcome:** Reduction in buyer power risk by providing institutional buyers with verifiable supply chain integrity data; reduction in regulatory risk under EU CSDDD; restoration of moral legitimacy.

### Recommendation 4: Commit Substantively to the Protein Transition
**Priority: Medium-term (3–5 years)**

**Why the evidence warrants this conclusion:** The Five Forces analysis identifies a rising substitution threat from alternative proteins. The Ansoff analysis identifies product development as a necessary strategic vector. CP Foods aims to become the leading Asian brand of plant-based meat substitutes, with Meat Zero released in May 2021 and a deal struck with KFC Thailand to supply Meat Zero—but this remains a peripheral activity relative to the core business. The evidence warrants a more substantive commitment because: (a) the competitive threat is structural and growing; (b) CP Foods' vertical integration capabilities (feed formulation, protein processing, distribution) provide genuine competitive assets in alternative proteins that rivals lack; and (c) institutional investors increasingly treat alternative protein investment as a signal of strategic foresight.

**Action Plan:**

2. **Explore strategic acquisitions or partnerships in precision fermentation**, where CP Foods' fermentation and protein processing expertise could provide genuine competitive advantage.
3. **Reframe the alternative protein strategy** not as a hedge against the core business but as a natural extension of the "Kitchen of the World" vision: the company that feeds Asia's protein needs, whatever form those proteins take.

### Recommendation 5: Close the Credibility Gap Through Independent ESG Verification
**Priority: Ongoing**

**Why the evidence warrants this conclusion:** To achieve its net-zero goals of reducing 42% of Scope 1 and 2 emissions and 30.3% of Scope 3 emissions by 2030, CP Foods needed to implement solutions that could streamline GHG data and enable regulatory compliance—but data collection and compliance are prerequisites for credibility, not sufficient conditions. The FAIRR Index's continued "medium risk" rating, despite CP Foods' extensive ESG commitments, signals that the market does not yet fully credit the company's pledges. The company has not publicly disclosed a specific methane emission reduction target or its development status, illustrating the disclosure gaps that undermine credibility. The evidence therefore warrants a structural upgrade to verification and disclosure—because credibility is the mechanism through which all other strategic commitments are converted into commercial value.

**Action Plan:**
1. **Commission annual, independent third-party verification** of ESG performance data, including supply chain emissions, deforestation metrics, and labour standards—going beyond the current triennial HRDD cycle.
2. **Publish a publicly accessible ESG performance dashboard** tracking progress against SBTi targets, deforestation commitments, and labour standards on a quarterly basis.
3. **Engage proactively with the FAIRR Index process** to improve disclosure quality, with a target of upgrading from "medium risk" to "low risk" within three years.
4. **Establish an independent ESG Advisory Board** comprising credible external experts—including representatives from labour rights organisations, environmental NGOs, and academic institutions—with a mandate to provide public assessments of the company's ESG performance.

CP Foods is a company of extraordinary strategic complexity. Yet the analysis presented here shows that this model carries within it a structural tension—between the governance boundaries of the firm and the boundaries of its reputational accountability—that has already produced severe commercial disruption and will continue to do so unless resolved.

The five recommendations form a coherent chain: closing the governance gap (Recommendation 1) is the foundational action, because without supply chain integrity, the market development strategy in Western markets (Recommendation 3) cannot succeed, and the capital raised through the Vietnam IPO (Recommendation 2) cannot be deployed effectively. The protein transition strategy (Recommendation 4) provides the long-run strategic hedge that the current business model requires, and independent ESG verification (Recommendation 5) is the mechanism through which all other commitments are made credible.

The underlying logic—connecting the Five Forces analysis, the RBV, and legitimacy theory—is that competitive advantage in the modern food industry is not separable from legitimacy. A company that cannot demonstrate verifiable supply chain integrity will find its market access progressively constrained by buyer power and regulation, regardless of its operational excellence. Conversely, a company that builds genuine legitimacy infrastructure—not as a compliance exercise but as a strategic asset—can convert its scale and integration advantages into a differentiated competitive position that rivals cannot easily imitate. This is the strategic transformation that CP Foods must undertake: not abandoning its core model, but extending the governance reach of that model to match the full perimeter of its reputational accountability.
