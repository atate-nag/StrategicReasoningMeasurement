# Strategic Case Study: Vattenfall AB — Navigating the Dual Mandate in Sweden's Energy Transition

## A Strategic Analysis Using Industry Frameworks

---

## 1. INTRODUCTION AND CASE OVERVIEW

This case study examines the strategic situation facing **Vattenfall AB**, Sweden's wholly state-owned energy company, as it navigates one of the most complex junctures in its 115-year history. The central strategic problem is a **dual-mandate tension**: Vattenfall is simultaneously expected to generate commercially competitive returns for its state shareholder and to serve as the primary instrument of Sweden's public energy and climate policy. This tension has become acute because the two imperatives are currently pulling in opposite directions — the most commercially rational investment decisions (e.g., pausing offshore wind, divesting loss-making assets) conflict with Sweden's stated policy goals of energy security, decarbonisation, and meeting surging industrial electricity demand. The case traces the structural causes of this tension, applies a multi-framework strategic analysis, and proposes a coherent set of recommendations.

---

## 2. COMPANY BACKGROUND

Vattenfall was formed in 1909 as a Swedish government agency, whose task was to exploit large-scale hydropower. The rationale was explicitly strategic: the Swedish government created Vattenfall to accelerate the electrification of the country, meet the growing needs of energy-intensive industry, and prevent the formation of a private electricity monopoly. This founding logic — state energy ownership as a tool of industrial and social policy — remains embedded in Vattenfall's DNA and explains the persistent dual-mandate tension.

In 1992, Vattenfall was reformed as the joint-stock company Vattenfall AB. At the same time, the transmission grid was transferred to the newly formed state agency Svenska kraftnät. This corporatisation was part of Sweden's broader electricity market liberalisation: the liberalised electricity market of Sweden has not seen significant privatisations, but rather changes in ownership and increases in market concentration. Vattenfall was legally privatised, but remained completely state owned.

Today, Vattenfall AB is wholly owned by the Swedish state. Through a general meeting resolution on the content of the Articles of Association, the shareholder makes decisions on the company's operations. Through 2024–2025, the Swedish government reaffirmed full ownership, valuing Vattenfall's equity at roughly 160 billion SEK, using the company as a strategic vehicle for energy security and climate targets while mandating commercially minded operations.

Financially, the Swedish state-owned power utility Vattenfall posted 2024 results of revenue of SEK 246bn (€22bn, -15% compared to 2023) and profit of SEK 33bn (€3bn, x3.3). The company's power generation declined by 1% to 100 TWh.

## 3. THE STRATEGIC SITUATION: DEFINING THE PROBLEM

The core strategic problem can be stated as follows: **Vattenfall faces a portfolio investment crisis in which its most commercially viable assets (legacy hydro and nuclear) are finite and slow-growing, its highest-growth opportunity (offshore wind) has become commercially unviable in Sweden, and its state shareholder is simultaneously demanding new nuclear investment, energy security, and fossil-free industrial supply — goals that require capital commitments exceeding what commercial logic alone would sanction.**

Four specific tensions define the situation:

**Tension 1 — The Offshore Wind Impasse.** Vattenfall has decided to pause the development of the offshore wind power project Swedish Kriegers Flak until further notice, due to unviable investment prerequisites in Sweden. This is not an isolated project failure; it reflects a systemic cost problem: overall, Vattenfall sees cost increases up to 40%. This development affects future profitability and means that Vattenfall makes an impairment for wind power in Norfolk, UK, with a total impact on earnings of SEK 5.5 billion. The commercial logic of pausing is sound, but it directly contradicts Sweden's renewable build-out targets.

**Tension 2 — The Nuclear Bet.** Vattenfall aims to deploy new nuclear capacity at the site in the early 2030s. In August 2025, Vattenfall confirmed its decision to use SMRs for 1.5 GW of new nuclear capacity on the Värö Peninsula, where the Ringhals plant is located. The utility shortlisted two technologies: GE Vernova Hitachi's BWRX-300 and the Rolls-Royce SMR. This is a high-conviction, high-capital, long-horizon bet whose commercial viability is unproven at scale — yet it is also a direct response to government instruction: the Swedish government gave instructions to the state-owned Vattenfall to "immediately commence with the planning of new nuclear power… and to become a leader in the development of fossil-free electricity production."

**Tension 3 — The Grid Bottleneck.** Sweden's electricity system is structurally split: Sweden is one of few EU countries using zonal pricing. Its current four-zone setup reflects recurring, structural congestions from north-to-south flows of hydro and wind power in SE1–SE2 to demand centres in SE3–SE4. There is no shortage of power generation; the issue is transmission and grid capacity. Growing demand is balanced by increased generation, especially from wind power plants. However, the majority of added wind power capacity is in the north, while the ageing nuclear plants in the south are phased out one by one. This constrains Vattenfall's ability to monetise its northern generation assets at southern market prices.

**Tension 4 — The Industrial Demand Surge.** The combination of Sweden's well-functioning, market-based electricity system and near zero-emissions power mix (relying primarily on hydropower, nuclear and wind) provides a strong foundation upon which to electrify the wider energy system. This will require a large build-out of new clean power generation capacity accompanied by broader reforms. The demand side is already materialising: in 2016, Hybrit Development AB was formed as a joint venture by state-owned behemoths SSAB (steelmaking), LKAB (mining) and Vattenfall (power generation), aiming to decarbonise steel production using green hydrogen. This industrial transformation will require electricity volumes that Vattenfall's current portfolio cannot guarantee.

## 4. ANALYTICAL FRAMEWORK 1: PORTER'S FIVE FORCES — INDUSTRY STRUCTURE

Porter's Five Forces framework analyses the structural attractiveness of an industry by examining competitive rivalry, supplier power, buyer power, threat of new entrants, and threat of substitutes (Porter, 1980). For a state-owned utility, this framework must be adapted: Vattenfall simultaneously *operates within* competitive electricity markets and *shapes* those markets through its ownership structure and policy mandate.

### 4.1 Competitive Rivalry — **High and Intensifying**

Sweden's electricity market was liberalised in 1996, and the window of opportunity provided by liberalisation was used especially by the "big three" (E.ON, Fortum and Vattenfall) in order to strengthen market position early. However, European integration has increased rivalry: Vattenfall now competes with pan-European utilities (RWE, Ørsted, Iberdrola) for offshore wind permits, supply contracts, and industrial customers. The sale of the Norfolk Offshore Wind Zone to RWE — at an agreed enterprise value of £963 million — illustrates that rivals are willing and able to absorb assets that Vattenfall finds uneconomical, intensifying competitive pressure on capital allocation.

**Analytical inference:** High rivalry means Vattenfall cannot rely on market position alone to protect returns. It must compete on cost efficiency and innovation, yet its state-ownership constraints (dividend obligations, policy mandates) make pure cost competition harder than for private peers. This reinforces the need for a differentiated rather than cost-leadership strategy.

### 4.2 Threat of New Entrants — **Moderate, but Rising in Renewables**

Legacy thermal and nuclear generation has very high barriers to entry (capital intensity, regulatory licensing, nuclear safety regimes). However, renewable energy — especially onshore wind and solar — has seen entry barriers fall dramatically with declining technology costs. Vattenfall's growth investments total SEK 92 billion, with the largest share, SEK 59 billion, planned for investment in new wind power. The willingness of new entrants (including technology companies and sovereign wealth funds) to finance renewables at scale represents a structural threat to Vattenfall's wind business margins.

**Analytical inference:** Because entry barriers are falling in wind but remain high in nuclear and hydro, Vattenfall's sustainable competitive advantage lies in its legacy baseload assets and its state-backed ability to absorb nuclear development risk — not in wind, where it faces commoditised competition.

### 4.3 Supplier Power — **High and Structurally Problematic**

The offshore wind supply chain crisis is the most vivid illustration of supplier power. Swedish energy company Vattenfall halted its Norfolk Boreas development in July 2023, citing a 40% rise in costs driven by the ripple effects of higher global gas prices. Turbine manufacturers (Vestas, Siemens Gamesa) and specialist installation vessels are in short supply globally, giving suppliers pricing power that erodes project economics. For SMR development, supplier power is even more acute: Vattenfall has narrowed its SMR supplier selection to two finalists — GE Vernova Hitachi's BWRX-300 and the Rolls-Royce SMR, meaning the company is dependent on a duopoly of technology providers for its most strategically critical investment.

**Analytical inference:** High supplier power in both offshore wind and nuclear SMRs structurally compresses Vattenfall's margins and increases execution risk. This makes the case for either vertical integration into supply chains or risk-sharing partnerships (e.g., the BASF co-investment model in Nordlicht) rather than sole-developer positions.

### 4.4 Buyer Power — **Increasing Among Industrial Customers**

Large industrial customers — particularly those pursuing decarbonisation — have growing leverage. They can negotiate long-term Corporate PPAs, play suppliers against each other, or self-generate. Vattenfall is working to accelerate the transition to fossil freedom in sectors such as steel, chemicals, oil, cement, logistics and food. In addition to supplying fossil-free electricity or heat (for example, through long-term PPAs), direct and indirect electrification using fossil-free hydrogen are potential solutions for industry decarbonisation. While this represents an opportunity, it also means large buyers increasingly seek bespoke, long-term supply agreements rather than spot market purchases, requiring Vattenfall to build a more sophisticated B2B commercial capability.

**Analytical inference:** Rising buyer power among industrial customers is, paradoxically, strategically favourable for Vattenfall if it can lock in long-term PPAs backed by its fossil-free generation portfolio — converting buyer power into a revenue-stabilising mechanism rather than a margin threat.

### 4.5 Threat of Substitutes — **Low for Baseload, High for Peak**

Electricity as a product has few substitutes in an electrifying economy. However, distributed energy resources (rooftop solar, battery storage, demand response) threaten Vattenfall's retail and peak-generation margins. The more significant substitution threat is *within* generation: if SMR costs prove prohibitive, industrial customers may substitute with imported hydrogen or offshore wind from other European producers.

**Five Forces Summary:** The overall industry structure is moderately attractive for incumbents with large baseload assets (hydro, nuclear), but structurally challenging for offshore wind development. This analysis supports a strategic pivot *away* from being a pure wind developer and *toward* leveraging Vattenfall's unique nuclear and hydro capabilities — a conclusion reinforced by the Resource-Based View analysis below.

## 5. ANALYTICAL FRAMEWORK 2: THE RESOURCE-BASED VIEW (RBV) — COMPETITIVE ADVANTAGE

The Resource-Based View (Barney, 1991; Wernerfelt, 1984) argues that sustainable competitive advantage derives from resources and capabilities that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN). Applied to Vattenfall, this framework identifies where its genuine strategic edge lies.

### 5.1 Core Resources and Capabilities Assessment

| Resource/Capability | Valuable? | Rare? | Inimitable? | Non-substitutable? | Strategic Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Hydropower portfolio (Sweden/Nordic) | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ (geography) | Partly | **Core competence** |
| Nuclear operating expertise (Forsmark, Ringhals) | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ (regulatory/technical) | Partly | **Core competence** |
| State ownership / sovereign balance sheet | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | **Unique strategic asset** |
| Offshore wind development capability | ✓ | ✗ (many rivals) | ✗ | ✗ | **Contested capability** |
| Industrial B2B PPA relationships | ✓ | Partly | Partly | ✗ | **Developing capability** |
| Distribution grid ownership (Sweden) | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ (regulatory moat) | ✓ | **Regulated monopoly asset** |

Vattenfall's ambition is to be a world leader in the operation of both nuclear and hydro power with high safety and sustainability standards as well as cost-efficient fossil-free electricity production with high availability. This self-articulation is consistent with the RBV diagnosis: nuclear and hydro are Vattenfall's VRIN resources. Offshore wind, by contrast, is a valuable but not rare capability.

The state-ownership resource deserves particular attention. Vattenfall said the SMR project is necessary to meet rising electricity demand, bolster energy security, and advance the country's net-zero targets. The Swedish utility's board formally decided on July 18, 2025, to build the new project. A private firm facing equivalent commercial uncertainty in SMR technology would be unlikely to make this Final Investment Decision. The state balance sheet and political mandate constitute an inimitable resource that enables Vattenfall to absorb long-horizon, high-uncertainty investments that private capital would not fund at equivalent cost.

**Analytical inference:** The RBV analysis reveals that Vattenfall's competitive advantage is *not* in being a pan-European wind developer (where it faces commoditised competition) but in being Sweden's sovereign energy anchor — providing baseload nuclear and hydro power, developing next-generation nuclear technology, and enabling industrial decarbonisation. This suggests a strategic focus on deepening VRIN resources rather than expanding into contested wind markets.

## 6. ANALYTICAL FRAMEWORK 3: THE ANSOFF MATRIX — GROWTH STRATEGY EVALUATION

Ansoff's (1957) Growth Matrix classifies strategic options along two dimensions: products (existing vs. new) and markets (existing vs. new). It provides a structured way to evaluate Vattenfall's portfolio of strategic moves.

### 6.1 Current Strategic Moves Mapped to Ansoff

**Market Penetration (existing products, existing markets):**
Vattenfall's investment in extending the operational life of existing nuclear plants — at Ringhals, the operating time of the existing nuclear power plants is planned to be extended from 60 to 80 years — is a classic market penetration move: extracting more value from an existing, proven asset in an existing market. This is low-risk and high-return given the sunk costs of nuclear construction.

**Product Development (new products, existing markets):**
The SMR programme represents product development: a new generation technology deployed in Vattenfall's existing Swedish nuclear market. Vattenfall says the project will be for 1.5 GW capacity — so either five BWRX-300s or three of the 500 MW Rolls-Royce SMRs. This carries high technical and regulatory risk but is strategically coherent given Vattenfall's nuclear competence base.

**Market Development (existing products, new markets):**
The HYBRIT green steel partnership represents market development: Vattenfall's existing capability in fossil-free electricity generation is being deployed into the new market of industrial decarbonisation. LKAB and SSAB are not the only cleantech projects planning to use cheap electricity in Sweden's north. Others want to produce green fertilizers, EV batteries, or green methanol. This market is nascent but structurally large.

**Diversification (new products, new markets):**
The Berlin heat business, now divested, represented a diversification that proved strategically misaligned. Since the divestment of Heat Berlin in May 2024, Vattenfall no longer has any coal-related activities in any market. The divestment is strategically correct under the RBV lens: the Berlin heat business was neither rare nor inimitable, and its coal exposure contradicted Vattenfall's fossil-free mandate.

**Ansoff Analysis Verdict:** Vattenfall's portfolio is currently shifting from a diversification/market development posture (international wind, German heat) toward market penetration and product development in its core nuclear-hydro-Sweden domain. This is strategically sound but requires the company to manage the transition risk of exiting higher-revenue international activities while new nuclear capacity is not yet online.

## 7. ANALYTICAL FRAMEWORK 4: STAKEHOLDER ANALYSIS AND THE PRINCIPAL-AGENT PROBLEM

State-owned enterprises face a distinctive version of the principal-agent problem (Jensen & Meckling, 1976). In a private firm, shareholders (principals) have a single objective (profit maximisation). In Vattenfall's case, the principal (the Swedish state) has multiple, sometimes conflicting objectives: commercial return, energy security, climate policy, industrial policy, and regional development.

### 7.1 Principal-Agent Tensions

Now, since the "new normal" has arrived and Vattenfall is facing problems, just as the entire energy industry, the state rule of the company is put into question. No one is asking for total privatisation which would be politically impossible. However, there are strong demands for a private partner and eventually quotation on the stock exchange in order to bring more discipline to Vattenfall.

This captures the principal-agent tension precisely: the state's multi-objective mandate creates governance ambiguity that reduces managerial accountability. When Vattenfall pauses Kriegers Flak on commercial grounds, is it acting as a disciplined commercial entity or failing its energy security mandate? The answer depends on which objective the principal prioritises — and that prioritisation shifts with electoral cycles.

### 7.2 Key Stakeholder Map

| Stakeholder | Primary Interest | Power | Alignment with Vattenfall Strategy |
|---|---|---|---|
| Swedish Government (owner) | Energy security, climate, returns | Very High | Partially aligned — nuclear yes, offshore wind pause = tension |
| Swedish Energy Market Inspectorate | Grid regulation, consumer pricing | High | Regulatory constraint on distribution margins |
| Industrial customers (SSAB, LKAB, data centres) | Long-term fossil-free supply at competitive price | High (growing) | Aligned — demand for PPAs and green hydrogen |
| International wind competitors (RWE, Ørsted) | Market share in European wind | Medium | Competitive threat |
| SMR technology suppliers (GE Vernova, Rolls-Royce) | Technology licensing and supply contracts | High (duopoly) | Dependency risk |
| Swedish citizens / electricity consumers | Affordable, reliable electricity | Medium | Tension — price zone disparities create regional inequity |

Steps taken to open the investment environment to nuclear will help to ensure that future fossil-free generation can meet the rising demand from electrification and green industry. The IEA's framing confirms that the government's nuclear instruction to Vattenfall is consistent with international best-practice energy policy — but the IEA also notes that a policy priority is to ensure that this is accompanied by an efficient licensing and financial risk-sharing model, if Sweden's goal of 2.5 GW of new nuclear power capacity by 2035 is to be realised. This is the critical governance gap: without a clear risk-sharing framework between Vattenfall and the state, the company bears excessive financial risk for a project that is fundamentally a public good.

## 8. ANALYTICAL FRAMEWORK 5: PESTLE ANALYSIS — MACRO-ENVIRONMENTAL CONTEXT

### Political
Sweden's 2022 change of government triggered a decisive shift in energy policy: the government gave instructions to the state-owned Vattenfall to "immediately commence with the planning of new nuclear power… and to become a leader in the development of fossil-free electricity production." This political direction provides strategic clarity but also creates a risk: Vattenfall's investment decisions are now entangled with electoral cycles, creating long-term capital commitment under short-term political uncertainty.

### Economic
This improvement was primarily driven by higher achieved prices in the Nordics and increased hydro, nuclear and wind power generation. Profit for the period rose to SEK 28.3 billion from SEK 4.7 billion. However, the revenue decline of 15% in 2024 reflects the structural problem of falling wholesale electricity prices after the 2022 energy crisis peak. Vattenfall's profitability is highly sensitive to Nordic spot prices, which creates earnings volatility that complicates long-horizon capital planning.

### Social
The energy crisis of 2022 created significant social pressure around electricity affordability, particularly in southern Sweden (SE3/SE4 price zones). A major explanation is the energy crisis that has driven up electricity prices in SE3 and SE4 as a result of integration with Europe. The war in Ukraine has not affected prices particularly much in SE1 and SE2 compared to the levels that can be considered normal. This north-south price divergence creates political pressure on Vattenfall as the dominant incumbent, even though the cause is grid infrastructure rather than Vattenfall's commercial decisions.

### Technological
The SMR technology bet is the defining technological uncertainty. Ontario Power Generation (OPG) in Canada has made a Final Investment Decision to build the first BWRX-300 SMR reactor from GE Vernova, which is expected to be operational in 2029. This provides a reference point, but SMR technology at commercial scale remains unproven, and cost overruns in nuclear construction are historically frequent. Vattenfall is taking on first-mover risk in a technology domain where the downside is severe.

### Legal/Regulatory
The liberalised electricity market of Sweden has been in place since 1 January 1996, when production and trading became subject to competition. Within this framework, Vattenfall operates under dual legal constraints: commercial company law (requiring fiduciary duty to its shareholder) and the Swedish state's ownership policy (requiring alignment with public policy objectives). These dual legal frameworks are the formal expression of the principal-agent tension identified above.

### Environmental
Since the divestment of Heat Berlin in May 2024, Vattenfall no longer has any coal-related activities in any market. Emissions have been reduced by 53% since the base year 2017, excluding the effect of divestments. The environmental trajectory is positive, but Vattenfall has set science-based targets for 2030 and 2040 to transition away from fossil fuels and align its business with the requirements to limit global warming to 1.5°C. Meeting these targets while simultaneously investing in capital-intensive nuclear development requires a level of financial capacity that may strain the balance sheet without state support.

## 9. SYNTHESIS: THE COHERENT STRATEGIC DIAGNOSIS

The five frameworks converge on a unified diagnosis:

**Vattenfall's strategic problem is not primarily about any single investment decision. It is a structural misalignment between the company's VRIN resources (nuclear, hydro, sovereign balance sheet), its stated strategy (fossil-free energy leader), and its governance framework (unresolved dual mandate).**

The chain of reasoning is as follows:

1. **Porter's Five Forces** shows that offshore wind is a structurally unattractive market for Vattenfall given high supplier power, low barriers to entry for rivals, and cost inflation — making the Kriegers Flak pause commercially rational.

2. **The RBV** confirms that Vattenfall's genuine competitive advantage lies in nuclear and hydro, not wind — supporting a strategic refocus on these VRIN assets.

3. **The Ansoff Matrix** shows that the most strategically coherent growth options are (a) market penetration through nuclear life extension, (b) product development through SMR deployment, and (c) market development through industrial B2B PPA supply — not continued international wind diversification.

4. **Stakeholder/Principal-Agent analysis** identifies that the binding constraint on executing this focused strategy is governance ambiguity: without a clear state risk-sharing framework for SMR investment and a transparent mandate hierarchy, Vattenfall cannot make optimal capital allocation decisions.

5. **PESTLE** confirms that the macro-environment (surging industrial electricity demand, geopolitical energy security imperatives, falling wind economics) reinforces the nuclear-hydro-industrial supply strategy — but also highlights that the grid bottleneck and SMR technology risk are execution constraints that require policy intervention, not just corporate strategy.

## 10. RECOMMENDATIONS AND ACTION PLAN

The following recommendations are organised in a logical sequence: governance first (as the enabling condition), then portfolio strategy, then execution mechanisms.

---

### Recommendation 1: Formalise a State Risk-Sharing Framework for SMR Investment (Governance — Immediate Priority)

**The problem:** The SMR programme is a public good investment (energy security, climate, industrial policy) being carried on a commercial balance sheet. This creates a principal-agent distortion: Vattenfall's management bears fiduciary duty to minimise commercial risk, while the state's policy interest requires accepting that risk.

**The recommendation:** The Swedish government should negotiate a formal Contracts for Difference (CfD) or minimum revenue guarantee mechanism for the SMR programme, ring-fencing the nuclear investment from Vattenfall's commercial operations. This is directly supported by the IEA's finding that a policy priority is to ensure that this is accompanied by an efficient licensing and financial risk-sharing model, if Sweden's goal of 2.5 GW of new nuclear power capacity by 2035 is to be realised.

**Why this follows from the analysis:** The RBV identifies the state balance sheet as an inimitable resource. The principal-agent analysis shows that without formal risk-sharing, Vattenfall's management will rationally under-invest in nuclear relative to the social optimum. Formalising the state's risk-sharing role converts an implicit subsidy (state ownership as implicit guarantee) into an explicit, accountable mechanism — resolving the governance ambiguity while preserving commercial discipline in Vattenfall's non-nuclear operations.

---

### Recommendation 2: Adopt a "Focused European Baseload" Portfolio Strategy — Divest Contested Wind, Deepen Nuclear-Hydro (Portfolio — Medium Term)

**The problem:** Vattenfall's current portfolio spans offshore wind development across five European markets, nuclear operations in Sweden, hydro in the Nordics, distribution networks, and EV charging. This breadth disperses capital and management attention across activities with very different competitive dynamics.

**The recommendation:** Vattenfall should adopt a focused strategy centred on its VRIN resources: (a) complete the SMR programme in Sweden; (b) maximise value from the existing hydro and nuclear fleet through life extension; (c) selectively participate in offshore wind only where risk can be shared (as in the BASF Nordlicht partnership model); and (d) divest or partner on wind assets where sole-developer economics are unviable.

This is consistent with the strategic logic already evident in Vattenfall's recent decisions: through strategic divestments of the heating operations in Berlin and wind power projects off the coast of Norfolk in the UK, Vattenfall is strengthening its preparedness for future investments. The recommendation is to make this pattern explicit and systematic rather than reactive.

**Why this follows from the analysis:** Porter's Five Forces shows wind development is structurally unattractive for Vattenfall. The RBV shows nuclear and hydro are the VRIN resources. The Ansoff Matrix shows market penetration and product development in existing nuclear-hydro markets are lower-risk, higher-return strategies than international wind diversification. Focusing the portfolio is therefore not a retreat but a strategic concentration on genuine competitive advantage.

---

### Recommendation 3: Build a Dedicated Industrial B2B Energy Partnership Division (Market Development — Medium Term)

**The problem:** The surge in Swedish industrial electricity demand — from green steel (HYBRIT), green hydrogen, EV battery manufacturing, and data centres — represents a structural demand shift that Vattenfall's current commercial model (largely spot market and retail) is not designed to capture.

**The recommendation:** Vattenfall should establish a dedicated Industrial Energy Partnership division with the mandate to originate, structure, and manage long-term Corporate PPAs and hydrogen supply agreements with large industrial customers. Vattenfall's Corporate PPA capability increases its competitive advantage in wind auctions by enabling stable revenues through Corporate PPA. This capability should be extended beyond wind to cover nuclear and hydro output, providing industrial customers with long-term fossil-free baseload supply — a product that competitors without baseload assets cannot offer.

**Why this follows from the analysis:** The stakeholder analysis identifies large industrial customers as a high-power, strategically aligned stakeholder group. The Ansoff Matrix identifies market development (existing fossil-free generation into new industrial markets) as a coherent, lower-risk growth vector. The PESTLE analysis confirms that industrial electricity demand is the primary growth driver in the Swedish market. Long-term PPAs also address the earnings volatility problem identified in the economic PESTLE analysis by providing revenue certainty that reduces dependence on spot prices.

---

### Recommendation 4: Advocate for Grid Investment and Market Design Reform (Policy Engagement — Ongoing)

**The problem:** Vattenfall's strategic options are constrained by Sweden's north-south grid bottleneck. The majority of added wind power capacity is in the north, while the ageing nuclear plants in the south are phased out one by one. This structural mismatch means that even if Vattenfall builds new generation in the north, it cannot reliably deliver power to the high-demand southern zones — undermining the commercial case for northern investment.

**The recommendation:** Vattenfall should use its unique position as Sweden's dominant state-owned energy company to actively advocate — at the government and regulatory level — for accelerated transmission investment and market design reform. Specifically, this means supporting Svenska kraftnät's grid development programme and engaging in the debate on Sweden's zonal pricing model, which reflects recurring, structural congestions from north-to-south flows of hydro and wind power.

**Why this follows from the analysis:** The PESTLE analysis identifies grid infrastructure as a binding constraint on Vattenfall's strategy. The stakeholder analysis shows Vattenfall has both the incentive and the standing to shape this policy debate. The Porter's analysis shows that resolving the grid bottleneck would increase the structural attractiveness of northern generation investment — directly benefiting Vattenfall's hydro and wind assets. This is a case where Vattenfall's dual mandate (commercial and public policy) is *aligned* rather than in tension: advocating for grid investment serves both its commercial interests and Sweden's energy policy goals.

---

### Recommendation 5: Institutionalise a Transparent Dual-Mandate Governance Framework (Governance — Immediate Priority)

**The problem:** The fundamental source of strategic incoherence at Vattenfall is the unresolved dual mandate. Without a transparent framework specifying when commercial logic should prevail and when policy objectives should override it, every major investment decision becomes a political negotiation rather than a strategic choice.

**The recommendation:** The Swedish government and Vattenfall's Board should jointly develop and publish a **Mandate Hierarchy Document** that explicitly specifies: (a) which investment decisions are subject to commercial return thresholds; (b) which decisions are state-directed policy investments (with explicit state risk-sharing as per Recommendation 1); and (c) the governance process for resolving conflicts between the two. This is consistent with the OECD's Guidelines on Corporate Governance of State-Owned Enterprises, which recommend that state objectives be clearly defined and publicly disclosed.

**Why this follows from the analysis:** The principal-agent analysis shows that governance ambiguity is the root cause of strategic incoherence. The stakeholder analysis shows that multiple stakeholders (management, industrial customers, capital markets, regulators) are disadvantaged by this ambiguity. Formalising the mandate hierarchy does not eliminate the dual mandate — it makes it legible, accountable, and strategically manageable.

## 11. CONCLUSION

Vattenfall's strategic situation is not a crisis of competence but a crisis of strategic clarity. The company possesses genuinely rare and inimitable resources — its hydro fleet, nuclear operating expertise, and sovereign balance sheet — that position it as an irreplaceable actor in Sweden's energy transition. The strategic error of the past decade was to treat these advantages as a platform for broad international diversification (offshore wind across five markets, German heat) rather than as the foundation of a focused, deepened competitive position in fossil-free baseload supply.

The convergence of five analytical frameworks — Porter's Five Forces, the Resource-Based View, the Ansoff Matrix, Stakeholder/Principal-Agent analysis, and PESTLE — points consistently toward the same strategic direction: **concentrate on VRIN resources, formalise the state risk-sharing framework for nuclear, develop the industrial B2B market, and resolve the governance ambiguity that currently prevents coherent capital allocation.**

The recommendations are sequenced deliberately: governance reform (Recommendations 1 and 5) must precede portfolio strategy (Recommendation 2) and market development (Recommendation 3), because without governance clarity, even the correct strategic choices will be undermined by principal-agent conflicts. Grid advocacy (Recommendation 4) is an ongoing activity that reinforces all other recommendations by improving the structural environment in which Vattenfall operates.

Executed coherently, this strategy would transform Vattenfall from a diversified European utility struggling with an unresolved dual mandate into what its founding logic always intended: Sweden's sovereign energy anchor — commercially disciplined, strategically focused, and indispensable to the nation's industrial and climate future.
