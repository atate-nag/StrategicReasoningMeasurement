# CASE STUDY: SALESFORCE, INC. — NAVIGATING THE GROWTH-PROFITABILITY TRANSITION IN THE AGE OF AGENTIC AI

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This case study analyses the strategic situation facing Salesforce, Inc. (NYSE: CRM) between 2022 and 2025 — a period defined by three simultaneous pressures: a sharp deceleration in revenue growth from hyper-expansion rates to single digits; an activist-investor-driven restructuring that forced a painful pivot toward profitability; and an existential competitive challenge from AI-native rivals threatening Salesforce's dominance in the $80 billion CRM market. Using Porter's Five Forces, the VRIO framework, and the Ansoff Matrix, the analysis identifies the strategic situation, diagnoses its causes, and produces a connected set of actionable recommendations.

---

## PART I: THE CASE — SITUATION DESCRIPTION

### 1.1 Company Background

Salesforce was founded in 1999 by Marc Benioff as the world's first Software-as-a-Service (SaaS) CRM company. Over two decades it grew into the world's dominant CRM vendor, expanding beyond sales-force automation into service, marketing, commerce, analytics (Tableau), integration (MuleSoft), and collaboration (Slack). By FY2024 (ending January 2024), Salesforce reported $34.9 billion in revenue, up 11% year-over-year, with a non-GAAP operating margin of 30.5% and operating cash flow of $10.2 billion. In 2024, the global CRM market grew to $80 billion, marking a 10.5% year-over-year increase, with Salesforce leading with approximately 26.1% market share.

### 1.2 The Strategic Problem: Three Converging Crises

**Crisis 1 — The Growth Deceleration.** Salesforce's revenue growth rate, which exceeded 20% annually for much of the 2010s, slowed dramatically. FY25 revenue guidance was set at $37.7–38.0 billion, representing only 8–9% year-over-year growth — a historically low rate for the company. Q1 FY2025 revenue was $8.25 billion, an increase of only 11% year-over-year, and by the third quarter of FY2025, revenue for the nine months ended October 31, 2024 was $27.9 billion, an increase of just 9% year-over-year. This deceleration signalled market saturation at the enterprise tier and intensifying competition.

**Crisis 2 — Activist Pressure and Restructuring.** In January 2023, Salesforce announced a layoff of approximately 10% of its workforce, or roughly 8,000 positions. According to Benioff, the company had hired too aggressively during the COVID-19 pandemic. The company expected to incur $1.4 billion to $2.1 billion in charges associated with the layoffs and restructuring. This was driven in part by activist investors: Elliott Management took a multibillion-dollar activist stake in Salesforce, with firms like Elliott and Starboard typically looking for belt-tightening. The restructuring delivered improved margins but at a cost to Salesforce's identity as a progressive, growth-at-all-costs employer.

**Crisis 3 — The AI Transition and Monetisation Gap.** Salesforce's strategic response to both crises was an aggressive "hard pivot" to agentic AI via its Agentforce platform, launched in late 2024. However, adoption of Agentforce has been slower than anticipated; as of early 2025, the company reported 5,000 deals, with 3,000 paid customers and 2,000 in testing phases, with many deals bundled with broader Salesforce packages and active usage remaining limited. Critically, Agentforce accounts for less than 3% of Salesforce's total revenue, raising questions about scalability.

## PART II: INDUSTRY ANALYSIS — PORTER'S FIVE FORCES

Porter's Five Forces framework (Porter, 1980) examines the structural attractiveness of an industry by analysing five competitive forces. The purpose here is to establish *why* Salesforce faces the pressures it does — not simply to list them, but to connect each force to the strategic problem identified above.

### Force 1 — Threat of New Entrants: LOW-TO-MODERATE

The CRM market has high barriers to entry in the enterprise segment. Salesforce's R&D spending in 2024 was $7.2 billion, representing 20.3% of total revenue, and the average time to replicate Salesforce's core CRM technology is estimated at 4–6 years. Salesforce holds over 2,300 active patents. These structural barriers protect the core.

*However*, the AI era is lowering barriers in adjacent segments: large firms with deep engineering teams now build bespoke CRM using internal data lakes and open-source AI models, with a 2024 IDC study finding that 38% of enterprises increased in-house app development versus 2022. This matters strategically because it means the threat of new entrants is not from traditional CRM startups but from enterprises partially internalising functions Salesforce previously monopolised — directly contributing to growth deceleration.

### Force 2 — Bargaining Power of Suppliers: MODERATE

Salesforce depends on cloud infrastructure providers (primarily AWS, Azure, and Google Cloud). Salesforce's cloud infrastructure investment as of 2024 stood at $26.8 billion, with annual infrastructure spending of $4.3 billion. Concentration among three hyperscalers gives those suppliers moderate leverage, though Salesforce's scale provides countervailing power. This force is not a primary driver of the strategic crisis but creates a structural cost floor that limits margin expansion.

### Force 3 — Bargaining Power of Buyers: MODERATE (but rising)

This force directly explains the growth deceleration. High switching costs curb customer bargaining power: migrating terabytes of CRM data, integrations, and retraining staff creates months-long projects and costs often exceeding $500,000 for mid-market firms, so clients rarely threaten churn. This is a key structural advantage. *Nevertheless*, buyer power is increasing at the margin because of two dynamics: first, enterprise customers are consolidating tech spending post-pandemic, scrutinising SaaS licences more carefully; second, vertical SaaS funding hit $28.7 billion in 2024, with vertical SaaS providers reporting ARR growth above 60%, offering built-in compliance and workflows that Salesforce requires heavy customisation to replicate. Rising buyer power — in the form of credible alternatives — is therefore a direct cause of slowing net-new revenue.

### Force 4 — Threat of Substitutes: MODERATE AND RISING

Open-source CRM projects (such as ERPNext and Odoo) and open large language models like Llama 2 and Mistral offer firms control over data sovereignty and cut licensing costs; 2024 surveys show 32% of mid-market IT buyers consider open-source CRM a viable replacement for SaaS. This substitution threat is strategically significant: it disproportionately threatens Salesforce's mid-market and SMB segments, which are precisely the growth markets Salesforce needs to address to offset enterprise saturation.

### Force 5 — Competitive Rivalry: HIGH

This is the most consequential force for Salesforce's strategic situation. Microsoft is the nearest CRM rival, earning $5.45 billion in CRM revenue in 2024 — almost four times less than Salesforce — yet ServiceNow has stated its aim to eventually become the CRM market leader. In the AI agent space specifically, ServiceNow ranked number one for AI Agents in the 2025 Gartner Critical Capabilities report, with its AI Agent Orchestrator and Control Tower, while Microsoft Copilot introduced Agent 365 and GPT-5 integration. Microsoft unveiled Copilot Agents in 2024, a suite of autonomous tools built on its cloud infrastructure designed for heavy-duty enterprise tasks like processing customer returns, managing invoices, and optimising supply chain workflows.

**Five Forces Summary and Connection to the Strategic Problem:** The industry analysis reveals that competitive rivalry and rising buyer power are the primary structural forces driving Salesforce's growth deceleration. The company's historical moat — built on switching costs — remains intact at the enterprise level but is eroding at the margins through AI-native competition and vertical SaaS substitution. This structural finding motivates the internal analysis: *what resources does Salesforce possess that can be leveraged to defend and extend its position?*

## PART III: INTERNAL ANALYSIS — THE VRIO FRAMEWORK

The VRIO framework (Barney, 1991) evaluates whether a firm's resources and capabilities are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Organised — the conditions for sustained competitive advantage. The purpose here is to identify which of Salesforce's assets can anchor its strategic response.

### Resource 1 — The AppExchange Ecosystem

- **Valuable:** The Salesforce AppExchange features over 7,000 ready-to-install applications and solutions built by thousands of partners, allowing customers to extend Salesforce for highly specific needs, from accounting integrations to industry-specific compliance tools. This breadth makes the platform indispensable.
- **Rare:** No other CRM provider, including giants like Microsoft or Oracle, has a marketplace that comes close in terms of size, maturity, or partner engagement.
- **Inimitable:** The AppExchange ecosystem is deeply embedded in customer implementation strategies, with 100% of the Fortune 100 having installed at least one Salesforce partner solution. The global Salesforce Consulting Service market was valued at $9.07 billion in 2024. The depth of third-party investment creates a self-reinforcing lock-in that rivals cannot replicate quickly.
- **Organised:** The depth of Salesforce's platform and partner ecosystem yields network effects and high switching costs, with more than half of its large customers using multiple Salesforce clouds and retention above 90%.

**VRIO Verdict: Sustained Competitive Advantage.** The AppExchange ecosystem is the single most durable asset Salesforce possesses. It directly counters the competitive rivalry and substitution threats identified in the Five Forces analysis, because a customer deeply integrated across multiple Salesforce clouds and dozens of AppExchange applications faces prohibitive switching costs.

### Resource 2 — Customer 360 Data Platform

- **Valuable:** Salesforce's unified data architecture — combining Data Cloud, MuleSoft, Tableau, and Slack — creates a proprietary enterprise data layer that AI agents require to function. Salesforce's Data and AI offering reached $1.2 billion in Q2 FY2025, growing 120% year-over-year.
- **Rare:** Few competitors can offer a single unified data platform spanning CRM, integration, analytics, and collaboration at enterprise scale.
- **Inimitable:** Salesforce's strategic acquisitions — MuleSoft for integration, Tableau for analytics, Slack for collaboration, and a planned $8 billion acquisition of Informatica for data management — have expanded its ecosystem into a one-stop shop for enterprise cloud needs. Each acquisition deepens the data moat.
- **Organised:** The planned Informatica acquisition is explicitly designed to strengthen this resource: MuleSoft combined with Informatica's advanced data integration and governance capabilities will create a comprehensive end-to-end integration offering that empowers customers to unlock all enterprise data and systems for the AI era.

**VRIO Verdict: Sustained Competitive Advantage (Developing).** The data platform is a rare and increasingly inimitable resource, but its competitive advantage is contingent on Salesforce successfully integrating its acquisitions — a significant organisational challenge.

### Resource 3 — Agentforce AI Platform

- **Valuable:** Agentforce addresses the growing enterprise demand for autonomous AI. Agentforce has delivered results including reducing Engine's average customer case handle time by 15%, autonomously resolving 70% of 1-800Accountant's administrative chat engagements, and increasing Grupo Globo's subscriber retention by 22%.
- **Rare:** While Microsoft and ServiceNow have competing platforms, Salesforce's CRM-native data advantage gives Agentforce a differentiated foundation.
- **Inimitable:** At present, *not fully inimitable*. Microsoft Copilot agents leverage Microsoft's robust AI ecosystem and deep integration with Microsoft 365, representing a credible parallel capability.
- **Organised:** Execution is the current weakness. Pricing complexity and slower-than-expected adoption — only 3,000 paid customers as of early 2025 — highlight the need for continued innovation in simplifying deployment.

**VRIO Verdict: Temporary Competitive Advantage (at risk of parity).** Agentforce is valuable and potentially rare, but its advantage is not yet sustained because Microsoft and ServiceNow are rapidly closing the capability gap, and Salesforce has not yet organised itself to monetise it at scale. This is the most urgent strategic gap.

### Resource 4 — Brand and Enterprise Customer Base

Over 90% of the Fortune 500 are Salesforce customers. Salesforce claims the largest CRM customer base by far, with 327,297 customers. This installed base is both valuable and rare — it provides a captive market for upselling Agentforce and AI features. However, it is primarily an *exploitation* resource rather than an *exploration* resource; it sustains existing revenues but does not by itself generate new growth.

**VRIO Summary and Connection to Strategy:** The analysis reveals a clear pattern: Salesforce's *defensive* resources (ecosystem, switching costs, customer base) are strong and sustained, but its *offensive* resource (Agentforce) is not yet organised for monetisation. The strategic challenge is therefore to convert a temporary AI advantage into a sustained one before Microsoft and ServiceNow achieve parity — while simultaneously using the defensive moat to protect existing revenues during this transition.

## PART IV: GROWTH STRATEGY ANALYSIS — THE ANSOFF MATRIX

The Ansoff Matrix (Ansoff, 1957) maps four growth strategies across two dimensions: existing vs. new products, and existing vs. new markets. The Ansoff Matrix divides growth strategies into four categories: Market Penetration, Market Development, Product Development, and Diversification, each representing a different level of risk. Applied to Salesforce, the matrix identifies where growth can realistically come from given the structural constraints identified above.

### Quadrant 1 — Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets): NECESSARY BUT INSUFFICIENT

Salesforce's primary revenue base — Sales Cloud, Service Cloud, and Marketing Cloud — is sold to enterprise customers who already use Salesforce. More than half of Salesforce's large customers use multiple Salesforce clouds, suggesting meaningful cross-sell penetration already. However, the CRM market is expected to continue growing, but Salesforce and Microsoft are likely to maintain their leadership positions while Zoho and HubSpot are poised for further growth, particularly within the SMB segment. The enterprise tier is approaching saturation; market penetration alone cannot restore double-digit growth.

*Warrant:* Because over 90% of the Fortune 500 are already Salesforce customers, incremental penetration in the enterprise tier yields diminishing returns. The Five Forces analysis confirmed that buyer power is rising precisely because enterprise customers have leverage as incumbents. Market penetration is therefore a defensive strategy — necessary to protect the base — but insufficient as a growth engine.

### Quadrant 2 — Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets): HIGH PRIORITY

Two underpenetrated markets offer significant opportunity:

**(a) SMB and Mid-Market:** Zoho and HubSpot are poised for further growth particularly within the SMB segment, meaning Salesforce is ceding this tier to lower-cost rivals. HubSpot's reported acquisition interest from Salesforce in 2024 at a $40 billion-plus valuation underscored how seriously the industry views HubSpot as a competitive threat. This reveals a strategic gap: Salesforce has historically under-invested in the SMB market, and that gap is being exploited by HubSpot.

**(b) International Markets:** In 2025, the Middle East and Africa CRM market represented $0.89 billion, accounting for 4.3% of the worldwide market — a region growing rapidly but where Salesforce's presence remains limited relative to its global share.

*Warrant:* Market development addresses the growth deceleration directly by opening new customer pools. Because Salesforce's existing products (Sales Cloud, Service Cloud) are already proven, the risk profile is lower than diversification, making this a credible near-term growth lever.

### Quadrant 3 — Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets): THE CORE BET

This is Salesforce's primary strategic wager. One of the best Ansoff Matrix examples of product development is Salesforce itself, which has consistently launched new product lines (Tableau, MuleSoft, Slack, Einstein, Agentforce) into its existing customer base. Agentforce represents the most ambitious product development move in Salesforce's history — a shift from selling software licences to selling autonomous digital labour.

The consumption-based pricing model ($2 per conversation) is strategically significant: it aligns Salesforce's revenue with customer value realisation rather than seat counts, potentially unlocking a much larger addressable market. By the end of FY26, Salesforce had delivered 2.4 billion Agentforce Work Units, growing 57% in a single quarter, shifting the conversation from licence sales to measurable AI output.

*Warrant:* Product development into AI agents is justified by the VRIO analysis: Salesforce's Customer 360 data platform gives Agentforce a structural advantage over AI agents that lack CRM-native data. The risk is that this advantage is not yet monetised at scale — Agentforce represents less than 3% of revenue — meaning the transition period is a window of vulnerability.

### Quadrant 4 — Diversification (New Products, New Markets): SELECTIVE

Agentic AI is driving Salesforce and ServiceNow to expand beyond their traditional domains of ITSM and CRM, suggesting both companies are exploring diversification into each other's markets. Salesforce's planned acquisition of Informatica represents a move into data management — a new product category for new enterprise buyers. The proposed Informatica acquisition is described as a timely opportunity to further Salesforce's leadership in the AI revolution, with Informatica's data management capabilities integrated with Data Cloud.

*Warrant:* Diversification carries the highest risk in the Ansoff framework, and Salesforce's acquisition history is mixed (the Slack acquisition has not yet delivered its promised revenue synergies at scale). Diversification should therefore be selective and limited to adjacencies that reinforce the AI data platform strategy rather than represent unrelated expansion.

## PART V: SYNTHESIS — THE STRATEGIC SITUATION

The three frameworks converge on a coherent diagnosis:

1. **The Five Forces** show that Salesforce's industry position is structurally strong but under increasing pressure from AI-native competition (rivalry) and vertical SaaS alternatives (substitutes), with enterprise market saturation suppressing growth (buyer power).

2. **The VRIO analysis** shows that Salesforce's defensive moat (AppExchange, switching costs, customer base) is genuinely sustained, but its offensive AI capability (Agentforce) is not yet organised for monetisation and faces competitive parity risk from Microsoft and ServiceNow.

3. **The Ansoff Matrix** shows that market penetration alone cannot restore growth; the company must simultaneously pursue market development (SMB/international) and product development (Agentforce monetisation), while managing the risk that the AI transition period creates a revenue gap.

The core strategic tension is therefore: **Salesforce must accelerate Agentforce monetisation before Microsoft and ServiceNow achieve capability parity, while simultaneously defending its enterprise base and opening new market segments — all under activist investor pressure to maintain profitability.**

## PART VI: RECOMMENDATIONS AND ACTION PLAN

The following recommendations are derived directly from the analytical findings above. Each recommendation is connected to a specific analytical finding and carries an explicit warrant.

---

### Recommendation 1: Simplify and Standardise Agentforce Pricing and Onboarding

**Evidence:** Pricing complexity is one of the primary barriers to Agentforce adoption, with the consumption-based model ($2 per conversation, $75 per user per month) lacking definition and hindering ROI clarity for users. Customers need to adapt their corporate culture to fully integrate AI agents into their workforce.

**Connection to Analysis:** The VRIO analysis identified Agentforce as a resource with temporary rather than sustained competitive advantage, precisely because it is not yet organised for scale deployment. The Ansoff product development strategy depends on Agentforce becoming a revenue engine, which requires removing friction from adoption.

**Warrant:** If pricing complexity is the primary barrier to adoption, then simplifying the pricing model and creating standardised "starter packages" for specific use cases (e.g., customer service deflection, sales pipeline management) will directly accelerate the conversion of the 2,000 customers in testing phases into paying customers, thereby closing the monetisation gap before competitors achieve parity.

**Action:** Introduce three tiered Agentforce packages (Starter, Professional, Enterprise) with transparent, outcome-based pricing. Create a 90-day ROI guarantee programme backed by Salesforce Success Plans. Target the conversion of the existing 2,000 trial customers within two quarters.

---

### Recommendation 2: Launch a Dedicated SMB and Mid-Market Growth Programme

**Evidence:** Zoho and HubSpot are poised for further growth particularly within the SMB segment, and HubSpot's competitive threat is so significant that Salesforce reportedly considered a $40 billion-plus acquisition of HubSpot in 2024.

**Connection to Analysis:** The Five Forces analysis showed that buyer power is rising in the enterprise tier, while the Ansoff Matrix identified market development into SMB as a high-priority, lower-risk growth path. The VRIO analysis confirmed that Salesforce's brand and AppExchange ecosystem are valuable across market segments, but the company has not organised these resources to serve SMB customers cost-effectively.

**Warrant:** If enterprise growth is decelerating due to market saturation and Salesforce is already considering acquiring HubSpot to address the SMB gap, then building a native SMB offering (rather than acquiring at $40 billion) is both more capital-efficient and strategically consistent with the margin-improvement mandate from activist investors. A simplified, lower-cost tier of Salesforce Starter with AI features would directly address the substitution threat from HubSpot and Zoho.

**Action:** Develop a "Salesforce SMB Suite" — a pre-configured, lower-cost bundle of Sales Cloud, Service Cloud, and Agentforce Starter — with self-service onboarding and a partner-led distribution model. Price it competitively with HubSpot's Professional tier. Target 50,000 net-new SMB customers within 24 months.

---

### Recommendation 3: Accelerate the Informatica Integration to Cement the Data Moat

**Evidence:** The Informatica acquisition will create a comprehensive end-to-end integration offering that empowers customers to unlock all enterprise data for the AI era. Technical debt in legacy Salesforce orgs complicates Agentforce implementation, as AI agents depend on clean, structured data.

**Connection to Analysis:** The VRIO analysis identified the Customer 360 data platform as a resource with sustained competitive advantage potential, but noted that its advantage is contingent on successful integration of acquisitions. The Five Forces analysis identified AI-native competition from Microsoft and ServiceNow as the primary competitive threat. The data quality problem is the single largest technical barrier to Agentforce adoption, and resolving it would simultaneously strengthen the data moat and accelerate AI monetisation.

**Warrant:** If Agentforce's value proposition depends on clean enterprise data, and if Informatica is the leading data governance and integration platform, then accelerating the Informatica integration directly addresses the primary technical barrier to Agentforce adoption while creating a data moat that Microsoft and ServiceNow cannot easily replicate — because neither has Salesforce's combination of CRM-native data, integration middleware (MuleSoft), and enterprise data governance (Informatica).

**Action:** Establish a dedicated Informatica-Salesforce integration taskforce with a 12-month delivery mandate. Prioritise the "Data Readiness for Agentforce" module as the first joint product, enabling customers to assess and remediate their data quality before deploying AI agents. Offer free data health assessments to all existing Salesforce enterprise customers.

---

### Recommendation 4: Invest in Vertical AI Agent Packages for High-Value Industries

**Evidence:** Agentforce's expansion into industry-specific domains, such as public sector and manufacturing, suggests a long-term strategy of vertical differentiation. Vertical SaaS providers offer built-in compliance and workflows that Salesforce requires heavy customisation to replicate, with vertical SaaS funding hitting $28.7 billion in 2024.

**Connection to Analysis:** The Five Forces analysis identified vertical SaaS as a substitution threat. The Ansoff Matrix identified product development as the primary growth strategy. Combining these insights suggests that Salesforce should develop pre-built vertical AI agent packages — not generic agents that require customisation, but industry-specific agents with embedded compliance, workflows, and data models.

**Warrant:** If vertical SaaS is growing at over 60% ARR because it offers built-in industry compliance that Salesforce cannot match without heavy customisation, then Salesforce can neutralise this substitution threat by building that compliance and specialisation into Agentforce itself. This leverages the VRIO-identified competitive advantage of the AppExchange partner ecosystem (which already contains thousands of industry-specific apps) to create vertical AI agent templates co-developed with leading ISVs.

**Action:** Launch five industry-specific Agentforce packages (Financial Services, Healthcare, Manufacturing, Retail, Public Sector) within 18 months, co-developed with leading AppExchange ISV partners. Each package should include pre-built compliance guardrails, industry data models, and outcome benchmarks.

---

### Recommendation 5: Defend the Enterprise Base Through Proactive Multi-Cloud Expansion

**Evidence:** More than half of Salesforce's large customers use multiple Salesforce clouds, and retention is above 90%. Salesforce's Data and AI offering reached $1.2 billion in Q2 FY2025, growing 120% year-over-year.

**Connection to Analysis:** The VRIO analysis confirmed that the AppExchange ecosystem and multi-cloud adoption create sustained switching costs. The Five Forces analysis showed that competitive rivalry from Microsoft and ServiceNow is the primary threat to enterprise accounts. The Ansoff market penetration strategy — while insufficient for growth alone — is essential for defending the base.

**Warrant:** If retention is above 90% and multi-cloud customers have the highest switching costs, then proactively migrating single-cloud enterprise customers to multi-cloud configurations (by bundling Agentforce with Data Cloud, Slack, and MuleSoft) will both increase per-customer revenue and deepen the switching cost moat — directly countering the competitive rivalry threat from Microsoft, which is attempting to leverage its own multi-product ecosystem (Azure, Teams, Dynamics) to displace Salesforce.

**Action:** Launch a "Customer 360 AI Expansion Programme" targeting the approximately 50% of large customers using only a single Salesforce cloud. Offer discounted multi-cloud bundles that include Agentforce at no additional cost for the first year, converting the AI trial into a long-term revenue stream while deepening platform lock-in.
