# COMAC AND THE C919: BREAKING THE DUOPOLY — A STRATEGIC CASE STUDY

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This case study examines the Commercial Aircraft Corporation of China (COMAC) and its flagship C919 narrowbody jet as a strategic situation defined by acute tension between state-sponsored ambition and structural vulnerability. Using Porter's Five Forces, a SWOT analysis, the Resource-Based View (RBV), and a PESTEL framework, the analysis diagnoses a company that possesses unparalleled domestic demand and unlimited state financial backing, yet is simultaneously held hostage by foreign-controlled critical inputs and locked out of the international markets it must eventually penetrate to justify its mandate. The analysis leads to a four-pillar action plan: (1) accelerate indigenous engine certification as the single most urgent strategic priority; (2) pursue EASA certification through a dedicated regulatory engagement programme; (3) execute a differentiated "Global South First" internationalisation strategy; and (4) build a maintenance, repair, and overhaul (MRO) ecosystem to reduce operator switching costs.

## 1. BACKGROUND AND SITUATION OVERVIEW

COMAC (the Commercial Aircraft Corporation of China) is a Chinese state-owned aerospace manufacturer established on 11 May 2008 in Shanghai, with headquarters in Pudong. Its founding was an explicit act of industrial policy: the Chinese government deployed multiple policy instruments to develop its domestic commercial aviation manufacturing industry, merging multiple aviation industry corporations to form COMAC — a state-owned company that designs and manufactures commercial jets in China.

The flagship product is the C919 narrowbody jet. The C919's development programme was launched in 2008 as a competitor to the Airbus A320 and Boeing 737. Production began in December 2011, with the first prototype rolled out on 2 November 2015; the maiden flight took place on 5 May 2017. On 29 September 2022 the C919 received its CAAC type certificate. The aircraft entered commercial service in May 2023 with China Eastern Airlines and has since logged over 1.5 million passengers on domestic routes.

The **strategic situation** is one of escalating contradiction. COMAC holds a domestic order backlog of over 1,000 aircraft and enjoys unlimited state support, yet its production trajectory is deeply troubled. COMAC originally aimed to deliver 75 C919s in 2025; subsequently it slashed that figure to 25. Even so, the actual result was approximately 16, only marginally ahead of the 13 delivered in 2024. Meanwhile, the geopolitical environment has sharpened into an existential supply-chain threat: on 28 May 2025, reports emerged that the United States had halted certain sales of vital technologies to China, specifically targeting jet engines destined for COMAC. This case study analyses why COMAC finds itself in this position and what it must do to resolve it.

## 2. INDUSTRY ANALYSIS: PORTER'S FIVE FORCES

Porter's Five Forces framework (Porter, 1980) identifies the structural determinants of industry profitability and competitive intensity. Applied to the commercial narrowbody aircraft manufacturing industry, it reveals why COMAC faces a near-impossible competitive environment in global markets while simultaneously enjoying near-perfect conditions in its domestic one.

### 2.1 Threat of New Entrants — LOW (for global market), but COMAC *is* the entrant

The commercial aircraft industry has some of the highest barriers to entry of any manufacturing sector. Capital requirements run to billions of dollars, regulatory certification processes span years, and the technological learning curve is measured in decades. The Chinese government understood that the development of a new airplane is a long and expensive process and provided financial and non-financial aid hand-over-fist to the industry. In 2009, COMAC was allotted a paid-up capital of $2.8 billion along with a $4.4 billion credit line from the Bank of Communications to begin operations. Only a state actor could absorb these costs without commercial return pressure. This means that while the threat of *further* new entrants is low (protecting the incumbents Airbus and Boeing from additional challengers), COMAC itself is the disruptive entrant attempting to scale these very barriers — and the difficulty of doing so is precisely what explains its production shortfalls.

### 2.2 Bargaining Power of Suppliers — CRITICALLY HIGH (COMAC's most acute vulnerability)

This is the central strategic problem for COMAC. The C919 relies heavily on Western suppliers for its most critical components, including the CFM International LEAP-1C engines, Honeywell avionics, and Safran flight-control systems. The degree of dependency is quantified: the core issue lies in COMAC's reliance on foreign suppliers for 50–60% of the C919's components. This is not merely a commercial inconvenience — it is a geopolitical weapon. The US Commerce Department revoked several licenses that previously allowed American companies to provide products and technology to COMAC for developing its flagship C919 aircraft, affecting not just engines but broader technology transfers critical to the aircraft's development and production.

The supplier power is further compounded by market structure: these suppliers have faced their own production pressures, as the same LEAP engines also power Airbus's A320neo and Boeing's 737 MAX. With demand for those aircraft running at record levels, COMAC has found itself competing for scarce engine slots, creating cascading delays that ripple through the entire production schedule. COMAC thus faces a double bind: its suppliers are simultaneously constrained by commercial demand from COMAC's own competitors and are subject to political direction from a hostile government. No strategic framework better captures this than supplier power analysis — and no single force is more urgently in need of resolution.

### 2.3 Bargaining Power of Buyers — MODERATE (domestically low; internationally high)

In China's domestic market, buyer power is structurally suppressed. COMAC benefits from protectionist policies and state-directed procurement, ensuring a growing share of the Chinese aviation market, the world's second largest. The "buyers" — China Eastern, Air China, and China Southern — are themselves state-owned entities subject to government direction. China has banned its airlines from accepting deliveries of Boeing airplanes, a move in retaliation against President Donald Trump's boosting tariffs on Chinese goods to 145%. This political compulsion converts what would otherwise be free buyer choice into captive demand — effectively eliminating buyer power in the domestic context.

Internationally, however, buyer power is very high. Non-Chinese leasing companies and airlines have indicated they would require EASA or FAA validation before considering the aircraft for their fleets. Without certification, international buyers cannot legally operate the aircraft in most jurisdictions, meaning COMAC has no leverage over them whatsoever. The only confirmed international order belongs to GallopAir of Brunei, a startup backed by Chinese investors, which ordered 15 C919s as part of a US$2 billion deal signed in September 2023. This asymmetry — captive domestic buyers vs. entirely elective international ones — defines COMAC's dual-market challenge.

### 2.4 Threat of Substitutes — HIGH

From the perspective of airlines choosing aircraft, the A320neo and Boeing 737 MAX are direct substitutes for the C919. The competitive comparison is unfavourable for COMAC on most technical dimensions: the C919 may have an edge over its rivals in terms of acquisition price and political support from its home market, but it falls short in terms of capacity, range, fuel efficiency, and operating costs. On price, the advantage COMAC once claimed has eroded: the new price reflects an increase of $9 million compared to the previous year, and the aircraft is now more expensive than the entry-level Boeing 737 MAX 7, which costs $99.7 million. This matters because the primary rationale for an international airline to accept the certification risk and MRO complexity of a new Chinese aircraft was price — if that advantage disappears, the substitute threat intensifies.

### 2.5 Rivalry Among Existing Competitors — INTENSE

The Airbus-Boeing duopoly controls more than 90 percent of the global narrowbody market. In 2024, Airbus secured a total of 878 gross orders while Boeing secured 569 aircraft orders; Airbus delivered 766 commercial aircraft in 2024, while Boeing delivered 348. The incumbents have backlogs measured in years and established global MRO networks, regulatory relationships, and customer trust built over decades. Rivalry is further intensified by the fact that both Airbus and Boeing are actively competing for Chinese airline contracts that COMAC is simultaneously trying to capture through state mandate. The competitive rivalry facing COMAC is therefore not merely commercial but existential — it must displace entrenched global champions while still building its own basic manufacturing competence.

**Porter's Five Forces Summary Verdict:** The structural forces are almost uniformly hostile to COMAC's global ambitions. The one exception — suppressed buyer power in the domestic market — is the strategic resource upon which all other plans must be built.

## 3. INTERNAL ANALYSIS: RESOURCE-BASED VIEW AND SWOT

### 3.1 Resource-Based View (RBV)

The RBV (Barney, 1991) holds that sustained competitive advantage derives from resources that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN). Applied to COMAC, the analysis reveals a striking imbalance: COMAC's most powerful resources are institutional and political rather than technological.

**Valuable and Rare: Sovereign demand guarantee.** COMAC's most powerful resource is access to a captive, state-directed domestic market of extraordinary scale. China will more than double its commercial airplane fleet by 2043 as its aviation industry expands and modernises to meet growing demand for passenger and cargo air travel, with annual passenger traffic growth of 5.9% exceeding the global average of 4.7%. Boeing forecasts demand for 8,830 new planes over the next 20 years, with about 60% for growth and 40% replacing older jets with more fuel-efficient models. COMAC, as the state-designated national champion, has first claim on a portion of this demand. No private competitor can replicate this institutional positioning — it is genuinely rare and non-substitutable.

**Valuable but Not Yet Inimitable: State financial backing.** COMAC received a capital injection of 44 billion yuan (US$6.2 billion) from its stakeholders, boosting its total registered capital by 88 per cent to more than 94 billion yuan, led by the State Council's State-owned Assets Supervision and Administration Commission. Backed by state subsidies, COMAC doesn't face the same market pressures as Boeing or Airbus. It can afford to move slowly, absorb losses, and focus on long-term geopolitical goals. This financial patience is a strategic asset, but it is not inimitable — it merely reflects state ownership rather than firm-specific capability.

**Weakness: Absence of VRIN technological capability.** The RBV diagnosis is damning here. COMAC's critical technological inputs — the engine, avionics, flight control systems — are sourced externally and are therefore neither rare nor inimitable from COMAC's perspective. COMAC's dependence on the LEAP-1C engine is expected to persist for several more years. The company is developing an indigenous alternative, the CJ-1000A, but that programme is unlikely to enter serial production before 2030. Until COMAC controls its own propulsion technology, it cannot claim a VRIN technological resource base — and without that, its competitive advantage is structurally fragile.

### 3.2 SWOT Analysis

**Strengths:**
- Captive domestic order backlog: despite a domestic order backlog exceeding 1,000 aircraft, the C919 has yet to secure firm international orders beyond smaller deals with allies like Brunei and Cambodia. The backlog itself is a strength; its geographic concentration is the weakness.
- Unlimited state capital: the 44 billion yuan injection demonstrates the state's commitment to COMAC's survival regardless of commercial performance.
- Growing domestic utilisation: COMAC Boost C919 production plans reflect that during the 2024 Lunar New Year period, C919 flights saw a 40% increase in utilisation compared to the previous year.

**Weaknesses:**
- Supply chain dependency: about 40 per cent of the C919's systems come from Western suppliers, including engines and avionics, making COMAC vulnerable to export controls, geopolitical disputes and supply-chain hiccups.
- Certification gap: the C919 is certified only by China's Civil Aviation Administration, leaving it unable to fly commercially in North America or Europe.
- Production immaturity: the delivery record — 1 in 2022, 2 in 2023, 13 in 2024, approximately 16 in 2025 — reveals a manufacturing ramp-up that is years behind schedule.
- Geopolitical stigma: in January 2025, COMAC was added to a United States Department of Defense list of companies that allegedly work with the PLA. This designation complicates relationships with Western lessors, insurers, and component suppliers.

**Opportunities:**
- Western delivery backlogs: production delays at Airbus and Boeing have created openings for new players, and some industry leaders in the Gulf and Asia are watching closely.
- Southeast Asian market: COMAC is positioning the C919 to expand beyond China. In Indonesia, COMAC is negotiating a possible C919 agreement with national carrier Garuda Indonesia. China is positioning itself as a reliable and timely aircraft supplier amid growing dissatisfaction with US trade policies.
- BRI diplomatic leverage: the Belt and Road Initiative is now entering phase 2.0 amid the intensifying US-China trade war, with Chinese President Xi Jinping's state visits to Southeast Asia opening doors for COMAC to enter the ASEAN market.

**Threats:**
- US export controls: halting exports of the CFM engines to China would ultimately slow down C919 production and deliveries. With the LEAP-1C being the exclusive powerplant for the C919, aircraft currently being produced will be sitting on the ground without engines.
- EASA certification timeline: approval from Western regulators like the FAA or EASA remains years away, likely 2028–2031, limiting the aircraft's export potential, critical for competing globally.
- Pentagon listing consequences: newly identified entities including COMAC were determined to meet statutory criteria tied to state ownership, military-civil fusion activity, or affiliation with China's defence-industrial authorities. This listing creates legal and reputational barriers for potential Western customers and partners.

## 4. MACRO-ENVIRONMENT ANALYSIS: PESTEL

The PESTEL framework (Johnson, Scholes & Whittington, 2017) maps the macro-environmental forces shaping COMAC's strategic context.

**Political:** The US-China trade war is the defining political force. China has banned its airlines from accepting deliveries of Boeing airplanes, in retaliation against President Donald Trump's boosting tariffs on Chinese goods to 145%. This political escalation is simultaneously an opportunity (it redirects Chinese airline demand towards COMAC) and a threat (it provoked the US engine export ban that directly crippled COMAC's production). The political environment is therefore double-edged and volatile.

**Economic:** The macro-economic opportunity is enormous. China will overtake North America and Europe to become the largest market for aviation services by 2043, with its market value almost tripling from US$23bn in 2024 to US$61bn in 2043. COMAC is positioned — by state fiat — to capture a portion of this growth. However, the economic model of COMAC is unusual: as a state-owned enterprise, it is not optimising for profit but for strategic national objectives, which means standard economic metrics of viability are less constraining than they would be for a private firm.

**Social:** Growing Chinese national pride in domestic technology ("Made in China 2025") creates a social tailwind for C919 adoption among Chinese carriers and passengers. The 40% increase in C919 utilisation during the 2024 Lunar New Year period is consistent with this dynamic.

**Technological:** The technological gap is COMAC's most pressing strategic concern. The CJ-1000A is being developed as the domestic powerplant for the C919, initiated in the early 2010s to reduce China's dependence on foreign engines amid geopolitical tensions and supply chain vulnerabilities. However, the design of the CJ-1000A had been completed and certification was ongoing; the engine is thought likely to be installed in a first COMAC jet for daily use between 2028 and 2029. This timeline means COMAC faces a minimum three-to-four year window of continued vulnerability. Meanwhile, COMAC is aggressively localising its supply chain as China seeks technological self-reliance, with Chinese firms like AVIC Avionics Systems, CASC, and BAIC being nurtured to replace Western suppliers like Honeywell, Thales, and Collins Aerospace.

**Environmental:** Sustainability regulation is a growing force in aviation. The C919's reliance on the LEAP-1C engine — the same engine used on the A320neo and 737 MAX — means its fuel efficiency profile is competitive with those aircraft. However, COMAC has not yet established a credible sustainability reporting framework or SAF (Sustainable Aviation Fuel) integration roadmap, which will become increasingly important for European market entry.

**Legal:** The certification regime is the key legal constraint. Certification by EASA is seen as critical to the international success of the C919, as most countries require commercial aircraft to be approved by major regulators such as EASA or the US FAA. EASA has entered an advanced stage of the certification process for the C919, with a strong focus on in-flight testing and technical validation, as part of a structured four-stage approval process. Initial flight evaluations began in late 2024. The legal pathway exists but is long: EASA's executive director noted in 2025 that while certification for established manufacturers takes one to two years, COMAC lacks the established regulatory relationship that would accelerate this process.

## 5. STRATEGIC POSITION SYNTHESIS

Connecting the frameworks produces a coherent strategic diagnosis. Porter's Five Forces reveals that COMAC's only structurally advantaged competitive position is in the domestic market, where buyer power is suppressed by state direction and new entrants are absent. The RBV reveals that COMAC's only genuinely VRIN resource is its institutionally guaranteed access to that domestic demand — not its technology, which is externally sourced and therefore neither inimitable nor controlled. The SWOT confirms that the most critical weakness (supplier dependency) and the most critical threat (US export controls) are two expressions of the same underlying problem: COMAC has built its flagship product on a foundation of foreign-controlled critical inputs. The PESTEL analysis confirms that the political environment is making this dependency more dangerous, not less, while simultaneously creating diplomatic openings in the Global South that COMAC has not yet fully exploited.

The coherent chain of reasoning is therefore: **COMAC's strategic situation is defined by a race between two timelines** — the timeline on which it achieves indigenous engine certification and EASA approval (enabling genuine competitive independence), and the timeline on which US export controls escalate to the point where C919 production is permanently impaired. Everything else — market share, international orders, MRO network — is secondary to winning this race.

## 6. RECOMMENDATIONS AND ACTION PLAN

The following four-pillar action plan flows directly from the strategic analysis above. Each recommendation is warranted by the evidence and connected to the analytical conclusions.

### Pillar 1: Accelerate CJ-1000A Certification as the Single Highest Strategic Priority

**Warrant:** The Five Forces analysis identifies supplier power as COMAC's most acute structural vulnerability. The RBV demonstrates that COMAC cannot claim a VRIN technological resource while its propulsion system is externally controlled. The PESTEL analysis confirms the political risk of this dependency is intensifying. Therefore, eliminating the LEAP-1C dependency is not merely a technical objective — it is the precondition for every other strategic goal.

**Action:** COMAC should redirect a disproportionate share of its newly injected state capital towards the CJ-1000A certification programme. The current timeline — mass production expected from around 2030 — should be treated as unacceptable given the export control environment. COMAC should pursue a parallel-path certification strategy: continue LEAP-1C-powered production to fulfil existing domestic orders while fast-tracking CJ-1000A flight testing using a dedicated airframe. The Chinese government should further accelerate this by treating CJ-1000A certification as a national security priority, applying the same urgency as a military programme.

**Metric:** First CJ-1000A-powered C919 commercial delivery by 2028; full serial production by 2030.

### Pillar 2: Pursue EASA Certification Through a Dedicated Regulatory Engagement Programme

**Warrant:** The buyer power analysis shows that international airlines will not purchase the C919 without Western certification. The SWOT identifies this as the primary barrier to international market entry. The C919's export potential is critically limited by the absence of EASA or FAA approval, with approval likely 2028–2031. Without international orders, COMAC cannot achieve the production scale needed to drive down unit costs and become genuinely competitive. The certification pathway is therefore the gateway to commercial viability beyond China.

**Action:** COMAC should establish a permanent EASA liaison office in Cologne, staffed by dedicated engineers and regulatory specialists, to manage the four-stage approval process in real time. EASA has entered an advanced stage of the certification process, with in-flight testing and technical validation underway. COMAC should prioritise transparency with EASA — providing full design documentation, test data, and manufacturing process access — to build the institutional trust that EASA's executive director identified as the primary bottleneck. COMAC should not pursue FAA certification in the current political climate (it has already paused this effort), but should focus exclusively on EASA as the pathway to the 100+ countries that accept EASA validation.

**Metric:** EASA type certificate awarded by 2028; first delivery to a non-Chinese airline by 2029.

### Pillar 3: Execute a Differentiated "Global South First" Internationalisation Strategy

**Warrant:** The Five Forces analysis shows that buyer power is highest in markets with access to Airbus and Boeing alternatives. The PESTEL analysis identifies a political opening in BRI-aligned countries where Western aircraft manufacturers are less entrenched and Chinese diplomatic relationships are strong. COMAC is positioning the C919 as a lower-cost rival drawing attention across Southeast Asia, with its strategy aligning with current market needs: fast delivery times, competitive pricing, and sufficient capability for short-to-medium haul routes. This strategy is correct in direction but insufficiently resourced.

**Action:** COMAC should prioritise five to eight BRI-aligned markets — Indonesia, Malaysia, Kazakhstan, Cambodia, Vietnam, and potentially Pakistan and Ethiopia — where Chinese diplomatic relationships are strong and Western delivery backlogs create genuine openings. AirAsia has expressed strong interest in purchasing the COMAC C919, with CEO Tony Fernandes confirming active talks at the Belt and Road Summit in Hong Kong. COMAC should convert this interest into a firm order by offering a package that goes beyond aircraft price: include Chinese state-backed financing (via China Development Bank or Export-Import Bank of China), training for local pilots and engineers, and a guaranteed MRO support agreement. This "total solution" approach addresses the switching cost concerns of international buyers and leverages China's comparative advantage in state-backed financing — a resource Airbus and Boeing cannot easily match.

**Metric:** Five firm international orders (non-Chinese buyers) by end of 2027; first international commercial service by 2026.

### Pillar 4: Build a Domestic MRO Ecosystem to Reduce Operator Switching Costs and Validate Reliability

**Warrant:** The RBV analysis shows that COMAC's competitive advantage must ultimately rest on capabilities that are inimitable. A dense, proven MRO network is one such capability — it takes years to build, is geographically specific, and creates high switching costs for operators. Currently, COMAC's lack of an international MRO network is a major barrier to international adoption: Rob Morris, Global Head of Consultancy at Cirium, highlighted the support challenge as a key concern for potential international buyers. Domestically, building MRO depth also generates the operational data needed to demonstrate reliability to international regulators and customers.

**Action:** COMAC should partner with China's three major state-owned carriers to establish dedicated C919 MRO centres in Shanghai, Beijing, and Guangzhou, staffed by COMAC engineers and operating under CAAC-approved maintenance programmes. Simultaneously, COMAC should negotiate MRO agreements in target international markets — beginning with Malaysia and Indonesia — to demonstrate the support infrastructure that international buyers require. The capital injection of 44 billion yuan provides the resources to fund this infrastructure investment without compromising the CJ-1000A programme.

**Metric:** Three domestic MRO centres operational by 2026; two international MRO agreements signed by 2027.
