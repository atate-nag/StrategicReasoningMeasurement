# Shell plc: Strategic Case Study
## *"Navigating the Energy Trilemma: Shell's Pivot to LNG Leadership and the Credibility Cost of Retreating from Renewables"*

---

## 1. Executive Summary

Shell plc is one of the world's largest integrated energy companies. Since 2023, under CEO Wael Sawan, it has executed a decisive strategic pivot: retreating from offshore wind, solar, and green hydrogen while doubling down on liquefied natural gas (LNG) and high-return upstream assets. This case study examines whether that pivot is strategically coherent, analyses the tensions it creates across financial, competitive, and legitimacy dimensions, and proposes a set of recommendations to resolve those tensions.

The central argument is this: Shell's LNG-led strategy is internally consistent from a resource-based perspective, but it generates a dangerous **legitimacy gap** that, if unmanaged, will erode its social licence to operate and expose it to material long-run financial risk. The chain of reasoning is explicit: *Shell's competitive advantage is real but narrowly concentrated in LNG and trading → the energy transition creates a long-run threat to that advantage → near-term financial performance cannot be sustained if legitimacy collapses → therefore, restoring climate credibility is not a constraint on the strategy but a precondition for its success.*

## 2. Case Background & Situation Overview

### 2.1 Company Profile

Shell plc (LON/NYSE: SHEL) is a British-Dutch multinational energy company headquartered in London. It operates in more than 70 countries and is organised into five reporting segments: Integrated Gas, Upstream, Marketing, Chemicals and Products, and Renewables and Energy Solutions.

### 2.2 The Strategic Situation: An Energy Trilemma

Shell's strategic situation is best characterised as an **energy trilemma**: the simultaneous pressure to (a) deliver competitive financial returns to shareholders, (b) provide secure and affordable energy to customers and governments, and (c) reduce its carbon footprint in line with the Paris Agreement. These imperatives are not easily reconciled, and Shell's choices since 2023 reveal a clear prioritisation hierarchy.

**The 2023 Strategic Reset.** When Wael Sawan took over as CEO, he announced a strategy centred on three principles. "Performance, discipline, and simplification will be our guiding principles as we allocate capital to enhance shareholder distributions, while enabling the energy transition," he declared at the June 2023 Capital Markets Day. The plan targeted 10% per annum free cash flow per share growth through to 2025, a structural cost reduction of $2–3 billion by end-2025, and capital expenditure capped at $22–25 billion per year.

**The LNG Bet.** The centrepiece of the strategy is LNG. Shell will reinforce its leadership position in LNG by growing sales by 4–5% per year through to 2030, and at the 2025 Capital Markets Day, Sawan declared the ambition to become "the world's leading integrated gas and LNG business and the most customer-focused energy marketer and trader, while sustaining a material level of liquids production."

**The Renewables Retreat.** Since taking over in 2023, Sawan has cut wind, solar, and hydrogen investments, pivoting capital to gas projects in Abu Dhabi, Nigeria, Canada, and Qatar. Shell in recent months scaled back operations in offshore wind, solar and hydrogen, sold retail power assets, and in 2024 Shell refreshed its renewable generation, energy marketing, and gas and power trading strategy, shifting Shell's asset portfolio towards energy storage and flexible generation with an increased focus on power trading and minimising new investments in offshore wind projects.

**The Legal and Governance Context.** In November 2024, the Hague Court of Appeal delivered a pivotal ruling. On 12 November 2024, the Court of Appeal in The Hague ruled that Shell has an obligation to combat dangerous climate change. While it overturned the specific 45% emissions-reduction order, the appeals court maintained the District Court's stance that there exists a private law duty of care to reduce emissions. Meanwhile, at the May 2025 AGM, more than a fifth of shareholders voted against Shell's current climate strategy during the company's annual general meeting, at the first AGM since Shell scaled back several climate commitments.

## 3. Environmental Analysis: PESTEL

### 3.1 Political

The energy geopolitics of the 2020s have been transformed by the 2022 Russian invasion of Ukraine, which triggered a European energy security crisis. Shell's own Energy Transition Strategy acknowledges that "the world needs a balanced and orderly transition away from fossil fuels to maintain secure energy supplies, while accelerating the transition to affordable low-carbon solutions." This political environment provides Shell with a favourable policy tailwind for its gas strategy, while the EU's Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism and tightening methane regulations create divergent regulatory pressures across its operating geographies.

### 3.2 Economic

Oil and gas prices remain the dominant driver of Shell's performance. Shell is exposed to macroeconomic risks, including fluctuating prices of crude oil, natural gas, oil products and chemicals, and its ability to deliver competitive returns "depends in part on the accuracy of its price assumptions." Total shareholder distributions in Q4 2024 amounted to $5.7 billion comprising repurchases of shares of $3.6 billion and cash dividends paid to Shell plc shareholders of $2.1 billion. However, Shell's full-year 2024 net profits fell approximately 16% to $23.7 billion, driven partly by lower LNG trading and optimisation margins.

### 3.3 Social

Public attitudes towards fossil fuel companies are increasingly hostile. A record 27 institutional investors holding nearly €4 trillion in assets co-filed the climate resolution at the 2025 AGM. The Milieudefensie litigation reflects a broader social expectation that major emitters bear responsibility for climate change. The social licence to operate is an increasingly material strategic asset.

### 3.4 Technological

The cost of solar PV and onshore wind has fallen dramatically, making renewables competitive with gas-fired power in many markets. However, hard-to-abate sectors remain difficult to decarbonise with current technology. Shell became one of the world's largest traders and suppliers of sustainable aviation fuel in 2024. It achieved this because of its long-term agreements with producers, the strength of its customer relationships, and strategic investments in logistics around key terminals and airports.

### 3.5 Environmental

The potential impact of physical risks comes from both acute and chronic climate hazards. Acute hazards, such as flooding and droughts, wildfires and more severe tropical storms, and chronic hazards, such as rising temperatures and rising sea levels, could impact some of Shell's facilities, operations and supply chains. The transition risk is equally significant: under the IEA's Net Zero Emissions (NZE) scenario, there is no need for any new LNG projects (beyond existing and under-construction projects) until 2040 at the earliest, with significant oversupply expected before then.

### 3.6 Legal

The November 2024 ruling was a qualified win for Shell. The court concluded that "companies like Shell, which…have an obligation to limit CO₂ emissions to counter dangerous climate change, even if this obligation is not explicitly laid down in (public law) regulations of the countries in which the company operates." This creates ongoing legal exposure and sets a precedent for future climate litigation globally. Shell now has the right to adjust its own emissions reductions targets as it sees fit. However, the appeals court maintained the District Court's stance that there exists a private law duty of care to limit CO₂ emissions.

## 4. Industry Analysis: Porter's Five Forces

Porter's (1980) Five Forces framework analyses the structural attractiveness of an industry by examining five competitive forces.

### 4.1 Threat of New Entrants — LOW to MODERATE

The upstream oil and gas sector has extremely high barriers to entry: capital intensity, technical expertise, access to reserves, and regulatory licences. However, in LNG specifically, around 300 billion cubic metres per year of new liquefied natural gas export capacity is expected to be added worldwide by 2030, primarily supported by liquefaction capacity expansions in the United States and Qatar, intensifying competition at the trading level.

### 4.2 Bargaining Power of Suppliers — MODERATE

Oilfield services companies and specialised equipment manufacturers have moderate bargaining power, particularly during periods of high upstream activity. Shell's scale gives it some countervailing leverage. The offshore wind sector illustrated how supply chain bottlenecks can erode project economics—one reason Shell exited that space.

### 4.3 Bargaining Power of Buyers — LOW to MODERATE, but Rising

Industrial and government buyers of LNG have some bargaining power through long-term contract negotiations. Critically, when considering price trajectories informed by current forward curves, global LNG demand growth is not expected to absorb all incremental LNG supply over the 2024–30 period in the base case, which will shift bargaining power towards buyers and compress trading margins.

### 4.4 Threat of Substitutes — HIGH and Rising *(Most Strategically Significant)*

This is the dominant force for Shell over a 10-year horizon. Renewable electricity is now cost-competitive with gas in power generation. EVs are displacing petrol and diesel demand. Under the IEA's most conservative transition scenario, aligned with 2.4°C of warming, there is no need for any new LNG projects beyond existing and under-construction projects until 2040 at the earliest. Shell's own stress-testing of its LNG portfolio against IEA Net Zero pathways assesses resilience under this scenario, testing the portfolio across progressively more stringent scenarios to ensure assessment of resilience under both the most probable policy environment and the most challenging credible transition outcomes, demonstrating that the LNG portfolio retains value.

### 4.5 Competitive Rivalry — HIGH

In Q2 2024, the seven largest oil majors collectively posted approximately $31.65 billion in net profits, illustrating the scale of rivalry. TotalEnergies has pursued a more aggressive integrated renewables strategy, while ExxonMobil and Chevron have remained more firmly committed to hydrocarbons. Shell's net margin of 5.27% recently was surpassed by some competitors. Companies like PetroChina, Sinopec, Rosneft, Lukoil, Gazprom, Petrobras, ConocoPhillips, Equinor, and Eni also represent significant competition, often with strong regional presences.

**Five Forces Summary:** The industry structure is moderately attractive for incumbents in the short-to-medium term, but the threat of substitutes and increasing buyer power in LNG markets represent the dominant strategic risks over a 10-year horizon.

## 5. Internal Analysis: Resource-Based View (VRIO)

The Resource-Based View (Barney, 1991) holds that sustainable competitive advantage derives from resources that are **Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Organised** (VRIO). Shell's strategic pivot can be evaluated through this lens.

### 5.1 LNG Trading and Optimisation Capability ✅ *Sustained Competitive Advantage*

Shell sells nearly three times the energy products that it produces, meeting customers' demand for oil and gas and low-carbon products through its integrated model. This trading capability is:

- **Valuable**: Generates margin across the full LNG value chain, not just from production.
- **Rare**: Very few companies combine Shell's physical LNG assets, long-term supply contracts, and global trading infrastructure.
- **Inimitable**: Built over decades; combines proprietary market intelligence, counterparty relationships, and risk management systems that are extremely difficult to replicate.
- **Organised**: Shell has deliberately organised its business around this capability, with Strategy reporting to the CFO to enable "more streamlined planning and better capital allocation decisions."

**Verdict: Sustained competitive advantage.** This is Shell's strongest VRIO asset and the primary justification for its LNG-led strategy.

### 5.2 Downstream Customer Reach ✅ *Competitive Advantage (under long-run threat)*

Shell's downstream marketing operations provide access to more customers than any competitor, creating opportunities to introduce low-carbon solutions as market demand develops. The scale of Shell's retail network—built over decades of brand investment—is difficult to match. However, EV adoption poses a long-run threat to petrol station revenues.

### 5.3 Deep-Water Upstream Expertise ✅ *Competitive Parity*

In upstream operations, Shell focuses on deep-water production assets that offer favourable breakeven costs and produce lower CO₂ emission barrels. With prolific basins in the US Gulf of Mexico and Brazil, Shell has structured its upstream around sustaining 1.4 million barrels per day of liquids production to 2030.

### 5.4 Renewables and Low-Carbon Capabilities ⚠️ *Competitive Disadvantage*

Shell currently has around 3.4 gigawatts (GW) of renewable capacity in operation. This is modest relative to Shell's scale and is shrinking as a strategic priority. Shell has affirmed an investment of up to $1 billion annually in hydrogen and CCS technology in 2024 and 2025, which is disciplined but limited compared to its overall capex of $22–25 billion. Shell's exit from offshore wind development has materially weakened this capability, while rivals like TotalEnergies are building harder-to-imitate integrated renewables positions.

**Verdict: Competitive disadvantage relative to TotalEnergies; risk of capability atrophy.**

### 5.5 Brand and Social Licence ⚠️ *Weakening Asset*

Shell is one of the world's most recognised energy brands, but its reputational capital has been eroded. AXA IM has expressed concern about "the lack of absolute Scope 3 emissions reduction targets and…an excessive (relative to peers) reliance on nature-based offsets." Critics note that Shell scrapped its 2035 target in 2024, just before it moved into its ten-year planning period, raising questions about the credibility of its 2050 net-zero ambition.

## 8. Recommendations & Action Plan

The recommendations are organised around three strategic imperatives: **Defend the Core**, **Build the Bridge**, and **Restore Legitimacy**.

---

### Imperative 1: Defend the Core — Protect and Extend LNG Leadership

**Recommendation 1.1: Execute the LNG growth plan with discipline, but stress-test against IEA NZE scenarios.**

Shell's LNG trading capability is its most defensible competitive advantage. The plan to grow LNG sales by 4–5% per year to 2030 is commercially rational. However, around 300 bcm/year of new LNG export capacity is expected to be added by 2030, and global LNG demand growth is not expected to absorb all incremental supply over the 2024–30 period in the base case. Shell should therefore:

- **Prioritise long-term contracted volumes** over spot trading exposure to hedge against oversupply.
- **Publicly disclose** scenario analysis of LNG portfolio resilience under IEA NZE pathways. Shell already stress-tests its LNG portfolio against IEA Net Zero pathways that assume faster policy action, rapid electrification, accelerated coal phase-out and an earlier peak in fossil fuel demand—this analysis should be made public to address the transparency demands raised by ShareAction and ACCR at the 2025 AGM.
- Ensure new LNG investments meet a **carbon cost hurdle** (e.g., $80/tonne CO₂) to screen out assets that would become stranded under accelerated transition.

**Recommendation 1.2: Decisively complete the Chemicals restructuring.**

Shell has announced plans to seek strategic partners for its chemical portfolio in the United States and potentially close some or all of its chemical operations in Europe, following three consecutive years of losses in the chemical division. This restructuring should be completed decisively. Capital released from underperforming chemicals assets should be redeployed into the selective low-carbon platforms identified below.

---

### Imperative 2: Build the Bridge — Selective Low-Carbon Platform Development

The VRIO analysis identifies three low-carbon areas where Shell has genuine competitive advantage and where market demand is real: SAF trading, CCS, and power trading/battery storage.

**Recommendation 2.1: Scale SAF to a market-defining position.**

Shell became one of the world's largest traders and suppliers of SAF in 2024 because of its long-term agreements with producers, the strength of its customer relationships, and strategic investments in logistics around key terminals and airports. Aviation is a hard-to-abate sector where LNG-style trading capabilities—aggregation, optimisation, long-term contracting—are directly transferable. Shell should:

- Expand SAF supply agreements beyond its initial 2 million tonnes/year target.
- Invest in SAF production capacity (via the Rotterdam biofuels plant and similar capital-light partnerships) to capture upstream margin.
- Position SAF as the "LNG of aviation"—a bridging fuel that Shell can dominate through its trading infrastructure.

**Recommendation 2.2: Commit to CCS as an industrial service business.**

Shell has affirmed an investment of up to $1 billion annually in hydrogen and CCS technology in 2024 and 2025, thereby making investments in a disciplined manner to create options for the future. CCS is uniquely suited to Shell's capabilities: it requires subsurface expertise (directly transferable from upstream), large-scale project management, and long-term industrial customer relationships. Shell should:

- Focus CCS investment on industrial clusters (Rotterdam, Oman) where it can serve multiple emitters and achieve economies of scale.
- Develop CCS as a **fee-based service business**, reducing capital intensity and creating recurring revenue streams.
- Pioneer cross-border CCS via liquefied CO₂ shipping—a capability no other company is currently positioned to lead at scale.

**Recommendation 2.3: Build a power trading and battery storage business.**

Shell's decision to focus on power trading and battery storage—rather than owning offshore wind assets—is strategically sound given its trading capabilities. Shell, one of the world's largest energy traders, will focus on selling power to customers and developing battery storage sites, as it sees increasing value in using batteries and flexible gas-fired power plants to manage intermittency and help meet customers' needs as renewables play increasing roles in power markets. Shell should deploy this strategy via capital-light, debt-financed, partnership-based models, consistent with its aim to maximise returns from onshore positions using capital-light business models, debt finance and partnerships.

---

### Imperative 3: Restore Legitimacy — Close the Credibility Gap

This is the most urgent recommendation, because the legitimacy gap is a **strategic risk**, not merely a reputational one.

**Recommendation 3.1: Adopt absolute Scope 3 emissions reduction targets.**

Shell's current approach relies on **net carbon intensity (NCI)** targets, which allow absolute emissions to rise if production grows. Although Shell has an ambition to reach net-zero by 2050 in absolute terms, Shell's 'intensity' pledges for 2030 and 2035 are not the same as commitments to achieve near-term absolute reductions in fossil fuels. Shell should set absolute Scope 3 emissions reduction targets for 2030, aligned with a well-below-2°C pathway, and publish a credible transition pathway showing how the 2050 net-zero ambition will be achieved through binding intermediate milestones—not just an aspirational 2050 endpoint. The scrapping of the 2035 NCI target was particularly damaging: it is very telling that Shell scrapped its 2035 target in 2024, just before it moved into its ten-year planning period, raising the question of what value the 2050 net-zero target holds if it remains permanently outside the planning horizon.

**Recommendation 3.2: Publish transparent LNG scenario disclosure.**

The ShareAction/ACCR resolution at the 2025 AGM specifically requested transparency on how Shell's LNG growth strategy is compatible with its climate commitments. Shell has committed to "engage with shareholders and…prepare a note on its website related to the LNG market, Shell's LNG business, and how its LNG business reconciles with its climate commitments." This commitment should be fulfilled with a rigorous, independently verified disclosure that includes: LNG portfolio carbon intensity benchmarked against IEA scenarios; sensitivity analysis of LNG asset values under 1.5°C, 2°C, and 2.4°C warming pathways; and a clear explanation of the methane leakage assumptions underlying Shell's claim that LNG displaces coal in Asia.

**Recommendation 3.3: Seek Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi) Validation.**

A substantial majority of corporate net zero commitments do not meet the SBTi standard or have been set without any independent validation. Shell's targets currently fall into this category. Seeking SBTi validation—even if this requires strengthening near-term targets—would provide independent credibility, reduce the risk of future greenwashing litigation, and address the institutional investor concerns that drove the 20%+ dissent vote at the 2025 AGM.

## 9. Conclusion

Shell's strategic situation is one of the most complex in global business. CEO Wael Sawan's "performance, discipline, and simplification" strategy is a coherent response grounded in Shell's genuine competitive advantages. The VRIO analysis confirms that Shell's LNG trading capability, downstream customer reach, and deep-water upstream expertise are valuable, rare, inimitable, and organised—the foundations of sustainable competitive advantage in Barney's (1991) framework.

However, the strategy as currently executed generates a dangerous legitimacy gap. The retreat from renewables, the reliance on intensity-based rather than absolute emissions targets, and the scrapping of the 2035 NCI target have eroded Shell's climate credibility at precisely the moment when climate litigation risk is rising and institutional investors are increasing their scrutiny. The Hague Court of Appeal ruled that Shell has an obligation to combat dangerous climate change, and that companies like Shell have an obligation to limit CO₂ emissions even where this is not explicitly required by public law. The 20%+ shareholder dissent at the 2025 AGM, driven by a record 27 institutional investors holding nearly €4 trillion in assets, is a leading indicator of a legitimacy crisis that could become a financial one.

The recommended "Selective Transition Leadership" strategy resolves this tension by maintaining LNG as the near-term cash engine while selectively building defensible low-carbon positions—SAF, CCS, power trading—where Shell's existing capabilities provide genuine competitive advantage. It couples this with a credibility restoration programme: absolute Scope 3 targets, transparent LNG scenario disclosure, and SBTi engagement. This is not altruism; it is strategic risk management. The logic is coherent and explicit: a company that loses its social licence in Europe, faces escalating litigation, and sees institutional capital withdraw is a company whose long-run cost of capital rises—precisely the outcome that "performance, discipline, and simplification" is designed to prevent.
