# BAE Systems at the Strategic Inflection Point: Navigating the Paradox of Record Demand and Structural Vulnerability

## A Strategic Case Study

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

BAE Systems plc — Europe's largest defence contractor — faces a paradox that defines the strategic situation examined in this case study: it sits atop a record £77.8 billion order backlog at precisely the moment when its internal capacity to convert that backlog into delivered revenue is most strained. The firm operates in an environment of surging geopolitical demand, yet confronts simultaneous structural threats from technology-native disruptors, a deepening talent crisis, supply chain fragility, and ethical scrutiny. This case applies Porter's Five Forces, the Resource-Based View (RBV) and Dynamic Capabilities, Porter's Value Chain, and the Ansoff Growth Matrix to diagnose the situation and produce a coherent, prioritised action plan.

---

## SECTION 1: CASE CONTEXT AND SITUATION OVERVIEW

### 1.1 The Company

BAE Systems plc is a British multinational defence, security, and aerospace company. Formed in 1999 from the merger of British Aerospace and Marconi Electronic Systems, BAE Systems has evolved into a global systems business with a significant U.S. presence and is Europe's largest defence contractor, ranking seventh globally. Its portfolio spans air platforms (Eurofighter Typhoon, F-35 components), naval vessels, armoured vehicles, electronic warfare systems, and cybersecurity solutions. BAE Systems is also the largest foreign investor in the U.S. defence industry and plays a crucial role in international defence programmes like the Global Combat Air Programme (GCAP).

### 1.2 The Strategic Situation

BAE Systems reported a 14% revenue increase and record profits in 2024, with 2025 sales projected to exceed £30 billion. As of May 2025, it reported a revenue growth of 14.50% and a return on equity (ROE) of 18.14%, with free cash flow expected to exceed £1.5 billion for 2024.

BAE Systems reported record order backlog growth of 11% to £77.8bn ($98.06bn) in 2024 with 14% growth in sales and underlying EBIT. The CEO, Charles Woodburn, stated: "We're supporting our customers around the world, while shaping our portfolio towards higher growth and strategically important markets. Across our business, we're also investing in our people, facilities and technologies to drive efficiencies, boost capacity and increase our agility to deliver in a rapidly evolving environment."

Yet behind this headline performance, three structural tensions define the strategic situation:

1. **The Execution Gap**: The backlog is growing faster than BAE's production capacity, creating a conversion risk.
2. **The Competitive Disruption**: Technology-native firms are entering the defence market and challenging the traditional prime-contractor model.
3. **The Talent-Technology Bind**: Scaling up requires STEM and AI talent that is in acute shortage across the industry.

---

## SECTION 2: ENVIRONMENTAL ANALYSIS — PORTER'S FIVE FORCES

Porter's Five Forces framework maps the structural attractiveness of the defence industry and identifies where competitive pressure on BAE Systems is most acute. The framework is applied here not as a static snapshot but as a diagnostic of *which forces are shifting*, since it is the *direction of change* in each force that determines strategic urgency.

---

### Force 1: Threat of New Entrants — **RISING (High Strategic Urgency)**

Historically, the defence industry's barriers to entry — security clearances, long procurement cycles, capital intensity, and regulatory complexity — made it nearly impenetrable. That calculus is changing.

Anduril Industries was founded in 2017 by Palmer Luckey, a virtual-reality headset designer, with the goal of incorporating artificial intelligence into novel weapons systems, supported by prominent Silicon Valley investors including Peter Thiel's Founders Fund. Anduril subsequently won contracts that displaced traditional primes: it was one of two under-the-radar companies that left three major defence heavyweights in the dust by winning the contract to build a prototype of the Collaborative Combat Aircraft.

The challenge has since metastasised into a coalition. In late 2024, Palantir was reportedly building and leading a consortium with another dozen contractors, including SpaceX, OpenAI, and Saronic, to challenge the domination of the traditional "primes." Furthermore, in March 2024, the Army chose Palantir as the prime contractor on the Tactical Intelligence Targeting Access Node (TITAN) programme — beating Raytheon — for the Other Transaction Agreement prototyping effort.

**Analytical inference**: The traditional moat of relationships and clearances is being eroded by government procurement reform (Other Transaction Agreements) that explicitly bypasses traditional acquisition rules, giving technology-native firms a foothold. For BAE Systems, this force is therefore not merely a future threat but a present competitive displacement risk, particularly in AI-enabled command, control, and autonomous systems — segments where BAE has historically been less dominant than in platforms.

---

### Force 2: Bargaining Power of Buyers — **STRUCTURALLY HIGH BUT GEOPOLITICALLY MODERATED**

Defence markets are characterised by monopsony — the government is, in most cases, the sole buyer. Delivering complex military systems requires a long multi-stage value chain from initial concept to long-term support, with primary activities including R&D and design, contracting/procurement, manufacturing, systems integration, and sustainment. In each stage, the buyer (government) sets the specification, owns the intellectual property rights in many cases, and controls the contract structure.

However, the current geopolitical environment has meaningfully shifted the balance. For contractors, the NATO spending surge means larger addressable markets, longer programme backlogs, improved pricing power, and higher utilisation of production capacity, which together support stronger revenue visibility and margin expansion. Critically, NATO spending targets act as a policy-driven demand floor for the industry, anchoring growth for prime contractors and tier-one and tier-two suppliers alike over the next decade.

**Analytical inference**: Buyer power remains structurally high — governments can cancel programmes, change specifications, and impose cost controls — but the *urgency* of demand created by the Ukraine war and NATO rearmament has temporarily shifted negotiating leverage toward capable suppliers. BAE must exploit this window to lock in long-duration contracts and favourable pricing terms before the political cycle turns.

---

### Force 3: Threat of Substitutes — **MODERATE BUT STRUCTURALLY SHIFTING**

Traditional substitutes for BAE's platforms (e.g., buying from rival NATO allies or purchasing second-hand equipment) remain limited. However, the *nature* of warfare is shifting toward autonomous systems, drones, and AI-enabled software, which are cheaper, faster to produce, and less dependent on traditional manufacturing scale. This constitutes a *functional substitute* for high-cost, low-volume platforms.

The NATO summit in June 2025 marked a pivotal moment, with member states edging closer to adopting a historic 5% GDP defence spending target — split into 3.5% for "hard" military capabilities (e.g., missiles, munitions) and 1.5% for "soft" defence enablers (e.g., cybersecurity, infrastructure). The growing share allocated to "soft" enablers represents a category where autonomous and AI-driven substitutes are most viable.

**Analytical inference**: The threat of substitution is not immediate for BAE's core platforms (fighter jets, warships, armoured vehicles), which require decades-long programmes. However, the growing budget allocation to autonomous and cyber capabilities represents a substitution risk for BAE's electronic systems and intelligence segments, where tech-native firms can deliver faster and at lower cost.

---

### Force 4: Bargaining Power of Suppliers — **RISING (Critical Vulnerability)**

BAE Systems' reliance on a complex network of international suppliers means that even localised issues can have cascading effects across its operations. Geopolitical risks and trade policy shifts in 2023–2024 create persistent uncertainty in global supply chains, with production delays leading to extended lead times for critical components, impacting BAE Systems' ability to fulfil orders on schedule.

This is not a BAE-specific problem but an industry-wide structural constraint. Today, the defence backlog tells the story: a $747 billion defence backlog, up 25% in just two years, with the backlog now standing at 10+ years of production at current rates — underscoring the opportunity cost of inaction. The supply base is simply not scaled to match demand. The three largest US defence primes together hold a combined backlog of US$557 billion, equivalent to 2.7 times 2025 annual sales, implying an order coverage horizon of 30 months at current revenue run rates.

**Analytical inference**: Supplier power is rising because specialist sub-tier suppliers — particularly in semiconductors, energetics, and advanced materials — face their own capacity constraints and can extract higher prices. For BAE, this squeezes margins precisely when governments expect cost discipline, creating a structural profitability risk that is embedded in the very success of the order backlog.

---

### Force 5: Rivalry Among Existing Competitors — **INTENSIFYING**

BAE competes against Lockheed Martin, Northrop Grumman, Thales, Leonardo, and Rheinmetall, all of whom are benefiting from the same demand surge. BAE Systems faces the challenge of intense competition within the defence sector, particularly from state-backed enterprises and agile private firms. Lockheed Martin recorded net sales of $67.6 billion in 2023, dwarfing BAE's £23.2 billion, highlighting the scale disparity in the U.S. market.

**Analytical inference**: Rivalry is intensifying not through price competition (contracts are typically cost-plus or fixed-price with government-set terms) but through *technological differentiation* and *programme capture*. Winning the next-generation programme (like GCAP) locks in revenue for decades; losing it is existential. This makes R&D investment and partnership strategy the primary competitive battleground.

### Five Forces Summary Table

| Force | Current Intensity | Trend | Key Implication for BAE |
|---|---|---|---|
| New Entrants | Moderate → High | ↑ Rising | Tech-native disruptors threaten AI/autonomous segments |
| Buyer Power | High (structural) | ↓ Moderating | Demand surge improves pricing window; exploit now |
| Substitutes | Low → Moderate | ↑ Rising | Autonomous systems threaten platform dominance long-term |
| Supplier Power | Moderate → High | ↑ Rising | Supply chain fragility is the primary execution risk |
| Rivalry | High | ↑ Rising | Programme capture (GCAP, F-35) is existential |

## SECTION 3: MACRO-ENVIRONMENTAL CONTEXT — THE DEMAND SURGE

Before proceeding to internal analysis, it is essential to establish why the external environment creates both opportunity and pressure simultaneously — a duality that makes this situation strategically complex rather than simply favourable.

Global defence spending has increased dramatically since the outbreak of the war in Ukraine in February 2022. As Russia has poured resources into funding its invasion and ongoing operations, NATO allies have sought to boost their own defence capabilities in light of the threat on their borders. Meanwhile, China has continued to report sustained annual growth in its defence budget as it modernises and grows its military. In the summer of 2025, Congress provided an additional $156 billion for defence as a one-time supplemental fund.

Progress toward the 2% NATO target has accelerated dramatically, driven by Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine in 2022. In 2014, only three allies met or exceeded the guideline. By 2024, that number had risen significantly, and in 2025 — for the first time since the target was set — all 32 NATO members are expected to meet or surpass the 2% threshold. Total NATO defence spending reached around $1.6 trillion in 2025.

This demand surge is the central fact that makes BAE's backlog comprehensible — but it also means that the firm's inability to convert backlog into deliveries is not merely a missed revenue opportunity; it is a strategic and national security failure with reputational and contractual consequences.

## SECTION 4: INTERNAL ANALYSIS — RESOURCE-BASED VIEW AND DYNAMIC CAPABILITIES

The Resource-Based View (RBV), developed by Barney (1991), holds that sustained competitive advantage derives from resources and capabilities that are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN). Teece, Pisano, and Shuen's (1997) Dynamic Capabilities extension argues that in rapidly changing environments, firms must also demonstrate the ability to *sense, seize, and reconfigure* their resource base. Both lenses are applied here.

---

### 4.1 VRIN Resource Audit

**Strength 1: Programme Heritage and Security Clearances (Valuable, Rare, Inimitable)**

BAE's involvement in the F-35 programme and the Eurofighter Typhoon represents decades of accumulated systems-integration knowledge that cannot be replicated quickly. The company's strategic positioning on key international defence programmes, such as the F-35 fighter jet and the Eurofighter Typhoon, underscores its market dominance. These programmes also embed BAE into government procurement systems in ways that create switching costs — governments cannot easily replace a prime contractor mid-programme without catastrophic cost and schedule overruns.

**Strength 2: Order Backlog as a Strategic Asset (Valuable, Rare)**

BAE Systems benefits from a robust order backlog, reaching a record £77.8 billion by the end of 2024. This significant figure provides exceptional visibility into future revenue streams, offering a stable foundation for the company's financial performance. This backlog is rare in its scale — it represents approximately 2.7 years of revenue at current run rates — and provides a planning horizon that most industries cannot match.

**Strength 3: AI Integration Capability (Valuable, Rare, Potentially Inimitable)**

BAE Systems is uniquely positioned to dominate the military AI sector not by necessarily creating the single most advanced algorithm, but by mastering the integration of diverse, trusted AI capabilities into complex, mission-critical systems at scale. This dominance is achieved through a synergistic combination of deep domain expertise, a trust-centric strategic framework, a comprehensive technology ecosystem, and a proven ability to deliver assured outcomes to its government partners. This "AI with Purpose" philosophy — emphasising human-in-the-loop design, governance, and assurance — is a source of differentiation that pure-tech entrants struggle to replicate because it is embedded in regulatory relationships and procurement trust, not just algorithmic performance.

**Strength 4: The Ball Aerospace Acquisition (Valuable, Rare — Dynamic Capability in Action)**

On February 16, 2024, BAE announced the completion of its acquisition of Ball Aerospace from Ball Corporation, re-branding it as BAE Space and Mission Systems (SMS), for a purchase price of $5.5 billion. At the time of the acquisition, approximately 5,200 employees were transferred. The acquisition places BAE Systems at the forefront of the aerospace and defence industry's evolution, integrating space and cyber domains. By incorporating Ball Aerospace's advanced space technologies, BAE Systems diversifies its product portfolio and secures a leadership position in the converging sectors of defence, space, and cyber technologies. This acquisition exemplifies the *seizing* dimension of dynamic capabilities — recognising and acting on an opportunity to reconfigure the resource base toward higher-growth domains.

---

### 4.2 Key Weaknesses — Resources Under Stress

**Weakness 1: Human Capital Deficit**

BAE Systems' substantial workforce, numbering over 100,000 employees globally, presents a significant human capital challenge. Attracting, retaining, and developing specialised talent, especially in highly technical fields like AI and cybersecurity, is crucial but demanding.

This is not a BAE-specific failure but a structural industry problem. The impending "silver tsunami" of more than 2.5 million baby-boomer retirements from the industry by 2025 is contributing to a skills gap that will continue to grow until a solution is found. "The skills gap is not the manufacturing industry's problem to solve alone — it will require collaboration between industry, government, academia and local communities."

BAE has responded: Addressing the skills gap, BAE has launched apprenticeships and STEM partnerships with universities. Its "Digital Academy" trains employees in AI and data science, ensuring a tech-savvy workforce, and R&D investment, exceeding £1.5 billion annually, drives breakthroughs in quantum computing and materials science. However, these initiatives are supply-side responses to a demand shock — they build the pipeline but cannot immediately close the gap.

**Weakness 2: Supply Chain Fragility**

Supply chain volatility often results in higher raw material and logistics costs, squeezing profit margins. The unpredictable nature of international relations means BAE Systems must constantly adapt its sourcing strategies, which can introduce operational inefficiencies and increase overheads.

Companies across the supply chain face a classic dilemma — they know they should expand, in some cases doubling capacity, yet hesitate without long-term contractual commitments, further worsening the bottleneck. The path forward lies in targeted investments that expand capacity, boost productivity, and strengthen risk management.

**Weakness 3: Ethical and Reputational Exposure**

A 2024 report from Action on Armed Violence investigates BAE Systems' secretive client list spanning the previous decade, scrutinising the ethical implications of arming countries with dubious human rights records and unstable political regimes. BAE Systems has definite or reported trading relationships with 93 countries around the world — just under half the globe. This reputational exposure creates ESG investor risk and could limit access to capital markets or government contracts in jurisdictions with stricter ethical procurement standards.

## SECTION 6: GROWTH STRATEGY ANALYSIS — ANSOFF MATRIX

The Ansoff Matrix provides a structured framework for evaluating BAE's growth options against risk. Each quadrant is assessed in terms of evidence and strategic fit.

### Quadrant 1: Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets) — **Active**

BAE is already pursuing market penetration through deeper engagement with existing government customers. BAE Systems recorded a strong order intake of £33.7bn in 2024, which, when combined with a £3bn backlog in Space & Mission Systems, pushed the order backlog to record levels. The risk here is not in winning orders but in *executing* them — market penetration without capacity expansion leads to delivery failure, contract penalties, and reputational damage.

### Quadrant 2: Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets) — **Active via GCAP**

In the UK, BAE Systems will act as prime contractor for GCAP and handle the airframe, with Rolls-Royce providing the engines, Leonardo's UK division the electronics, and MBDA UK the weapons. The GCAP joint venture, to be stood up in mid-2025, will support the "design, development and delivery" of the trilateral next-generation fighter jet, with the manufacturer noting that the era of the future aircraft is "expected" to last "beyond 2070." BAE Systems, Leonardo, and JAIEC will each hold a 33.3% shareholding in the new joint venture.

GCAP exemplifies market development: it takes BAE's existing combat air expertise into new geographic markets (Japan) while creating export opportunities to third nations. The risk is programme complexity and the political fragility of trilateral government commitments.

### Quadrant 3: Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets) — **Active via Ball Aerospace and AI**

BAE Systems is now applying generative AI for spacecraft and mission design and using ML for on-board data processing to enhance satellite capabilities. The Ball Aerospace acquisition is the clearest product development move — it brought space systems capabilities into BAE's portfolio for existing government customers who are increasingly operating in the space domain.

### Quadrant 4: Diversification (New Products, New Markets) — **Selective**

BAE's cybersecurity and intelligence services represent a controlled diversification into adjacent commercial markets. This is the highest-risk quadrant, and BAE's approach of leveraging existing government trust relationships into adjacent cyber markets (rather than entering entirely new industries) is strategically prudent.

### Ansoff Summary

The analysis reveals that BAE is simultaneously pursuing strategies across all four quadrants, which creates a resource allocation challenge. The most strategically urgent priority is ensuring that *market penetration* (the backlog) does not collapse due to execution failure, since failure to deliver existing contracts would undermine the credibility required for market development (GCAP) and product development (space, AI).

## SECTION 7: SYNTHESIS — THE STRATEGIC PARADOX

The analyses converge on a single, coherent diagnosis: **BAE Systems faces a paradox of abundance**. The external environment has never been more favourable — demand is structural, long-duration, and government-backed. Yet the internal environment has never been more strained — talent, supply chain, and production capacity are all operating near or at their limits.

This paradox is captured precisely by the PwC observation that A&D executives increasingly recognise the need for additional capacity, but acknowledgment alone isn't enough. Companies across the supply chain face a classic dilemma — they know they should expand, in some cases doubling capacity, yet hesitate without long-term contractual commitments, further worsening the bottleneck.

The RBV explains *why* BAE has won the orders (VRIN resources in systems integration and programme heritage). Porter's Five Forces explains *why* the environment is favourable (demand surge moderating buyer power; high barriers protecting against most new entrants). The Value Chain explains *where* the execution risk is concentrated (procurement, manufacturing). The Ansoff Matrix explains *where* BAE is placing its growth bets (all four quadrants simultaneously, creating resource stretch). Together, these frameworks produce a coherent chain of reasoning: BAE must resolve the execution gap *before* it can safely pursue the growth agenda.

## SECTION 8: RECOMMENDATIONS AND ACTION PLAN

The following recommendations are ordered by strategic priority, with each recommendation logically derived from the analytical chain established above.

---

### Recommendation 1: Resolve the Execution Gap — Invest in Production Capacity Now (Priority: Critical, Horizon: 0–18 months)

**The problem**: The record backlog (£77.8bn) is only valuable if it can be converted into delivered revenue. The Five Forces analysis showed that supplier power is rising and capacity is constrained; the Value Chain analysis identified manufacturing and procurement as the primary value-destruction risks.

**The recommendation**: BAE should immediately commit to a multi-year capital investment programme in manufacturing capacity, funded by the free cash flow (>£1.5bn in 2024) and supported by long-term government-backed contracts. Specifically:
- Negotiate multi-year, volume-committed supply agreements with tier-2 and tier-3 suppliers to give them the demand certainty they need to invest in their own capacity — directly addressing the PwC-identified dilemma.
- Accelerate factory automation and digital manufacturing (smart factory initiatives) to reduce dependence on scarce skilled labour for routine manufacturing tasks.
- Establish a dedicated supply chain resilience function with authority to dual-source critical components and hold strategic inventory buffers for long-lead-time items.

**Why this warrants priority**: In high-intensity, contested environments, demand doesn't ramp — it spikes. Many of these systems are high-unit-cost, long-lead-time items constrained by specialised components, energetics capacity, and a limited pool of qualified tier-2 and tier-3 suppliers. Failing to deliver on existing contracts would trigger penalties, damage government relationships, and undermine BAE's credibility for future programme bids — including GCAP.

---

### Recommendation 2: Talent as a Strategic Investment, Not an HR Function (Priority: High, Horizon: 0–36 months)

**The problem**: The RBV analysis identified human capital as a critical resource under acute stress. The talent shortage is structural (silver tsunami of retirements), cyclical (defence boom competing for the same STEM graduates as technology firms), and self-reinforcing (tech-native disruptors like Anduril and Palantir offer equity upside and cultural appeal that BAE cannot easily match).

**The recommendation**:
- Scale the Digital Academy from a training programme to a *talent acquisition pipeline* — partner with 10–15 leading universities to co-design defence-specific AI and systems engineering degrees, with guaranteed employment pathways. This directly builds the supply side of the talent market rather than competing for existing talent.
- Introduce a differentiated compensation structure for AI, cybersecurity, and autonomous systems engineers, including equity-linked instruments, to compete with tech-sector compensation.
- Establish a "Defence Tech Fellows" programme — senior engineers from Silicon Valley firms seconded into BAE for 12–24 months — to accelerate cultural and technical cross-pollination.

**Why this is warranted**: "The skills gap is not the manufacturing industry's problem to solve alone — it will require collaboration between industry, government, academia and local communities to develop programmes which encourage young people to focus on acquiring STEM skills." BAE's existing STEM partnerships are a foundation, but the scale of investment must match the scale of the problem. The significant knowledge gap created by retiring veterans necessitates robust transfer systems and creative recruitment strategies, with forward-thinking organisations implementing apprenticeship programmes and educational partnerships that build sustainable talent pipelines.

---

### Recommendation 3: Defend the Systems Integration Core Against Tech-Native Disruptors (Priority: High, Horizon: 12–48 months)

**The problem**: The Five Forces analysis showed that the threat of new entrants is rising, specifically in AI-enabled and autonomous systems. Palantir and Anduril are winning contracts by offering software-first, agile development approaches that traditional primes cannot match on speed.

**The recommendation**: BAE should adopt a two-track competitive response:
- **Track 1 — Absorb**: Acquire or take strategic minority stakes in 2–3 defence-tech startups in the autonomous systems and AI space, following the logic of the Ball Aerospace acquisition but at smaller scale and faster pace. This acquires capabilities and talent while denying competitors potential partners.
- **Track 2 — Differentiate**: Lean into the "AI with Purpose" trust-and-assurance positioning as a durable differentiator. BAE Systems holds a unique competitive position against both traditional defence incumbents and agile, tech-first disruptors. While its peers focus on specific algorithmic advantages or rely heavily on partnerships, BAE Systems leverages its heritage and global footprint to act as the premier integrator of systems of systems. In an environment where interoperability and assurance are paramount, its ability to select, validate, secure, and integrate best-of-breed technologies is its core franchise.

**Why this is warranted**: The Dynamic Capabilities framework requires firms to *sense* emerging threats, *seize* opportunities, and *reconfigure* resources. BAE has demonstrated this with Ball Aerospace. The same capability must now be applied to the autonomous systems and AI space before Anduril and Palantir establish the same programme-lock that BAE enjoys in traditional platforms.

---

### Recommendation 4: Concentrate the Ansoff Portfolio — Sequence, Don't Spread (Priority: Medium, Horizon: 12–24 months)

**The problem**: The Ansoff analysis revealed that BAE is simultaneously pursuing all four growth quadrants. While individually rational, the aggregate effect is resource overextension — management attention, capital, and talent are being spread across too many strategic fronts simultaneously.

**The recommendation**: Sequence the growth agenda explicitly:
- **Phase 1 (0–18 months)**: Concentrate resources on market penetration — execute the existing backlog. This is the prerequisite for everything else.
- **Phase 2 (18–36 months)**: Invest in product development (AI/space capabilities from Ball SMS) and market development (GCAP export pipeline to partner nations).
- **Phase 3 (36+ months)**: Selective diversification into commercial cybersecurity and space-as-a-service markets, leveraging the capabilities built in Phase 2.

**Why this is warranted**: The Ansoff Matrix explicitly identifies diversification as the highest-risk quadrant. Pursuing it simultaneously with market penetration, market development, and product development creates the risk that none is executed well. The sequencing recommendation is grounded in the RBV insight that BAE's competitive advantage in systems integration is *execution-dependent* — it must be demonstrated, not merely claimed.

---

### Recommendation 5: Proactively Manage ESG and Ethical Risk (Priority: Medium, Horizon: 0–24 months)

**The problem**: BAE Systems has definite or reported trading relationships with 93 countries around the world — just under half the globe. The 2024 AOAV report scrutinises the ethical implications of arming countries with dubious human rights records and unstable political regimes. This creates investor ESG risk, regulatory risk in key markets, and reputational risk that could affect talent acquisition (particularly among younger STEM graduates who are sensitive to ethical considerations).

**The recommendation**:
- Publish a detailed, independently audited Arms Export Ethics Report annually, going beyond regulatory minimums to proactively disclose end-use monitoring and human rights due diligence processes.
- Establish an independent Ethics Advisory Board with external civil society representation, with a mandate to review and publicly report on export decisions.
- Embed ESG metrics — including responsible trading performance — into executive compensation, creating accountability at the leadership level.

**Why this is warranted**: The stakeholder theory of the firm (Freeman, 1984) holds that long-term value creation requires managing the interests of all stakeholders, not merely shareholders. ESG failure creates material financial risk through investor exclusion (ESG-screened funds represent a growing share of institutional capital) and talent loss (the primary target recruits — STEM graduates — are disproportionately sensitive to employer ethics). Proactive management is therefore a financial imperative, not merely a moral one.

## CONCLUSION

BAE Systems stands at a genuine strategic inflection point. The external environment — geopolitically driven demand, NATO rearmament, and the space race — has never been more structurally favourable for a firm of BAE's heritage and capabilities. Yet the internal environment is strained in precisely the ways that matter most for converting opportunity into performance: talent, supply chain, and production capacity.

The analytical chain established in this case study leads to a clear and coherent conclusion: **the greatest strategic risk BAE faces is not competitive displacement in the short term, but self-inflicted execution failure**. A record backlog that cannot be delivered is not an asset — it is a liability. Porter's Five Forces identifies the structural forces at work; the RBV identifies the resources that must be protected and built; the Value Chain pinpoints where value is being destroyed; and the Ansoff Matrix reveals the sequencing discipline that is currently absent.

The recommended action plan is therefore not a collection of independent initiatives but a *sequenced, coherent response* to a single underlying tension: BAE must first secure its ability to execute before it can safely pursue its ambition to grow. Execution capability, built through supply chain investment and talent development, is the prerequisite for everything else — GCAP, Ball SMS, AI leadership, and ESG credibility alike.
