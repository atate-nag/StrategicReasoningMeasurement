# ExxonMobil and the Strategic Paradox of the Energy Transition: A Case Study

## 1. Introduction and Strategic Situation

ExxonMobil (NYSE: XOM) is one of the world's largest publicly traded oil and gas companies, with operations spanning more than 70 countries. The company sits at the epicentre of a strategic paradox that defines the entire fossil-fuel sector in the 2020s: it must generate competitive financial returns in the near term from a commodity business whose long-run demand trajectory is contested, while simultaneously managing mounting regulatory, reputational, and investor pressure to decarbonise. This case study diagnoses ExxonMobil's strategic position using **PESTLE analysis**, **Porter's Five Forces**, the **Resource-Based View (RBV)**, and **stakeholder mapping**, before constructing a coherent set of strategic recommendations grounded in those analyses.

The central strategic question is: *Can ExxonMobil's current "advantaged-asset + conditional low-carbon" strategy sustain competitive advantage through the energy transition, or does the strategy's internal tension between fossil-fuel expansion and decarbonisation commitments create an unresolvable paradox that will erode long-term value?*

## 2. PESTLE Analysis

### 2.1 Political

ExxonMobil operates across jurisdictions with sharply divergent energy policies. In the United States, the Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) created tax incentives for CCS and clean hydrogen that ExxonMobil has explicitly cited as conditions for scaling its Low Carbon Solutions (LCS) business. ExxonMobil's updated Corporate Plan states that future deployment of capital into carbon capture, hydrogen, renewable fuels and other emerging low-carbon businesses will depend heavily on policy support and market formation. This policy-contingency is strategically significant: it means ExxonMobil's LCS growth is a *dependent variable* on political outcomes rather than a self-executing plan. The implication for strategy is that political risk is not merely an environmental variable but a direct constraint on the company's own stated growth pathway.

At the same time, the election of Donald Trump and the US withdrawal from the Paris Agreement has reduced near-term regulatory pressure domestically, which reduces the urgency of transition for ExxonMobil's core business. CEO Darren Woods took the chance at COP29 to distance himself from the nihilistic climate policies of US President-elect Donald Trump and outlined his vision for a comprehensive system of climate accounting—but on one point he was quite clear: for the time being, at least, fossil-fuel demand would continue to rise. This signals a deliberate political positioning: neither full climate alignment nor outright denial, but a conditional engagement that preserves optionality.

### 2.2 Economic

ExxonMobil delivered $33.7 billion in earnings and $55.0 billion in cash flow from operations in 2024—the third best year in a decade—while achieving record production in the Permian Basin and Guyana, and record sales volumes of high-value products. These results demonstrate that the core hydrocarbon business remains highly cash-generative at current oil prices, which provides the financial foundation for any transition investment. Critically, return on capital employed led industry for the year at 12.7% and for the five-year average at 10.8%, while free cash flow of $34.4 billion in 2024 covered industry-leading shareholder distributions of $36.0 billion—$16.7 billion of dividends and $19.3 billion of share repurchases. This financial performance matters strategically because it demonstrates that ExxonMobil's incumbent model is currently rewarding shareholders, which reduces internal pressure to pivot rapidly to lower-margin transition businesses.

However, economic conditions are inherently volatile: the company's 2024 earnings fell from $36.0 billion in 2023 as industry refining margins and natural gas prices declined, illustrating the commodity-price dependency that makes long-term planning difficult. The International Energy Agency forecasts that global oil oversupply could average 4.1 million barrels per day in 2026, which would compress margins and intensify the competitive logic for cost reduction.

### 2.3 Social

Social attitudes toward fossil fuels are bifurcating. In developed markets, activist pressure on ExxonMobil is intensifying. ExxonMobil filed a lawsuit on January 22, 2024, to exclude Arjuna Capital and Follow This's shareholder proposal from its 2024 proxy statement—a move described by critics as an unprecedented attack on its own shareholders aimed at stifling investor concerns related to the company's management of climate-related financial risk. The reputational cost of this litigation is analytically important: by suing its own shareholders rather than engaging with their concerns, ExxonMobil risks deepening the perception that it is suppressing legitimate governance debate, which can accelerate divestment from ESG-sensitive institutional investors.

Conversely, in developing economies, energy access remains the dominant social priority. Rising living standards increase energy use by 25% in developing countries, and half the world's population currently lacks the energy needed to address basic human needs. ExxonMobil's management uses this structural reality to argue that sustained hydrocarbon investment is socially necessary, and this argument has genuine merit in the near-to-medium term.

### 2.4 Technological

Technology is the pivotal variable in ExxonMobil's transition strategy. The company is betting that CCS, hydrogen, and direct lithium extraction (DLE) will become commercially viable at scale, justifying its "conditional" low-carbon posture. ExxonMobil has successfully produced lithium carbonate from the Smackover formation in southern Arkansas using Direct Lithium Extraction (DLE) technology, with planned production of Mobil™ Lithium using the company's core capabilities in subsurface exploration, drilling, and chemical processing. This is analytically significant because it illustrates how ExxonMobil is attempting to *transfer* its existing technological competencies—subsurface expertise, drilling, chemical processing—into new energy vectors, which is a classic RBV-informed diversification strategy (discussed further in Section 4).

However, the technology risks are real. Fellow miners embracing DLE have reported challenges on the road to proving the technology, with Rio Tinto flagging delays and raising cost estimates from $140 million to $335 million due to changes in scope and higher inflation. This evidence warrants caution: if DLE and CCS do not scale as projected, ExxonMobil's LCS earnings potential of $13 billion by 2040 may not materialise, undermining the strategic rationale for the conditional pivot.

### 2.5 Legal

ExxonMobil faces escalating legal exposure on two fronts. First, the Arjuna Capital lawsuit has set a precedent with broad implications for shareholder rights. Troves of company documents have revealed that ExxonMobil spent decades delegitimising climate science and stymieing climate policy despite knowing about the dangers of burning fossil fuels, leading dozens of states, municipalities, and tribal nations to take legal action. This historical liability is not merely reputational; it creates material financial risk through potential litigation settlements or regulatory penalties.

Second, ExxonMobil's climate commitments contain significant legal ambiguity. In the company's 2024 10-K, ExxonMobil discusses the risks of climate change and states that "the company's objective is to play a leading role in the energy transition," but it does not categorically commit to net-zero targets for Scope 1 and 2 emissions, describing this only as an ambition "contingent on technology development and supportive prices." This conditional framing, while commercially rational, exposes the company to greenwashing claims if its public sustainability messaging is perceived to outrun its actual commitments.

### 2.6 Environmental

The environmental dimension is the most structurally threatening to ExxonMobil's long-term model. ExxonMobil has not set a company-wide net-zero emissions target consistent with the Paris Agreement temperature goals; its Scope 3 emissions—those arising from the combustion of its products—were disclosed at 730 million tonnes of CO₂ equivalent in 2019, roughly equivalent to the emissions of Canada, and these are approximately seven times larger than its Scope 1 and 2 emissions combined. The strategic implication is stark: ExxonMobil's operational emissions (Scope 1 and 2) are manageable and declining, but the emissions *embedded in its products* (Scope 3) are structurally tied to its core revenue model. Any genuine Paris-aligned strategy would require either a dramatic reduction in fossil-fuel sales or massive CCS deployment at a scale that does not yet exist.

Carbon Tracker analysis finds that 100% of ExxonMobil's capex for unsanctioned oil fields is outside the IEA's "Beyond 2 Degrees" scenario, and 95% is outside the "Sustainable Development Scenario," placing ExxonMobil in the third quartile of 36 global oil and gas companies analysed in terms of stranded-asset risk in a 1.65°C world. This evidence directly warrants the conclusion that ExxonMobil's capital allocation strategy is misaligned with the most likely policy trajectories if the Paris Agreement goals are pursued, creating a material long-term financial risk that the market may currently be underpricing.

## 3. Porter's Five Forces Analysis

### 3.1 Threat of New Entrants — *Low to Moderate*

The upstream oil and gas industry has extremely high barriers to entry: massive capital requirements, proprietary geological data, regulatory licencing, and decades of operational expertise. ExxonMobil's scale—with a market capitalisation of approximately $530 billion—reinforces these barriers. However, the *relevant* new entrant threat in the strategic context of the energy transition is not traditional oil majors but renewable energy companies and technology firms entering adjacent energy markets (hydrogen, battery supply chains, CCS services). ExxonMobil's LCS business is, in part, a pre-emptive response to this threat.

### 3.2 Bargaining Power of Suppliers — *Low to Moderate*

ExxonMobil's massive scale translates directly into leverage when negotiating with suppliers; the projected cash capital expenditure for 2025 is set between $27 billion and $29 billion, giving ExxonMobil significant weight in volume negotiations across its broad portfolio of projects. The company actively suppresses supplier power by centralising procurement and leveraging volume, combining bids from different divisions to gain better pricing on large, complex tenders. This cost discipline is a direct source of competitive advantage and is analytically connected to the company's structural cost-savings programme (discussed in Section 4).

### 3.3 Bargaining Power of Buyers — *Moderate*

Crude oil and refined products are global commodities, meaning individual buyers have limited pricing power. However, in ExxonMobil's growing chemicals and speciality products segments, and particularly in its nascent LCS business (where offtake agreements are critical), buyer power increases. Roughly 60% of ExxonMobil's $20 billion in planned lower-emission investments between 2025 and 2030 is aimed at reducing emissions for third-party customers, meaning that the commercial viability of the LCS business is directly dependent on large industrial customers committing to long-term CCS and hydrogen purchase agreements—a dependency that confers meaningful buyer power to those counterparties.

### 3.4 Threat of Substitutes — *High and Rising*

This is the most strategically significant force for ExxonMobil's long-run positioning. The IEA's current "Stated Policies Scenario" projects that global demand for key fossil fuels will reach a peak around 2030. The IEA's World Energy Outlook 2025 outlines that global demand for key fossil fuels, particularly oil and coal, is expected to reach a peak around 2030 in its scenario based on governments' current policies—the first time this has happened. However, the picture is not uniform: the IEA also predicts that global demand for oil and gas will rise well beyond 2030 under current weak climate action and energy security fears, effectively delaying the peak of the fossil fuel era. This divergence between scenarios is itself the strategic risk: ExxonMobil's capital allocation is calibrated to the more optimistic demand scenario, but if policy accelerates, the substitution threat from renewables, EVs, and electrification becomes existential for significant portions of the portfolio.

### 3.5 Competitive Rivalry — *High*

Rivalry in the oil and gas industry is exacerbated by the current market structure, characterised by industry overcapacity and the inherent low differentiation of commodity products. ExxonMobil's response is explicit: the company is actively widening its cost advantage as a direct countermeasure to rivalry, targeting cumulative structural cost savings of $18 billion by 2030. This cost-leadership strategy is analytically coherent: in a commodity market where price differentiation is impossible, the only durable competitive advantage is a lower cost base that allows profitability at lower oil prices than rivals can sustain.

## 4. Resource-Based View (RBV) Analysis

The RBV framework (Barney, 1991) holds that sustained competitive advantage derives from resources and capabilities that are **Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN)**. ExxonMobil's strategic logic can be assessed through this lens.

### 4.1 Advantaged Asset Portfolio

ExxonMobil has systematically concentrated its upstream portfolio in the lowest-cost, highest-return assets. The Pioneer Natural Resources acquisition is the clearest expression of this. The merger combines Pioneer's more than 850,000 net acres in the Midland Basin with ExxonMobil's 570,000 net acres in the Delaware and Midland Basins, creating the industry's leading high-quality undeveloped US unconventional inventory position, with an estimated 16 billion barrels of oil equivalent resource in the Permian, and ExxonMobil's Permian production volume more than doubling to 1.3 million barrels of oil equivalent per day at close. This asset concentration is a VRIN resource: the Permian Basin's geology, scale, and proximity to US refining infrastructure is rare and non-substitutable. The acquisition is therefore not merely a volume play but a deliberate strengthening of a resource that competitors cannot easily replicate.

Simultaneously, in Guyana, three FPSOs—Destiny, Unity, and Prosperity—are now producing some 650,000 barrels of oil per day, with gross production increasing 58% year-over-year with the addition of the Prosperity FPSO. The Guyana resource is similarly advantaged: low-cost, high-quality deepwater oil with a competitive fiscal regime, making it one of the most valuable upstream positions in the industry.

### 4.2 Technological Capabilities

ExxonMobil's strategy is to capitalise on core competencies to build proprietary technology positions, exercising unique geoscience capabilities and understanding of the global hydrocarbon endowment to identify and prioritise high-quality resources in a cost-effective manner. These capabilities—geoscience, subsurface engineering, chemical processing—are genuinely inimitable in the short term because they are embedded in decades of organisational learning and proprietary data.

The strategic question is whether these capabilities are *transferable* to the low-carbon economy. ExxonMobil's answer is yes: its DLE lithium programme explicitly leverages subsurface drilling expertise; its CCS business leverages geological storage knowledge; its hydrogen and biofuels programmes leverage chemical processing. In 2021, ExxonMobil established its Low Carbon Solutions business focused on carbon capture and storage, low-carbon hydrogen, ammonia, lithium, and advanced fuels, targeting hard-to-abate sectors which together account for about 80% of global energy-related CO₂ emissions. This is analytically important: by targeting hard-to-abate sectors, ExxonMobil is positioning in areas where its industrial-scale capabilities are genuinely differentiated from renewable energy competitors, whose strengths lie in power generation rather than industrial decarbonisation.

### 4.3 Structural Cost Advantage

ExxonMobil achieved $12.1 billion in cumulative structural cost savings since 2019, more than offsetting inflation and growth costs. This sustained cost discipline is a VRIN capability because it is embedded in organisational processes—procurement centralisation, technology deployment, operational standardisation—that rivals cannot replicate quickly. The structural cost savings programme directly supports the competitive strategy of cost leadership identified in the Five Forces analysis, creating a coherent chain from capability to competitive position to financial performance.

### 4.4 CCS Infrastructure as a Durable Asset

The acquisition of Denbury provides ExxonMobil with the largest owned and operated CO₂ pipeline network in the US at 1,300 miles, including nearly 925 miles of CO₂ pipelines in Louisiana, Texas, and Mississippi, as well as 10 strategically located onshore sequestration sites, underpinning multiple low-carbon value chains including CCS, hydrogen, ammonia, biofuels, and direct air capture. This infrastructure is a rare, non-substitutable asset: CO₂ pipeline networks cannot be replicated quickly, and their geographic positioning within the largest US industrial emissions corridor creates a natural monopoly-like position in Gulf Coast CCS services. This asset therefore satisfies all four VRIN criteria and represents ExxonMobil's clearest durable competitive advantage in the low-carbon economy.

## 5. Stakeholder Analysis

A stakeholder map reveals that ExxonMobil faces a **high-power/high-interest** conflict between two groups whose demands are structurally incompatible in the short term.

**Shareholders and financial investors** (high power, high interest) currently reward ExxonMobil's fossil-fuel-expansion strategy. The company delivered industry-leading total shareholder returns of 11%, 25%, and 14% for the last one, three, and five years respectively, and plans to extend its annual $20 billion share-repurchase programme through 2026. This financial performance creates a powerful incentive to maintain the status quo, and management's strategy is explicitly calibrated to this constituency.

**Climate-activist investors and regulators** (high power, high interest) are demanding a fundamentally different strategy. ExxonMobil's climate strategy—which seeks to increase oil and gas production, sets production and greenhouse gas reduction targets that are misaligned with the objectives of the Paris Agreement, and overrelies on untested carbon capture technologies—poses significant company-specific and portfolio-wide risks to diversified investors seeking long-term returns. The Arjuna Capital lawsuit and the broader ESG investor pressure represent a growing coalition that, while currently losing the shareholder vote, is building institutional momentum.

**Governments in developing economies** (high power, moderate interest) are a third critical stakeholder: their energy security priorities align with ExxonMobil's continued hydrocarbon supply, providing political legitimacy for the company's argument that fossil fuels remain socially necessary.

The stakeholder tension is analytically important because it reveals that ExxonMobil's conditional low-carbon strategy is partly a *stakeholder management* device: it is calibrated to satisfy financial shareholders in the near term while providing sufficient ESG signalling to avoid regulatory or reputational catastrophe. CEO Darren Woods' strategy is to be a reasonable actor in the climate debate and participate in low-carbon technologies when the business case and tax breaks are right—but not to fall into the same trap as European counterparts by setting overly ambitious decarbonisation targets that cut into profitability.

## 6. The Core Strategic Tension: Synthesis

The analyses above converge on a single structural tension. ExxonMobil's current strategy rests on three simultaneous bets:

1. **Fossil-fuel demand will remain robust** beyond 2030, justifying the Pioneer acquisition and Guyana expansion.
2. **Low-carbon technologies will become commercially viable** at scale, justifying the $20 billion LCS investment.
3. **Policy environments will remain supportive** of both hydrocarbon production and low-carbon subsidies, allowing both bets to pay off.

The PESTLE analysis shows that all three assumptions are contestable. The Five Forces analysis shows that the substitution threat is rising and that rivalry is intensifying. The RBV analysis shows that ExxonMobil has genuine VRIN capabilities—particularly in CCS infrastructure and geoscience—but that these are only valuable if the LCS business scales. The stakeholder analysis shows that the company faces irreconcilable short-term pressure from financial investors to maintain hydrocarbon returns and long-term pressure from climate stakeholders to decarbonise.

The critical inferential step is this: **ExxonMobil's conditional low-carbon strategy is only strategically coherent if the policy and technology conditions it depends upon materialise on schedule.** If they do not—because political support for carbon pricing weakens, or because CCS and DLE fail to scale—then the company will have neither fully exploited its hydrocarbon assets nor built a viable transition business, leaving it exposed to stranded-asset risk and reputational damage simultaneously.

Academic valuation modelling shows that ExxonMobil's market overvaluation relative to an adequate climate policy scenario has increased from 50% to 70% of equity value at risk, with policy delays actually decreasing share value due to continued wasted capital expenditures on "stranded assets" and steeper required growth rate declines to achieve net zero. This evidence warrants the conclusion that the market is currently pricing in a "business as usual" future that may not materialise, and that the longer the company delays a more decisive transition, the more costly the eventual adjustment will be.

## 7. Strategic Recommendations

The following recommendations are derived directly from the analytical chain above. Each is designed to resolve a specific gap identified in the analysis, and each is warranted by the evidence cited.

### Recommendation 1: Accelerate CCS Commercialisation as the Primary Transition Vehicle

**Rationale:** The RBV analysis identifies the Denbury CO₂ pipeline network as ExxonMobil's clearest VRIN asset in the low-carbon economy. The Five Forces analysis identifies CCS as an area where ExxonMobil faces low competitive rivalry and high barriers to entry for competitors. The PESTLE analysis confirms that hard-to-abate industrial sectors—which CCS serves—will remain a structural need regardless of the pace of the energy transition.

**Action:** ExxonMobil should prioritise commercial offtake agreements with industrial emitters along the Gulf Coast corridor, using the Denbury infrastructure to lock in long-term revenue streams before rivals can build competing networks. The company is already pursuing a portfolio of opportunities in lithium, hydrogen, biofuels, and carbon capture and storage that in aggregate is expected to generate returns of approximately 15% and could reduce third-party emissions by more than 50 million tonnes per annum by 2030. The recommendation is to *front-load* the CCS component of this portfolio, because it is the sub-segment with the most defensible competitive moat.

### Recommendation 2: Establish Unconditional Scope 3 Reduction Targets with a Credible Pathway

**Rationale:** The PESTLE legal and environmental analysis shows that ExxonMobil's failure to address Scope 3 emissions is its most material long-term liability, both legally and reputationally. The stakeholder analysis shows that climate-activist investors are gaining institutional momentum. The RBV analysis shows that the company's LCS business is only valuable if it is perceived as credible—and credibility requires measurable commitments.

**Action:** ExxonMobil should adopt absolute Scope 3 reduction targets, phased over 5-year intervals to 2040, linked to portfolio decisions (e.g., divestment of highest-carbon assets, acceleration of biofuels and SAF production). As critics have noted, "by not making any commitment to reduce the emissions that come from burning oil and gas, known as Scope 3, ExxonMobil is shifting blame for the bulk of its emissions onto consumers." Addressing this gap would reduce litigation risk, improve access to ESG-sensitive institutional capital, and strengthen the credibility of the LCS business with potential offtake partners who need to demonstrate their own decarbonisation progress to regulators.

### Recommendation 3: Decouple LCS Investment from Policy Contingency for Core CCS and Lithium Programmes

**Rationale:** The PESTLE political analysis shows that ExxonMobil's current strategy makes LCS investment conditional on policy support, which creates strategic fragility. The RBV analysis shows that first-mover advantage in CCS infrastructure and DLE lithium is time-limited: if ExxonMobil waits for policy certainty before investing, competitors (including technology firms and mining companies) will capture the market. The Five Forces analysis shows that the threat of new entrants into CCS and lithium is currently low but rising.

**Action:** ExxonMobil should ring-fence the CCS pipeline/storage business and the DLE lithium programme as *unconditional* investments, funded from the strong free cash flow generated by the Permian and Guyana assets. The company's broader portfolio of new low-carbon businesses, including carbon products, lithium, and CCS-enabled data centres, is estimated to carry $13 billion in earnings potential by 2040—but much of that remains dependent on regulatory clarity. The recommendation is to treat the CCS infrastructure and lithium programme as strategic infrastructure investments whose value is intrinsic (given the VRIN characteristics identified above), rather than as optionality bets on policy outcomes.

### Recommendation 4: Restructure Stakeholder Engagement to Replace Litigation with Dialogue

**Rationale:** The stakeholder analysis shows that ExxonMobil's decision to sue Arjuna Capital and Follow This is creating reputational damage that outweighs any tactical benefit from excluding the shareholder proposal. The PESTLE social analysis shows that public pressure from ESG investors is structurally increasing. The Five Forces analysis shows that buyer power in the LCS business depends on attracting large industrial customers who are themselves under ESG pressure from their own investors.

**Action:** ExxonMobil should establish a formal Transition Advisory Board comprising institutional investors (including ESG-focused funds), industrial CCS customers, and independent climate scientists, tasked with co-developing the company's transition roadmap. This would transform the relationship with activist shareholders from adversarial to collaborative, reducing litigation costs, improving reputational positioning, and generating credible external validation for the LCS strategy. Investors and environmental activists have previously forced ExxonMobil's assessment of its trade association memberships through the successful passage of shareholder resolutions—demonstrating that structured engagement, rather than resistance, can produce outcomes that are both commercially manageable and reputationally constructive.

### Recommendation 5: Maintain Permian and Guyana Production as the Financial Engine, but Reinvest a Fixed Proportion of Free Cash Flow into Transition

**Rationale:** The financial analysis shows that ExxonMobil's hydrocarbon assets are generating exceptional returns that fund both shareholder distributions and transition investment. The Five Forces analysis shows that the company's cost-leadership position in the Permian is a durable competitive advantage. The PESTLE economic analysis shows that demand for hydrocarbons will persist through at least 2030 under any realistic scenario.

**Action:** ExxonMobil should formalise a commitment to reinvest a minimum of 15% of annual free cash flow into LCS businesses—unconditionally, not contingent on policy—rising to 25% by 2030. In 2024, free cash flow was $34.4 billion; 15% would represent approximately $5.2 billion per year, materially above current LCS spending rates. This creates a predictable, credible funding mechanism for the transition that does not require sacrificing near-term shareholder returns, while building the LCS business to the scale needed for it to become a material earnings contributor by 2035. ExxonMobil's 2030 Corporate Plan targets approximately $25 billion of earnings growth and $35 billion of cash-flow growth by 2030 versus 2024 on a constant price-and-margin basis—a financial trajectory that makes the 15% reinvestment commitment fiscally feasible without cutting dividends or repurchases.

## 9. Conclusion

ExxonMobil occupies a strategically paradoxical position: it is simultaneously the industry's most financially successful major, with VRIN capabilities in geoscience, cost management, and CCS infrastructure, and the company most exposed to the long-run substitution threat from the energy transition. Its current "advantaged-asset plus conditional low-carbon" strategy is internally coherent under optimistic assumptions about policy and technology, but is fragile if those assumptions fail. The recommendations above are designed to resolve this fragility by: (1) accelerating the one transition business (CCS) where ExxonMobil has a genuinely defensible competitive position; (2) establishing credible Scope 3 commitments that reduce legal and reputational risk; (3) decoupling core transition investments from policy contingency to capture first-mover advantage; (4) replacing shareholder litigation with structured dialogue; and (5) formalising a transition reinvestment commitment funded by the Permian and Guyana cash engines. Together, these actions would allow ExxonMobil to sustain near-term financial performance while building the strategic position needed to remain competitive as the energy system evolves—resolving the paradox not by choosing between fossil fuels and transition, but by using the former to finance a credible version of the latter.
