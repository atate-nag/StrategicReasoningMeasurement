## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Mercedes-Benz Group AG stands at a strategic inflection point. In 2024, Mercedes-Benz reported revenue of €145.6 billion, compared to €152.4 billion in 2023, reflecting a decline mainly caused by reduced unit sales, an unfavourable product and market mix, and negative net pricing effects. More dramatically, Mercedes Benz Group reported revenue of EUR 145.6 billion (down 4.5% YoY), EBIT of EUR 13.6 billion (down 30.8% YoY), and net profit of EUR 10.4 billion (down 28.4% YoY). These figures are not merely cyclical — they reflect three converging structural forces: a deteriorating position in China (its largest single market), a stalling electric vehicle transition, and intensifying competitive pressure from both legacy rivals and agile new entrants. This case study argues that Mercedes-Benz faces a *luxury-electrification paradox*: the qualities that define its brand — exclusivity, craftsmanship, heritage — are difficult to communicate through the software-centric, features-on-demand logic of the EV era, yet failure to transition risks ceding the future of premium mobility to Tesla, BYD, and Chinese tech-car brands. Drawing on PESTEL, Porter's Five Forces, VRIO, the Value Chain, SWOT, and the Ansoff Matrix, this study develops a coherent analytical chain culminating in a prioritised, evidence-based action plan.

## 2. CASE BACKGROUND & SITUATIONAL OVERVIEW

### 2.1 Company Profile

Founded in 1926 and headquartered in Stuttgart, Germany, Mercedes-Benz Group AG is one of the world's most recognised luxury automotive brands. The 2024 footprint near 2.0 million cars and 0.45–0.48 million vans underscores its premium scale and margin focus. Its portfolio spans the core segment (C-Class, E-Class, GLC), the top-end segment (S-Class, G-Class, AMG, Maybach), and the emerging EQ electric sub-brand.

### 2.2 The Strategic Problem

In July 2021, the Mercedes-Benz Group announced that "by the end of the 2020s, all new cars sold by the Mercedes-Benz Group (in markets where possible) will be 'EV-only,'" but in February 2024 the company revised that plan. The pace of electrification and the transition to all-electric (BEVs) will be determined by customer and market conditions, so the company will be tactically flexible. This reversal was not a minor course correction; it was a signal that the original strategy had overestimated EV demand and underestimated the structural complexity of the transition.

Simultaneously, the company's most important geographic market began to erode. In China, Mercedes's biggest single market, sales dropped seven percent in 2024. German manufacturers all invested heavily in China in recent decades but are facing fierce competition from local rivals. Sales of top-end cars like the S-Class and Maybach sedans and sport utility vehicles fell 14% in 2024.

Germany's automakers are struggling in the world's biggest auto market, where local manufacturers led by BYD Co. are taking over. They're also contending with weak demand for EVs in Europe after several countries reduced subsidies.

The company's response — the "Next Level Performance" programme — is ambitious. At the 2024 Mercedes-Benz annual financial briefing in Sindelfingen, CFO Wilhelm detailed the company's "Next Level Performance" cost-reduction plan for the next three years (2025–2027), aiming to save €5 billion, with a particular focus on reducing costs by 20% at Beijing Benz. The strategic question is whether cost restructuring alone is sufficient, or whether a more fundamental repositioning of the brand's competitive logic is required.

## 3. ENVIRONMENTAL ANALYSIS: PESTEL

A PESTEL analysis maps the macro-environmental forces shaping Mercedes-Benz's strategic context.

### 3.1 Political

**Threat — Trade Policy Volatility.** Mercedes-Benz's export-heavy model makes it chronically exposed to protectionist risk. Tariffs and trade regulations are pivotal for Mercedes-Benz. Any new tariffs or trade barriers imposed can increase the cost of importing or exporting cars and parts, affecting their pricing strategy and profitability. A US-EU trade deal in 2025 reduced tariffs on European car imports from 27.5%, but political cycle risk remains. Chinese brands (BYD, Xiaomi) and tech entrants target the premium EV segment with feature-rich, lower-cost offerings, challenging Mercedes-Benz's strategy to preserve price premium and market share.

**Opportunity — EU Industrial Policy Flexibility.** The EU is under pressure from carmakers to review the rigidity of 2030 and 2035 emissions targets, creating political space for Mercedes-Benz to maintain a hybrid and ICE presence longer than originally planned.

### 3.2 Economic

**Threat — Slowing Luxury Demand in China.** "Slowing economic growth is one key driver behind weaker demand for premium cars," said Claire Yuan, director of corporate ratings for China autos at S&P Global Ratings. The share of premium car sales fell to 14% in 2024 and to 13% in the first nine months of 2025.

**Threat — EV Subsidy Withdrawal.** Several European countries reduced EV subsidies, contributing to weak demand and a stall in BEV adoption rates. This has slowed the revenue ramp of Mercedes-Benz's EQ range.

**Opportunity — Resilient Ultra-High-Net-Worth Spending.** Top-End vehicle sales including Mercedes-AMG, Mercedes-Maybach and G-Class are up 52% in Q4 2024 compared to Q4 2023. It was a record quarter for Mercedes-AMG high-performance models, totalling 16,169 units and representing a 50% increase. This demonstrates that the highest-margin stratum of the portfolio is not uniformly under threat.

### 3.3 Social

**Opportunity — Sustainability as a Luxury Signal.** 2024 carbon emissions were cut 75% (one year ahead of the 2030 target), with the Rastatt plant using 100% green energy for electric CLA production. These achievements can be leveraged as authentic luxury differentiators rather than mere compliance exercises.

**Threat — Reputational Sensitivity.** Mercedes-Benz faces weaknesses including high maintenance costs, complex technology in some models, and occasional quality control issues that impact their reputation for reliability. As a globally recognised brand, even minor lapses are amplified.

### 3.4 Technological

**Opportunity — Software-Defined Vehicles.** Mercedes-Benz's strategy of developing its own automotive OS aligns with this trend. By 2025, Mercedes-Benz intends to target €1 billion from software-enabled, features-on-demand. "MB.OS is a platform that connects all parts of our business," CEO Källenius noted.

**Threat — Chinese Tech-Automotive Convergence.** In 2024, the combined market share of the Big Three (BBA) was only 35%. At the same time, Li Auto accounted for nearly 10% of the luxury car market, the market share of Wenjie rose to 7.6%, and NIO accounted for 40% of the pure-electric vehicle market above 300,000 yuan.

**Opportunity — Battery Cost Reduction.** For the batteries used in the CLA, the cost per kilowatt hour has been cut by about 30% compared to its predecessor generation. Further improvements in energy density and cost efficiency are being worked on, in addition to advances in cell chemistry.

### 3.5 Environmental

**Threat — EU CO₂ Compliance Risk.** Mercedes-Benz is among EU carmakers not on track to meet CO₂ targets, and would need to pay Volvo Cars and Polestar to purchase credits from them in a so-called pooling deal. This represents both a financial cost and a reputational risk.

**Opportunity — Green Production as Differentiator.** Mercedes-Benz has identified 24 raw materials with increased human rights and environmental risks. In 2024, the company assessed 65% of these materials. Demonstrating supply chain sustainability can attract ESG-conscious institutional investors and UHNW consumers.

### 3.6 Legal

**Threat — Autonomous Driving Liability.** The rise of autonomous driving introduces intricate legal challenges, particularly concerning liability and regulatory compliance. In 2024, legal battles over autonomous vehicles intensified.

**Threat — Data Privacy.** The MB.OS platform collects extensive vehicle and user data. GDPR in Europe and evolving data sovereignty laws in China create compliance complexity and potential liability.

## 4. INDUSTRY ANALYSIS: PORTER'S FIVE FORCES

### 4.1 Competitive Rivalry — HIGH

The luxury and premium automotive industry is very competitive. The competitors of Mercedes-Benz include BMW, Audi, Lexus, Volvo, Jaguar, and Porsche. The vehicles made by these brands all differ significantly in design, functions, luxuries, and other technical aspects. Beyond legacy rivals, Tesla competes on software and performance. The company's 2024 operating results showed a 31% decline to 13.6 billion euros, with passenger car segment margins falling from 12.6% to 8.1%. This margin compression is a direct indicator of rivalry intensity.

### 4.2 Threat of New Entrants — MODERATE AND RISING

The strategy targets 10% production cost cuts by 2027 and 12 new EV models to compete with Tesla and Chinese rivals. The EV transition has lowered certain barriers: software-centric architectures reduce the mechanical complexity advantage of incumbents, and Chinese state-backed manufacturers can absorb losses to gain market share. The threat is concentrated in the premium EV segment rather than the ultra-luxury tier.

### 4.3 Threat of Substitutes — MODERATE AND RISING

The new S-Class with MB.OS, which will be equipped with the NVIDIA DRIVE Hyperion architecture and full-stack NVIDIA DRIVE AV L4 software, is designed to support future robotaxi operations. This reveals Mercedes-Benz's awareness that the ownership model itself may eventually be substituted by autonomous mobility-as-a-service — and its attempt to participate in that substitution rather than be displaced by it.

### 4.4 Bargaining Power of Suppliers — MODERATE

Premium volumes and halo effects make Mercedes a marquee customer — Mercedes-Benz delivered ~2.0 million vehicles in 2024, so suppliers accept tighter margins to secure reference business and scale. However, the EV transition introduces new dependencies in battery cells, rare earth materials, and semiconductors. Mercedes-Benz has identified 24 raw materials with elevated supply chain risk.

### 4.5 Bargaining Power of Buyers — MODERATE TO HIGH (Segment-Dependent)

Customers in the premium and luxury automotive segment, where Mercedes-Benz operates, wield considerable power due to brand loyalty and choice. However, this power is highly segment-dependent. In China, the terminal prices of the representative models "34C" (BMW 3 Series, Audi A4L, Mercedes-Benz C-Class) have significantly dropped to the 200,000 yuan range — indicating elevated buyer power in the core segment. In the ultra-luxury tier, Maybach and G-Class buyers are identity-driven and far less price-sensitive.

**Five Forces Summary:** The luxury automotive industry is structurally challenging, with high rivalry, rising new entrant threats from tech-automotive hybrids, growing substitution risk from MaaS, and segment-dependent buyer power. The strategic implication is unambiguous: Mercedes-Benz must protect margin through differentiation at the top end rather than volume competition at the core.

## 5. INTERNAL ANALYSIS: VRIO FRAMEWORK & VALUE CHAIN

### 5.1 VRIO Analysis

| Global manufacturing footprint | ✓ | Mod. | Mod. | ✓ | **Competitive parity** |
| Battery cost reduction capability | ✓ | Mod. | Mod. | Developing | **Temporary advantage** |

Mercedes-Benz's brand is a significant competitive advantage, built on a legacy of quality and luxury. This strong brand equity fosters customer loyalty, allowing the company to maintain premium pricing. Critically, only 12% of consumers in the German market are willing to pay the same premium for BYD as they would for BBA (Mercedes-Benz, BMW, Audi). Most consumers still position BYD as "high-configuration and affordable models." This confirms that the heritage premium is real and constitutes a genuine VRIO advantage.

The MB.OS platform is a developing advantage. Starting in 2024, MB.OS is being rolled out across the fleet of next-generation Mercedes-Benz vehicles, enabling them with upgradable automated driving functions. From 2025 onwards, certain functionalities can be upgraded over the vehicle lifecycle through the Mercedes me Store. The organisational capability to fully exploit this platform remains the key gap.

### 5.2 Value Chain Analysis

**Primary Activities:**

- **Operations:** Mercedes-Benz intends to reduce total production capacity from 2.5 million units to 2–2.2 million units, with reductions achieved through production optimisation. This is a value-chain rationalisation: fewer, higher-margin units rather than volume-driven throughput.

- **Marketing & Sales:** The "Economics of Desire" framework concentrates on Mercedes-Maybach, Mercedes-AMG and G-Class to lift margins and capture ultra-wealthy buyers. The target is to raise top-end sales share by about 60% versus 2019 by 2026.

- **Service:** From 2025 onwards, customers can expand the level of advanced driver assistance systems in their cars through the MB.DRIVE programme. This post-sale revenue stream is structurally new and represents the most promising value chain innovation.

**Support Activities:**

- **Human Resource Management:** Workforce adjustments, including offering voluntary buyouts, reducing hiring, and freezing executive salaries, aim to cut fixed costs. By 2025, the company anticipates €2.5 billion in savings from these measures. However, the transition significantly requires integrating new talents with specific EV competencies and upskilling the existing workforce, reflecting the company's recognition of evolving vehicle development requirements. This tension — cutting costs while building new capabilities — is the central HRM challenge.

## 7. STRATEGIC OPTIONS: ANSOFF MATRIX

The Ansoff Matrix (1957) maps four growth strategies against existing/new products and existing/new markets, with risk increasing from market penetration (lowest) to diversification (highest). Each quadrant is assessed against the evidence developed above.

### Quadrant 1: Market Penetration (Existing Products, Existing Markets) — *Pursue Selectively*

Top-End vehicle sales in the US are up 52% in Q4 2024 compared to Q4 2023, with Mercedes-AMG achieving a record quarter of 16,169 units and a 50% increase. This confirms that the brand retains pricing power with affluent American consumers, making selective penetration in the US and Middle East viable. However, aggressive price competition in China would erode the brand equity that is Mercedes-Benz's core asset. The Economics of Desire framework concentrates on Mercedes-Maybach, Mercedes-AMG and G-Class to lift margins and capture ultra-wealthy buyers, with the target to raise top-end sales share by about 60% versus 2019 by 2026.

### Quadrant 2: Market Development (Existing Products, New Markets) — *Prioritise*

India and Southeast Asia represent the highest-potential new markets. There has been 1% growth in unit sales in Korea and India and 5% in Thailand through market penetration efforts — suggesting nascent demand that a more deliberate strategy could amplify. These markets offer the advantage that local EV competition is less advanced and the German luxury brand premium remains fully intact.

### Quadrant 3: Product Development (New Products, Existing Markets) — *Core Strategic Bet*

This is Mercedes-Benz's primary growth lever. Mercedes-Benz's product launch program starts in 2025 with the CLA, followed by a major upgrade of the S-Class in 2026, an all-electric GLC and C-Class, as well as a string of BEV and electrified high-tech ICE launches at Mercedes-AMG. Overall, sales are expected to gain traction after dozens of new or refreshed models reach the markets until 2027. The software dimension of product development is equally critical: MB.DRIVE allows customers to expand the level of advanced driver assistance systems in their cars — an entirely new product capability that does not exist in the ICE model.

### Quadrant 4: Diversification (New Products, New Markets) — *Explore Cautiously*

Denza was later restructured, and BYD took control of the brand after Mercedes-Benz withdrew in 2024. This withdrawal from the BYD joint venture was the correct strategic decision: competing on volume against BYD in China's mass-market EV space is not a game Mercedes-Benz can win on cost. However, **adjacent diversification into premium autonomous mobility services** — leveraging the Level 4-ready S-Class architecture — represents a lower-risk play that builds on existing technological assets. This should be explored as a long-term option, not a near-term priority.

## 9. CONCLUSION

Mercedes-Benz's strategic situation in 2024–2025 is characterised by a convergence of cyclical headwinds and structural challenges that demand a coherent, multi-horizon response. The year 2024 has been a difficult year for Mercedes-Benz. Between the slowdown in demand for electric vehicles, the stagnation of the Chinese market, the crisis in the luxury sector and the difficulties of the German industry as a whole, the car manufacturer is showing a sharp decline in annual results.

The analytical chain developed in this case study is internally coherent. The PESTEL analysis establishes that the macro environment is hostile on multiple dimensions simultaneously: Chinese demand is structurally weakening, EU regulatory pressure is intensifying, and the technology landscape is being reshaped by software-first competitors. The Five Forces analysis confirms that the luxury automotive industry is becoming more competitive, particularly in the EV segment where new entrant barriers are lower. The VRIO and Value Chain analyses reveal that Mercedes-Benz's most durable advantages — brand equity, the ultra-luxury portfolio, and the emerging MB.OS platform — are concentrated at the top of the market, not in the volume-driven core. The SWOT synthesis crystallises the imperative: protect and extend the top end, accelerate the software platform, localise in China, and develop new markets in Asia. The Ansoff Matrix confirms that product development (new EV and software products for existing luxury markets) is the primary growth lever, with market development (India/Southeast Asia) as the medium-term horizon.

The company's "Next Level Performance" strategy, launched in 2024, aims to reverse these trends through cost-cutting, operational efficiency, and product innovation. But with market headwinds — including a struggling Chinese market and supply chain complexities — the key question is whether Mercedes-Benz's strategic initiatives can outpace the headwinds. This case study's answer is: yes, but only if the cost programme is understood as a *precondition* for transformation, not as the transformation itself.

The Mercedes-Benz model demonstrates that success in the electric transition depends on a strategy-market fit, not a universal approach. While Tesla succeeds through minimalist innovation and Toyota through hybrid pragmatism, Mercedes must chart its own course. That course, this analysis argues, is to become a *luxury technology platform company* — one that uses its unmatched brand heritage and ultra-luxury portfolio as the emotional foundation, and MB.OS as the commercial engine that generates recurring, software-driven revenue streams across the vehicle lifecycle.

The risk of inaction — or of a purely cost-focused response — is that Mercedes-Benz exits this decade with a smaller customer base, a software platform that never achieved scale, and a China position that has permanently contracted. The risk of the proposed strategy is execution complexity: simultaneously restructuring costs, launching 12 new EV models, building a software business, and developing new markets demands exceptional organisational capability. Managing that complexity — with clear ownership, disciplined prioritisation, and honest performance measurement — is ultimately the test of leadership that will determine whether the three-pointed star remains the world's most aspirational automotive brand, or becomes a cautionary tale of a legacy company that could not reinvent itself fast enough.
