# Shell plc: The Strategic Tension Between Value Creation and Energy Transition

## A Case Study in Corporate Strategy Under Structural Industry Disruption

---

## 1. INTRODUCTION AND CASE OVERVIEW

This case study examines the central strategic dilemma confronting Shell plc between 2023 and 2025: how to simultaneously satisfy capital markets demanding near-term returns, regulators and civil society demanding accelerated decarbonisation, and long-run competitive logic demanding positioning for a low-carbon energy system. Under CEO Wael Sawan — appointed in January 2023 — Shell has made a series of consequential strategic choices that constitute a coherent, if contested, repositioning. The case analyses those choices through multiple strategic frameworks, builds an integrated assessment of Shell's competitive position, and concludes with a set of recommendations.

The strategic situation is not merely operational. It is a textbook example of what Teece, Pisano and Shuen (1997) call a "dynamic capabilities" problem: an incumbent with formidable assets must sense, seize and reconfigure in the face of a transition whose speed and direction are genuinely uncertain.

## 2. CONTEXT: THE MACRO-ENVIRONMENT (PESTEL)

### 2.1 Political

In 2024, the world experienced continued geopolitical volatility. Energy security — accelerated by Russia's invasion of Ukraine — has become a primary political objective across Europe, Asia and the United States, paradoxically rehabilitating fossil gas as a "bridge fuel" and giving political cover to LNG expansion. Geopolitical rivalries, trade protectionism, conflict, competition for resources, cyberattacks, and climate events place severe strains on globally exposed energy, natural resources and chemicals companies.

On the legal-political front, a landmark ruling in November 2024 reshaped Shell's regulatory exposure. On 12 November 2024, a Dutch appeals court ruled that Shell does not have to reduce its CO₂ emissions by 45% by 2030 compared to 2019 levels, as previously ordered by the Hague District Court on 26 May 2021. Shell now has the right to adjust its own reduction targets. However, the Court of Appeal confirmed that Shell plc has a duty of care to reduce its greenhouse gas emissions to combat dangerous climate change — meaning legal risk has diminished but not disappeared.

### 2.2 Economic

Full-year adjusted earnings for 2024 were $23.7 billion. Cash flow from operations for the full year 2024 was $54.7 billion — the second-best year on record. Yet lower oil prices relative to the 2022 windfall year meant Shell's headline income fell. Full-year cash flow from operating activities came in at $54.68 billion, beating analyst expectations, while net debt at the end of 2024 was $4.7 billion lower than at the beginning of the year. The macroeconomic environment is therefore one of solid but moderating cash generation — tightening the trade-off between investment and distribution.

### 2.3 Social

Investor and civil society pressure on climate targets intensified. Shell published its updated Energy Transition Strategy 2024 (ETS24). In response, global headlines were almost univocal: Shell 'waters down' or 'weakens' its emissions reduction targets. Meanwhile, critics argue Shell's short-term strategy undermines long-term sustainability and ignores the urgent need to transition away from fossil fuels, while the company reported better-than-expected profits driven largely by gas sales.

### 2.4 Technological

LNG liquefaction, deepwater extraction, and AI-driven trading optimisation remain areas where Shell has accumulated proprietary advantage. The emergence of cheaper renewables, however, is altering the long-run economics of the energy system. Global demand for liquefied natural gas is estimated to rise by more than 50% by 2040, as several Asian countries use more LNG to support their economic development — a projection Shell uses to justify its LNG pivot, though this forecast is contested.

### 2.5 Environmental

Shell has abandoned its medium-term climate targets that were set in 2021. The company lowered its target and abandoned the 2035 net carbon intensity goal. Specifically, Shell has "chosen to retire" its 2035 target of a 45% reduction in net carbon intensity due to "uncertainty in the pace of change in the energy transition". Shell's 2024 Energy Transition Strategy now targets a 15–20% reduction in net carbon intensity by 2030, down from the prior 20% commitment. Shell spent seven times more on oil and gas than on renewables in 2024, with capital expenditure on its oil and gas businesses topping £14 billion, compared to just over £2 billion on renewables and energy solutions.

### 2.6 Legal

Beyond Milieudefensie, Shell faces a growing wave of climate litigation globally. The Milieudefensie ruling was regarded as a watershed moment in the climate battle and sparked a wave of lawsuits against other fossil fuel companies. Even with the appeal victory, the legal and reputational environment remains hostile in European jurisdictions.

## 3. INDUSTRY ANALYSIS: PORTER'S FIVE FORCES

Porter's Five Forces (1980) provides the structural context within which Shell's strategic choices must be evaluated.

### Force 1: Rivalry Among Existing Competitors — **High**

The integrated oil and gas (IOC) sector is an oligopoly of globally scaled incumbents. TotalEnergies and BP compete head-to-head with Shell in LNG contracts, North Sea offshore wind and European renewable projects. ExxonMobil competes across the entire energy value chain. Rivalry is intensifying as each major attempts to differentiate its transition narrative while defending hydrocarbon cash flows. The competitive dynamic is particularly acute in LNG: Shell's 66 million tonnes of annual LNG sales would swell to 103 million tonnes with BP's 37 million tonnes — securing over 20% of the global LNG market — which would dwarf rivals like TotalEnergies, whose 25 million tonnes pale in comparison. This illustrates how consolidation potential is reshaping competitive rivalry.

### Force 2: Threat of New Entrants — **Low to Moderate**

Capital requirements, regulatory complexity, and the need for integrated value chains create formidable barriers. However, National Oil Companies (NOCs) — state-backed entities in Saudi Arabia, Abu Dhabi and Qatar — are expanding internationally and represent a structurally different form of competition, unconstrained by Western shareholder return expectations. In renewables, new entrants (utilities, pure-play developers) are eroding Shell's potential future market position.

### Force 3: Bargaining Power of Suppliers — **Moderate**

The market for advanced seismic survey technology is dominated by a handful of firms, giving them considerable leverage in pricing and contract terms. Oilfield services companies (Schlumberger, Halliburton, Baker Hughes) retain pricing power during periods of high activity. However, Shell's scale and long-term contracting relationships partially offset this.

### Force 4: Bargaining Power of Buyers — **Increasing**

In LNG, long-term offtake contracts historically locked in buyers. But as spot markets mature and alternative suppliers proliferate, buyer power is growing. In retail and downstream, the rise of EVs and energy efficiency is structurally reducing demand for petroleum products, giving consumers more exit options over time.

### Force 5: Threat of Substitutes — **High and Growing**

The long-run substitution threat from renewables, EVs and green hydrogen is the defining strategic challenge. In the short-to-medium term, however, Shell's 2025 Energy Security Scenarios outline how intensifying geopolitics, economic fragility, technological disruption and climate pressures are driving a global polycrisis that shapes three divergent pathways — implying that the pace of substitution is deeply uncertain. Shell is betting that this uncertainty extends its LNG runway.

**Five Forces Summary:** The industry structure is one of high rivalry, growing substitution threats, and moderate-to-high buyer power — a combination that compresses long-run profitability and demands strategic differentiation. Shell's response is to double down on the segment (integrated LNG) where its competitive advantage is most durable.

## 4. INTERNAL ANALYSIS: RESOURCES, CAPABILITIES AND THE VALUE CHAIN

### 4.1 Resource-Based View (RBV)

Barney's (1991) VRIO framework asks whether a firm's resources are Valuable, Rare, Inimitable and Organised to exploit. Shell's most strategically significant resources are:

**LNG Integration Capability:** With supply from all the major gas producing regions, long-term sales focused on growth markets in Asia, and world-class trading and optimisation capabilities, Shell is building a cost- and carbon-competitive portfolio. Shell accounts for nearly 17% of global LNG trading volume, supplying 67 million tonnes of LNG in 2023 — making it the world's largest LNG trader with extensive international operations. This integrated position — spanning liquefaction, shipping, trading and end-customer sales — is rare and difficult to replicate quickly.

**Trading Capability:** Shell's global trading operation is a genuine source of competitive advantage. Shell became one of the world's largest traders and suppliers of sustainable aviation fuel in 2024, achieved because of long-term agreements with producers, the strength of customer relationships, and strategic investments in logistics around key terminals and airports. The trading book generates returns that are largely decorrelated from commodity price cycles.

**Financial Firepower:** Free cash flow for 2024 was $39.5 billion, and Shell returned over $22.5 billion to shareholders in 2024. This financial capacity to simultaneously invest, distribute and reduce debt is itself a competitive resource.

**Brand and Operational Scale:** Shell operates in over 70 countries, with a global retail network, proprietary technology and decades of relationships with governments and NOCs. These are assets that new entrants or pure-play renewables companies cannot replicate on relevant timescales.

### 4.2 The BCG Portfolio Matrix Applied to Shell's Business Units

The BCG Growth-Share Matrix (Henderson, 1970) classifies business units by market growth rate and relative market share:

| Business Unit | Market Growth | Relative Market Share | BCG Classification |
|---|---|---|---|
| Integrated Gas / LNG | High (>50% demand growth to 2040 projected) | Very High (world's largest trader) | **Star** |
| Upstream Oil | Moderate/Declining long-run | High | **Cash Cow** |
| Chemicals & Products | Low/Declining | Moderate | **Dog / Divest** |
| Renewables & Energy Solutions | High | Low (nascent) | **Question Mark** |
| Marketing (Retail/EV) | Moderate | High (in retail) | **Cash Cow transitioning** |

This portfolio mapping explains Shell's capital allocation logic under Sawan: milk the upstream cash cow, invest in the LNG star, rationalise the chemicals dog, and remain cautious about the renewables question mark pending proof of returns.

## 5. SHELL'S STRATEGIC CHOICES: THE SAWAN PIVOT

### 5.1 The "Performance, Discipline, Simplification" Strategy

At the June 2023 Capital Markets Day, Sawan articulated a clear strategic logic. Shell set out how its strategy delivers more value with less emissions, emphasising the "more value" part, with performance, discipline and simplification as guiding principles for capital allocation to enhance shareholder distributions while enabling the energy transition. The strategy included a target of 10% per annum free cash flow per share growth through to 2025, with a ruthless focus on performance, discipline and simplification, enabled by a structural cost reduction of $2–3 billion by end 2025 and a lowering of capital spend to $22–25 billion per annum over 2024 and 2025.

### 5.2 The 2025 Capital Markets Day: Doubling Down

By March 2025, Shell had accelerated this logic further. Shell is strengthening its commitment to value creation, with spend lowered to $20–22 billion per year for 2025–2028, and a target to grow free cash flow per share by more than 10% per year through to 2030, while maintaining the climate targets set out in Shell's Energy Transition Strategy 2024.

Shareholder returns were raised: Shell bolstered shareholder distributions to 40–50% of cash flow from operations, up from a 30–40% range previously.

On LNG specifically: Shell is reinforcing its leadership in LNG, targeting 4–5% annual sales growth through 2030.

Cost discipline was tightened: Shell increased its structural cost reduction target from $2–3 billion by the end of 2025 to a cumulative $5–7 billion by the end of 2028, compared to 2022.

### 5.3 Retreat from Renewables

The strategic retreat from low-carbon diversification is measurable. Shell spent seven times more on oil and gas than on renewables in 2024, with capital expenditure on oil and gas topping £14 billion compared to just over £2 billion on renewables and energy solutions. In EV charging, Shell has pivoted from a strategy of rapid, volume-driven expansion through acquisitions to a focused approach targeting profitability, including the divestment of Volta and the discontinuation of third-party software support.

## 6. STRATEGIC ANALYSIS: ANSOFF MATRIX

Ansoff's (1957) Product-Market Matrix helps characterise Shell's growth vectors:

| | Existing Markets | New Markets |
|---|---|---|
| **Existing Products** | **Market Penetration** — LNG volume growth 4–5% p.a. to 2030; deepwater upstream; retail fuels | **Market Development** — LNG into new Asian import markets (India, SE Asia) |
| **New Products** | **Product Development** — Bio-LNG, sustainable aviation fuel (SAF), hydrogen | **Diversification** — (Retreating) EV charging, utility-scale renewables |

Shell is concentrating investment in the bottom-left (Market Penetration) and bottom-right (Market Development) quadrants — the lowest-risk growth strategies — while retreating from the high-risk Diversification quadrant. This is strategically coherent given uncertainty, but raises the question of whether it forecloses future optionality.

## 8. THE CORE STRATEGIC TENSION: A COHERENT CHAIN OF REASONING

The analytical chain from external environment through internal capability to strategic choice can now be stated explicitly:

**Premise 1 (External):** The energy transition is real but its pace is uncertain. Geopolitical shocks (Russia-Ukraine) have increased the near-term value of gas security. Regulatory pressure is rising but uneven globally.

**Premise 2 (Internal):** Shell's most valuable, rare and inimitable capability is its integrated LNG position. Its renewables capabilities are nascent and do not yet meet the company's internal IRR hurdle rates. Shell is prioritising competitive advantages in deepwater oil production, LNG leadership and trading capabilities, while requiring 10–15% IRR hurdles for new projects to improve capital allocation after historically poor performance.

**Premise 3 (Financial):** Capital markets are rewarding near-term cash returns and punishing strategic ambiguity. The buyback-and-dividend strategy is designed to close the valuation gap with US peers.

**Logical Conclusion:** Given (1) uncertain transition pace, (2) superior LNG competitive position, and (3) capital market pressure, concentrating on LNG and cash returns while maintaining a minimum-viable low-carbon portfolio is the *internally consistent* strategic choice.

**The Tension:** This logic is coherent in a world where the energy transition takes 30+ years. If transition accelerates — driven by policy shocks, technology breakthroughs or stranded asset risk — Shell's LNG-heavy portfolio could face a structural impairment that no amount of buybacks can reverse. The strategy is optimised for the most likely near-term scenario but may be insufficiently hedged against tail risks.

## 9. RECOMMENDATIONS AND ACTION PLAN

The following recommendations seek to resolve the tension between near-term value creation and long-run strategic resilience. They are grounded in the analytical findings above and designed to be sequenced and actionable.

---

### Recommendation 1: Protect the LNG Star — But with Selective Discipline

**Rationale:** The BCG and RBV analyses confirm LNG as Shell's star business and core source of competitive advantage. The 4–5% annual LNG sales growth target to 2030 is strategically sound. However, the volume of LNG traded in 2024 grew by its lowest level since 2012, signalling that not all LNG investments will be equally rewarded. Shell should be selective: prioritise portfolio-light, trading-oriented LNG growth (capital-light model) over capital-heavy liquefaction projects in politically unstable jurisdictions.

**Action:** Maintain the Abu Dhabi Ruwais LNG participation (a low-risk, stable-jurisdiction asset) while applying rigorous IRR discipline to new upstream liquefaction commitments. Grow the trading book — which generates returns without proportional capital commitment — as the primary LNG growth vehicle.

---

### Recommendation 2: Rebuild Climate Credibility Through Transparent Transition Milestones

**Rationale:** The retreat from the 2035 net carbon intensity target has damaged Shell's ESG credibility with institutional investors, increasing the cost of capital over time and exposing the company to regulatory and reputational risk. The Milieudefensie appeal victory reduces immediate legal exposure, but the Court of Appeal confirmed that Shell has a duty of care to reduce its greenhouse gas emissions to combat dangerous climate change — meaning the underlying legal obligation endures.

**Action:** Rather than setting aspirational top-down targets that are subsequently watered down, Shell should adopt a "milestone-based" transition disclosure framework: publish annual, audited progress reports against Scope 1 and Scope 2 reductions (where the court found Shell's targets adequate), and set credible, near-term Scope 3 intensity milestones tied to specific business unit actions. This shifts the narrative from ambition to accountability — a more defensible position with both investors and regulators.

---

### Recommendation 3: Scale Bio-LNG and SAF as the Transition Bridge

**Rationale:** The Ansoff analysis shows that product development within existing markets (Bio-LNG, SAF) offers Shell a path to lower-carbon revenues without abandoning its core infrastructure advantage. Shell's investments in low-carbon solutions in 2023 included electric vehicle charging, biofuels, renewable power, hydrogen and carbon capture and storage. However, these are scattered. Concentrating on Bio-LNG and SAF — products that use Shell's existing LNG and aviation logistics infrastructure — offers the highest probability of meeting IRR hurdles while reducing net carbon intensity.

**Action:** Designate Bio-LNG and SAF as priority "transition products" with dedicated capital allocation, separate from the broader Renewables and Energy Solutions segment. Set a target for Bio-LNG to account for 5% of total LNG sales volumes by 2030, leveraging existing terminal and shipping infrastructure.

---

### Recommendation 4: Rationalise the Chemicals and Products Segment Decisively

**Rationale:** The BCG analysis classifies Chemicals and Products as a "Dog" — low market growth, moderate market share, and structural margin pressure. In 2024, Shell refreshed its renewable generation, energy marketing, and gas and power trading strategy, shifting away from assets with infrastructure returns. Impairments in Singapore Chemicals assets signal that the rationalisation is underway but incomplete.

**Action:** Accelerate divestment of sub-scale refining and chemicals assets (following the Singapore precedent) and redeploy the proceeds into LNG trading infrastructure and Bio-LNG capacity. Set a clear 2026 deadline for completing the chemicals portfolio review. This frees management bandwidth and capital for higher-return segments.

---

### Recommendation 5: Develop a Credible Optionality Position in Hydrogen and CCS

**Rationale:** The "Question Mark" quadrant in the BCG matrix — Renewables and Energy Solutions — should not be abandoned entirely. Porter's Five Forces analysis shows that the long-run substitution threat from clean energy is high. If Shell exits renewables and hydrogen entirely, it forfeits the option to participate in what may become the dominant energy system by 2050. The cost of maintaining optionality is low relative to Shell's cash generation.

**Action:** Ring-fence a modest but committed capital allocation — $1–2 billion per annum — for CCS (where Shell has existing projects at Scotford, Canada) and green/blue hydrogen. Frame these explicitly as "option-value investments" rather than near-term profit centres, with clear stage-gate criteria for scaling or exiting. Shell has committed to maintaining the climate targets and ambition set out in Shell's Energy Transition Strategy 2024 — this recommendation operationalises that commitment with dedicated capital rather than rhetorical assurance.

---

### Recommendation 6: Address the Valuation Discount Structurally

**Rationale:** Shell trades at a persistent discount to US peers despite superior cash generation. The buyback programme is a necessary but insufficient response. The deeper issue is strategic clarity: investors cannot easily value a company that is simultaneously an IOC, an LNG trader, a chemicals business, a retailer and a nascent clean energy company.

**Action:** Following the completion of the Chemicals and Products rationalisation (Recommendation 4), consider a formal portfolio restructuring that creates a cleaner "Integrated Gas and Trading" core, with the Marketing and Retail segment either separately reported or partially divested. Greater segment transparency would allow the market to value Shell's LNG franchise at a multiple closer to its intrinsic worth, without requiring a full corporate break-up.

## 10. CONCLUSION

Shell's strategic situation between 2023 and 2025 is a case study in navigating genuine uncertainty under competing stakeholder pressures. The Sawan strategy — performance, discipline, simplification — is analytically grounded: it concentrates resources on Shell's most defensible competitive advantage (integrated LNG), generates cash to satisfy capital markets, and avoids over-investing in low-carbon businesses where Shell lacks a proven edge. The PESTEL analysis confirms that the macro-environment, particularly energy security concerns and the Milieudefensie appeal outcome, supports this posture in the near term. The Five Forces analysis shows that LNG is the segment where Shell's bargaining position is strongest and rivalry is most manageable. The RBV confirms that Shell's integrated LNG capability meets the VRIO test for sustained competitive advantage.

However, the analytical chain also reveals the strategy's vulnerability. The BCG matrix shows that Shell's cash cow (upstream oil) is in structural decline, and the Question Mark (renewables) has been de-prioritised before it could become a Star. The Ansoff matrix shows a retreat to the lowest-risk growth quadrants, which may be rational under uncertainty but forecloses the optionality needed if the energy transition accelerates. The SWOT analysis identifies the credibility gap on climate targets as a genuine weakness that compounds over time.

The recommendations above seek to thread this needle: protect and grow the LNG star with capital discipline; rebuild climate credibility through transparent milestones rather than retreating targets; use Bio-LNG and SAF as the transition bridge that leverages existing infrastructure; rationalise the portfolio decisively; maintain a minimum viable optionality position in hydrogen and CCS; and address the valuation discount through structural transparency rather than buybacks alone.

The overarching principle is that strategic coherence — not strategic ambiguity — is Shell's most valuable asset in a period of structural industry disruption. Every strategic choice should be defensible both to a capital market investor asking "why does this maximise returns?" and to a regulator asking "why does this reduce emissions?" The recommendations above are designed to pass both tests.
